What Is a Power Density Calculator?

A power density calculator determines the rate of energy delivery per unit volume or mass, expressed in watts per liter (W/L) or watts per kilogram (W/kg). A supercapacitor can deliver 10,000 W/L — enough to accelerate a 1,500 kg electric bus from 0 to 50 km/h in 8 seconds — but stores only enough energy for 30 seconds of operation. This power-energy trade-off defines the fundamental difference between power-dense devices (supercapacitors, flywheels) and energy-dense devices (batteries, fuels).

Electric grid operators use power density calculations to match storage technologies to service requirements. Frequency regulation requires response in 100 milliseconds with 15-minute duration — supercapacitors at 10,000 W/L excel at this task. Load leveling requires 4-8 hour discharge at moderate power — flow batteries at 50 W/L but 25 Wh/L energy density are cost-effective. A 100 MW frequency regulation facility using supercapacitors occupies 10,000 m³; the same power using lithium-ion batteries requires 200,000 m³ (20× larger) but provides 4 hours of energy storage instead of 15 minutes. Understanding power density enables optimal technology selection: high power density for short-duration, high-power applications (regenerative braking, grid frequency regulation, power tools); moderate power density for medium-duration applications (electric vehicles, UPS systems); low power density acceptable for long-duration applications (seasonal storage, base-load power).

Power Density Formula With Calculations

Power density is defined as power per unit volume or mass:

p_v = P / V (volumetric power density, W/L or W/m³)

p_m = P / m (gravimetric power density, W/kg)

Where P is power (W), V is volume (L or m³), and m is mass (kg). For electrical devices:

P = V × I (DC power)

P = V × I × cos(φ) (AC power, with power factor)

For batteries, maximum power is limited by internal resistance:

P_max = V² / (4 × R_internal) (maximum power transfer theorem)

For capacitors and supercapacitors:

P_max = V² / (4 × ESR) where ESR is equivalent series resistance

Specific power relates to specific energy through discharge time:

p = e / t where t is discharge time at that power level

Unit conversions: 1 kW/L = 1,000 W/L = 1 MW/m³; 1 W/kg = 0.001 kW/kg; 1 hp/L = 745.7 W/L.

Practical example: A lithium-ion battery cell has nominal voltage 3.7 V, capacity 3.5 Ah, internal resistance 0.003 Ω (3 mΩ), mass 48 g, volume 18 mL. Maximum continuous power (limited by heat): P_cont = 10C rate = 35 A × 3.7 V = 129.5 W. Peak power (10 seconds): P_peak = V² / (4R) = 3.7² / (4 × 0.003) = 13.69 / 0.012 = 1,141 W. Gravimetric power density: continuous = 129.5 W / 0.048 kg = 2,698 W/kg = 2.7 kW/kg; peak = 1,141 / 0.048 = 23,771 W/kg = 23.8 kW/kg. Volumetric power density: continuous = 129.5 W / 0.018 L = 7,194 W/L = 7.2 kW/L; peak = 1,141 / 0.018 = 63,389 W/L = 63.4 kW/L. Compare to supercapacitor: 3,000 F, 2.7 V, ESR 0.0003 Ω (0.3 mΩ), mass 500 g, volume 500 mL. P_peak = 2.7² / (4 × 0.0003) = 7.29 / 0.0012 = 6,075 W. Power density: 6,075 W / 0.5 kg = 12,150 W/kg = 12.2 kW/kg; 6,075 W / 0.5 L = 12,150 W/L = 12.2 kW/L. Supercapacitor has 5× higher continuous power density than battery but stores only 3 Wh vs. 13 Wh — the power-energy trade-off is evident.

6 Steps To Calculate Power Density

  1. Identify the power source and its rating: Determine maximum continuous power and peak power ratings. For batteries: continuous power = C-rate × capacity × voltage. A 100 Ah, 400 V battery pack with 2C continuous rating: P_cont = 2 × 100 Ah × 400 V = 80,000 W = 80 kW. Peak power (30 seconds): often 3-5C, so 120-200 kW. For engines: rated power at specific RPM. A 2.0 L turbocharged engine: 180 kW at 5,500 RPM. For fuel cells: rated power at optimal operating point. A 100 kW fuel cell stack delivers 100 kW continuously, 150 kW peak (30 seconds). For capacitors: calculate from ESR and voltage. Always distinguish continuous vs. peak — continuous power density determines sustained performance; peak power density determines acceleration, surge capability.
  2. Measure or obtain system volume and mass: Include all components required for power delivery. Battery pack: cells + cooling + BMS + housing + contactors. A Tesla Model 3 battery: 480 kg mass, 1,200 L volume (including structural pack integration). Engine: block + heads + accessories (alternator, water pump, turbocharger). A 2.0 L engine displacement requires ~0.5 m³ total volume including intake, exhaust, cooling. Fuel cell system: stack + balance of plant (compressors, humidifiers, cooling). A 100 kW automotive fuel cell: 85 kg stack, 200 kg complete system, 300 L volume. Supercapacitor module: cells + busbars + enclosure. A 500 V, 50 F supercapacitor bank: 150 kg, 200 L. Always specify system boundaries — cell-level power density is 3-5× higher than system-level due to inactive components. For fair comparisons, use system-level metrics.
  3. Determine operating conditions: Power density depends on temperature, state of charge, and age. Li-ion batteries: maximum power at 25-35°C, 50-80% state of charge (SOC). At -20°C, power density drops 60-80% due to increased internal resistance. At 10% SOC, voltage sag limits power. At 90% SOC, charging power limited to prevent lithium plating. A battery rated 3 kW/kg at 25°C, 50% SOC delivers only 1.2 kW/kg at -20°C, 10% SOC. Fuel cells: peak power at 80°C, 100% humidity. Below 60°C, membrane resistance increases, reducing power density. Above 90°C, membrane dries, also reducing power. Engines: peak power at sea level, 25°C. At 3,000 m altitude, air density drops 30%, turbocharged engine power drops 15-20%, naturally aspirated drops 30%. Always specify operating conditions when reporting power density — room-temperature, optimal SOC values don't reflect real-world extremes.
  4. Apply the power density formula: p = P / V or p = P / m. For a Formula E racing battery: 54 kWh capacity, 250 kW continuous power, 300 kg mass, 400 L volume. Power density: 250,000 W / 300 kg = 833 W/kg = 0.83 kW/kg; 250,000 W / 400 L = 625 W/L = 0.63 kW/L. Compare to Formula 1 engine (1.6 L V6 turbo hybrid): 750 kW total (550 kW ICE + 200 kW MGU-K + 120 kW MGU-H), 145 kg mass, 0.8 m³ volume. Power density: 750,000 / 145 = 5,172 W/kg = 5.17 kW/kg; 750,000 / 800 L = 938 W/L = 0.94 kW/L. The F1 powertrain has 6× higher gravimetric power density than Formula E battery — explaining why F1 cars are faster despite similar total power. However, F1 engines last 2,000-3,000 km; Formula E batteries last 100,000+ km. Power density vs. durability trade-off.
  5. Account for duty cycle and thermal limits: Continuous power density is limited by heat dissipation. A motor rated 100 kW continuous may deliver 300 kW for 30 seconds before overheating. Thermal time constant τ = m × Cp / (h × A), where m is mass, Cp is specific heat, h is heat transfer coefficient, A is surface area. For a 50 kg motor with Cp = 500 J/(kg·K), h = 50 W/(m²·K), A = 0.5 m²: τ = 50 × 500 / (50 × 0.5) = 25,000 / 25 = 1,000 seconds = 16.7 minutes. Temperature rise for 300 kW operation (200 kW excess over continuous, 10% converted to heat = 20 kW heating): ΔT = P_heat × τ / (m × Cp) = 20,000 × 1,000 / (50 × 500) = 20,000,000 / 25,000 = 800°C — far exceeds insulation limits (180°C for Class H). Peak power duration must be limited to prevent thermal damage. For 30-second peak: ΔT = 20,000 × 30 / 25,000 = 24°C — acceptable. Always verify peak power duration against thermal time constant.
  6. Compare to application requirements: Different applications have different power density thresholds. Power tools: >1 kW/kg required for cordless drills (current Li-ion: 2-3 kW/kg — adequate). Electric vehicles: >2 kW/kg for acceptable acceleration (current: 3-5 kW/kg system-level — adequate). Electric aircraft: >5 kW/kg for takeoff power (current: 2-3 kW/kg — insufficient, limits to small aircraft). Grid frequency regulation: >5 kW/L for compact installations (supercapacitors: 10-15 kW/L — ideal; Li-ion: 1-2 kW/L — requires 5-10× more space). Match power density to application: high power density for weight-critical, high-power applications (aviation, performance vehicles); moderate power density for ground vehicles; low power density acceptable for stationary applications where volume is cheap. Consider both continuous and peak power — some applications (hybrid vehicles, power tools) need high peak power for short bursts; others (grid storage, marine propulsion) need sustained continuous power.

4 Practical Power Density Examples

Example 1 — Electric Vehicle Acceleration Analysis: A Tesla Model 3 Performance has dual motors producing 340 kW (455 hp) peak power, 1,847 kg vehicle mass. Power-to-weight ratio: 340,000 W / 1,847 kg = 184 W/kg = 0.184 kW/kg. 0-100 km/h time: 3.1 seconds. Theoretical minimum time (100% traction, no losses): kinetic energy at 100 km/h = ½ × 1,847 × (27.78)² = 712,000 J. At 340 kW: t = 712,000 / 340,000 = 2.1 seconds. Real-world 3.1 seconds accounts for: traction limits (tire slip), drivetrain losses (10%), auxiliary loads (50 kW for battery heating, electronics), and power curve (peak power only available above 50 km/h). Compare to Porsche Taycan Turbo S: 560 kW, 2,295 kg, 184 W/kg (same power-to-weight), 0-100 km/h in 2.8 seconds (better traction, launch control). Compare to Bugatti Chiron: 1,177 kW, 1,995 kg, 590 W/kg, 0-100 km/h in 2.4 seconds — 3.2× higher power density enables faster acceleration despite similar weight. For electric vehicles, motor power density (5-10 kW/kg for automotive motors) is not limiting; battery power density (2-4 kW/kg continuous, 8-12 kW/kg peak) determines maximum power. High-performance EVs use batteries optimized for power (lower energy density, higher C-rate) vs. long-range EVs optimized for energy (higher energy density, lower C-rate).

Example 2 — Data Center UPS Power Density: A hyperscale data center requires 20 MW UPS with 10-second ride-through until generators start. Compare technologies by power density: (1) VRLA lead-acid: 400 W/kg continuous, 60 Wh/L energy density. For 20 MW: mass = 20,000,000 / 400 = 50,000 kg = 50 tonnes. Energy needed: 20 MW × 10 s = 200 MJ = 55.6 kWh. Volume = 55.6 / 0.060 = 927 L — but power density requires 50 tonnes, which at 2.5 kg/L density = 20,000 L = 20 m³. (2) Li-ion NMC: 2,000 W/kg continuous, 400 Wh/L. Mass = 20,000,000 / 2,000 = 10,000 kg = 10 tonnes. Energy volume = 55.6 / 0.400 = 139 L; power-limited volume = 10,000 kg / 2.5 kg/L = 4,000 L = 4 m³ — 5× smaller than lead-acid. (3) Supercapacitors: 10,000 W/kg, 5 Wh/L. Mass = 20,000,000 / 10,000 = 2,000 kg = 2 tonnes. Energy volume = 55.6 / 0.005 = 11,120 L = 11.1 m³ — supercapacitors are power-dense but energy-sparse, requiring 3× more volume than Li-ion for 10-second duration. Cost: lead-acid €100/kWh × 55.6 kWh = €5,560; Li-ion €200/kWh × 55.6 = €11,120; supercapacitors €500/kWh × 55.6 = €27,800. For 10-second bridge, Li-ion offers best balance of power density, energy density, and cost. For 1-second power quality (voltage sag correction), supercapacitors win despite cost — 10× faster response than batteries.

Example 3 — Cordless Power Tool Battery: A professional cordless drill requires 500 W output for drilling concrete, 1,200 W peak for hammer action. Battery: 18 V nominal, 5.0 Ah capacity. Continuous current: 500 W / 18 V = 27.8 A = 5.6C rate. Peak current: 1,200 / 18 = 66.7 A = 13.3C rate. Battery must deliver 13.3C without excessive voltage sag (>20% drop unacceptable). Internal resistance limit: V_sag = I × R, 20% of 18 V = 3.6 V. R_max = 3.6 V / 66.7 A = 0.054 Ω = 54 mΩ. High-power Li-ion cells (LCO or high-nickel NMC) achieve 20-30 mΩ for 5 Ah cell — adequate. Power density: 5 Ah × 18 V = 90 Wh energy, mass 350 g per cell, 5 cells in series = 1.75 kg. Peak power 1,200 W / 1.75 kg = 686 W/kg = 0.69 kW/kg. Volume: 5 cells × 50 mL = 250 mL = 0.25 L. Power density = 1,200 / 0.25 = 4,800 W/L = 4.8 kW/L. Compare to corded drill: unlimited power from grid, but cord limits mobility. Professional users accept 2-3× lower power for cordless convenience. Battery technology evolution: NiCd (1990s): 200 W/kg, 1-hour charge; NiMH (2000s): 400 W/kg, 2-hour charge; Li-ion (2010s+): 800-1,000 W/kg, 30-minute charge. Power density improvements enable cordless tools to match corded performance for 80% of applications.

Example 4 — Grid Frequency Regulation Storage: Electrical grid frequency (50 Hz Europe, 60 Hz North America) must stay within ±0.2 Hz. When demand exceeds supply, frequency drops; storage must inject power within 100-500 ms to stabilize. A 50 MW frequency regulation facility responds to 15-minute average signals. Compare technologies: (1) Flywheel: 500 kW per unit, 250 kg, 2 m³. Power density: 500,000 / 250 = 2,000 W/kg = 2 kW/kg; 500,000 / 2 = 250,000 W/m³ = 250 kW/m³ = 250 W/L. For 50 MW: 100 flywheels, 25 tonnes, 200 m³. Response time: <10 ms (mechanical inertia). (2) Li-ion battery: 2 MW per container (40 ft), 20 tonnes, 120 m³. Power density: 2,000,000 / 20,000 = 100 W/kg; 2,000,000 / 120 = 16,667 W/m³ = 16.7 W/L. For 50 MW: 25 containers, 500 tonnes, 3,000 m³. Response time: 100-500 ms (power electronics). (3) Supercapacitors: 500 kW per cabinet, 2 tonnes, 5 m³. Power density: 500,000 / 2,000 = 250 W/kg; 500,000 / 5 = 100,000 W/m³ = 100 W/L. For 50 MW: 100 cabinets, 200 tonnes, 500 m³. Response time: <10 ms. Flywheels have highest power density and fastest response — ideal for frequency regulation (high power, short duration). Li-ion has lowest power density but provides 2-4 hours of energy — overqualified for frequency regulation, better for load leveling. Supercapacitors balance power and response but cost 5× more than flywheels. PJM Interconnection (US grid operator) uses 150 MW of flywheel storage (Beacon Power) for frequency regulation — the technology with highest power density wins for this application.

4 Common Power Density Mistakes

Mistake 1 — Confusing Peak and Continuous Power Density: Peak power (10-30 seconds) can be 3-10× higher than continuous power (sustained indefinitely). A battery rated 3 kW/kg peak may only deliver 500 W/kg continuous. Specifying peak power density for continuous-duty applications causes overheating and premature failure. Example: electric boat motor — continuous 50 kW required for cruising at 20 knots. A motor rated 150 kW peak (3× continuous) at 5 kW/kg seems adequate (30 kg mass). But continuous rating is 50 kW / 5 kW/kg = 10 kg at 5 kW/kg continuous, not 30 kg. If the 5 kW/kg is peak, continuous may be 1 kW/kg — requiring 50 kg motor, not 10 kg. Always verify whether power density is peak or continuous. Marketing materials often cite peak values; datasheets specify both. For applications with intermittent duty (power tools, hybrid vehicles), peak power density matters. For continuous duty (marine propulsion, grid storage, industrial motors), continuous power density is the relevant metric.

Mistake 2 — Ignoring Voltage Sag at High Power: Power density calculations often assume nominal voltage, but voltage drops under load due to internal resistance. A 400 V battery pack with 0.1 Ω internal resistance delivering 500 A: V_terminal = 400 - (500 × 0.1) = 350 V. Power = 350 × 500 = 175 kW, not 400 × 500 = 200 kW — 12.5% reduction. At 800 A (higher power demand): V = 400 - 80 = 320 V, P = 256 kW vs. theoretical 320 kW — 20% reduction. The maximum power transfer theorem states maximum power occurs when load resistance equals internal resistance, at which point terminal voltage is 50% of open-circuit voltage and efficiency is only 50%. Practical systems operate at 20-30% voltage sag for peak power, 5-10% for continuous. A "5 kW/kg" battery may deliver only 4 kW/kg at continuous rating due to voltage sag. Always calculate power at operating voltage, not nominal voltage. Datasheets should specify power density at rated current with voltage sag included.

Mistake 3 — Not Accounting for Balance of Plant: Power density of the core device (battery cells, fuel cell stack, motor) is 2-5× higher than complete system including balance of plant (BOP). A fuel cell stack: 5 kW/kg. Complete system with compressors, humidifiers, cooling, power electronics: 1.5 kW/kg — 70% reduction. A Li-ion battery pack: cells 3 kW/kg, pack with BMS, cooling, contactors: 1.2 kW/kg — 60% reduction. An electric motor: motor alone 10 kW/kg, motor with inverter, gearbox, cooling: 4 kW/kg — 60% reduction. Comparing stack-level fuel cell power density to battery pack-level power density is misleading — always compare at same system boundary. Automotive applications: fuel cell system 1.5 kW/kg vs. battery system 1.2 kW/kg — similar! The "fuel cells have higher power density" claim is true only at stack level, not system level. For aircraft, every kg matters — system-level power density determines feasibility, not component-level. Always specify: cell-level, module-level, pack-level, or system-level when reporting power density.

Mistake 4 — Overlooking Temperature Dependence: Power density varies dramatically with temperature. Li-ion batteries: at 25°C, 3 kW/kg continuous; at -20°C, 0.6 kW/kg — 80% reduction due to increased internal resistance and slower ion diffusion. At 45°C, 4 kW/kg possible but accelerates degradation. Fuel cells: at 80°C (optimal), 2 kW/kg; at 20°C (cold start), 0.3 kW/kg — 85% reduction until waste heat warms the stack. Supercapacitors: at 25°C, 15 kW/kg; at -40°C, 10 kW/kg — only 33% reduction (best low-temperature performance). Motors: at 25°C, 5 kW/kg continuous; at 150°C (overheated), insulation fails — thermal limit defines maximum power density. Applications in extreme environments must derate power density: Arctic EVs (-40°C): battery power density 20% of nominal, requiring 5× larger battery for same acceleration. Desert solar inverters (50°C ambient): power electronics derated 30% to prevent overheating. Always verify power density at application operating temperature, not room temperature. Thermal management systems (heating for cold, cooling for hot) add mass and volume, reducing system-level power density by 10-30%.

4 Professional Tips for Power Density Calculations

Tip 1 — Use Ragone Plots for Technology Selection: Ragone plots display specific power (y-axis) vs. specific energy (x-axis) on logarithmic scales, revealing which technology fits your application's power-energy requirements. Plot your requirement as a point or region: electric aircraft needs 5 kW/kg and 500 Wh/kg — no current technology reaches this point (Li-ion: 2 kW/kg, 250 Wh/kg — falls short on both axes). Hybrid vehicles need 5 kW/kg peak (acceleration) and 50 Wh/kg (battery assists engine, doesn't provide full range) — Li-ion and NiMH both work; NiMH cheaper, Li-ion lighter. Grid frequency regulation: 10 kW/kg, 0.1 Wh/kg (15-minute duration at 50 kW/kg = 0.125 Wh/kg) — flywheels and supercapacitors excel; batteries overqualified on energy. The Ragone plot reveals gaps: no technology exists between 10-100 kW/kg and 10-100 Wh/kg — this is the "power battery" niche that researchers target for hybrid aviation and high-performance EVs. Use Ragone plots to identify whether your application is power-limited (need higher specific power) or energy-limited (need higher specific energy) — optimization strategies differ.

Tip 2 — Optimize for Pulse Power vs. Continuous Power: Many applications require high pulse power for short duration (acceleration, laser pulsing, welding) with low continuous power. Design for pulse power density, not continuous. Capacitor-assisted batteries: add supercapacitors in parallel with batteries. Battery provides continuous power (high energy density); supercapacitor provides pulse power (high power density). Example: hybrid bus: battery 100 kWh, 200 kW continuous; supercapacitor 1 kWh, 500 kW pulse. During acceleration: supercapacitor delivers 500 kW for 10 seconds; battery recharges supercapacitor during cruising. System mass: battery 400 kg, supercapacitor 50 kg = 450 kg total. Battery-only system for 500 kW: 500 kW / 2 kW/kg = 250 kg, but only 200 kW continuous — undersized for hills, HVAC. Optimized system: 300 kg battery (150 kW continuous) + 50 kg supercapacitor (500 kW pulse) = 350 kg total — 100 kg savings. Pulse power optimization reduces system mass by 20-40% for applications with intermittent high-power demands (hybrid vehicles, power tools, robotics, medical imaging).

Tip 3 — Apply Thermal Modeling for Continuous Power: Continuous power density is thermally limited — heat generation equals I²R losses, heat dissipation depends on surface area and cooling. Steady-state temperature: ΔT = P_loss × R_thermal, where R_thermal is thermal resistance (°C/W). For natural convection: R_thermal ≈ 50°C/W per m² surface area. A 10 kg motor with 0.1 m² surface area: R_thermal = 50 / 0.1 = 500°C/W. At 5 kW output, 10% losses = 500 W heating. ΔT = 500 × 500 = 250,000°C — impossible! Clearly, 5 kW continuous requires forced cooling. With liquid cooling (R_thermal = 0.1°C/W): ΔT = 500 × 0.1 = 50°C — acceptable. Cooling system mass: 5 kg (pump, radiator, coolant). System power density: 5,000 W / (10 + 5) kg = 333 W/kg vs. 500 W/kg without cooling — 33% reduction. Air cooling (fan + heatsink): 2 kg, R_thermal = 0.5°C/W, ΔT = 250°C — too hot. Optimization: increase surface area (fins), use higher-temperature insulation (Class H 180°C vs. Class B 130°C), or reduce continuous power rating. Thermal modeling determines maximum sustainable power density — electrical calculations alone overestimate by 2-10×.

Tip 4 — Consider Degradation Effects on Power Density: Power density decreases with age as internal resistance increases. Li-ion batteries: after 1,000 cycles, resistance increases 20-50%; after 2,000 cycles, 50-100%. A battery rated 3 kW/kg new delivers 2 kW/kg after 1,000 cycles, 1.5 kW/kg after 2,000 cycles. For applications requiring 10,000 cycles (grid storage, buses), end-of-life power density determines minimum acceptable specification. Oversize initially: install 5 kW/kg system to have 2.5 kW/kg at end of life. Alternatively, plan for mid-life augmentation (add parallel strings at year 5). Fuel cells: membrane degradation increases resistance 10% per 1,000 hours; after 5,000 hours (automotive target), power density drops 40-50%. Motors: bearing wear and insulation degradation are slow — power density stable for 20,000+ hours. Supercapacitors: capacitance drops 20% after 1 million cycles, ESR increases 50% — power density drops 30% after 10 years. For long-life applications, specify initial power density 2-3× higher than minimum requirement to account for degradation. Levelized cost of power (LCOP) should use average power density over life, not initial value: average = (initial + final) / 2.

4 Power Density FAQs

Photonic capacitors (experimental) achieve 10⁹ W/kg — a billion watts per kilogram — but store energy for only microseconds. Practical devices: detonating explosives have highest power density. TNT: 5 MJ/kg released in 10 microseconds = 500 GW/kg = 500,000,000 kW/kg. However, explosives are single-use and not rechargeable. For rechargeable devices: supercapacitors with graphene electrodes achieve 100 kW/kg (100,000 W/kg) in lab demonstrations; commercial supercapacitors: 10-20 kW/kg. Lithium-ion batteries: 3-5 kW/kg continuous, 10-20 kW/kg peak. Fuel cells: 2-3 kW/kg system-level. Internal combustion engines: 1-2 kW/kg (automotive), 5-10 kW/kg (racing). Electric motors: 5-10 kW/kg continuous, 20-50 kW/kg peak (with cooling). The power density hierarchy reflects application: explosives for projectiles (instantaneous power), supercapacitors for regenerative braking (10-second pulses), batteries for sustained power (minutes to hours), fuels for long-duration (hours to days). No single technology spans the full range — hybrid systems combine high-power and high-energy devices.

Takeoff requires maximum power for 30-60 seconds — a 2,000 kg aircraft needs 400-600 kW for rotation and climb. Current Li-ion batteries deliver 2-3 kW/kg continuous, 6-10 kW/kg peak. For 600 kW peak: battery mass = 600 / 8 = 75 kg — acceptable. But batteries must also provide cruise power (150 kW) for 2 hours = 300 kWh energy. At 250 Wh/kg: mass = 300 / 0.250 = 1,200 kg. Total: 1,275 kg — exceeds aircraft weight! The problem is energy density (250 Wh/kg vs. gasoline 12,900 Wh/kg), not power density. However, power density matters for takeoff: if battery can only deliver 3 kW/kg continuous, a 1,200 kg battery provides only 3,600 kW — adequate. But at -20°C (high-altitude cruise), power density drops to 1 kW/kg — only 1,200 kW available, risking inability to respond to turbulence or go-around. Electric aircraft are energy-limited for range, power-limited for safety margins at extreme conditions. Solid-state batteries (500 Wh/kg, 5 kW/kg) would address both constraints, enabling 500 km range with adequate power margins.

Charging power density (kW/kg or kW/L) determines how fast a device can accept energy. A battery rated 3 kW/kg discharge may only tolerate 1 kW/kg charge (3C discharge, 1C charge). Limiting factors: lithium plating at high charge rates (damages anode), heat generation (I²R losses during charging), and voltage limits (must not exceed maximum cell voltage). Fast-charging batteries optimize for charge power density: Tesla V3 Supercharger 250 kW, 15-minute charge to 80% — requires battery to accept 4C charge rate (1.5 kW/kg for 250 kg pack). Most Li-ion batteries tolerate only 1C continuous charge (0.3-0.5 kW/kg) — fast charging requires active cooling and sophisticated battery management to prevent lithium plating. Supercapacitors: charge power density equals discharge power density (10-20 kW/kg) — can charge in seconds. Flywheels: charge power limited by motor rating, typically 5-10 kW/kg — 5-minute full charge. For applications requiring rapid recharge (buses at stops, power tools during lunch break), charge power density is as important as discharge power density. Specify both when comparing technologies.

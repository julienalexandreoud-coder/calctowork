What Is an Electrical Cable Cross-Section Calculator?

An electrical cable cross-section calculator determines the minimum wire size (mm²) required for safe power delivery based on current load, circuit length, supply voltage, and acceptable voltage drop. For a 16 A circuit running 20 m at 230 V with 3% maximum drop, the calculator specifies 2.5 mm² copper conductors as minimum size.

Undersized cables overheat, causing insulation degradation, fire hazards, and equipment damage. A 2.5 mm² cable carrying 20 A continuously reaches 65-75°C versus rated 40°C, reducing cable life by 60-70%. Oversized cables waste €200-€500 per circuit in unnecessary copper costs.

The calculation follows IEC 60364-5-52 standards, accounting for conductor material (copper or aluminum), installation method (conduit, tray, buried), and ambient temperature corrections.

Cable Cross-Section Formulas With Calculations

Voltage Drop (V) = (2 × Length × Current × Resistivity) ÷ Cross-Section

Example: (2 × 20 m × 16 A × 0.0175 Ω·mm²/m) ÷ 2.5 mm² = 4.48 V

Voltage Drop (%) = (Voltage Drop ÷ Supply Voltage) × 100

4.48 V ÷ 230 V × 100 = 1.95% (within 3% limit for lighting circuits)

Minimum Cross-Section (mm²) = (2 × Length × Current × Resistivity) ÷ Max Voltage Drop

Max drop 3% of 230 V = 6.9 V. Minimum: (2 × 20 × 16 × 0.0175) ÷ 6.9 = 1.62 mm² → use 2.5 mm² standard

Current Rating Correction for Temperature:

At 30°C ambient: 1.0 factor (no correction)

At 40°C ambient: 0.87 factor (2.5 mm² rated 21 A becomes 18.3 A)

At 50°C ambient: 0.71 factor (2.5 mm² rated 21 A becomes 14.9 A)

6 Steps To Calculate Cable Cross-Section

  1. Determine design current (Ib) from load: Single-phase: Ib = Power (W) ÷ Voltage (V). Three-phase: Ib = Power (W) ÷ (√3 × Voltage × Power Factor). For 3,680 W at 230 V: Ib = 3,680 ÷ 230 = 16 A. Add 25% margin for motor loads with high starting current.
  2. Select protective device rating (In): Circuit breaker must satisfy Ib ≤ In ≤ Iz (cable current capacity). For 16 A load, choose 16 A or 20 A Type B MCB. Type C for motor circuits with 5-10× inrush current. Type D for transformers with 10-20× inrush.
  3. Choose installation reference method: Method A (cable in conduit in wall): reduced heat dissipation. Method C (clipped direct): best cooling. Method E (cable tray): moderate cooling. Reference tables show 2.5 mm² copper: Method A = 18 A, Method C = 27 A, Method E = 24 A.
  4. Calculate voltage drop over circuit length: Use formula: ΔU = (2 × L × Ib × 0.0175) ÷ S for single-phase. For 25 m circuit with 16 A and 2.5 mm²: ΔU = (2 × 25 × 16 × 0.0175) ÷ 2.5 = 5.6 V = 2.43%. Acceptable for power circuits (5% limit per IEC 60364).
  5. Apply correction factors for ambient conditions: Temperature factor (k1): 0.87 at 40°C. Grouping factor (k2): 0.80 for 4 cables in same conduit. Combined: 0.87 × 0.80 = 0.696. Adjusted capacity: 21 A × 0.696 = 14.6 A. If load exceeds this, increase to 4 mm² (28 A base rating).
  6. Verify short-circuit thermal withstand: Minimum cross-section for short-circuit: Smin = (Isc × √t) ÷ k. For 6 kA fault, 0.4 s clearing, k=115 (copper/PVC): Smin = (6000 × √0.4) ÷ 115 = 33.1 mm². However, upstream protective device limits actual let-through energy. Standard 2.5-4 mm² acceptable with proper MCB coordination.

4 Practical Examples

Example 1 — Residential Lighting Circuit: 1,840 W total lighting load at 230 V = 8 A. Circuit length 35 m. Maximum 3% drop (6.9 V). Calculation: (2 × 35 × 8 × 0.0175) ÷ 6.9 = 1.42 mm². Standard size: 1.5 mm² copper. Voltage drop actual: 5.8 V (2.5%). Protected by 10 A Type B MCB. Cable cost: €45-€65 for 50 m roll.

Example 2 — Electric Water Heater Circuit: 4,500 W heater at 230 V = 19.6 A. Length 18 m. Maximum 5% drop (11.5 V) for power circuits. Required: (2 × 18 × 19.6 × 0.0175) ÷ 11.5 = 1.07 mm². However, current capacity requires 2.5 mm² (21 A rating). Use 2.5 mm² with 20 A MCB. Drop: 4.2 V (1.8%). Cable: €85-€110 for 50 m.

Example 3 — Three-Phase Industrial Motor: 15 kW motor, 400 V, cos φ = 0.85, efficiency 0.92. Current: 15,000 ÷ (√3 × 400 × 0.85 × 0.92) = 28.5 A. Length 45 m. Maximum 5% drop (20 V). Three-phase formula: ΔU = (√3 × L × I × 0.0175) ÷ S. Required S = (√3 × 45 × 28.5 × 0.0175) ÷ 20 = 1.94 mm². Current capacity requires 6 mm² (34 A in conduit). Use 6 mm² 4-core cable. Drop: 6.7 V (1.7%). Cable cost: €420-€580 for 50 m.

Example 4 — Solar PV Array DC Cable: 8 kW PV array, 400 V DC, 20 A max. Length 60 m (roof to inverter). Maximum 1.5% drop for DC (6 V). Calculation: (2 × 60 × 20 × 0.0175) ÷ 6 = 7.0 mm². Use 10 mm² DC solar cable (UV resistant, double insulated). Drop: 4.2 V (1.05%). Cable: €180-€240 for 100 m. Undersizing to 6 mm² would lose 252 kWh annually (€63-€76 at €0.25-0.30/kWh).

4 Costly Mistakes To Avoid

  • Using aluminum cable without upsizing: Aluminum has 61% conductivity of copper. 10 mm² aluminum equals 6 mm² copper current capacity. Direct substitution without calculation causes overheating. Aluminum requires 1-2 sizes larger: replace 6 mm² copper with 16 mm² aluminum. Aluminum saves 40-50% material cost but requires larger conduits and special termination lugs (€8-€15 each).
  • Ignoring voltage drop on long circuits: Electricians often size by current capacity alone. A 4 mm² cable carrying 25 A over 80 m drops 24.5 V (10.6% at 230 V). Equipment receives 205.5 V, causing motors to overheat and electronics to malfunction. Always calculate voltage drop for circuits over 30 m. Upgrade to 10 mm² reduces drop to 9.8 V (4.3%).
  • Bundling too many cables in single conduit: Six 2.5 mm² cables in one 25 mm conduit derates each to 13.5 A (64% of single cable rating). Circuit breakers still trip at 16-20 A, allowing cable overheating. Separate into multiple conduits or upsized cables. Adding second conduit costs €40-€80 but prevents €2,000-€5,000 fire damage claims.
  • Connecting aluminum to copper without transition fittings: Direct aluminum-copper connections galvanically corrode within 12-18 months, increasing resistance from 0.001 Ω to 0.5-1.0 Ω. At 20 A, this dissipates 400-800 W as heat, melting insulation. Use bimetallic lugs (€6-€12 each) or antioxidant compound. Proper connections last 25-30 years versus 18-24 months for direct contact.

5 Professional Tips

  • Size main distribution cables for 125% of calculated load: Future expansion typically adds 20-40% load within 5 years. Main feeder sized at 100% capacity requires replacement when adding air conditioning or EV charger. Upsizing from 16 mm² to 25 mm² during construction costs €180-€280 more but accommodates 40% growth without rewiring.
  • Use cable colour coding per IEC 60446: Brown (L1), black (L2), grey (L3), blue (neutral), green-yellow (earth). Consistent colouring reduces troubleshooting time by 70-80%. Document colour scheme in electrical panel directory. Non-standard colours require tape markers at both ends (€12-€18 for marker set).
  • Install cable markers at both ends and junctions: Self-laminating cable markers (€25-€40 for 100) identify circuit function. Label format: "DB1-C15" (Distribution Board 1, Circuit 15). Saves 15-25 minutes per troubleshooting call. Electricians charge €60-€90/hour, paying for markers in 2-3 service calls.
  • Pull cables with lubricant on long runs: Cable pulling lubricant (€15-€25 per 5 L bucket) reduces friction coefficient from 0.5 to 0.2. Enables 60-80 m pulls versus 30-40 m dry. Prevents jacket damage that compromises insulation resistance. One bucket treats 800-1,200 m of cable pulls.
  • Test insulation resistance before energizing: Megger test at 500 V DC between live-earth and neutral-earth. Acceptable: >100 MΩ for new installations. Readings 10-100 MΩ indicate moisture ingress. Below 10 MΩ indicates damaged insulation requiring replacement. Rental megger: €40-€65/day. Prevents catastrophic short circuits on first energization.

Solid core (single-strand) cable costs 15-20% less and maintains shape in conduits, preferred for fixed building wiring. Use solid for building circuits, stranded for equipment whip cords under 2 m length.

What Is a Vehicle Amortisation Calculator?

A vehicle amortisation calculator is a specialized financial tool for construction businesses, contractors, and self-employed tradespeople that calculates the depreciation of commercial vehicles over their useful life. This calculator determines annual depreciation expenses, cost per kilometer, and residual values essential for accurate job costing, tax planning, and vehicle replacement decisions.

You may also find the Machinery Amortisation Calculator, Fuel Cost Calculator, and Hourly Rate Calculator useful.

Commercial vehicles are typically the second-largest asset investment for tradespeople after equipment — a fully fitted van costs €35,000-55,000, trucks €80,000-150,000+. Without proper amortisation tracking, businesses cannot accurately price jobs (vehicle costs must be recovered), plan for timely replacements, or optimize tax deductions. Vehicle depreciation represents 25-35% of total vehicle operating costs, making it the largest single expense category.

This calculator processes variables including vehicle acquisition cost, useful life in years, annual kilometers, and residual value to deliver annual depreciation amounts, monthly allocations, cost per kilometer, and remaining book value. Whether depreciating a solo contractor's van or a fleet of commercial trucks, accurate vehicle amortisation is fundamental to construction business financial management.

Formulas Explained: Vehicle Depreciation Calculations

Vehicle depreciation uses straight-line or declining balance methods, with kilometer-based allocation for job costing.

Annual Depreciation = (Acquisition Cost - Residual Value) ÷ Useful Life (years)

Cost Per Kilometer = Annual Depreciation ÷ Annual Kilometers

Monthly Depreciation = Annual Depreciation ÷ 12

Book Value (Year N) = Acquisition Cost - (Annual Depreciation × N years)

Residual Value Guidelines by Vehicle Type (European market 2024):

  • Small vans (Caddy, Berlingo, Connect): 35-42% after 3 years, 22-28% after 5 years, 12-18% after 7 years — high demand, strong residuals
  • Medium vans (Transit, Sprinter, Master): 38-45% after 3 years, 25-32% after 5 years, 15-22% after 7 years — workhorse segment, consistent demand
  • Large vans/craftsmen vehicles: 35-42% after 3 years, 23-30% after 5 years, 14-20% after 7 years — specialized, moderate residuals
  • Pickup trucks (Hilux, Ranger, Amarok): 45-55% after 3 years, 32-40% after 5 years, 22-30% after 7 years — exceptional residuals, high durability perception
  • Box trucks (7.5t-12t): 30-38% after 3 years, 20-28% after 5 years, 12-18% after 7 years — commercial demand, mileage-sensitive
  • Heavy trucks (18t+): 28-35% after 3 years, 18-25% after 5 years, 10-16% after 8 years — high mileage, specialized market

Useful Life Guidelines by Usage Pattern:

  • Light use (under 15,000 km/year): 7-8 years — primarily local jobs, minimal wear
  • Moderate use (15,000-25,000 km/year): 6-7 years — typical tradesperson, mixed local/regional
  • Heavy use (25,000-40,000 km/year): 5-6 years — regional coverage, multiple daily jobs
  • Very heavy use (40,000+ km/year): 4-5 years — long-distance, motorway-heavy, accelerated wear

Example Calculation: Medium van (Sprinter/Transit) acquired for €42,000, useful life 6 years, annual 28,000 km, residual 25% after 6 years:

Residual value: €42,000 × 25% = €10,500

Depreciable amount: €42,000 - €10,500 = €31,500

Annual depreciation: €31,500 ÷ 6 years = €5,250/year

Monthly depreciation: €5,250 ÷ 12 = €437.50/month

Cost per kilometer: €5,250 ÷ 28,000 km = €0.1875/km (18.75 cents/km)

Book value after 3 years: €42,000 - (€5,250 × 3) = €26,250

Depreciation as % of acquisition: 12.5% per year

Step-by-Step Guide

  1. Determine Total Vehicle Acquisition Cost: Acquisition cost includes all expenses to bring vehicle to working condition. Purchase price: €38,500 (negotiated). VAT (if not reclaimable): €8,085 (21% — sole traders often cannot reclaim). Delivery/registration: €850 (number plates, registration fee, delivery). Initial modifications: €2,800 (racking, shelving, work bench). Safety equipment: €650 (beacon, signage, first aid kit). Tool storage: €1,450 (van racking system). Total capitalized cost: €51,535. Exclude: fuel at purchase (expense), insurance (ongoing), routine maintenance (expense). For VAT-registered businesses, use net cost (excluding VAT). For sole traders/consumers, use gross cost (including VAT) as this is true economic cost. Document all costs — modifications and fittings are part of vehicle asset, not separate tools.
  2. Assess Appropriate Useful Life Based on Usage: Useful life depends on kilometers, not just years. Manufacturer design life: 10-12 years or 250,000-300,000 km. Economic life (optimal replacement point): when maintenance costs exceed depreciation savings. Track: annual kilometers, maintenance cost trends, downtime frequency, reliability impact on business. Example: electrician, 5 years in business, historical average 24,000 km/year, maintenance costs: years 1-3 €800-1,200/year, year 4 €2,100, year 5 €3,400 (major service + repairs). Downtime: 2 days year 4, 5 days year 5 (lost revenue €1,800). Optimal replacement: year 5-6. Select 6-year useful life for depreciation. Annual kilometers 24,000. Total expected life kilometers: 144,000. Review annually — if business expands and kilometers increase to 35,000/year, reduce remaining useful life proportionally.
  3. Estimate Realistic Residual Value: Research current market values for equivalent age/mileage vehicles. Sources: AutoTrader, Mobile.de, van-specific sites (VanWorx, VanMonster), dealer trade-in valuations, auction results. Adjust for: expected condition (maintenance history affects value 10-20%), mileage (high mileage reduces value 15-25% vs. average), specification (popular colors, automatic transmission may command premium), market trends (electric vehicles gaining residual strength as diesel faces restrictions). Example: 6-year-old Transit with 140,000 km: asking prices €9,500-12,500. Realistic sale (private): €10,000-11,000. Trade-in: €8,500-9,500. Use €10,500 (25% of €42,000) for depreciation. Conservative approach preferred — overstating residual reduces annual depreciation, creates loss on disposal. Electric vans: currently uncertain residuals (technology evolving, battery degradation concerns). Use 18-22% after 6 years until market matures.
  4. Calculate Depreciation and Cost Per Kilometer: Apply formula: (€42,000 - €10,500) ÷ 6 = €5,250/year depreciation. Monthly: €437.50. Per kilometer: €5,250 ÷ 28,000 = €0.1875/km. This is depreciation only. Full vehicle operating cost includes: fuel (€0.12-0.18/km diesel, €0.08-0.12/km electric), maintenance (€0.04-0.08/km), insurance (€0.02-0.04/km), tires (€0.015-0.025/km), road tax (€0.005-0.015/km). Total operating cost: €0.38-0.52/km diesel, €0.30-0.42/km electric. Job costing: track kilometers per job. Job requiring 45 km travel: vehicle cost = 45 × €0.45 (average) = €20.25. Include in job estimate. Monthly allocation: charge €437.50 depreciation to overhead, recover through job pricing. Annual budget: €5,250 depreciation + €3,500 fuel + €1,400 maintenance + €950 insurance + €450 tires/tax = €11,550 total vehicle cost. Recover through job pricing at €0.45/km × 28,000 km = €12,600 (includes small margin).
  5. Track Kilometers for Accurate Job Costing and Tax Deductions: Maintain detailed mileage log: date, start location, end location, purpose (client job, supplier, training), starting odometer, ending odometer, kilometers. Categorize: billable (direct to specific job), non-billable business (supplier runs, admin), personal (must be excluded from business expenses). Digital tools: mileage tracking apps (MileIQ, Drivewise, business-specific), spreadsheet, or paper logbook. Legal requirement (tax authorities): contemporaneous records, not reconstructed. Sample period: some jurisdictions accept 3-month representative sample, others require full year. Example week: Monday 45 km (Job #234), Tuesday 62 km (Jobs #235, #236), Wednesday 28 km (supplier) + 35 km (Job #237), Thursday 58 km (Jobs #238, #239), Friday 22 km (admin). Weekly total: 250 km. Billable: 200 km (80%). Non-billable business: 50 km (20%). Annual: 28,000 km × 80% = 22,400 billable km. Vehicle cost allocation: 80% to jobs, 20% to overhead. Tax deduction: 100% business use (if no personal use).
  6. Plan Vehicle Replacement and Funding: Vehicle replacement is predictable — set aside monthly from day 1. Monthly depreciation €437.50 + inflation provision €150 (vehicle costs rise 3-4%/year) = €587.50/month to vehicle replacement reserve. After 6 years: €42,300 + interest ≈ €45,000-48,000. Combined with residual realization (€10,500): €55,500-58,500 available. New equivalent van (6 years future): €42,000 × (1.035)^6 = €51,600. Reserve adequate for replacement plus initial modifications. Alternative: contract hire/leasing. Monthly payment €550-750 (36-48 months, 10,000-15,000 km/year limit). Advantages: predictable cost, maintenance often included, no disposal hassle, VAT recovery on payments. Disadvantages: no equity, mileage restrictions, condition charges, continuous payments. Buy vs. lease analysis: buying cheaper long-term if vehicle kept full life. Leasing advantageous for: cash flow management, VAT recovery, avoiding disposal risk, regular vehicle renewal (image, reliability).

Common Mistakes

Mistake 1: Not Tracking Personal vs. Business Use — Tax authorities require clear separation. Using vehicle 100% for business when 20% personal use is tax evasion. Example: van used for family holidays, school runs, shopping (personal), plus work (business). Total 28,000 km/year, 6,000 km personal (21%). Claiming 100% business expenses: vehicle costs €14,000, tax deduction €14,000. Correct: 79% business = €11,060 deductible. Over-claiming €2,940. Tax audit: back taxes, penalties (50-100% of evaded tax), interest. Solution: maintain mileage log from day 1. Categorize every journey. If personal use minimal (under 5%), document specific instances. If regular personal use, accept reduced deduction. Alternative: lease vehicle through company, pay benefit-in-kind tax on personal use (formal, compliant). Self-employed: honest apportionment prevents problems. Personal use includes: home to regular workplace (if van kept at home), family trips, non-business errands.

Mistake 2: Extending Vehicle Life Beyond Economic Optimum — "It still runs" doesn't mean "keep it." Economic optimum: replace when annual maintenance + downtime costs exceed annual depreciation of replacement. Example: 9-year-old van, fully depreciated (book value = residual €8,000). Year 9 costs: maintenance €4,200 (gearbox, clutch, injectors), downtime 8 days (€2,400 lost revenue), fuel €4,800 (inefficient old engine). Total: €11,400. New van (6-year life): depreciation €6,000/year, maintenance €1,200, downtime €300, fuel €3,800. Total: €11,300. Similar cost, but new van: warranty, reliability, safety features, fuel efficiency, image, no major repair risk. Keeping old van false economy. Rule of thumb: replace when single repair exceeds 50% of annual depreciation, or annual maintenance exceeds 25% of vehicle value, or downtime exceeds 10 working days/year.

Mistake 3: Using Fuel-Only Reimbursement Instead of Full Cost Recovery — Many contractors reimburse vehicle costs at fuel rate only (€0.14-0.18/km) or actual fuel receipts. This ignores depreciation, maintenance, insurance — subsidizing business from personal funds. Example: tradesperson uses personal vehicle for work, 25,000 km/year. Business reimburses fuel only: €0.16 × 25,000 = €4,000. True cost: €0.45 × 25,000 = €11,250. Subsidy: €7,250/year. Over 5 years: €36,250 personal subsidy. Business appears more profitable than reality. Correct approach: reimburse at approved mileage rate (tax authority rates typically €0.40-0.50/km, designed to cover all costs) or allocate vehicle costs to jobs through pricing. Self-employed: include full vehicle costs in job pricing, not just fuel. Track all vehicle expenses, recover through comprehensive pricing.

Mistake 4: Not Budgeting for Large Irregular Vehicle Expenses — Annual budgeting for regular costs (fuel, insurance) but surprised by irregulars: tires (€400-800 every 2-3 years), major service (€800-1,500 every 4 years), unexpected repairs (€500-3,000). Example: year 1-3 maintenance €600-900/year (routine). Year 4: major service €1,200 + tires €650 = €1,850. Year 5: clutch €1,400. Average €1,050/year, but cash flow peaks create problems. Solution: monthly provision for irregulars. Tires: €650 ÷ 36 months = €18/month. Major service: €1,200 ÷ 48 months = €25/month. Contingency repairs: €50/month. Total: €93/month to vehicle maintenance reserve. When expense occurs, pay from reserve, not operating account. Smooths cash flow, no surprises. Include in job pricing: €93 × 12 = €1,116/year ÷ 28,000 km = €0.04/km tire/maintenance provision.

Pro Tips

Tip 1: Choose Vehicle Specification for Residual Value — Residual value differences of 10-15% are common between specifications. High-residual choices: popular colors (white, silver, grey for commercial), manual transmission (commercial buyers prefer), mid-range specification (base too sparse, top-spec too expensive for used buyers), single owner, full service history, no modifications affecting utility (excessive racking reduces buyer pool). Example: Transit Custom, 3 years, 90,000 km. White manual Trend: €18,500. Blue automatic Titanium: €16,200. Difference: €2,300 (12.5% of original €44,000). Over 3 years: €767/year depreciation difference. Color choice alone worth €64/month. Keep vehicles clean, repair minor damage before sale (€400 paint repair increases value €1,200), maintain complete service records (adds 8-12% value), avoid excessive modifications (limits buyer pool). Small efforts significantly impact residuals.

Tip 2: Time Vehicle Purchases for Best Value — Vehicle prices fluctuate seasonally and with model cycles. Best buying times: Q4 (October-December) — dealers clearing stock for year-end targets, 8-15% discounts common. End of model year — outgoing models discounted 10-20% vs. new model (often minimal changes). Registration plate months (March/September in UK, varies EU) — high supply, competitive pricing. Avoid: Q1 (January-March) — new year, full prices, high demand. Avoid: plate month purchases if selling same spec — market flooded 6 months later. Demo vehicles: 6-12 months old, 10,000-20,000 km, 15-25% below new, full warranty. Ex-fleet: 2-3 years old, well maintained, 25-35% below new. Private purchase vs. dealer: dealer 5-10% premium but warranty, finance options, part-exchange. Calculate total cost, not just purchase price.

Tip 3: Implement Driver Vehicle Inspection Reports (DVIR) — Daily/weekly walk-around checks prevent major failures, document condition, support warranty claims. 5-minute checklist: tires (pressure, tread depth, damage), lights (all functioning), fluids (levels, leaks), body (new damage, loose fittings), interior (controls functioning, warning lights). Document: date, odometer, defects found, corrective action. Digital: apps (Fleetio, DriverLink), photos of defects. Benefits: catch issues early (low-pressure tire → blowout prevention), warranty protection (documented defect timeline), resale value (maintenance records), safety (critical defects identified), accountability (driver awareness). Commercial operators: legal requirement in many jurisdictions. Solo traders: equally valuable. Weekly DVIR takes 5 minutes, prevents €2,000+ repairs, supports warranty claims, documents condition for resale.

Tip 4: Optimize Routes to Reduce Vehicle Costs — Vehicle costs are distance-dependent. Route optimization reduces kilometers, fuel, time, wear. Techniques: cluster jobs geographically (all north zone Monday, south zone Tuesday), schedule by location not urgency (where possible), combine supplier runs with nearby jobs, use route planning apps (Google Maps multi-stop, Route4Me, Circuit), avoid peak traffic (fuel consumption 30-40% higher in stop-start), maintain steady speeds (80-90 km/h optimal fuel efficiency). Example: unoptimized week — 320 km. Optimized: 245 km. Reduction: 75 km (23%). Annual: 28,000 → 21,500 km. Savings: 6,500 km × €0.45 = €2,925/year. Time savings: 75 km × 1.5 min/km (average) = 112 minutes/week. Annual: 97 hours. Value: €2,925 fuel/wear + €2,425 labour (97 hours @ €25/hour) = €5,350/year. Route optimization pays for itself many times over.

Tip 5: Consider Total Cost of Ownership When Selecting Vehicles — Purchase price is 45-60% of TCO over vehicle life. Compare alternatives: Van A €42,000 diesel, 6 years, 28,000 km/year, 25% residual, €0.16/km fuel, €0.05/km maintenance. Van B €52,000 electric, 6 years, 22,000 km/year (range limited), 18% residual, €0.06/km electricity, €0.03/km maintenance. 6-year TCO: Van A: €42,000 - €10,500 residual + €26,880 fuel + €8,400 maintenance + €5,040 insurance = €71,820. Van B: €52,000 - €9,360 residual + €7,920 electricity + €3,960 maintenance + €4,320 insurance = €58,840. Van B €12,980 less over 6 years despite €10,000 higher purchase price. But: Van B limited to 22,000 km/year, charging infrastructure required, residual uncertainty. TCO analysis reveals true cost. Use spreadsheet: purchase, residual, fuel, maintenance, insurance, tax, tires, financing. Compare 3-5 year TCO for shortlisted vehicles. Choose lowest TCO meeting operational requirements, not lowest purchase price.

FAQs

Buy if: cash available or favorable finance, planning to keep vehicle 5+ years, high mileage (lease limits expensive), want to modify freely, VAT not reclaimable (lease VAT on full payment, buy VAT once). Lease if: cash flow priority (lower monthly outlay), VAT reclaimable (recover VAT on all payments), want predictable costs (maintenance packages available), prefer regular new vehicles (image, warranty, reliability), want to avoid disposal hassle. Typical comparison: €45,000 van, 4 years, 30,000 km/year. Buy: monthly finance €850, own at end, total €40,800 + residual risk. Lease: monthly €620 inc. maintenance, return at end, total €29,760, no asset. Buy builds equity, lease is operating expense. TCO similar, cash flow different. Self-employed, VAT not reclaimable: usually better to buy used (2-3 years old), avoid steepest depreciation. VAT-registered, cash-sensitive: lease attractive. Calculate both scenarios for your situation.

Two methods: 1) Kilometer-based: track km per job × full cost per km. Job 45 km × €0.45/km = €20.25 vehicle cost. Accurate, fair, requires mileage tracking. 2) Percentage of revenue: allocate X% of job value to vehicle costs. Typical: 8-12% for local work, 15-20% for regional. €800 job × 10% = €80 vehicle allocation. Simple, less accurate. Best practice: kilometer-based for estimates, percentage for quick quotes. Implement: estimate form includes distance field, calculator multiplies by cost per km, total displayed to customer (included in price or line item). Monthly: compare actual vehicle costs to allocated amounts. If allocation < actual, increase cost per km or percentage. Target: allocation 105-110% of actual (small buffer). Annual review: update cost per km based on actual expenses.

Deductible expenses (business use portion): fuel, electricity charging, oil/fluids, maintenance/repairs, tires, insurance, road tax, MOT/inspections, parking (business), tolls (business), washing/cleaning, lease payments, loan interest (not capital), depreciation (if self-employed, through capital allowances). Not deductible: fines/speeding tickets, personal use portion, commuting (home to regular workplace if vehicle kept at home), luxury accessories, non-business modifications. Record-keeping: receipts for all expenses, mileage log for business/personal split, bank statements showing payments. Methods: actual costs (track everything, apportion personal use) or standard mileage rate (simpler, rate covers all costs). Choose method year 1, generally must continue. Consult accountant for jurisdiction-specific rules — regulations vary significantly across EU countries.

Optimal replacement indicators: 1) Maintenance costs exceed 25% of vehicle value annually. 2) Downtime exceeds 10 working days/year. 3) Single repair exceeds 50% of annual depreciation. 4) Reliability affecting business (missed appointments, stranded). 5) Safety concerns (failed inspections, recurring issues). 6) Image important and vehicle visibly worn. 7) Efficiency gap significant (new vehicle 20%+ more fuel-efficient). 8) Regulatory compliance (emissions zones, new requirements). Typical replacement: 5-7 years, 150,000-200,000 km for vans. Pickups: 7-9 years, 200,000-250,000 km. Trucks: 6-8 years, 300,000-400,000 km. Plan replacement 6-12 months ahead: research, budget, arrange finance, time purchase for best value. Don't wait for failure — breakdown during busy period costs far more than timely replacement.

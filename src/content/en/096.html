What Is Break-Even Calculator?

Break-Even Calculator determines the exact number of units you must sell or jobs you must complete to cover all business costs — the point where revenue equals expenses and you start making profit. For construction contractors, renovators, and tradespeople, knowing your break-even point prevents underpricing, guides pricing strategy, and reveals the minimum workload needed for financial sustainability.

You may also find the Profit Margin Calculator, Hourly Rate Calculator, and Site Water Consumption Calculator useful.

Consider a painting contractor with monthly fixed costs of €3,200 (van payment €450, insurance €180, tools amortization €120, phone €45, advertising €300, and owner's target salary €2,105). Average job price: €850. Variable costs per job (paint, supplies, fuel): €285. Contribution margin: €850 - €285 = €565 per job. Break-even: €3,200 ÷ €565 = 5.66 jobs monthly. The contractor must complete at least 6 jobs monthly to cover all costs. Job 7 onwards generates pure profit.

Break-even analysis separates viable businesses from struggling ones. Without knowing this number, contractors accept any work at any price, often losing money on jobs while wondering why the bank account shrinks. This calculator provides the mathematical foundation for pricing decisions, capacity planning, and financial forecasting across all construction trades.

How Break-Even Calculator Works: Formulas Explained

The core break-even formula is: Break-Even Units = Fixed Costs ÷ (Price Per Unit - Variable Cost Per Unit). The denominator (Price - Variable Cost) is called "contribution margin" — the amount each sale contributes toward covering fixed costs after paying variable expenses.

Fixed costs explained: These expenses remain constant regardless of work volume. Examples: van payments (€350-650/month), liability insurance (€120-250/month), tool loans (€80-150/month), phone/internet (€45-75/month), advertising (€200-500/month), software subscriptions (€30-80/month), warehouse rent (€400-1,200/month), and owner's target salary. Total fixed costs for a solo contractor typically range €2,500-4,500/month. For a 10-person company: €18,000-35,000/month.

Variable costs explained: These expenses scale directly with each job. Examples: materials (typically 25-40% of job price), consumables (blades, bits, sandpaper), fuel for job sites (€15-45/job), subcontractor fees (€180-350/day), waste disposal (€25-80/job), and payment processing fees (1.5-2.9%). Variable costs usually range 30-45% of revenue for most trades.

Contribution margin calculation: Price Per Job - Variable Cost Per Job = Contribution Margin. If you charge €1,200 for a bathroom renovation and materials + supplies cost €420, contribution margin is €1,200 - €420 = €780. This €780 contributes to covering fixed costs and profit. Higher contribution margins mean fewer jobs needed to break even.

Working through a complete example: An electrician has fixed costs of €4,150/month (van €520, insurance €195, tools €145, phone €55, advertising €380, software €65, salary €2,790). Average service call: €385. Variable costs per call (wire, connectors, fuel): €95. Contribution margin: €385 - €95 = €290. Break-even: €4,150 ÷ €290 = 14.31 calls monthly. Rounded up: 15 service calls monthly, or approximately 4 calls per week.

Step-by-Step Guide to Using Break-Even Calculator

  1. Calculate total monthly fixed costs accurately. List every recurring business expense: vehicle payment, insurance premiums (divide annual by 12), equipment loans, phone/internet, software subscriptions, advertising budget, rent, utilities, and your target salary. Don't omit small expenses — €30/month subscriptions add up. Example: €480 (van) + €185 (insurance) + €125 (tools) + €55 (phone) + €350 (ads) + €75 (software) + €2,800 (salary) = €4,070/month fixed costs.
  2. Determine your average price per job or unit. Review your last 20-30 invoices and calculate the average. A plumber might find: €245 (unclog drain), €580 (fix leak), €1,250 (water heater), €85 (service call) — averaging €465/job. Don't use your highest or typical job — use the mathematical average across all work types. If you charge hourly, multiply your hourly rate by average job duration.
  3. Calculate variable cost per job precisely. Track material costs, consumables, fuel, and subcontractor fees for 10-15 recent jobs. Sum all variable expenses and divide by number of jobs. Example: 12 jobs used €2,840 in materials + €385 in fuel + €180 in disposal = €3,405 ÷ 12 = €283.75 average variable cost per job. Include everything that varies with each job.
  4. Compute contribution margin. Subtract variable cost from price: €465 (average price) - €283.75 (variable cost) = €181.25 contribution margin. This means each €465 job contributes €181.25 toward covering fixed costs. After covering fixed costs, this becomes profit. Higher contribution margins provide more financial cushion and faster profit generation.
  5. Calculate break-even point. Divide fixed costs by contribution margin: €4,070 ÷ €181.25 = 22.45 jobs monthly. Round up to 23 jobs. This is your minimum workload. Divide by 4.33 weeks: 23 ÷ 4.33 = 5.3 jobs weekly, or about 1-2 jobs per day depending on job complexity. This number tells you exactly how much work you need.
  6. Analyze and optimize your break-even point. If 23 jobs/month seems too high, explore three levers: (1) Increase prices — raising average price to €520 increases contribution margin to €236.25, reducing break-even to 17 jobs. (2) Reduce variable costs — negotiating 10% material discounts saves €28/job, increasing margin to €209.25, reducing break-even to 19 jobs. (3) Cut fixed costs — reducing advertising from €350 to €200 saves €150, reducing break-even to 21 jobs.

Real-World Break-Even Examples

Example 1: Solo General Contractor. Monthly fixed costs: €3,850 (van €420, insurance €165, tools €180, phone €50, advertising €280, software €55, salary €2,700). Average project: €4,200 (small renovations, repairs). Variable costs: €1,540 (materials 32%, fuel €45, disposal €35). Contribution margin: €4,200 - €1,540 = €2,660. Break-even: €3,850 ÷ €2,660 = 1.45 projects monthly. This contractor needs just 2 small projects monthly to cover all costs. Project 3+ is pure profit. Low break-even reflects high-value projects with strong margins.

Example 2: HVAC Service Company (3 technicians). Monthly fixed costs: €18,450 (3 vans €1,380, warehouse €1,100, insurance €485, tools €340, phone/internet €125, advertising €850, software €180, dispatcher salary €2,400, 3 technician salaries €9,000, utilities €290, misc €300). Average service call: €285. Variable costs: €78 (refrigerant, parts, fuel). Contribution margin: €285 - €78 = €207. Break-even: €18,450 ÷ €207 = 89.13 calls monthly. Per technician: 30 calls monthly, or 7-8 calls weekly. This translates to 1-2 calls per day per technician — achievable during peak seasons, challenging in off-seasons.

Example 3: Tile Installation Specialist. Monthly fixed costs: €2,920 (van €380, insurance €145, tools €95, phone €45, advertising €220, software €35, salary €2,000). Average room: €1,850 (materials included in pricing). Variable costs: €685 (tile adhesive €120, grout €85, leveling system €45, tiles €380 if supplied, fuel €55). Contribution margin: €1,850 - €685 = €1,165. Break-even: €2,920 ÷ €1,165 = 2.51 rooms monthly. This specialist needs 3 rooms monthly to break even. At 4-5 rooms monthly (1 per week), profit reaches €1,165-2,330 monthly.

Example 4: Electrical Contracting Business (5 electricians). Monthly fixed costs: €28,750 (5 vans €2,100, warehouse €1,800, insurance €720, tools €580, phone/internet €185, advertising €1,200, software €280, office admin €2,800, 5 electrician salaries €17,500, utilities €420, misc €165). Average commercial job: €3,850. Variable costs: €1,285 (wire, panels, fixtures, conduit, fuel). Contribution margin: €3,850 - €1,285 = €2,565. Break-even: €28,750 ÷ €2,565 = 11.21 jobs monthly. With 5 electricians, that's 2.2 jobs per electrician monthly, or about 1 job every 2 weeks per person. At 15-20 jobs monthly, the company generates €9,675-22,425 profit.

Example 5: Window Cleaning Business. Monthly fixed costs: €1,680 (van €290, insurance €95, equipment €45, phone €40, advertising €180, salary €1,030). Average residential job: €185 (single-family home). Variable costs: €32 (cleaning solution, squeegee wear, fuel). Contribution margin: €185 - €32 = €153. Break-even: €1,680 ÷ €153 = 10.98 jobs monthly. Approximately 11 homes monthly, or 3 homes weekly. At 25 jobs weekly (5 per day), monthly revenue reaches €4,625 with €2,145 profit after all costs. Low barrier to entry, quick break-even.

Common Mistakes in Break-Even Calculations

Excluding owner's salary from fixed costs. Many contractors calculate break-even without including their own target salary, producing artificially low break-even points. A contractor with €1,800 in business expenses but no salary line shows break-even at 8 jobs. Adding €2,800 target salary increases fixed costs to €4,600, raising break-even to 21 jobs. Without this reality check, contractors work constantly but cannot pay themselves. Always include your target salary as a fixed cost.

Using best-case prices instead of averages. A painter might charge €2,500 for large houses but €650 for small rooms. Using €2,500 as "average price" distorts break-even analysis. If 70% of jobs are small rooms at €650, the weighted average might be €980. Using €2,500 suggests break-even at 3 jobs; reality requires 8 jobs. Review actual invoices from the past 3-6 months and calculate the true mathematical average across all job types.

Ignoring seasonal variability. Break-even calculated annually (€45,000 fixed costs ÷ €450 contribution margin = 100 jobs yearly) suggests 8-9 jobs monthly. But HVAC companies earn 60% of revenue in winter, 25% in summer, 15% in shoulder seasons. Monthly break-even fluctuates: 15 jobs in winter, 6 jobs in summer. Plan cash reserves to cover fixed costs during low seasons. Annual break-even masks monthly cash flow challenges.

Underestimating variable costs. Contractors often count only materials as variable costs, forgetting fuel, disposal fees, payment processing, warranty repairs, and consumables. A roofer counting only shingles (€850) misses: underlayment €180, nails €45, disposal €125, fuel €65, warranty reserve €85 — total variable cost €1,350, not €850. This €500 difference reduces contribution margin by 37%, dramatically increasing break-even. Track every variable expense.

Pro Tips for Break-Even Analysis

Calculate break-even for each service line separately. A plumbing company offering service calls (€285 average), installations (€1,850), and repairs (€620) should calculate break-even for each. Service calls: €195 contribution margin, installations: €1,180, repairs: €385. Knowing each service's contribution helps prioritize marketing. If marketing budget is limited, focus on services with highest contribution margin or fastest sales cycle.

Build a break-even cushion into pricing. Add 15-20% to your calculated break-even point as a safety margin. If break-even is 18 jobs monthly, price and plan for 22 jobs. This cushion absorbs unexpected cost increases, slow payment from clients, equipment breakdowns, and illness. Contractors operating at exactly break-even have zero resilience — one bad month creates debt.

Review break-even quarterly, not annually. Fixed costs change: insurance renewals, vehicle payments ending, salary increases, new software subscriptions. Variable costs fluctuate with material prices. A 2021 break-even calculation became obsolete when material costs rose 23% in 2022. Review fixed costs monthly, recalculate break-even quarterly, and adjust pricing when contribution margins shrink below target levels.

Use break-even for "make or buy" decisions. When considering subcontracting vs. hiring, calculate the break-even volume where hiring becomes cheaper. Subcontractor costs €350/day with no fixed costs. Hiring an employee costs €4,200/month fixed (salary + benefits) but only €85/day variable. Break-even: €4,200 ÷ (€350 - €85) = 15.85 days monthly. If you need 16+ days monthly, hire. Below 16 days, subcontract.

Frequently Asked Questions

Recalculate break-even quarterly at minimum, and immediately after any significant change: price increases, major equipment purchases, hiring/firing, insurance renewals, or material cost changes exceeding 10%. Construction material prices can swing dramatically — lumber varied 40% in 2020-2022. Quarterly reviews catch margin erosion before it creates losses. Set a calendar reminder for the first week of each quarter.

If break-even requires 40 jobs monthly but you average 15, you have three options: (1) Raise prices — a 20% increase might reduce required jobs to 28. (2) Reduce fixed costs — eliminate unnecessary subscriptions, downsize warehouse, refinance vehicle loans. (3) Reduce variable costs — negotiate supplier discounts, reduce waste, find cheaper material alternatives. Sometimes the answer is a combination: 10% price increase + 15% fixed cost reduction + 10% variable cost reduction can cut required jobs by 40%.

For basic break-even, exclude income taxes — focus on operational break-even before taxes. However, if you're a corporation paying corporate tax, include an estimate (typically 20-25% of profit) in your target profit calculations. For VAT/GST, exclude from both revenue and variable costs since VAT is collected and remitted, not kept as revenue. Work with net amounts (excluding VAT) for accurate operational break-even analysis.

Use break-even to set minimum acceptable bid prices. If your contribution margin must be €450/job to hit break-even, and a project has €1,200 variable costs, your minimum bid is €1,650. Bidding below €1,650 means you lose money on the job — you'd be paying to work. For competitive bids, calculate the absolute minimum (break-even price) and your target price (break-even + 20% profit margin). Never bid below absolute minimum unless strategically necessary to cover fixed costs during slow periods.

What Is Centripetal Force?

Centripetal force is the inward force that keeps an object moving in a circular path, always directed perpendicular to the velocity and toward the center of rotation. A 1,000 kg car traveling at 20 m/s (72 km/h) around a 50 m radius curve requires 8,000 N of centripetal force — provided by friction between tires and road. Without this inward force, the car would continue in a straight line (Newton's first law), sliding off the road tangent to the curve. The term "centripetal" literally means "center-seeking," distinguishing it from the fictitious centrifugal force that appears in rotating reference frames.

Engineers calculate centripetal force for roller coaster loops, banked turns on racetracks, satellite orbits, and centrifuge rotors. A roller coaster car (500 kg with passengers) at the top of a 15 m radius loop traveling 12 m/s experiences centripetal force F = mv²/r = 500 × 144 / 15 = 4,800 N downward. Gravity provides 4,905 N (500 × 9.81), so the track must push down with only 105 N — barely maintaining contact. At 10 m/s, centripetal force drops to 3,333 N, less than gravity — passengers would fall out without restraints.

Centripetal Force Formula With Worked Calculations

Centripetal force derives from mass (m), velocity (v), and radius (r):

Fc = mv² / r

Where Fc is centripetal force in newtons, m is mass in kg, v is tangential velocity in m/s, and r is radius in meters.

Using angular velocity (ω) instead of linear velocity:

Fc = mω²r

Where ω is angular velocity in rad/s (ω = v/r).

For orbital motion with gravitational force providing centripetal acceleration:

Fc = GMm / r²

Where G = 6.674×10⁻¹¹ N·m²/kg² (gravitational constant) and M is the central body's mass.

Practical example: A 0.5 kg ball on a 1.2 m string swings in a horizontal circle at 3 m/s. Centripetal force: Fc = 0.5 × 3² / 1.2 = 0.5 × 9 / 1.2 = 3.75 N. The string tension must provide at least 3.75 N horizontal force. If the ball completes one revolution in 2.5 seconds: ω = 2π / 2.5 = 2.51 rad/s. Using angular formula: Fc = 0.5 × 2.51² × 1.2 = 0.5 × 6.30 × 1.2 = 3.78 N (matches within rounding).

For a satellite in low Earth orbit (altitude 400 km, orbital radius r = 6,771 km = 6.771×10⁶ m, Earth mass M = 5.972×10²⁴ kg): Fc = GMm/r² = (6.674×10⁻¹¹ × 5.972×10²⁴ × 1,000) / (6.771×10⁶)² = 3.986×10¹⁴ / 4.585×10¹³ = 8,694 N for a 1,000 kg satellite. Orbital velocity: v = √(GM/r) = √(3.986×10¹⁴ / 6.771×10⁶) = √58,880,000 = 7,673 m/s (27,623 km/h).

6 Steps To Calculate Centripetal Force

  1. Identify the rotating object and its mass: Determine what's moving in a circle and measure its mass. For vehicles, include passengers and cargo. For rotating machinery, use the mass at the effective radius (not total rotor mass). A 1,500 kg car with driver and fuel may weigh 1,650 kg total. For a centrifuge with samples, add tube mass (50 g each) plus sample mass (10 g) to the rotor arm's effective mass. Convert to kg: 1 lb = 0.4536 kg, 1 g = 0.001 kg.
  2. Measure the radius of rotation: Find the distance from the rotation axis to the object's center of mass. For a car on a curve, measure from the curve's center to the car's center of gravity (typically 0.5-0.6 m above ground, centered laterally). For a ball on a string, radius equals string length. For a washing machine drum (diameter 500 mm), clothes at the drum wall have r = 250 mm = 0.25 m. Measure in meters for SI calculations.
  3. Determine tangential velocity: Measure the object's speed along its circular path. Use a speedometer (vehicles), radar gun (projectiles), or calculate from RPM: v = 2πr × RPM / 60. A car tire (r = 0.32 m) at 800 RPM: v = 2π × 0.32 × 800 / 60 = 26.8 m/s (96.5 km/h). For orbital motion, use v = √(GM/r). Convert units: km/h to m/s (divide by 3.6), mph to m/s (multiply by 0.447).
  4. Apply the centripetal force formula: Fc = mv² / r. For a 1,200 kg car at 25 m/s on a 100 m radius curve: Fc = 1,200 × 25² / 100 = 1,200 × 625 / 100 = 7,500 N. Using angular velocity: if the car completes the 100 m radius turn in 25.1 seconds (half-circle, πr = 314 m at 25 m/s), ω = π / 25.1 = 0.125 rad/s. Fc = 1,200 × 0.125² × 100 = 1,200 × 0.0156 × 100 = 1,875 N — wait, this is wrong because ω should be v/r = 25/100 = 0.25 rad/s. Correct: Fc = 1,200 × 0.25² × 100 = 7,500 N (matches).
  5. Identify the force provider: Determine what physical mechanism supplies the centripetal force. For cars: friction between tires and road (μ × normal force). For satellites: gravity. For ball on string: tension. For roller coaster loops: normal force from track plus gravity. For centrifuges: structural strength of rotor arms. Calculate maximum available force: friction Fmax = μmg (μ = 0.7 for dry asphalt, 0.3 for wet). A 1,200 kg car on dry road: Fmax = 0.7 × 1,200 × 9.81 = 8,240 N. If required Fc = 7,500 N, the car can make the turn safely.
  6. Check safety margins: Compare required centripetal force to maximum available. Safety factor = Favailable / Frequired. Minimum safety factor of 1.2-1.5 for vehicles; 2.0+ for amusement rides; 5.0+ for aerospace. For the car above: SF = 8,240 / 7,500 = 1.10 — marginal. Reduce speed to 22 m/s: Fc = 1,200 × 22² / 100 = 5,808 N, SF = 8,240 / 5,808 = 1.42 — acceptable. For wet roads (μ = 0.3): Fmax = 3,532 N, requiring speed reduction to 17 m/s (61 km/h) for SF = 1.0.

4 Practical Centripetal Force Examples

Example 1 — Highway Curve Design: A highway curve has radius 200 m with 6° banking (θ = 6°). Design speed for zero lateral friction: tan θ = v² / (rg). Solve: v = √(rg tan θ) = √(200 × 9.81 × tan 6°) = √(200 × 9.81 × 0.105) = √206 = 14.4 m/s (52 km/h). At this speed, the normal force from the banked road provides all centripetal force — no friction needed. For 90 km/h (25 m/s) traffic: required Fc = m × 25² / 200 = m × 3.125 m/s². Banking provides m × 9.81 × tan 6° = m × 1.03 m/s². Friction must supply the difference: m × (3.125 - 1.03) = m × 2.095 m/s². Required μ = 2.095 / 9.81 = 0.21 — achievable in dry conditions (μ = 0.7) but marginal when wet (μ = 0.3).

Example 2 — Centrifuge Sample Force: A laboratory centrifuge spins at 15,000 RPM with 150 mm radius. A 10 mL sample tube (mass = 15 g with liquid) experiences: ω = 2π × 15,000 / 60 = 1,571 rad/s. Fc = mω²r = 0.015 × 1,571² × 0.15 = 0.015 × 2,468,041 × 0.15 = 5,553 N. Express as "× g": 5,553 / (0.015 × 9.81) = 5,553 / 0.147 = 37,776 × g. This enormous force separates blood components in minutes instead of hours. Rotor arms must withstand 5,553 N per tube × 8 tubes = 44,424 N total outward force — equivalent to hanging 4,500 kg from the rotor.

Example 3 — Loop-the-Loop Roller Coaster: A roller coaster car (800 kg empty, 1,200 kg loaded) enters a 12 m radius vertical loop at 15 m/s. At the bottom: Fc = 1,200 × 15² / 12 = 1,200 × 225 / 12 = 22,500 N. Track must push up with normal force N = Fc + mg = 22,500 + 11,772 = 34,272 N (2.91 × g riders feel). At the top, energy conservation: initial KE = 0.5 × 1,200 × 15² = 135,000 J. Height gain = 24 m (diameter). PE gain = 1,200 × 9.81 × 24 = 282,528 J. Wait — PE gain exceeds initial KE, so the car won't complete the loop. Minimum entry speed: need KE ≥ PE at top, so 0.5mv² ≥ mg(2r). vmin = √(4gr) = √(4 × 9.81 × 12) = √471 = 21.7 m/s. At 22 m/s entry: top velocity from energy: 0.5 × 1,200 × 22² - 1,200 × 9.81 × 24 = 290,400 - 282,528 = 7,872 J remaining. vtop = √(2 × 7,872 / 1,200) = 3.63 m/s. Fc,top = 1,200 × 3.63² / 12 = 1,318 N. Gravity = 11,772 N downward. Track must push down: 11,772 - 1,318 = 10,454 N to keep car on track.

Example 4 — Washing Machine Spin Cycle: A front-load washer spins at 1,400 RPM with 250 mm drum radius. Wet clothes (5 kg distributed at drum wall) experience: ω = 2π × 1,400 / 60 = 146.6 rad/s. Fc = 5 × 146.6² × 0.25 = 5 × 21,492 × 0.25 = 26,865 N. Expressed as × g: 26,865 / (5 × 9.81) = 548 × g. This force extracts water through drum holes — water droplets (0.1 g each) experience 0.54 N outward force, overcoming surface tension. Drum bearings must support 26,865 N radial load plus drum weight. Unbalanced load (2 kg on one side) creates rotating 10,746 N imbalance force at 23.3 Hz — vibration isolation springs prevent this from shaking the machine apart.

4 Common Centripetal Force Mistakes

Mistake 1 — Confusing Centripetal and Centrifugal Force: Centripetal force is real (inward, toward center); centrifugal force is fictitious (outward, felt in rotating frame). A car turning left: centripetal force pushes the car left (friction from road); passengers feel "centrifugal force" pushing them right (actually their inertia trying to continue straight). In inertial (non-rotating) reference frames, only centripetal force exists. Using "centrifugal force" in F = ma equations produces sign errors. Exception: analyzing from the rotating object's perspective (e.g., stress in a spinning rotor) legitimately uses centrifugal force as a pseudo-force for convenience.

Mistake 2 — Using Diameter Instead of Radius: The formula requires radius (center to object), not diameter (edge to edge). A 60 cm diameter circle has r = 30 cm = 0.3 m. Using d = 0.6 m in F = mv²/r produces half the correct force — a 50% error. For a car on a 100 m diameter roundabout, r = 50 m, not 100 m. Fc at 15 m/s with correct r: 1,500 × 225 / 50 = 6,750 N. With wrong d: 1,500 × 225 / 100 = 3,375 N — underestimating required friction by 3,375 N could cause loss of control.

Mistake 3 — Forgetting Velocity Squared: Centripetal force scales with v², not v. Doubling speed quadruples force, not doubles. A car at 60 km/h (16.7 m/s) on a 50 m curve: Fc = 1,500 × 16.7² / 50 = 8,367 N. At 120 km/h (33.3 m/s): Fc = 1,500 × 33.3² / 50 = 33,333 N — 4× higher, not 2×. If maximum friction is 10,000 N, the car can make the turn at 60 km/h (SF = 1.2) but slides off at 120 km/h (requires 3.3× available friction). This non-linear relationship explains why high-speed curves require much larger radii.

Mistake 4 — Ignoring Vertical Forces in Loops: In vertical circular motion, gravity adds or subtracts from centripetal force depending on position. At the bottom of a loop: N - mg = mv²/r (normal force exceeds weight). At the top: N + mg = mv²/r (normal force plus weight provide centripetal). At the sides: N = mv²/r (normal force alone, gravity acts perpendicular). A common error is using Fc = mv²/r without accounting for gravity's contribution. For a 500 kg coaster at 10 m/s in a 10 m radius loop: Fc = 5,000 N always. Bottom: N = 5,000 + 4,905 = 9,905 N. Top: N = 5,000 - 4,905 = 95 N (barely contacting track). Side: N = 5,000 N, gravity acts downward separately.

4 Professional Tips for Centripetal Force Applications

Tip 1 — Bank Curves for Design Speed: Banking angle θ eliminates lateral friction requirement when tan θ = v²/(rg). For a 100 km/h (27.8 m/s) highway curve with r = 300 m: tan θ = 27.8² / (300 × 9.81) = 773 / 2,943 = 0.263. θ = arctan(0.263) = 14.7°. At exactly 100 km/h, vehicles experience no sideways force — passengers don't feel pushed laterally. Slower vehicles feel inward force (tend to slide down the bank); faster vehicles feel outward force (tend to slide up). Maximum safe speed with friction: vmax = √[rg(tan θ + μ)/(1 - μ tan θ)]. For μ = 0.7: vmax = √[300 × 9.81 × (0.263 + 0.7)/(1 - 0.7 × 0.263)] = √[2,943 × 0.963 / 0.816] = √3,472 = 58.9 m/s (212 km/h).

Tip 2 — Use Free-Body Diagrams: Draw all forces acting on the object: weight (mg down), normal force (perpendicular to surface), friction (parallel to surface, toward center), tension (along string/rod). Sum forces radially: ΣFradial = mv²/r. Sum forces vertically: ΣFvertical = 0 (if no vertical acceleration). For a conical pendulum (ball on string swinging in horizontal circle): tension T has vertical component T cos θ = mg and horizontal component T sin θ = mv²/r. Divide: tan θ = v²/(rg). Measure θ to find v without timing revolutions.

Tip 3 — Account for Non-Uniform Circular Motion: When speed changes during circular motion, tangential acceleration (at = dv/dt) exists alongside centripetal acceleration (ac = v²/r). Total acceleration: a⃗ = acr̂ + att̂ (radial plus tangential vectors). Magnitude: |a| = √(ac² + at²). A car braking while turning experiences both deceleration (at negative) and centripetal acceleration. Tire friction must provide both: Ftotal = m√(ac² + at²). If ac = 5 m/s² and at = -3 m/s²: |a| = √(25 + 9) = 5.83 m/s². Combined force exceeds either alone — braking hard while turning can exceed tire friction limits even if neither alone would.

Tip 4 — Calculate G-Forces for Human Factors: Express centripetal acceleration as multiples of g (9.81 m/s²) for human tolerance assessment. ac/g = v²/(rg). Fighter pilots tolerate +9gz (chest-to-back) for seconds with G-suits; sustained +4gz causes gray-out. Roller coasters limit to +5gz max, -1gz (ejection from seat) max. Lateral g-forces (side-to-side) are less tolerated — limit to ±1.5g for comfort. For a 20 m radius turn at 20 m/s: ac = 400/20 = 20 m/s² = 2.04g lateral — uncomfortable but tolerable briefly. Increase radius to 50 m: ac = 400/50 = 8 m/s² = 0.82g — comfortable for extended periods.

4 Centripetal Force FAQs

Gravitational attraction between the planet and Sun provides centripetal force. For Earth (mass m = 5.97×10²⁴ kg, orbital radius r = 1.496×10¹¹ m, orbital speed v = 29,780 m/s): Fc = mv²/r = 5.97×10²⁴ × 29,780² / 1.496×10¹¹ = 3.54×10²² N. Gravitational force: Fg = GMm/r² = 6.674×10⁻¹¹ × 1.989×10³⁰ × 5.97×10²⁴ / (1.496×10¹¹)² = 3.54×10²² N — exactly equal, confirming gravity provides the centripetal force. Orbital velocity derives from equating these: v = √(GM/r) = 29,780 m/s for Earth's orbit.

At the top of the swing, both gravity and your arm's downward pull provide centripetal force. Minimum speed occurs when gravity alone provides centripetal force: mg = mv²/r, so vmin = √(gr). For r = 1 m: vmin = √9.81 = 3.13 m/s. At this speed, the bucket accelerates downward at 9.81 m/s² — exactly matching gravity's acceleration on the water. The water is in free fall along with the bucket, so it doesn't fall out relative to the bucket. Swing faster (v > 3.13 m/s) and the bucket must push down on the water (normal force) to provide extra centripetal acceleration — water presses against the bucket bottom.

Astronauts aren't beyond Earth's gravity — at 400 km altitude (ISS orbit), g = 8.69 m/s² (89% of surface gravity). They feel weightless because both they and the ISS are in free fall, accelerating toward Earth at 8.69 m/s². The ISS's tangential velocity (7,670 m/s) means it continuously "misses" Earth, falling around the curve. Centripetal acceleration v²/r = 7,670² / 6,771,000 = 8.69 m/s² exactly equals gravitational acceleration — the definition of orbit. Inside the ISS, everything accelerates identically, so no normal force exists between astronauts and surfaces — the sensation of weight (normal force) disappears.

Work = force × displacement × cos(θ), and θ = 90° means cos(90°) = 0. A satellite in circular orbit maintains constant kinetic energy despite continuous centripetal force from gravity. However, if the orbit is elliptical, gravity has a tangential component that does work — speeding up the satellite as it falls toward periapsis, slowing it as it climbs to apoapsis. In uniform circular motion, centripetal force changes direction but not speed, so no work is done.

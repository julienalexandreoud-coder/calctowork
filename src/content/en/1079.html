Wheatstone Bridge Calculator: Measure Unknown Resistance with Precision

The Wheatstone bridge calculator determines the value of an unknown resistor by balancing two legs of a bridge circuit. Invented by Samuel Hunter Christie in 1833 and popularized by Sir Charles Wheatstone, this elegant circuit remains one of the most accurate methods for measuring resistance without requiring a calibrated meter. Whether you are working with strain gauges, temperature sensors, or precision resistor matching, this calculator gives you the unknown resistance instantly from three known values.

Wheatstone Bridge Formula

Rx = R3 × (R2 / R1)

When the bridge is balanced, the ratio of resistances in one leg equals the ratio in the other: R1 / R2 = R3 / Rx. At this balance condition, the voltage between the two midpoints is zero and no current flows through the center branch. R1 and R2 form the ratio arm, R3 is the standard variable resistor, and Rx is the unknown resistance.

The bridge circuit consists of four resistors arranged in a diamond shape with a DC voltage source connected across one diagonal and a sensitive galvanometer across the other. To find the unknown resistance, you adjust R3 until the galvanometer reads zero, then apply the balanced bridge equation. This null-balance method eliminates errors from meter calibration because the measurement depends only on resistor ratios, not on absolute voltage or current values.

Worked Examples

Example 1: Unknown Resistor Identification

You have an unmarked resistor and want to determine its value. You set up a Wheatstone bridge with R1 = 100 Ω, R2 = 220 Ω, and adjust the variable resistor R3 until the galvanometer shows zero. The balance occurs at R3 = 470 Ω.

Calculation: Rx = 470 × (220 / 100) = 470 × 2.2 = 1034 Ω

The unknown resistor is approximately 1.03 kΩ. This value matches a standard 1 kΩ resistor with a 5% tolerance band (brown-black-red-gold). The bridge measurement confirms the resistor is within its specified tolerance.

Example 2: Strain Gauge Application

A strain gauge with an unstrained resistance of 350 Ω is placed in the Rx position. The bridge has R1 = 350 Ω, R2 = 350 Ω, and R3 = 350 Ω for initial balance. When a load is applied, the bridge becomes unbalanced and R3 needs adjustment to 351.2 Ω for rebalancing.

Calculation: Rx = 351.2 × (350 / 350) = 351.2 × 1.0 = 351.2 Ω

The strain gauge resistance changed from 350.0 Ω to 351.2 Ω, a change of 1.2 Ω or 0.34%. For a typical gauge factor of 2.0, this corresponds to a strain of 0.17% (1700 microstrain) in the test material, which is consistent with moderate structural loading.

Common Uses

  • Precision measurement of unknown resistors without requiring a calibrated ohmmeter or multimeter
  • Strain gauge instrumentation for measuring mechanical deformation in structural testing, aerospace components, and civil engineering
  • Temperature measurement using resistance temperature detectors (RTDs) in industrial process control and HVAC systems
  • Resistor matching and tolerance verification in precision analog circuits, audio equipment, and measurement instruments
  • Pressure and force transducer design where resistance changes correlate with applied mechanical load
  • Educational demonstration of circuit analysis principles and the concept of null-balance measurement techniques

Common Mistakes

  • Assuming the bridge is balanced without confirming zero galvanometer deflection — a small current may still flow and introduce measurement error
  • Using a low-sensitivity galvanometer that cannot detect small imbalance currents, reducing measurement accuracy especially for precision applications
  • Ignoring the internal resistance of the voltage source and the galvanometer, which can affect the bridge balance condition in sensitive measurements
  • Applying the formula when the bridge is not truly balanced — the Wheatstone bridge equation only holds at the exact null point
  • Neglecting temperature effects on the resistors during measurement — self-heating from the excitation voltage can cause resistance drift

Pro Tip

For the highest accuracy, use a four-terminal Kelvin connection for the unknown resistor and the variable standard resistor. This eliminates lead resistance errors that can be significant for low-value resistors (below 10 Ω). Also, choose R1 and R2 so that their ratio is close to unity — a 1:1 ratio gives maximum bridge sensitivity. If you need to measure very small resistance changes, consider using a Wheatstone bridge configured as a differential amplifier with an instrumentation amplifier IC such as the AD620 or INA128, which can detect resistance changes of a few parts per million.

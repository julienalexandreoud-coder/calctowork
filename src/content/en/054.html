What Is an Air Duct Calculator?

An air duct calculator determines the proper duct diameter or rectangular dimensions needed to deliver required airflow (measured in cubic meters per hour or CFM) at acceptable air velocity and pressure drop. Proper duct sizing ensures HVAC systems operate efficiently, quietly, and deliver balanced airflow to all rooms. Undersized ducts create noise, increase energy consumption, and starve rooms of conditioned air. Oversized ducts waste material costs and can cause poor air distribution.

The calculator uses the Darcy-Weisbach equation and friction charts to relate airflow, duct size, velocity, and pressure loss. For a given airflow, larger ducts have lower velocity and less friction but cost more and take up more space. Smaller ducts save material but increase fan energy and noise. The optimal balance depends on application: residential systems target 3-5 m/s velocity, commercial systems 5-8 m/s, and industrial systems 8-12 m/s.

Duct design affects system performance more than equipment selection. A perfectly sized 24,000 BTU heat pump connected to undersized ducts will underperform a correctly ducted 18,000 BTU unit. Static pressure builds when ducts are too small, reducing airflow across the coil. This causes poor heat transfer, frozen evaporators in cooling mode, and shortened equipment life. The calculator prevents these problems by specifying ducts that maintain static pressure within equipment tolerances.

Formulas Explained: Duct Sizing Equations

The fundamental duct sizing relationship connects airflow, velocity, and cross-sectional area:

Area (m²) = Airflow (m³/h) / (Velocity (m/s) × 3,600)

For 500 m³/h at 4 m/s: Area = 500 / (4 × 3,600) = 0.0347 m²

Circular duct diameter derives from area:

Diameter (mm) = √(4 × Area / π) × 1,000

For 0.0347 m²: Diameter = √(4 × 0.0347 / 3.14159) × 1,000 = 210 mm

Pressure drop per meter follows the Darcy-Weisbach equation simplified for HVAC:

ΔP (Pa/m) = 0.0226 × (Q^1.85 / D^4.97)

Where Q is airflow in m³/h and D is diameter in meters. For 500 m³/h in 210mm duct: ΔP = 0.0226 × (500^1.85 / 0.21^4.97) = 1.2 Pa/m. This low pressure drop indicates efficient duct sizing.

Rectangular duct equivalent diameter uses the Huebscher formula:

D_eq = 1.30 × (a × b)^0.625 / (a + b)^0.25

Where a and b are duct dimensions in mm. A 300mm × 150mm rectangular duct: D_eq = 1.30 × (45,000)^0.625 / (450)^0.25 = 224 mm equivalent. This rectangular duct performs like a 224mm round duct.

Total system pressure drop sums all components:

Total ΔP = (Duct Length × ΔP/m) + Fittings + Equipment

Typical fitting losses: 90° elbow = 0.3 velocity pressure, tee = 0.6 VP, damper = 0.2 VP. A 20m duct run at 1.2 Pa/m with 4 elbows and 2 tees: 24 + (4 × 0.3 × 10) + (2 × 0.6 × 10) = 24 + 12 + 12 = 48 Pa total.

Step-by-Step Guide to Size Your Ductwork

Step 1: Determine Required Airflow
Calculate CFM or m³/h for each room based on heating/cooling load. Rule of thumb: 50-60 m³/h per ton (12,000 BTU) of cooling. A 3-ton system needs 150-180 m³/h total. Distribute proportionally by room load: a 20 m² bedroom gets 20% of a 100 m² house's total airflow, approximately 35 m³/h.

Step 2: Select Target Velocity
Choose velocity based on application and noise tolerance. Main trunks: 4-6 m/s residential, 6-8 m/s commercial. Branch ducts: 3-4 m/s residential, 4-5 m/s commercial. Final runs to diffusers: 2-3 m/s. Higher velocities save material but increase noise—each doubling of velocity increases noise 15-18 dB.

Step 3: Calculate Duct Diameter
Enter airflow and velocity into the calculator. For 300 m³/h at 4 m/s: Area = 300 / 14,400 = 0.0208 m². Diameter = 162 mm. Round up to standard size: 180mm or 200mm duct. The calculator shows both circular diameter and equivalent rectangular sizes.

Step 4: Check Pressure Drop
Verify pressure drop stays within acceptable limits. Residential systems should have total external static pressure below 125 Pa (0.5 inches water column). If calculator shows 2 Pa/m and your longest run is 25m, that's 50 Pa for duct friction, leaving 75 Pa for fittings, filters, and coils—acceptable.

Step 5: Select Rectangular Dimensions (If Needed)
When space constraints prevent round ducts, use rectangular equivalents. A 200mm round duct (0.0314 m² area) could be 300mm × 125mm (0.0375 m², slightly larger) or 250mm × 150mm (0.0375 m²). The calculator provides multiple rectangular options with their equivalent pressure drops.

Step 6: Plan Fitting Layout
Minimize elbows and transitions. Use 45° elbows instead of 90° when possible (half the pressure drop). Install turning vanes in large rectangular elbows. Space fittings at least 2 duct diameters apart to allow airflow to stabilize. Each fitting adds equivalent length: 90° elbow = 10-15 diameters, so a 200mm elbow adds 2-3m equivalent straight duct.

6 Real-World Examples with Specific Calculations

Example 1: Residential Main Trunk Duct
A 3-ton (36,000 BTU) system requires 180 m³/h total airflow. Main trunk carries full airflow at 5 m/s target velocity. Area = 180 / (5 × 3,600) = 0.01 m². Diameter = √(4 × 0.01 / π) × 1,000 = 113 mm. This seems small—checking pressure drop: ΔP = 0.0226 × (180^1.85 / 0.113^4.97) = 3.8 Pa/m, acceptable. However, standard practice uses 250-300mm main trunks for 3-ton systems to reduce noise and allow future capacity. Final selection: 300mm diameter at 2.8 m/s velocity, 0.4 Pa/m pressure drop.

Example 2: Bedroom Branch Duct
Master bedroom needs 45 m³/h (25% of total 180 m³/h). Branch duct at 3.5 m/s: Area = 45 / 12,600 = 0.00357 m². Diameter = 67 mm. Standard size: 80mm or 100mm flexible duct. At 100mm: velocity = 45 / (0.00785 × 3,600) = 1.6 m/s, very quiet. Pressure drop: 0.15 Pa/m. For 8m run: 1.2 Pa friction, plus 2 elbows (6 Pa) = 7.2 Pa total—well within limits.

Example 3: Commercial Office Supply Duct
Open office needs 2,400 m³/h supply air. Rectangular duct fits above ceiling with 400mm height constraint. At 6 m/s velocity: Area = 2,400 / 21,600 = 0.111 m². With height 400mm: width = 0.111 / 0.4 = 0.278m = 278mm. Use 300mm × 400mm rectangular duct. Equivalent diameter: 1.30 × (120,000)^0.625 / (700)^0.25 = 378 mm. Pressure drop: 0.9 Pa/m. For 30m run: 27 Pa friction, acceptable for commercial VAV system.

Example 4: Kitchen Exhaust Duct
Range hood requires 600 m³/h exhaust. Kitchen ducts need higher velocity (7-9 m/s) to carry grease particles. At 8 m/s: Area = 600 / 28,800 = 0.0208 m². Diameter = 162 mm. Use 180mm galvanized steel duct (required for grease-laden air). Velocity in 180mm: 6.6 m/s, acceptable. Pressure drop: 2.1 Pa/m. With 6m vertical run, cap, and 2 elbows: (6 × 2.1) + 15 + (2 × 8) = 12.6 + 15 + 16 = 43.6 Pa. Select exhaust fan rated for 600 m³/h at 50+ Pa static pressure.

Example 5: Basement Workshop Ventilation
Workshop needs 400 m³/h for air quality. Flexible spiral duct runs 15m with 4 bends. At 5 m/s: Area = 400 / 18,000 = 0.0222 m². Diameter = 168 mm. Use 200mm flexible duct (readily available). Actual velocity: 3.6 m/s. Flexible duct has 20-30% higher friction than rigid: effective ΔP = 1.3 × 0.8 = 1.04 Pa/m. Total: (15 × 1.04) + (4 × 10) = 15.6 + 40 = 55.6 Pa. Select inline fan rated 400 m³/h at 60 Pa.

Example 6: Dual-Zone System Balancing
Two-zone system: Zone A (downstairs) 120 m³/h, Zone B (upstairs) 80 m³/h. Common trunk carries 200 m³/h at 4.5 m/s: 200mm diameter. Zone A branch: 120 m³/h at 3.5 m/s = 150mm duct. Zone B branch: 80 m³/h at 3.5 m/s = 125mm duct. To balance, install dampers in each branch. Zone B damper partially closed to create additional pressure drop matching Zone A's longer run length. Without dampers, air follows path of least resistance, starving one zone.

4 Common Duct Sizing Mistakes

Mistake 1: Using Flexible Duct for Long Runs
Flexible duct has 20-30% higher friction than rigid metal and sags over time, creating additional restrictions. Limit flexible duct to 1.5m final connections at diffusers. One installer used 8m flexible duct for a bedroom branch, causing 40% less airflow than calculated. The sagging created low spots that acted as water traps and increased pressure drop. Always use rigid duct for runs over 2m.

Mistake 2: Ignoring Fitting Losses
Calculating only straight duct friction underestimates total pressure drop by 30-50%. Each 90° elbow adds 10-15 equivalent meters of straight duct. A 10m run with 4 elbows behaves like 50-70m of straight duct. Use the calculator's fitting loss feature or add 50% to straight-run pressure drop for preliminary estimates. Design for total system pressure, not just duct friction.

Mistake 3: Abrupt Transitions Between Sizes
Connecting 300mm duct directly to 150mm duct creates turbulence and noise. Use tapered transitions at 15-30° angle. A sudden contraction loses 0.5 velocity pressure; a gradual transition loses only 0.1 VP. For 5 m/s airflow, sudden transition wastes 15 Pa that gradual transition reduces to 3 Pa. Over a system with 10 transitions, this saves 120 Pa—significant for low-pressure residential systems.

Mistake 4: Not Accounting for Duct Leakage
Poorly sealed ductwork leaks 10-30% of conditioned air into unconditioned spaces. A system designed for 200 m³/h delivery might only deliver 150 m³/h to rooms if ducts leak 25%. Seal all joints with mastic (not duct tape, which fails within 2-3 years). Test with duct blaster after installation. Leaky ducts increase energy bills 20-40% and can create negative pressure, pulling radon or combustion gases into the home.

5 Pro Tips for Ductwork Installation

Tip 1: Keep Duct Runs Short and Direct
Every meter of duct adds friction and cost. Route ducts along the shortest path, even if it requires more fittings. A 20m indirect run has double the pressure drop of a 10m direct run. Plan duct layout before framing so chases and openings align. One builder saved €800 in duct material and reduced fan size by 0.5 kW by relocating the air handler 3m closer to the longest branch.

Tip 2: Insulate Ducts in Unconditioned Spaces
Ducts in attics lose 20-30% of heating/cooling energy through the duct walls. Wrap with R-6 (50mm) duct insulation minimum. In hot attics (60°C+), use R-8 (75mm). Seal insulation seams with foil tape. Insulated ducts also prevent condensation on cooling ducts, eliminating mold growth. Payback period: 2-4 years through reduced energy bills.

Tip 3: Use Manual D or ACCA Standards
Follow established duct design standards like ACCA Manual D (residential) or SMACNA (commercial). These specify maximum velocities, fitting types, and balancing procedures. Systems designed to standard perform predictably and can be properly balanced. Guesswork duct sizing leads to comfort complaints and callbacks. Professional HVAC designers use these standards for every installation.

Tip 4: Install Takeoffs Properly
Branch duct takeoffs from main trunks affect airflow distribution. Use 45° wye fittings instead of 90° tees. Space takeoffs at least 2 duct diameters apart. For rectangular ducts, install takeoffs on the long side, not the short side. Improper takeoffs create turbulence and uneven airflow. One technician fixed a "mystery" no-flow bedroom by relocating the takeoff from the trunk's narrow side to the wide side—airflow increased from 15 to 40 m³/h.

Tip 5: Balance the System After Installation
Even perfectly designed ducts need field balancing. Install balancing dampers in each branch. Use a flow hood or anemometer to measure actual airflow at each diffuser. Adjust dampers until each room receives design airflow. Document final damper positions with photos or tags. Balancing takes 2-3 hours but ensures the system performs as designed. Unbalanced systems waste 15-25% of capacity.

4 FAQs About Duct Calculations

Round ducts are more efficient—same airflow with 10-15% less pressure drop and less material. They're also easier to seal. Rectangular ducts fit better in tight spaces like above ceilings or in chases. Use round ducts wherever space allows, rectangular only when necessary. For equivalent performance, rectangular ducts need 10-15% more cross-sectional area.

Signs of undersized ducts: excessive noise at registers, weak airflow, rooms that never reach setpoint, frozen evaporator coils, high static pressure readings (>0.5" WC). Measure airflow with an anemometer—multiply velocity (m/s) by duct area (m²) × 3,600 = m³/h. Compare to design requirements. If airflow is 20%+ below design, ducts are likely undersized or blocked.

Reducing duct size increases velocity and pressure drop. You can safely increase velocity 1-2 m/s above standard, but noise increases significantly. For space-constrained areas, use rectangular ducts that fit the available space while maintaining cross-sectional area. A 200mm round duct (0.0314 m²) can become 400mm × 100mm flat duct, fitting in tight joist spaces.

Galvanized steel is standard for most applications—durable, fireproof, smooth interior. Aluminum is lighter and corrosion-resistant for coastal areas. Flexible duct is acceptable only for final 1-2m connections. Fiberglass-lined duct reduces noise but can harbor mold if wet. For residential, use rigid galvanized steel for mains, flexible for branch connections to diffusers.

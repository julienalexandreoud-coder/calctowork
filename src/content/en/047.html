What Is an Electrical Panel Calculator?

An electrical panel calculator sizes the main distribution board, main breaker, and circuit count based on contracted power, supply voltage, and diversity factor. For a 9.2 kW contracted power at 230 V with 0.7 diversity factor, the calculator specifies a 63 A main breaker, 12-16 circuit panel, and 25 mm² main cable entry.

Undersized panels cause frequent breaker trips, overheating busbars, and fire hazards. A 40 A main breaker on a 9.2 kW load (40 A demand) trips within 15-30 minutes under full load. Oversized panels waste €400-€800 on unnecessary capacity and require larger utility service connections costing €600-€1,200 in upgrade fees.

The calculation follows IEC 61439 low-voltage switchgear standards, applying diversity factors to prevent over-sizing while ensuring all circuits can operate safely under normal conditions.

Electrical Panel Formulas With Calculations

Maximum Current (A) = Contracted Power (kW) × 1,000 ÷ Voltage (V)

Example: 9.2 kW × 1,000 ÷ 230 V = 40 A

Design Current with Diversity (A) = Maximum Current × Diversity Factor

Residential diversity: 0.6-0.7. Commercial: 0.7-0.8. Industrial: 0.8-0.9

40 A × 0.7 = 28 A design current for circuit planning

Main Breaker Rating (A) = Next Standard Size Above Maximum Current

Standard sizes: 16, 20, 25, 32, 40, 50, 63, 80, 100, 125 A

40 A maximum → 50 A or 63 A main breaker (allow 25% headroom)

Number of Circuits = Total Load Points ÷ Points per Circuit

Lighting: 16 points per 10 A circuit. Power: 20 outlets per 16 A circuit

25 lighting points ÷ 16 = 1.56 → 2 lighting circuits

45 power outlets ÷ 20 = 2.25 → 3 power circuits

Busbar Current Rating = Sum of Circuit Breaker Ratings × Diversity

12 circuits × 16 A average × 0.6 diversity = 115 A busbar minimum

Use 125 A busbar panel for safety margin

6 Steps To Size Electrical Distribution Panels

  1. Determine contracted power from utility agreement: Residential typical: 5.75 kW, 7.36 kW, 9.2 kW, 11.5 kW. Commercial: 15-50 kW. Industrial: 50-500 kW. Contracted power sets maximum current draw before utility fuse blows. For 9.2 kW single-phase: 40 A. Three-phase 15 kW: 25 A per phase. Verify contract before sizing panel.
  2. Calculate main breaker rating with 25% margin: Main breaker must handle contracted power continuously without nuisance tripping. 40 A load requires 50 A minimum breaker. Standard practice: next size up plus 25% for future growth. 40 A × 1.25 = 50 A. Use 63 A for panels with electric heating or EV charging planned.
  3. List all circuits by type and load: Lighting circuits (10 A MCB, 1.5 mm²): count light points. Power circuits (16 A MCB, 2.5 mm²): count outlets. Dedicated appliance circuits (20-32 A): oven, hob, washing machine, dishwasher, water heater, air conditioning. Special circuits (32-63 A): electric vehicle charger, heat pump, sauna. Total circuit count determines panel way count.
  4. Apply diversity factors to prevent oversizing: Not all circuits operate at full load simultaneously. Residential diversity: 0.6 for lighting, 0.5 for general power, 1.0 for dedicated appliances. Example: 20 lighting points × 100 W = 2,000 W × 0.6 = 1,200 W design. 40 outlets × 200 W = 8,000 W × 0.5 = 4,000 W design. Total diversified: 5,200 W versus 10,000 W connected.
  5. Select panel enclosure with adequate ways: Panel ways = total circuits + 20% spare capacity. 12 circuits + 20% = 14.4 → 16-way panel. Surface-mount enclosures (€85-€140) for retrofit. Flush-mount (€120-€180) for new construction. IP65 rated (€180-€280) for garages, outdoors, damp locations. Ensure 300 mm clearance above and below panel per IEC 61439.
  6. Size main cable entry and earth bonding: Main cable from utility meter to panel: 16 mm² for 50 A, 25 mm² for 63 A, 35 mm² for 80 A. Earth bonding conductor: minimum 16 mm² copper to main earth terminal. Bond all metallic services (water, gas, heating) to main earth. Earth rod resistance: <10 Ω for residential, <1 Ω for commercial with sensitive electronics.

4 Costly Mistakes To Avoid

  • Mixing MCB brands in same panel: Different manufacturers have varying trip curves and physical dimensions. Mixing Schneider, ABB, and Siemens breakers voids UL/CE certification and insurance coverage. Busbar compatibility varies by brand—forcing incompatible breakers damages busbar contacts, increasing resistance from 0.0001 Ω to 0.01 Ω. At 40 A, this dissipates 16 W as heat versus 0.16 W, causing thermal runaway. Single-brand panels cost 5-10% more but maintain warranty and compliance.
  • Omitting RCD/GFCI protection on final circuits: IEC 60364 requires 30 mA RCD on all socket outlets up to 32 A and all lighting circuits in residential. Omission saves €25-€40 per RCD but creates lethal shock hazard. RCD prevents electrocution at 30 mA threshold (heart fibrillation occurs at 50-100 mA). Non-compliant installations fail inspection and void property insurance for electrical accidents. Retrofit RCD after wall closure: €180-€350 per circuit versus €35-€55 during construction.
  • Installing panel in inaccessible location: IEC 61439 requires 1 m clear working space in front of panel, 300 mm above and below. Panels behind furniture, in closets, or above 2 m height violate code. Emergency shutoff delayed by 2-5 minutes in fire situations. Fire department cannot isolate power quickly. Relocating panel: €1,200-€2,500 including new cable runs, patching, and utility company disconnection/reconnection fees.
  • Undersizing neutral conductor in three-phase panels: Three-phase panels with significant single-phase loads (LED lighting, computers, VFD drives) generate third harmonic currents in neutral. Neutral current can exceed phase current by 20-40%. Standard practice: size neutral equal to phase conductors. Undersized neutral (50% of phase) overheats at 150-200°C, melting insulation and causing phase-to-neutral faults. Harmonic-rated panels with 200% neutral capacity cost 15-20% more but prevent catastrophic failures.

5 Professional Tips

  • Label every circuit clearly on panel directory: Use permanent engraved labels or laminated cards, not handwritten paper. Format: "C1 - Living Room Lights", "C6 - Kitchen Hob". Include circuit breaker rating and cable size. Update directory after any modifications. Clear labeling reduces troubleshooting time by 70-80%. Electricians charge €60-€90/hour—proper labeling pays for itself in 1-2 service calls.
  • Install surge protection device (SPD) at main panel: Type 2 SPD (€85-€140) clamps voltage spikes from lightning and grid switching. Protects all downstream electronics. Type 1+2 combination (€180-€250) for lightning-prone areas. SPD lifetime: 8-12 years, indicated by green/red status window. Without SPD, 230 V spikes reach 2,000-6,000 V during storms, destroying TVs, computers, and appliances. Insurance claims for surge damage: average €8,000-€15,000 per incident. SPD prevents 90-95% of surge damage.
  • Use RCBOs instead of separate MCB + RCD: RCBO (Residual Current Breaker with Overcurrent) combines MCB and RCD in single module (€45-€65 versus €35-€50 for separate). Advantages: individual circuit RCD protection (one trip doesn't kill power to entire house), saves panel space (1 module versus 2), easier fault finding. For 12-circuit panel, RCBOs cost €180-€240 more upfront but eliminate 60-70% of nuisance trip service calls.
  • 预留 25% spare ways for future expansion: Typical home adds 2-4 circuits within 10 years (EV charger, heat pump, solar inverter, home office). 12-circuit panel filled completely requires expensive panel replacement (€800-€1,500) when expansion needed. 16-way panel with 4 spares costs €60-€100 more initially. Future circuit addition: €120-€180 with spare capacity versus €1,000+ for panel replacement.
  • Test RCD trip time and current annually: RCD tester (€180-€350 purchase, €40-€65 rental) measures trip time at 1× IΔn (30 mA) and 5× IΔn (150 mA). Acceptable: trip within 300 ms at 1×, within 40 ms at 5×. Slow or non-tripping RCDs fail to protect against shock. Test button on RCD only verifies mechanical function, not trip characteristics. Annual testing required for commercial properties, recommended for residential. Document test results for insurance compliance.

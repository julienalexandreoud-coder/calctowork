Resistor Color Code Calculator: Decode Your Resistors Instantly

The Resistor Color Code calculator translates the colored bands printed on through-hole resistors into their numerical resistance value, tolerance, and temperature coefficient. Whether you are an electronics hobbyist building your first circuit, a student learning about Ohm's law, or a technician troubleshooting a PCB, reading resistor bands correctly is a fundamental skill.

Resistor Color Code System

R = (Digit&sub1; × 10 + Digit&sub2;) × 10Multiplier

R = (Digit&sub1; × 100 + Digit&sub2; × 10 + Digit&sub3;) × 10Multiplier (5–band)

The resistor color code uses a standard mapping: Black (0), Brown (1), Red (2), Orange (3), Yellow (4), Green (5), Blue (6), Violet (7), Gray (8), and White (9). The multiplier band indicates the power of ten to multiply by, and the tolerance band is typically Gold (±5%) or Silver (±10%). Resistors with no tolerance band have a default tolerance of ±20%.

For 4-band resistors, the first band is the first digit, the second band is the second digit, the third band is the multiplier, and the fourth band is the tolerance. For 5-band resistors, an additional digit band provides three significant figures for higher precision. The reading direction is determined by the gap between the tolerance band and the other bands.

Worked Examples

Example 1: 4-Band Resistor (Yellow-Violet-Red-Gold)

A resistor has bands of Yellow, Violet, Red, and Gold. Reading from left to right:

Calculation: Yellow = 4, Violet = 7, so digits = 47. Red multiplier = 10² = 100. R = 47 × 100 = 4,700 Ω (4.7 kΩ). Gold band means ±5% tolerance, so the actual value is between 4.465 kΩ and 4.935 kΩ.

A 4.7 kΩ resistor is one of the most common values in electronics, frequently used as pull-up resistors in digital circuits and current-limiting resistors for LEDs.

Example 2: 5-Band Precision Resistor (Brown-Black-Black-Red-Brown)

A precision resistor has bands of Brown, Black, Black, Red, and Brown.

Calculation: Brown = 1, Black = 0, Black = 0, so digits = 100. Red multiplier = 10² = 100. R = 100 × 100 = 10,000 Ω (10 kΩ). Brown tolerance band means ±1%, so the actual value ranges from 9.9 kΩ to 10.1 kΩ.

The 5-band code allows for three significant figures, providing finer granularity than the 4-band system. This 10 kΩ precision resistor is commonly used in voltage divider circuits and operational amplifier configurations where accuracy matters.

Common Uses

  • Identifying unknown resistors in electronics kits, bins, or salvage components for project building
  • Verifying resistor values during circuit assembly to prevent costly wiring errors and board damage
  • Selecting the correct replacement resistor when repairing consumer electronics, appliances, or industrial equipment
  • Teaching electronics fundamentals to students learning about color coding, Ohm's law, and circuit design
  • Determining the tolerance and precision of resistors for sensitive analog circuits like audio amplifiers and sensors
  • Checking resistor temperature coefficient for applications involving high temperatures or thermal cycling

Common Mistakes

  • Reading the resistor from the wrong end — the tolerance band (gold or silver) should be on the right; if there is no tolerance band, the wider band indicates the multiplier side
  • Confusing similar colors — red and orange are frequently mistaken in poor lighting, as are blue and violet; use good lighting and a magnifying glass when needed
  • Misinterpreting the multiplier band — a gold multiplier band means ×0.1, not ×10; this is used for resistors below 10 Ω
  • Forgetting that black and brown can both look similar on aged or heat-damaged resistors — a resistor that has overheated may have bands that are difficult to read accurately

Pro Tip

When you cannot read the bands due to age or damage, use a digital multimeter to measure the resistance directly. However, remember that in-circuit measurements can be inaccurate because parallel paths affect the reading. Always desolder at least one leg of the resistor from the circuit board for an accurate measurement. For surface-mount resistors (SMD), the value is printed as a 3-digit or 4-digit code. A code of 472 means 47 × 10² = 4.7 kΩ, and 1001 means 100 × 10¹ = 1.0 kΩ.

Frequently Asked Questions

A 5-band resistor has three digit bands, one multiplier band, and one tolerance band. The first three bands represent significant digits, the fourth band is the multiplier, and the fifth is tolerance. For example, Brown-Black-Black-Red-Brown means 100 × 100 = 10,000 Ω with 1% tolerance. Five-band resistors are used when tighter precision is needed.

The sixth band on a 6-band resistor indicates the temperature coefficient of resistance (TCR), measured in parts per million per degree Celsius (ppm/°C). A brown band means 100 ppm/°C, meaning the resistance changes by 0.01% for every 1°C temperature change. This is important for precision circuits operating in varying thermal environments.

Resistors follow standard values defined by the E-series (E6, E12, E24, etc.). The E12 series includes 1.0, 1.2, 1.5, 1.8, 2.2, 2.7, 3.3, 3.9, 4.7, 5.6, 6.8, and 8.2 in each decade. So 4.7 kΩ exists because 4.7 is an E12 value. These values were chosen so that the tolerance ranges just barely overlap, minimizing manufacturing cost.

Zero-ohm resistors are used as jumpers on PCBs and have a single black band in the center. They look like resistors but provide a direct electrical connection. They are useful when automated assembly equipment cannot handle wire jumpers. Their nominal resistance is approximately 0 Ω with a typical tolerance of 50 mΩ or less.

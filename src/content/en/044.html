What Is a Voltage Drop Calculator?

A voltage drop calculator determines the actual voltage loss along an electrical circuit based on cable cross-section, length, current, and supply voltage. For a 2.5 mm² copper cable carrying 16 A over 30 m at 230 V, the calculator shows 6.72 V drop (2.92%), confirming compliance with the 3% lighting circuit limit or 5% power circuit limit per IEC 60364-5-52.

Excessive voltage drop causes motors to overheat, lights to dim, and electronic equipment to malfunction. A 10% voltage drop on a 230 V circuit delivers only 207 V to equipment, reducing motor torque by 19% and increasing current draw by 11%, shortening equipment life by 40-50%.

The calculator uses conductor resistivity (0.0175 Ω·mm²/m for copper, 0.0282 Ω·mm²/m for aluminum), circuit length, and load current to compute voltage loss in volts and percentage.

Voltage Drop Formulas With Calculations

Single-Phase Voltage Drop (V) = (2 × Length × Current × Resistivity) ÷ Cross-Section

Example: 2.5 mm² copper, 30 m, 16 A: (2 × 30 × 16 × 0.0175) ÷ 2.5 = 6.72 V

Voltage Drop (%) = (Voltage Drop ÷ Supply Voltage) × 100

6.72 V ÷ 230 V × 100 = 2.92%

Three-Phase Voltage Drop (V) = (√3 × Length × Current × Resistivity) ÷ Cross-Section

Example: 6 mm² copper, 45 m, 32 A at 400 V: (1.732 × 45 × 32 × 0.0175) ÷ 6 = 7.27 V

Three-Phase Drop (%) = 7.27 V ÷ 400 V × 100 = 1.82%

Power Loss in Cable (W) = Current² × Resistance

Resistance = (2 × Length × Resistivity) ÷ Cross-Section = (2 × 30 × 0.0175) ÷ 2.5 = 0.42 Ω

Power loss = 16² × 0.42 = 107.5 W (dissipated as heat in cable)

6 Steps To Calculate and Verify Voltage Drop

  1. Identify circuit parameters: Record supply voltage (230 V single-phase or 400 V three-phase), cable cross-section (mm²), one-way length (m), and design current (A). For existing installations, measure actual current with clamp meter rather than relying on nameplate ratings.
  2. Determine conductor material and resistivity: Copper: 0.0175 Ω·mm²/m at 20°C. Aluminum: 0.0282 Ω·mm²/m. Adjust for operating temperature: at 70°C, copper resistivity increases to 0.021 Ω·mm²/m (20% higher than 20°C). Use operating temperature resistivity for accurate results.
  3. Apply correct formula for system type: Single-phase uses factor 2 (outbound and return conductors). Three-phase uses √3 (1.732) accounting for phase relationships. DC circuits use factor 2 like single-phase. Select formula before calculation to avoid 73% error from using wrong factor.
  4. Calculate voltage drop in volts and percentage: Compute absolute drop first, then divide by nominal voltage. Compare against limits: 3% for lighting (6.9 V at 230 V), 5% for power (11.5 V at 230 V, 20 V at 400 V). Record both values for documentation.
  5. Verify against equipment tolerance: Check equipment nameplate for voltage tolerance. Motors typically ±10%, electronics ±5%, LED drivers ±10%. If calculated drop exceeds tolerance, increase cable size. For sensitive equipment, limit drop to 2% maximum regardless of code minimums.
  6. Calculate annual energy loss from cable heating: Power loss = I²R. For 107.5 W loss operating 8 hours daily: 107.5 W × 8 h × 365 = 313.9 kWh annually. At €0.28/kWh, wasted energy costs €88/year. Upsizing cable may pay back in 3-5 years through reduced losses.

4 Practical Examples

Example 1 — Garden Lighting Circuit: 12 × 50 W LED floodlights = 600 W at 230 V = 2.6 A. Cable: 1.5 mm² copper, length 65 m. Drop: (2 × 65 × 2.6 × 0.0175) ÷ 1.5 = 3.95 V = 1.72%. Acceptable for lighting. However, startup current for some LED drivers is 5× nominal (13 A), causing momentary 19.8 V drop (8.6%). Lights may flicker on startup. Solution: upsize to 2.5 mm², reducing startup drop to 11.9 V (5.2%).

Example 2 — Workshop Air Compressor: 3 kW motor, 230 V, single-phase = 13 A full load, 65 A starting (5×). Cable: 4 mm² copper, 28 m length. Running drop: (2 × 28 × 13 × 0.0175) ÷ 4 = 3.19 V = 1.39%. Starting drop: (2 × 28 × 65 × 0.0175) ÷ 4 = 15.9 V = 6.9%. Motor contactor may chatter during start. Upsize to 10 mm²: starting drop 6.4 V (2.8%), reliable starting guaranteed.

Example 3 — Apartment Building Riser: Three-phase 400 V, 63 A main feed, 18 m vertical riser. Cable: 16 mm² copper 4-core. Drop: (1.732 × 18 × 63 × 0.0175) ÷ 16 = 2.15 V = 0.54%. Excellent. However, branch circuits from each floor meter to apartments add additional drop. Top floor apartment with 10 mm² subfeed over 35 m at 32 A: (1.732 × 35 × 32 × 0.0175) ÷ 10 = 3.4 V. Total drop from street to apartment: 2.15 + 3.4 = 5.55 V (1.39% at 400 V). Well within 5% limit.

Example 4 — Agricultural Irrigation Pump: 7.5 kW three-phase pump, 400 V, 13.5 A, located 180 m from supply. Initial design: 10 mm² aluminum cable. Drop: (1.732 × 180 × 13.5 × 0.0282) ÷ 10 = 11.9 V = 2.98%. Borderline acceptable. However, aluminum expands 35% more than copper with heat, loosening terminations. Upgrade to 16 mm² copper: drop 4.6 V (1.15%). Additional cable cost €340-€420, but prevents voltage-related motor failures costing €1,200-€1,800 per replacement.

4 Costly Mistakes To Avoid

  • Using nominal length instead of actual cable route: Electricians measure wall-to-wall distance (25 m) but cable routes through ceilings, under floors, and around obstacles, totaling 38 m actual. Voltage drop calculated at 25 m shows 2.1%, but actual 38 m run drops 3.2%, exceeding lighting circuit limits. Always add 20-35% to measured distance for routing contingencies.
  • Ignoring harmonic currents in neutral conductors: Modern LED lighting and switch-mode power supplies generate third harmonic currents that add in neutral conductors. Neutral current can exceed phase current by 20-40%. Standard voltage drop calculations assume balanced neutral current. For circuits with >50% electronic loads, size neutral same as phase conductors and calculate drop using actual measured neutral current.
  • Calculating drop at 20°C but operating at 70°C: Cable resistance increases 28% from 20°C to 70°C. A circuit showing 4% drop at 20°C actually drops 5.1% at operating temperature, exceeding the 5% power circuit limit. Always multiply calculated drop by 1.28 temperature factor for final verification. Alternatively, use 0.021 Ω·mm²/m resistivity for copper at 70°C directly.
  • Forgetting to account for transformer impedance: Voltage drop calculations assume infinite bus (constant source voltage). Real transformers have 4-6% impedance. A 100 kVA transformer at 80% load already drops 4-5% at secondary terminals. Adding 4% cable drop results in 8-9% total drop at equipment. Always measure actual supply voltage at distribution board under load before calculating cable drops.

5 Professional Tips

  • Measure voltage at equipment terminals under load: Digital multimeter readings at equipment while running reveals actual voltage versus calculated. Difference indicates connection resistance or calculation errors. Acceptable variance: ±0.5% between calculated and measured. Larger discrepancies warrant investigation before equipment damage occurs.
  • Install voltage monitors on critical circuits: Digital voltage monitors (€45-€85) display real-time voltage and alert on undervoltage (<207 V) or overvoltage (>253 V). Install on server rooms, medical equipment, and production machinery. Early warning prevents 70-80% of voltage-related equipment failures. Data logging models record voltage history for troubleshooting.
  • Use parallel cables for high-current long runs: Two 95 mm² cables in parallel carry same current as one 185 mm² cable but cost 15-20% less and are easier to pull. Ensure equal length (±2 cm) to balance current sharing. Parallel cables require 100-150 A lugs at terminations (€18-€28 each). Total savings on 100 m run: €800-€1,400 versus single large conductor.
  • Apply antioxidant compound on aluminum terminations: Aluminum oxide forms instantly on exposure to air, increasing contact resistance 100-200×. Antioxidant compound (€25-€40 per 250 g tube) prevents oxidation, maintaining stable contact resistance under 0.0001 Ω. Required by IEC 61238 for all aluminum connections. One tube treats 80-120 terminations.
  • Document voltage drop calculations in handover dossier: Include circuit schedules showing calculated drop for each final circuit. Provides baseline for future troubleshooting and demonstrates code compliance. Facility managers use documentation when adding loads. Professional documentation format: circuit ID, cable size, length, current, calculated drop, measured voltage. Adds 2-3 hours to project but prevents 20-30 hours of troubleshooting over building lifetime.

These limits apply from origin of installation (main distribution board) to final outlet. For sensitive equipment (servers, medical devices), manufacturers often specify 2% maximum. Always follow equipment manufacturer requirements when stricter than code minimums.

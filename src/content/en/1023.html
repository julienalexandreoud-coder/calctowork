What Is Gravitational Potential Energy?

Gravitational potential energy (GPE) measures the energy stored in an object due to its position in a gravitational field. A 70 kg person climbing 10 meters up a staircase gains GPE = mgh = 70 × 9.81 × 10 = 6,867 joules — equivalent to the kinetic energy of a 1,000 kg car moving at 4.2 m/s (15 km/h). This energy comes from chemical energy in muscles and can be recovered: jumping down converts GPE back to kinetic energy, then to heat and sound on impact. Hydroelectric dams exploit this principle — water falling 100 m releases 981 kJ per kilogram, spinning turbines that generate electricity for homes and industries.

Engineers calculate GPE for roller coaster design, elevator systems, pumped-storage hydroelectricity, and spacecraft trajectories. A roller coaster car (800 kg with passengers) at the top of a 45 m hill has GPE = 800 × 9.81 × 45 = 353,160 J relative to the bottom. This converts to kinetic energy at the bottom: v = √(2gh) = √(2 × 9.81 × 45) = 29.7 m/s (107 km/h) — neglecting friction. In reality, 10-15% energy loss to friction and air resistance reduces speed to ~100 km/h. Pumped-storage facilities (like Bath County, Virginia, 38 GW·h capacity) pump water uphill during low-demand periods, recovering 70-80% of energy when generating during peak demand.

Gravitational Potential Energy Formulas With Worked Calculations

Near Earth's surface (constant g approximation):

U = mgh

Where U is gravitational potential energy in joules, m is mass in kg, g is gravitational acceleration (9.81 m/s²), and h is height above reference in meters.

For large altitude changes or orbital mechanics (variable g):

U = -GMm / r

Where G = 6.674×10⁻¹¹ N·m²/kg² (gravitational constant), M is the central body's mass, and r is distance from the center of mass.

Change in GPE between two altitudes:

ΔU = GMm(1/r1 - 1/r2)

For escape velocity calculations, total energy must equal zero:

½mv² - GMm/r = 0, giving vescape = √(2GM/r)

Practical example: A 1,500 kg elevator rises 30 floors (approximately 100 m). GPE gain: U = 1,500 × 9.81 × 100 = 1,471,500 J (1.47 MJ). If the elevator takes 20 seconds: power required = 1,471,500 / 20 = 73,575 W (73.6 kW or 99 HP). With 85% motor efficiency and counterweight balancing 50% of car weight: electrical power = (73.6 × 0.5) / 0.85 = 43.3 kW. Regenerative braking can recover 60-70% of this energy when descending with load.

For satellite launch: 1,000 kg satellite to 400 km altitude (r = 6,771 km from Earth center). Using variable-g formula: U = -GMm/r. Earth: GM = 3.986×10¹⁴ m³/s². At surface (r₁ = 6,371 km): U₁ = -3.986×10¹⁴ × 1,000 / 6.371×10⁶ = -62.56 GJ. At orbit (r₂ = 6,771 km): U₂ = -3.986×10¹⁴ × 1,000 / 6.771×10⁶ = -58.88 GJ. ΔU = -58.88 - (-62.56) = 3.68 GJ. Plus kinetic energy for orbital velocity (7,670 m/s): KE = 0.5 × 1,000 × 7,670² = 29.4 GJ. Total energy: 33.1 GJ — equivalent to 9,200 kWh or 780 kg of rocket fuel (assuming 100% efficiency; actual fuel is 10-20× higher due to rocket equation).

6 Steps To Calculate Gravitational Potential Energy

  1. Define the reference level: Choose where h = 0 (or U = 0) — typically ground level, floor level, or the lowest point in the system. GPE is relative; only changes (ΔU) matter physically. A book on a 1 m high table has U = mg × 1 m relative to the floor, but U = 0 relative to the table. For consistency, use the same reference throughout a problem. In orbital mechanics, the convention is U = 0 at infinity (r → ∞), making all bound orbits have negative total energy.
  2. Measure the object's mass: Weigh the object or obtain mass from specifications. Convert to kg: 1 lb = 0.4536 kg, 1 g = 0.001 kg. For vehicles, include passengers, cargo, and fuel. A 1,200 kg car with driver (75 kg), four passengers (4 × 70 kg), and half tank of fuel (40 kg) totals 1,595 kg. For fluids, use density × volume: 1,000 L of water = 1,000 kg. In GPE = mgh, mass scales energy linearly — doubling mass doubles GPE for the same height.
  3. Determine height change: Measure vertical displacement from reference to the object's position. Use a tape measure, laser rangefinder, or altimeter. For ramps and inclines, calculate vertical component: h = L sin(θ), where L is ramp length and θ is angle. A 10 m ramp at 15°: h = 10 × sin(15°) = 10 × 0.259 = 2.59 m vertical rise. For multi-story buildings, estimate 3-4 m per floor. Only vertical height matters — moving horizontally at constant altitude doesn't change GPE.
  4. Select the appropriate formula: Use U = mgh for altitude changes small compared to Earth's radius (Δh << 6,371 km). This applies to buildings, mountains, aircraft, and most engineering. Use U = -GMm/r for satellites, spacecraft, and planetary-scale problems. For a 10 km altitude change (aircraft): constant-g gives U = mg × 10,000. Variable-g gives ΔU = GMm(1/6,371,000 - 1/6,381,000) = mg × 9,984 — only 0.16% difference. Constant-g is accurate to 1% for altitudes up to 64 km.
  5. Calculate potential energy: Apply the formula with consistent SI units. For a 50 kg object lifted 3 m: U = 50 × 9.81 × 3 = 1,471.5 J. For a 2,000 kg roller coaster at 60 m: U = 2,000 × 9.81 × 60 = 1,177,200 J (1.18 MJ). For orbital: 500 kg satellite at 500 km altitude (r = 6,871 km). U = -3.986×10¹⁴ × 500 / 6.871×10⁶ = -29.0 GJ. Compare to surface: Usurface = -31.3 GJ. ΔU = 2.3 GJ gained in lifting. Always track signs: positive ΔU means energy input required; negative ΔU means energy released.
  6. Apply energy conservation: In closed systems, total mechanical energy (KE + PE) is conserved (neglecting friction). Initial E = final E. A pendulum released from h = 0.5 m: initial E = mgh = mg × 0.5. At bottom (h = 0): all PE converted to KE, so ½mv² = mg × 0.5. Mass cancels: v = √(2g × 0.5) = √9.81 = 3.13 m/s — independent of mass! For a roller coaster: Etop = mghtop + ½mvtop². At any point: mgh + ½mv² = constant. Solve for v at any height: v = √[2g(htop - h) + vtop²].

4 Practical Gravitational Potential Energy Examples

Example 1 — Hydroelectric Power Generation: A dam holds back a reservoir 80 m above the turbines. Water flow rate: 500 m³/s (500,000 kg/s). GPE released per second: P = ṁgh = 500,000 × 9.81 × 80 = 392,400,000 W = 392 MW. With 90% turbine efficiency and 95% generator efficiency: electrical output = 392 × 0.90 × 0.95 = 335 MW — enough for 250,000 homes (at 1.3 kW average consumption). Annual energy (assuming 60% capacity factor): 335 MW × 8,760 hr × 0.60 = 1,760 GWh. At $0.08/kWh: $141 million annual revenue. Reservoir volume: 1 km³ (10⁹ m³) provides 10⁹ / (500 × 3600) = 555 hours (23 days) of continuous full-power operation before depletion.

Example 2 — Pole Vault Physics: A pole vaulter (mass 70 kg) runs at 9 m/s. Initial KE = ½ × 70 × 9² = 2,835 J. At peak height, all KE converts to GPE (neglecting pole energy storage and air resistance): mgh = 2,835. h = 2,835 / (70 × 9.81) = 4.13 m above takeoff point. Add takeoff height (center of mass ~1 m above ground): total bar height ≈ 5.1 m. World record (6.23 m by Armand Duplantis) exceeds this because: (1) the pole stores elastic energy during bending, releasing it to add ~1 m; (2) the vaulter's center of mass passes ~0.5 m below the bar (body arches over); (3) the vaulter pushes off the ground, adding ~0.3 m. Elite vaulters convert 90%+ of kinetic energy to height.

Example 3 — Elevator Energy Recovery: A 2,000 kg elevator (car + passengers) descends 50 m from the 15th floor. GPE released: U = 2,000 × 9.81 × 50 = 981,000 J (0.273 kWh). With a counterweight (1,200 kg, balancing 60% of loaded car): net mass = 800 kg. Net GPE = 800 × 9.81 × 50 = 392,400 J (0.109 kWh). Regenerative drive (85% efficiency) returns 392,400 × 0.85 = 333,540 J (0.093 kWh) to the building's electrical system. For 100 trips per day: 9.3 kWh recovered — worth $1.30/day at $0.14/kWh, or $475/year. Over a 20-year elevator life: $9,500 savings, justifying the regenerative drive's $3,000 premium.

Example 4 — Rocket Escape Energy: A 10,000 kg spacecraft must escape Earth's gravity from the surface. Escape velocity: vesc = √(2GM/r) = √(2 × 3.986×10¹⁴ / 6.371×10⁶) = √(1.251×10⁸) = 11,186 m/s (40,270 km/h). Required KE: ½mv² = 0.5 × 10,000 × 11,186² = 625.6 GJ. GPE change from surface to infinity: ΔU = GMm/r = 3.986×10¹⁴ × 10,000 / 6.371×10⁶ = 625.6 GJ. Total energy: 1.25 TJ. Chemical rocket fuel (hydrazine) has 5 MJ/kg specific energy — theoretical minimum fuel: 1.25×10¹² / 5×10⁶ = 250,000 kg. But rockets must carry fuel to lift fuel (Tsiolkovsky rocket equation), requiring 10-50× more. A Falcon 9 first stage burns 395,000 kg of propellant to lift 22,800 kg to LEO — mass ratio 18:1.

4 Common Gravitational Potential Energy Mistakes

Mistake 1 — Using Slant Distance Instead of Vertical Height: GPE depends only on vertical displacement, not path length. Pushing a 100 kg cart up a 10 m ramp at 30° requires the same GPE gain as lifting it 5 m vertically: h = 10 × sin(30°) = 5 m. U = 100 × 9.81 × 5 = 4,905 J. Using the ramp length (10 m) instead of height gives U = 9,810 J — double the correct value. The ramp reduces required force (490 N parallel to ramp vs. 981 N vertical) but not total energy. Friction adds extra energy requirement on the ramp, but GPE change is identical regardless of path — gravity is a conservative force.

Mistake 2 — Confusing Weight and Mass: Weight (W = mg, in newtons) is the gravitational force; mass (m, in kg) is the amount of matter. On Earth, a 10 kg object has weight 98.1 N. On the Moon (g = 1.62 m/s²), the same 10 kg object weighs 16.2 N. GPE = mgh uses mass, not weight. Using weight directly: U = Wh (if W is in newtons, h in meters, gives joules correctly). But don't use "weight in kg" (actually mass) with g = 9.81 — that doubles the g factor. A 50 "kg weight" lifted 2 m: U = 50 kg × 9.81 × 2 m = 981 J. Not U = 50 × 9.81 × 9.81 × 2 = 4,811 J (wrong).

Mistake 3 — Forgetting That GPE Can Be Negative: In orbital mechanics, the convention U = -GMm/r sets U = 0 at infinity. Bound orbits (satellites, planets) have negative total energy. A satellite with U = -30 GJ and KE = 15 GJ has total E = -15 GJ — bound to Earth. If KE increases to 30 GJ (via rocket burn), total E = 0 — escape trajectory. If KE = 40 GJ, total E = +10 GJ — hyperbolic escape with excess velocity. Near Earth's surface, we typically set U = 0 at ground level, making all GPE positive. Both conventions are valid; just be consistent within a problem and understand that only ΔU matters physically.

p>Mistake 4 — Ignoring Air Resistance in Energy Conservation: Applying mgh = ½mv² assumes no energy losses. A skydiver falling 1,000 m would reach v = √(2gh) = √(2 × 9.81 × 1,000) = 140 m/s (504 km/h) without air resistance. In reality, terminal velocity is ~55 m/s (200 km/h) for a spread-eagle position — air resistance dissipates 85% of GPE as heat in the air, not kinetic energy. For dense, streamlined objects (steel ball, skydiver in head-down position), air resistance is minimal over short distances. For light or high-drag objects (feather, parachute), most GPE converts to heat, not KE. Include drag work: mgh = ½mv² + Wdrag.

4 Professional Tips for GPE Applications

Tip 1 — Use Energy Methods for Complex Systems: Energy conservation often simplifies problems that are difficult with forces. A roller coaster with multiple hills: instead of calculating forces at every point, use Etotal = mgh + ½mv² = constant. Given initial height and speed, find speed at any other height: v = √[v₀² + 2g(h₀ - h)]. For a loop-the-loop: minimum speed at top requires centripetal force ≥ gravity, so v²/r ≥ g, giving vmin = √(gr). Energy: ½mvbottom² = ½mvtop² + mg(2r). Substitute vtop² = gr: vbottom² = gr + 4gr = 5gr. Minimum entry height: h = vbottom²/(2g) = 5r/2 = 2.5r — the classic result.

Tip 2 — Account for Rotational Kinetic Energy: Rolling objects have both translational KE (½mv²) and rotational KE (½Iω²). A solid sphere rolling down a ramp: mgh = ½mv² + ½Iω². For a sphere, I = (2/5)mr² and ω = v/r (no slipping). So mgh = ½mv² + ½(2/5 mr²)(v²/r²) = ½mv² + (1/5)mv² = (7/10)mv². Solve: v = √(10gh/7) = √(1.43gh). Compare to sliding (no rotation): v = √(2gh) = √(2gh). The rolling sphere is slower by factor √(1.43/2) = 0.845 — 15.5% of GPE goes to rotation, not translation. Different shapes have different factors: hollow cylinder (I = mr²) has v = √(gh); solid cylinder (I = ½mr²) has v = √(4gh/3).

Tip 3 — Calculate Pumped-Storage Economics: Pumped hydro stores energy by moving water between reservoirs at different elevations. Energy capacity: E = ηρVgh, where η = round-trip efficiency (0.70-0.80), ρ = 1,000 kg/m³, V = volume in m³. For 100 MWh (360 GJ) storage at 500 m head with 75% efficiency: V = E/(ηρgh) = 360×10⁹ / (0.75 × 1,000 × 9.81 × 500) = 98,000 m³ (98 megaliters). Power rating depends on flow rate: P = ηρQgh. For 500 MW output: Q = P/(ηρgh) = 500×10⁶ / (0.90 × 1,000 × 9.81 × 500) = 113 m³/s. Reservoir empties in 98,000/113 = 867 seconds (14.5 minutes) at full power — typical for peaking plants that run 2-6 hours daily.

Tip 4 — Use GPE for Impact Analysis: Falling objects gain kinetic energy equal to lost GPE. A 1 kg tool dropped from 100 m: KE = mgh = 1 × 9.81 × 100 = 981 J. Impact velocity: v = √(2gh) = 44.3 m/s (159 km/h). Impact force depends on stopping distance: F = KE/d. Stopping in 0.01 m (hitting concrete): F = 981/0.01 = 98,100 N (10 tons). Stopping in 0.5 m (safety net): F = 981/0.5 = 1,962 N (200 kg) — survivable. This is why fall arrest systems include energy absorbers (tearing webbing, deformable lanyards) that extend stopping distance to 1-2 m, limiting force to <6 kN (OSHA limit). A 100 kg worker falling 2 m with 1 m arrest distance: F = 100 × 9.81 × 2 / 1 = 1,962 N — safe.

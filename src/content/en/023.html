What is the Subgrade Preparation Calculator?

The subgrade preparation calculator determines the volume of material required to construct the subgrade layer of a road, pavement, or platform. The subgrade is the prepared and compacted natural or imported soil layer that serves as the foundation for all subsequent pavement layers. Accurate subgrade volume calculation is essential for earthwork planning, material procurement, and ensuring adequate load-bearing capacity for the finished surface.

In Spanish construction, subgrade preparation follows CTE DB-SE-C (Seguridad Estructural - Cimientos) and PG-3 (Pliego de Prescripciones Técnicas Generales para Obras de Carreteras) standards. The calculator helps contractors working on urban roads (viales urbanos), industrial platforms (plataformas industriales), and parking areas (aparcamientos) determine material quantities for lime-stabilized soils, granular fills, and capping layers.

Formula Explained

V = L × W × E

Where V is the subgrade material volume in cubic metres (m³), L is the length of the subgrade area in metres (m), W is the width in metres (m), and E is the subgrade layer thickness in metres (m). This formula calculates the compacted volume of the prepared subgrade layer.

If improving poor native soil with imported granular material, the thickness represents the depth of the replacement or improvement layer. For soil stabilization calculations, multiply volume by soil density (typically 1.8-2.0 tonnes/m³) to determine stabilizing agent quantities.

Step-by-Step Calculation Process

  1. Survey the area: Measure total length (L) and width (W) of the area requiring subgrade preparation. For irregular shapes, divide into rectangles and calculate each separately, then sum the areas.
  2. Determine design thickness: Consult geotechnical report and pavement design specifications. Typical subgrade thickness ranges from 200 mm (light traffic) to 600 mm (heavy industrial). Thickness depends on CBR requirements and expected loading.
  3. Calculate compacted volume: Multiply length × width × thickness (V = L × W × E). This is the final compacted volume required after all earthwork operations are complete.
  4. Assess native soil conditions: Determine if existing soil is suitable for reuse. Soils with CBR < 5% require removal and replacement or stabilization. Organic soils, peat, and expansive clays must be removed entirely.
  5. Apply compaction factor: Multiply compacted volume by 1.15-1.25 to obtain loose material order quantity. Granular subgrade materials typically use 1.20 factor; stabilized soils may use 1.15.
  6. Calculate stabilizing agents (if applicable): For lime or cement stabilization, multiply soil volume by density (1.9 t/m³) and stabilization percentage (3-5% for lime, 4-6% for cement) to determine binder quantities in tonnes.

Real Examples with Specific Numbers

Example 1: Residential road subgrade preparation

A new residential access road (viales de urbanización) is 200 m long and 6.00 m wide. The specification calls for a 300 mm thick subgrade layer of imported granular fill (todo-uno 0/40 mm), compacted to 98% of Maximum Dry Density.

Calculation: V = 200 × 6.00 × 0.30 = 360.00 m³

A total of 360 m³ of granular fill material is required for the subgrade layer. Assuming a compaction factor of 1.20, the loose volume to be delivered is 360 × 1.20 = 432 m³, equivalent to approximately 43 truck loads of 10 m³ each. At €18/m³ delivered, material costs total €6,480.

Example 2: Industrial yard platform subgrade

An industrial storage yard (patio de almacenamiento) measures 45 m long by 30 m wide. The geotechnical report recommends removing the top 200 mm of unsuitable organic soil and replacing it with a 400 mm thick layer of selected granular subgrade material.

Calculation: V = 45 × 30 × 0.40 = 540.00 m³

The subgrade requires 540 m³ of selected granular material. Additionally, 45 × 30 × 0.20 = 270 m³ of unsuitable organic soil must be excavated and disposed of. At €15/tonne disposal fee and 1.6 t/m³ soil density, disposal costs reach €6,480. Subgrade fill at €20/m³ costs €10,800.

Example 3: Lime-stabilized subgrade for parking area

A commercial parking area (aparcamiento) measures 80 m × 25 m. The native clay soil has CBR = 3%, requiring stabilization. Design specifies 3% lime stabilization of 350 mm depth.

Calculation: Soil volume = 80 × 25 × 0.35 = 700.00 m³. Soil mass = 700 × 1.90 t/m³ = 1,330 tonnes. Lime required = 1,330 × 0.03 = 39.9 tonnes.

Order 40 tonnes of quicklime (cal viva). At €85/tonne, lime costs €3,400. Mixing and compaction labour adds €4-6/m², totaling €8,000-12,000 for the 2,000 m² area.

Example 4: Capping layer for weak subgrade

A warehouse access road (acceso a nave industrial) is 150 m long and 8.00 m wide. Geotechnical investigation reveals very weak subgrade (CBR < 2%) requiring a 500 mm capping layer of granular material before standard subgrade construction.

Calculation: Capping volume = 150 × 8.00 × 0.50 = 600.00 m³. With 1.22 compaction factor: 600 × 1.22 = 732 m³ loose.

This substantial capping layer requires 73 truck loads of granular fill. At €19/m³, the capping layer alone costs €11,400. This is in addition to the standard 300 mm subgrade above it (360 m³, €6,840).

Example 5: Cement-stabilized subgrade for heavy-duty platform

A logistics platform (plataforma logística) requires 5% cement stabilization for crane operating areas. The stabilized zone measures 60 m × 40 m × 0.40 m depth.

Calculation: Soil volume = 60 × 40 × 0.40 = 960.00 m³. Soil mass = 960 × 1.95 t/m³ = 1,872 tonnes. Cement required = 1,872 × 0.05 = 93.6 tonnes.

Order 94 tonnes of CEM II/B-S 32.5N cement. At €95/tonne, cement costs €8,930. Soil mixing with reclaimer/stabilizer equipment costs €3.50-5.00/m², adding €8,400-12,000 to the project.

Common Mistakes

  • Confusing subgrade thickness with total pavement thickness: Subgrade is only the lowest structural layer. A typical road may have 300 mm subgrade + 200 mm subbase + 150 mm base + 50 mm asphalt = 700 mm total. Calculate each layer separately with appropriate material specifications.
  • Not accounting for CBR requirements: Design thickness depends on required CBR and expected traffic loading. Using 200 mm thickness when 400 mm is required leads to premature pavement failure. Always verify thickness against geotechnical recommendations and PG-3 tables.
  • Ignoring compaction factor in material orders: Ordering compacted volume instead of loose volume results in 15-25% shortages. This causes work stoppages while waiting for additional deliveries, potentially delaying the entire project by days.
  • Failing to deduct existing structures: Utility manholes, drainage inlets, and foundation footings within the subgrade area displace material. For a 100 m² area with four 1 m² manholes, this saves 4 m³ of fill material - worth €80-100 on material costs.

Pro Tips from Specialists

  1. Review geotechnical CBR values before calculating: Spanish PG-3 specifications define subgrade quality zones (E1 through E4). E1 (CBR ≥ 20%) requires minimal improvement; E4 (CBR < 5%) needs capping layers or full replacement. Request CBR testing per UNE 103502 before finalizing material quantities.
  2. Calculate stabilizing agent by dry mass, not volume: Lime and cement percentages apply to dry soil mass. For 1,000 m³ of soil at 1.9 t/m³ with 5% moisture content, dry mass = 1,000 × 1.9 / 1.05 = 1,810 tonnes. At 4% lime, order 72.4 tonnes, not the 76 tonnes you'd calculate from wet mass.
  3. Plan for proof rolling before subgrade acceptance: Spanish specifications require proof rolling with 10-tonne roller to verify subgrade adequacy. Areas showing excessive deflection (>25 mm) require additional treatment. Budget 5-10% contingency for spot improvements identified during proof rolling.
  4. Separate calculations for different subgrade zones: Road subgrades often have different thicknesses under travel lanes versus shoulders. Calculate each zone separately: travel lanes at 400 mm, shoulders at 300 mm. This precision prevents over-ordering expensive granular materials.
  5. Document moisture content adjustments: Optimal compaction occurs at Proctor optimum moisture content (typically 8-12% for granular soils). If soil arrives too dry, add water at 2-3% of soil mass. For 500 tonnes of soil at 2% water addition, this requires 10,000 litres - factor water truck costs accordingly.

Frequently Asked Questions

The CBR is a measure of the load-bearing capacity of soil, expressed as a percentage relative to a standard crushed rock material. A higher CBR indicates stronger soil. For road subgrades, Spanish PG-3 defines: E1 (CBR ≥ 20%), E2 (10-20%), E3 (5-10%), E4 (< 5%). Minimum CBR of 5-15% is typically required depending on traffic loading. If natural soil has lower CBR, improve through replacement, additional thickness, or chemical stabilisation.

The subgrade is the prepared natural soil or imported fill at the bottom of the pavement structure. The subbase is a layer of granular material (todo-uno 0/40 mm) placed on top of the subgrade to distribute loads. The base course is a higher-quality crushed aggregate (0/31.5 mm or 0/22 mm) above the subbase. Together with the wearing course (aglomerado asfáltico), they form the complete pavement structure. Each layer has different material specifications per PG-3.

Subgrade thickness depends on soil strength (CBR) and expected traffic loading. For light residential roads (tráfico ligero), 200-300 mm subgrade is typical. For urban collector roads (viales de distribución), 300-400 mm. For heavy industrial roads or logistics platforms, 400-600 mm may be required. The geotechnical engineer's recommendation based on site-specific soil testing per UNE 103502 should always govern design thickness.

Soil stabilisation (with lime or cement) is generally more economical than full replacement when existing soil is moderately weak (CBR 2-5%) and required improvement is modest. Replacement is preferred when soil is highly organic, contaminated, or extremely weak (CBR < 2%). Stabilisation costs €8-15/m² versus €25-40/m² for full removal and replacement. Stabilisation also reduces haulage traffic since material is treated in-situ.

Spanish PG-3 specifications require 98% of Modified Proctor density (UNE 103500) for subgrade under paved areas, and 95% for unpaved shoulders. Achieve this with vibratory rollers (10-12 tonnes) for large areas or plate compactors for confined zones. Test density every 500-1,000 m² using nuclear density gauge or sand replacement method per UNE 103501.

What is the Curb/Kerb Calculator?

The Curb/Kerb Calculator determines concrete volume for precast or cast-in-situ concrete curbs used along roads, pavements, parking areas, and pedestrian zones. Municipal contractors and civil engineering teams throughout Spain use this tool when estimating materials for bordillos y aceras that define roadway boundaries, channel surface water, and provide lateral pavement support.

You may also find the Isolated Footing Calculator, Strip Foundation Calculator, and Mass Concrete Calculator useful.

Concrete curbs serve multiple functions: they separate vehicular carriageways from pedestrian footways, provide edge restraint for asphalt pavements, direct surface water to drainage inlets, and prevent vehicles from leaving designated driving areas. Standard Spanish curbs measure 150-250 mm wide, 150-300 mm high, installed in continuous runs along road alignments.

This calculator processes three inputs: length, width, and height. Results help contractors order correct concrete quantities for cast-in-situ curbs or calculate equivalent volumes for precast unit procurement. Accurate estimation prevents material delays on linear infrastructure projects where continuous progress is essential.

Formula Explained with Actual Calculations

Volume (m³) = Length (m) × Width (m) × Height (m)

The formula treats curbs as rectangular prisms. Many curbs feature shaped profiles (rounded tops, battered faces, haunches) slightly increasing volume. For precise shaped curb calculations, use manufacturer's cross-sectional area multiplied by length. This calculator provides accurate estimates for standard rectangular curbs.

Input Variables:

  • Length (L): Total linear curb length in meters. Example: 200 m for residential street both sides.
  • Width (W): Horizontal base thickness in meters. Typical: 0.15-0.25 m.
  • Height (H): Vertical exposed height in meters. Typical: 0.15-0.30 m.

Standard Curb Dimensions (Spain):

  • Residential streets: 200 mm wide × 250 mm high
  • Highway curbs: 250 mm wide × 300 mm high
  • Parking lot curbs: 150 mm wide × 200 mm high
  • Accessible dropped kerbs: 150 mm wide × 0 mm (flush)
  • Barrier curbs: 250 mm wide × 400 mm high
  • Concrete haunch beneath: typically 150×100 mm additional

Step-by-Step Calculation Process

Step 1: Measure total curb run length from road plans. Residential street 200 m long with curbs both sides = 400 m total.

Step 2: Identify curb cross-section from standard drawings. Typical: 200 mm wide × 250 mm high.

Step 3: Convert dimensions to meters: 200 mm = 0.20 m, 250 mm = 0.25 m.

Step 4: Calculate volume per meter: 0.20 × 0.25 = 0.05 m³/m.

Step 5: Calculate total volume: 0.05 m³/m × 400 m = 20.0 m³.

Step 6: Add haunch volume (if cast-in-situ): 400 m × 0.15 × 0.10 = 6.0 m³. Total: 26.0 m³.

Real Examples with Specific Numbers

Example 1: Residential Street Curb

A new residential street in Sevilla requires concrete curbs along both sides. Street length is 200 m, so total curb run is 400 m. Curb cross-section measures 200 mm wide and 250 mm high, cast-in-situ using steel formwork. HA-25 concrete with XF1 exposure specified.

Calculation:

Volume per meter: 0.20 × 0.25 = 0.05 m³/m

Total curb volume: 0.05 × 400 = 20.0 m³

Concrete haunch (150×100 mm): 0.15 × 0.10 × 400 = 6.0 m³

Total concrete: 20.0 + 6.0 = 26.0 m³

Material Order: Order 26 m³ of HA-25 with air-entrainment (XF1 exposure) at €95/m³ = €2,470. Use slip-form curb machine for efficient placement—outputs 200-500 m per day. Install expansion joints every 5 m with 10 mm compressible filler. Cure with spray-applied membrane within 15 minutes of finishing.

Example 2: Parking Lot Perimeter Curb (Precast)

A commercial parking lot requires perimeter curbs totaling 180 m. Precast units measure 1.0 m long, 150 mm wide, 200 mm high. Units bedded on concrete haunch. XF1 exposure class for outdoor conditions.

Calculation:

Volume per unit: 1.0 × 0.15 × 0.20 = 0.03 m³

Total volume: 0.03 × 180 = 5.4 m³

Concrete haunch (150×100 mm): 0.15 × 0.10 × 180 = 2.7 m³

Total concrete: 5.4 + 2.7 = 8.1 m³

Material Order: Order 180 precast curb units at €12/unit = €2,160 (includes 3% breakage allowance = 185 units ordered). Order 3 m³ of HA-25 for haunch at €92/m³ = €276. Order 1 m³ of mortar (1:4 cement:sand) for bedding at €120/m³ = €120. Install with string line for alignment, 10 mm joints filled with flexible sealant.

Example 3: Highway Median Barrier Curb

A highway median requires barrier curbs (New Jersey profile equivalent) along 2.5 km. Curb cross-section averages 400 mm wide at base, 400 mm high, tapering to 200 mm at top. Cast-in-situ using slip-form paver. HA-30 concrete with fiber reinforcement specified.

Calculation:

Average width: (0.40 + 0.20) / 2 = 0.30 m

Cross-section area: 0.30 × 0.40 = 0.12 m²

Total volume: 0.12 × 2,500 = 300.0 m³

With 5% waste: 300.0 × 1.05 = 315.0 m³

Material Order: Order 315 m³ of HA-30 with polypropylene fibers (0.9 kg/m³) at €105/m³ = €33,075. Slip-form paver outputs 300-500 m per day—project duration 5-8 days. Install steel reinforcement (Ø12 longitudinal bars top and bottom) continuously. Expansion joints every 10 m with dowel bars for load transfer.

Example 4: Sidewalk Curb with Accessible Ramps

An urban sidewalk renovation includes 500 m of standard curb (180×200 mm) plus 12 accessible dropped kerb ramps. Each ramp is 2.0 m wide with flush transition (0 mm height at crossing). HA-25 concrete specified.

Calculation:

Standard curb length: 500 m - (12 ramps × 2.0 m) = 500 - 24 = 476 m

Standard curb volume: 0.18 × 0.20 × 476 = 17.14 m³

Ramp volume (averaged 100 mm height): 0.18 × 0.10 × 24 = 0.43 m³

Concrete haunch (entire length): 0.15 × 0.10 × 500 = 7.5 m³

Total concrete: 17.14 + 0.43 + 7.5 = 25.07 m³

Material Order: Order 26 m³ of HA-25 at €92/m³ = €2,392. Install tactile paving (domed surface) at all ramp crossings for visually impaired pedestrians. Tactile paving: 24 ramps × 0.8 m² = 19.2 m² at €45/m² = €864. Provide 1:20 maximum slope on ramp transitions per accessibility regulations (CTE-SUA).

Example 5: Industrial Yard Curbs and Wheel Stops

A logistics center requires perimeter curbs (300 m, 250×300 mm) plus 50 parking bay wheel stops (each 2.0 m long, 200×150 mm). All elements cast-in-situ with HA-30 concrete for heavy vehicle resistance.

Calculation:

Perimeter curb: 0.25 × 0.30 × 300 = 22.5 m³

Wheel stops: 50 × 0.20 × 0.15 × 2.0 = 3.0 m³

Haunch for curbs: 0.20 × 0.15 × 300 = 9.0 m³

Total concrete: 22.5 + 3.0 + 9.0 = 34.5 m³

Material Order: Order 35 m³ of HA-30 at €98/m³ = €3,430. Wheel stops require Ø10 rebar anchors drilled into slab. Paint wheel stops with yellow/black hazard stripes after 28-day cure. Install reflective markers on perimeter curbs at 10 m intervals for nighttime visibility. Consider steel bollards at building corners for vehicle impact protection.

Common Mistakes

  • Forgetting concrete haunch volume: Curbs bedded on concrete haunches (typically 150×100 mm) add 25-40% to total concrete volume. A 400 m curb run at 0.05 m³/m = 20 m³ curb plus 0.015 m³/m × 400 = 6 m³ haunch. Omitting haunch creates 23% material shortage.
  • Not accounting for curved sections: Curved curb runs have longer outer radius than inner radius. For a 50 m radius curve, outer curb (50.25 m radius) is 1.5% longer than inner curb (50.00 m radius). Calculate inner and outer lengths separately for tight curves, use average for radii over 30 m.
  • Ignoring expansion joint material displacement: Cast-in-situ curbs require expansion joints every 4-6 m with 10-20 mm compressible filler. Joint material displaces concrete volume. For 400 m curb with joints every 5 m (80 joints × 0.015 m × 0.20 m × 0.25 m) = 0.06 m³—small but measurable on large projects.
  • Using exposed height instead of total height: Curb height should include buried portion below finished grade. A 250 mm exposed curb might have 100 mm buried for haunch embedment, totaling 350 mm. Using 250 mm underestimates volume by 29%. Always use total form height for calculations.

Pro Tips from Curb Construction Specialists

Tip 1: Specify freeze-thaw resistant concrete (XF1 or XF2 exposure class) for all exterior curbs exposed to de-icing salts. Air-entraining admixture creates microscopic air bubbles (4-6% content) accommodating ice expansion. Non-air-entrained curbs in freeze-thaw environments spall within 2-3 winters, requiring costly replacement.

Tip 2: Use slip-form curb machines for runs over 200 m. Slip-form pavers place 200-500 m of curb per day with consistent quality, eliminating formwork costs. Manual formwork suitable for short runs, tight curves, and complex transitions. Slip-form rental: €300-500/day plus operator €200/day. Break-even versus hand placement: approximately 150 m.

Tip 3: Install weep holes or drainage gaps at low points to prevent water accumulation behind curbs. Drill Ø50 mm holes at 3-5 m intervals through curb base, or leave 20 mm gaps between precast units at drainage locations. Water trapped behind curbs freezes in winter, causing heaving and misalignment.

Tip 4: Place control joints in cast-in-situ curbs at 4-6 m intervals using preformed joint strips or early-entry saws. Joints control shrinkage cracking, preventing random unsightly cracks. Joint depth: 25% of curb height (50-75 mm for 250 mm curb). Fill joints with flexible polyurethane sealant after 28-day cure.

Tip 5: For precast curb installation, prepare level bedding haunch before delivery. Use laser level to set haunch elevation within ±5 mm tolerance. Install string line 100 mm above finished curb elevation for alignment guide. Bed each unit in 10-15 mm mortar, tap to line with rubber mallet. Backfill immediately after installation to prevent movement.

Frequently Asked Questions

Standard curb heights: 150 mm for residential streets and parking lots, 200-250 mm for main roads and highways, 300 mm barrier curbs where vehicle containment required. Spanish regulations specify maximum 180 mm for accessible routes. Dropped kerbs at pedestrian crossings must be flush (0 mm) with tactile paving. Curb height selected based on function: drainage control, vehicle restraint, or pedestrian safety.

Precast curbs: faster installation, consistent factory quality, ideal for short runs and limited access sites. Cost: €10-15/m installed. Cast-in-situ curbs: more economical for runs over 200 m, follow complex curves easily, monolithic appearance. Cost: €8-12/m for slip-form, €15-25/m for hand-placed. Choice depends on project scale, budget, timeline, and equipment availability.

HA-25 (C25/30) standard for most curb applications. Exposure class XF1 (moderate freeze-thaw with de-icing salts) for outdoor curbs in cold climates, XC4 for non-freeze environments. Maximum water-cement ratio 0.50 for durability. Slump 60-90 mm for cast-in-situ curbs—stiff mix maintains shape during placement. Add integral color pigment for architectural curbs (€5-8/m³ additional).

Concrete haunch beneath curbs typically 100-150 mm deep and 150-250 mm wide. Frost-prone areas require haunch extension below frost line (800-1,200 mm in northern Spain), necessitating deeper excavation with granular sub-base beneath haunch. Haunch provides lateral stability preventing curb tipping under vehicle impact. Minimum haunch: 100 mm deep × 150 mm wide for pedestrian-area curbs.

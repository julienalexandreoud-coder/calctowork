What is Overtime Pay?

Overtime pay is premium compensation that employers must pay hourly workers for hours worked beyond the standard 40-hour workweek, typically at 1.5 times the regular hourly rate — commonly called "time and a half." This legal requirement, established by the Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA) in the United States, ensures workers are fairly compensated for extended hours and discourages employers from overworking staff.

Consider this concrete example: James works as an electrician earning €28 per hour. In a typical week, he works 40 hours and earns €1,120. During a busy project week, he works 52 hours. His employer doesn't simply pay 52 × €28 = €1,456. Instead, the first 40 hours pay at the regular rate (€1,120), and the 12 overtime hours pay at 1.5× the rate: €28 × 1.5 = €42 per overtime hour. Those 12 hours earn €504, bringing his total to €1,120 + €504 = €1,624. The overtime premium added €168 extra compared to straight-time calculation.

Not all workers qualify for overtime. Salaried exempt employees — typically executives, administrators, professionals, and outside salespeople earning above €684 per week (€35,568 annually) — don't receive overtime regardless of hours worked. This distinction creates significant income differences between hourly and salaried workers during heavy workloads.

How it Works: Formulas Explained

Overtime calculation follows a clear formula, but complications arise with shift differentials, bonuses, and multiple pay rates within the same week.

Basic Overtime Formula:

Regular Pay = Regular Hours × Hourly Rate
Overtime Rate = Hourly Rate × 1.5
Overtime Pay = Overtime Hours × Overtime Rate
Total Gross Pay = Regular Pay + Overtime Pay

Example with Standard Calculation:
Hourly Rate: €24/hour
Hours Worked: 47 hours
Regular Pay = 40 × €24 = €960
Overtime Rate = €24 × 1.5 = €36/hour
Overtime Pay = 7 × €36 = €252
Total Gross Pay = €960 + €252 = €1,212

Weighted Average for Multiple Rates: When you work different jobs at different rates in the same week, overtime is calculated using a weighted average rate.

Weighted Average Rate = Total Earnings at All Rates ÷ Total Hours
Overtime Rate = Weighted Average × 0.5 (the premium portion only)

Example: You work 30 hours at €20/hour (€600) and 15 hours at €30/hour (€450), total 45 hours.
Total Earnings = €600 + €450 = €1,050
Weighted Average = €1,050 ÷ 45 = €23.33/hour
Overtime Premium = €23.33 × 0.5 = €11.67 per overtime hour
Overtime Pay = 5 × €11.67 = €58.35
Total Pay = €1,050 + €58.35 = €1,108.35

Double Time Calculations: Some employers and union contracts pay double time (2× rate) for holidays, Sundays, or hours beyond 12 in a single day. The formula adjusts accordingly:

Double Time Rate = Hourly Rate × 2
Double Time Pay = Double Time Hours × Double Time Rate

Step-by-Step Guide

  1. Track all hours worked accurately: Record start time, end time, and meal breaks for each day. Federal law requires employers to keep time records for hourly workers. If you worked 8:00 AM to 5:30 PM with a 30-minute unpaid lunch, that's 9 hours worked. Use timesheets, time clocks, or apps — but keep your own records as backup.
  2. Identify regular versus overtime hours: Standard overtime kicks in after 40 hours in a workweek (Sunday-Saturday or any fixed 7-day period your employer defines). Some states like California also require daily overtime after 8 hours in a day. If you worked 10 hours Monday through Thursday (40 hours) and 6 hours Friday, you have 46 total hours — 40 regular, 6 overtime.
  3. Determine your regular rate of pay: For simple hourly workers, this is your stated hourly wage. If you receive non-discretionary bonuses, shift differentials, or commissions, these may need to be included in your regular rate. A €20/hour worker who receives a €100 weekly attendance bonus has an effective rate of (€800 + €100) ÷ 40 = €22.50/hour for overtime purposes.
  4. Calculate the overtime rate: Multiply your regular rate by 1.5 for standard overtime. €25/hour becomes €37.50/hour for overtime. For double time situations, multiply by 2.0. Keep track of which hours qualify for which rate — some employers track this automatically in payroll systems.
  5. Compute regular and overtime pay separately: Regular Pay = 40 × your hourly rate. Overtime Pay = overtime hours × overtime rate. Add them together for gross pay. Keeping them separate helps verify your paycheck and understand earnings composition.
  6. Account for taxes and deductions: Overtime pay is taxed as ordinary income, not at a special rate. However, the extra income can push you into a higher tax bracket for that paycheck, increasing withholding. Your actual tax liability is determined when you file your annual return — the withholding is just a prepayment.

Real-World Examples

Example 1: Retail Worker During Holiday Season
Hourly Rate: €16.50
Week 1 (Normal): 38 hours = €627
Week 2 (Black Friday): 54 hours
Regular Pay = 40 × €16.50 = €660
Overtime Pay = 14 × (€16.50 × 1.5) = 14 × €24.75 = €346.50
Week 2 Total = €660 + €346.50 = €1,006.50
The 16 extra hours earned €346.50 instead of €264 — an €82.50 overtime premium.

Example 2: Nurse with Shift Differential
Base Rate: €38/hour
Night Shift Differential: +€4/hour (€42/hour for night shifts)
Week Schedule: 3 day shifts (24 hours at €38) + 3 night shifts (24 hours at €42) = 48 hours
Total Earnings Before Overtime = (24 × €38) + (24 × €42) = €912 + €1,008 = €1,920
Weighted Average Rate = €1,920 ÷ 48 = €40/hour
Overtime Premium = €40 × 0.5 = €20/hour
Overtime Pay = 8 × €20 = €160
Total Gross Pay = €1,920 + €160 = €2,080

Example 3: Construction Worker with Per Diem
Hourly Rate: €32/hour
Hours Worked: 56 hours
Per Diem: €45/day (non-taxable allowance, doesn't count toward overtime rate)
Work Days: 6 days
Regular Pay = 40 × €32 = €1,280
Overtime Pay = 16 × (€32 × 1.5) = 16 × €48 = €768
Per Diem = 6 × €45 = €270 (separate from taxable wages)
Total Gross Taxable Pay = €1,280 + €768 = €2,048
Total Compensation = €2,048 + €270 = €2,318

Example 4: Server with Tips
Cash Wage: €7.25/hour (tipped minimum wage)
Average Tips: €18/hour
Hours Worked: 45 hours
Tips are not included in overtime calculation — only the employer-paid wage counts.
Regular Pay = 40 × €7.25 = €290
Overtime Pay = 5 × (€7.25 × 1.5) = 5 × €10.875 = €54.38
Gross Wages = €290 + €54.38 = €344.38
Tips (reported) = 45 × €18 = €810
Total Earnings = €344.38 + €810 = €1,154.38
Note: If tips + wages don't meet standard minimum wage (€7.25 × 45 = €326.25), employer must make up the difference.

Example 5: Software Contractor with Rush Project
Hourly Rate: €85/hour (1099 contractor — no legal overtime requirement)
Standard Week: 40 hours = €3,400
Rush Week: 65 hours
If treated as employee: Regular = 40 × €85 = €3,400; OT = 25 × €127.50 = €3,187.50; Total = €6,587.50
As contractor: 65 × €85 = €5,525 (no overtime premium)
Contractors negotiate higher base rates to compensate for lack of overtime and benefits. This contractor's €85/hour versus an employee's €60/hour roughly equals out when factoring overtime, benefits, and taxes.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake 1: Assuming all workers get overtime
Salaried exempt employees don't receive overtime regardless of hours worked. If you're classified as exempt but work 60+ hours weekly, your effective hourly rate plummets. A €75,000 salary working 40 hours equals €36/hour; working 60 hours drops it to €24/hour. Before accepting salaried positions, estimate realistic weekly hours and calculate effective hourly rate.

Mistake 2: Not counting all compensable time
Time spent in mandatory meetings, safety training, cleaning equipment, or waiting for assignments often counts as hours worked. If you arrive 15 minutes early for mandatory equipment checkout and stay 10 minutes late for shift reports, that's 25 minutes daily — over 2 hours weekly that should be compensated. Employers who ignore this face wage-and-hour lawsuits.

Mistake 3: Misunderstanding the workweek definition
Overtime is calculated per workweek, not per pay period. You can't average 80 hours over two weeks to avoid overtime. Week 1: 50 hours (10 OT); Week 2: 30 hours (0 OT) = 10 overtime hours total. Some employers try to average across biweekly periods — this violates FLSA rules.

Mistake 4: Forgetting that bonuses affect overtime rate
Non-discretionary bonuses (attendance, performance, production) must be included in your regular rate for overtime purposes. A €20/hour worker who earns a €100 weekly bonus has a regular rate of (€800 + €100) ÷ 40 = €22.50 for overtime calculation. The overtime premium increases from €10/hour to €11.25/hour. Employers often miss this requirement.

Pro Tips

Tip 1: Track your own hours independently
Don't rely solely on employer timekeeping systems. Keep a personal log with dates, start/end times, and total hours. Photograph timesheets or save digital copies. If disputes arise — unpaid overtime, incorrect hours, or termination — your records become crucial evidence. The Department of Labor investigates based on worker complaints with supporting documentation.

Tip 2: Understand your state's overtime laws
Federal law sets the 40-hour threshold, but states can be more generous. California requires overtime after 8 hours in a day (not just 40 per week) and double time after 12 hours. Alaska, Colorado, and Nevada have daily overtime rules. Washington recently increased the salary threshold for exemption. Know your state's specific requirements — they override federal law when more favorable to workers.

Tip 3: Negotiate overtime policies before accepting jobs
During job offers, ask about typical weekly hours, overtime frequency, and compensation structure. "What's the average weekly hour for this role?" "How often does the team work overtime?" "Is overtime compensated or expected as part of salary?" A €70,000 job with 50-hour weeks pays less per hour than a €60,000 job with 40-hour weeks. Calculate effective hourly rate before accepting.

Tip 4: Use overtime strategically for financial goals
When saving for a specific goal — emergency fund, vacation, down payment — volunteer for overtime during peak periods. Earning time-and-a-half accelerates savings: €500 weekly overtime pay becomes €750, adding €250 extra toward goals. However, avoid long-term reliance on overtime for baseline expenses — it's unsustainable and can lead to burnout.

Tip 5: Know your rights for reporting violations
Employers cannot retaliate against workers who report overtime violations. If you're denied rightful overtime, file a complaint with the Department of Labor's Wage and Hour Division or consult an employment attorney. Statute of limitations is typically 2 years (3 years for willful violations). Keep pay stubs, time records, and communication about hours worked as evidence.

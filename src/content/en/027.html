→ Tile area: 0.6 × 0.6 = 0.36 m²

How to Calculate How Many Tiles You Need

Tiles needed = (Area ÷ Tile area) × (1 + Waste%)   |   Boxes = ⌈Tiles ÷ Tiles per box⌉

Where Area is the total surface to tile in m² (or ft²), Tile area is the area of one tile (width × height), and Waste% is typically 10–15% for standard layouts, 15–20% for diagonal layouts or complex rooms.

Step-by-Step Process

  1. Measure your area: Multiply length × width for rectangular rooms. For L-shaped rooms, split into rectangles and add. For walls: measure height × width of each section, then subtract doors and windows.
  2. Convert tile size to matching units: If your room is in metres, convert tile size from cm to metres (30 cm = 0.30 m). One 30×30 cm tile covers 0.09 m²; one 60×60 cm tile covers 0.36 m².
  3. Calculate base tiles: Divide total area by single tile area. A 20 m² room with 60×60 cm tiles needs 20 ÷ 0.36 = 55.6 → 56 tiles minimum.
  4. Add waste percentage: Multiply by (1 + waste factor). For 10% waste: 56 × 1.10 = 61.6 → round up to 62 tiles. Always round up, never down.
  5. Calculate boxes: Divide tiles needed by tiles per box, rounding up. If each box contains 8 tiles: ⌈62 ÷ 8⌉ = 8 boxes.

How Much Tile Adhesive Do You Need?

Once you know the tile area, you can calculate tile adhesive (tile cement) and grout quantities:

  • Standard wall tile adhesive: 1.5–2 kg per m² for 3–4 mm bed thickness (smooth walls)
  • Floor tile adhesive: 3–4 kg per m² for 6–8 mm bed thickness
  • Large-format tile adhesive: 5–7 kg per m² using the full-bed or back-buttering method
  • Rapid-set adhesive: Same coverage but sets in 2–4 hours (useful for wet rooms)

Example: 15 m² kitchen floor with 60×60 cm tiles needs 15 × 4 kg = 60 kg of floor adhesive. A standard 20 kg bag covers 5 m² — you need 3 bags.

Grout Calculator — How Much Grout Do You Need?

Grout quantity depends on joint width and tile thickness. A simplified formula:

Grout (kg) = Area (m²) × Joint width (mm) × Tile thickness (mm) × 0.0016 × Grout density

For practical use: standard 2 mm grout joints with 8 mm thick tiles typically use 0.3–0.4 kg of grout per m². For 3 mm joints, use 0.5–0.6 kg/m². For 5 mm joints (natural stone), use 0.8–1.0 kg/m².

Common Mistakes When Calculating Tiles

  • Not adding waste: The most common mistake. Even a perfectly rectangular room has cuts at edges and around obstacles. Always add minimum 10%.
  • Forgetting dye lot matching: Order all tiles from the same batch (lot number). Tiles from different production runs can vary slightly in colour. If you run short later, you may not be able to match.
  • Using nominal tile size instead of actual: A "30×30 cm" tile is usually 29.5–29.7 cm actual size. Grout joints add back the difference. Use actual tile dimensions in the calculator.
  • Not accounting for pattern matching: Patterned or wood-effect tiles with "repeat patterns" need extra waste (20–25%) to align the design.
  • Ignoring diagonal layouts: Diagonal (45°) layouts increase waste to 15–20% because corner cuts are always large triangles, not narrow strips.
  • Confusing floor tile and wall tile adhesive: Floor adhesive is rated for load-bearing. Using wall adhesive on floors risks cracking and movement failures.

Pro Tips from Tiling Specialists

  1. Buy 10% extra as standard, 15% for complex rooms: For simple rectangular rooms, 10% covers standard breakage and edge cuts. For bathrooms with multiple walls, niches, and fixture cut-outs, use 15%. For diagonal or herringbone patterns, use 20%.
  2. Start your layout from the centre: Snap chalk lines from the centre of each wall to find the true centre of the room. Starting from a corner usually results in uneven cuts on the opposite wall. Centre-starting gives symmetrical cuts on all edges.
  3. Check floor flatness before tiling: Use a 2-metre straight edge. Gaps over 3 mm indicate a high spot or dip that must be levelled before tiling. Large-format tiles (60×60 cm and above) are unforgiving — even a 2 mm undulation causes hollow spots and eventual cracking.
  4. Use the right notch trowel: For tiles under 30×30 cm: 6 mm square-notch. For 30–60 cm tiles: 10 mm V-notch. For large-format over 60 cm: 12 mm square-notch with back-buttering. Correct trowel size ensures 95%+ contact area (the building code minimum).
  5. Keep the spare tiles: Store remaining tiles in a dry, cool location. If a tile chips years later, you'll need an exact match. Keep at least 3–5 tiles per room as spares for future repairs.

Work and Power Calculator

This calculator determines the mechanical work done by a force and the power required to perform it in a given time, fundamental concepts in physics and engineering.

You may also find the Kinetic Energy Calculator, Potential Energy Calculator, and One Rep Max Calculator useful.

Work and power formulas

The basic formulas are:

  • Work: W = F × d (force times distance)
  • Power: P = W / t (work per unit time)
  • Alternative power: P = F × v (force times velocity)

Work is measured in joules (J), power in watts (W) and 1 watt = 1 joule/second.

Example 1: pushing an object

Problem: A force of 50 N is applied to move an object 10 m in 5 seconds.

  1. Work:
    • W = 50 × 10 = 500 J.
  2. Power:
    • P = 500 / 5 = 100 W.

Answer: Work = 500 J, Power = 100 W.

Example 2: lifting a load

Problem: Lift 200 kg to 3 m height in 10 seconds.

  1. Force (weight):
    • F = m × g = 200 × 9.81 = 1,962 N.
  2. Work:
    • W = 1,962 × 3 = 5,886 J.
  3. Power:
    • P = 5,886 / 10 = 588.6 W.

Answer: Work = 5,886 J, Power = 588.6 W.

Common uses of work and power calculation

  • Sizing motors and machines for specific tasks.
  • Computing energy consumption of industrial processes.
  • Evaluating athlete performance in strength sports.
  • Designing lifting and transport systems.
  • Analyzing efficiency of tools and equipment.
  • Solving physics problems in education.

Common mistakes with work and power

  • Not considering the angle between force and direction of motion.
  • Confusing power (W) with work (J).
  • Not converting units correctly (horsepower to watts: 1 hp ≈ 745.7 W).
  • Forgetting that work is only done when there is displacement.

Pro tip

If the force is not parallel to the displacement, use W = F × d × cos(θ), where θ is the angle between the force and the direction of motion. When θ = 90°, the work is zero.

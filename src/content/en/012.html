What is the Floor Screed/Slab Calculator?

The Floor Screed/Slab Calculator determines concrete volume for ground-bearing floor slabs (soleras) and screed layers in residential, commercial, and industrial construction. Contractors throughout Spain use this tool when ordering ready-mix concrete for soleras de hormigón that serve as ground floors, workshop surfaces, and warehouse slabs.

You may also find the Hollow Brick Calculator, Concrete Block Calculator, and Stone Masonry Calculator useful.

While similar to plain concrete volume calculations, the solera calculator focuses specifically on floor slab applications where reinforcement placement, surface finishing, and joint layout critically affect performance. Floor screeds typically range from 80 mm for light residential use to 200+ mm for heavy industrial floors supporting forklift traffic.

This calculator processes three inputs: length, width, and thickness. Results help contractors order correct concrete quantities, plan reinforcement placement, and schedule power floating operations. Accurate volume estimation prevents costly delays from concrete shortages during critical floor placement.

Formula Explained with Actual Calculations

Volume (m³) = Length (m) × Width (m) × Thickness (m)

The formula calculates floor screed volume as a rectangular prism. Floor thickness determined by expected loads, subgrade quality, and reinforcement type. Light residential floors: 100-120 mm. Industrial floors with forklift traffic: 150-200 mm. Heavy industrial: 200-300 mm.

Input Variables:

  • Length (L): Longest floor dimension in meters. Example: 12.0 m for house ground floor.
  • Width (W): Shorter perpendicular dimension in meters. Example: 10.0 m.
  • Thickness (T): Vertical slab depth in meters. Example: 0.12 m (120 mm).

Typical Floor Screed Specifications:

  • Residential ground floor: 100-120 mm, HA-25, A-200 mesh
  • Garage floor: 120-150 mm, HA-25, A-200 mesh
  • Workshop floor: 150-180 mm, HA-30, A-300 mesh
  • Industrial warehouse: 180-250 mm, HA-30, A-400 mesh or fibers
  • Heavy industrial: 250-350 mm, HA-35, dual reinforcement layers

Step-by-Step Calculation Process

Step 1: Extract floor dimensions from architectural plans. Drawing might specify: "Solera S-1: 12.0×10.0×0.12 m"

Step 2: Verify dimensions are in meters. Convert: 120 mm = 0.12 m, 15 cm = 0.15 m.

Step 3: Calculate surface area: 12.0 m × 10.0 m = 120 m².

Step 4: Calculate volume: 120 m² × 0.12 m = 14.4 m³.

Step 5: Add 5% waste factor: 14.4 × 1.05 = 15.12 m³.

Step 6: Plan pour sequence: 15 m³ requires 2 truck deliveries (8 m³ each). Divide into 4 bays of 30 m² with construction joints.

Common Mistakes

  • Omitting sub-base preparation from estimate: Solera volume is concrete only. Granular sub-base (100-200 mm compacted gravel) is separate material cost. For 120 m² floor with 150 mm sub-base: 18 m³ gravel at €18/m³ = €324 often forgotten in budget estimates.
  • Forgetting edge thickening volume: Many floor slabs have thickened edges (150-200 mm instead of 100-120 mm) to support wall loads. Perimeter thickening 0.30 m wide × 0.05 m additional on 44 m perimeter adds 44 × 0.30 × 0.05 = 0.66 m³—significant for small floors.
  • Ignoring damp proof membrane requirement: DPM doesn't affect concrete volume but omitting from material list causes moisture problems. Ground-bearing slabs without DPM experience rising damp, floor finish failure, and indoor air quality issues. Always include 0.20 mm polyethylene with 300 mm laps in estimates.
  • Pouring excessive area without joints: Large unrestrained pours crack from shrinkage. Divide floors into bays (25-36 m² typical) with construction joints. Calculate each bay volume separately for scheduling. A 200 m² floor in single pour will develop random shrinkage cracks within weeks.

Pro Tips from Floor Slab Specialists

Tip 1: Specify power-floated surface (acabado helicóptero) for industrial and commercial floors. Two-pass floating: first pass with float blades flat to consolidate surface, second pass with blades angled to produce dense smooth finish. Timing critical—too early tears surface, too late concrete won't finish. For HA-30 concrete in 20°C weather: first pass 1-2 hours, second pass 3-4 hours after placement.

Tip 2: Add steel or polypropylene fibers to industrial floor mixes. Steel fibers (30-40 kg/m³) replace welded mesh, providing 3-dimensional crack control and improved impact resistance. Polypropylene fibers (0.9 kg/m³) reduce plastic shrinkage cracking. Fiber cost: €2-3/kg steel, €4-5/kg polypropylene. Labor savings from eliminating mesh installation often offset fiber costs.

Tip 3: Position welded mesh in upper third of slab (30 mm below surface) where shrinkage stresses highest. Use plastic spacer chairs (sillas) at 1 m intervals to maintain position during pour. Mesh on sub-base (common mistake) provides minimal shrinkage crack control. For 120 mm slab, mesh at 90 mm depth, not 30 mm depth.

Tip 4: Cure floor slabs minimum 7 days using curing compound, wet burlap, or plastic sheeting. Uncured slabs lose 30% potential strength through evaporation and develop surface dusting (polvillo). Apply curing compound within 20 minutes of final finishing while surface still dark. White-pigmented compounds provide visual coverage verification.

Tip 5: Install contraction joints by saw-cutting within 6-12 hours after placement (depending on temperature). Cut depth: 25% of slab thickness (30 mm for 120 mm slab). Spacing: 24-36 times slab thickness (3-4 m for 120 mm slab). Early-entry saws minimize raveling. Unjointed slabs crack randomly; saw-cut joints create controlled crack locations.

What is Prime Factorization?

Prime factorization breaks any whole number greater than 1 into its fundamental building blocks—the prime numbers that multiply together to create it. Think of primes as the atoms of arithmetic: just as molecules decompose into atoms, composite numbers decompose into primes.

Take the number 84. You can divide it by 2 to get 42, then divide 42 by 2 to get 21. Now 21 won't divide evenly by 2, so try the next prime: 21 ÷ 3 = 7. And 7 is itself prime. Putting this together: 84 = 2 × 2 × 3 × 7, or written with exponents: 84 = 2² × 3 × 7.

The Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic guarantees that every integer greater than 1 has exactly one prime factorization. Whether you start dividing by 2, 3, or any other prime, you'll always arrive at the same result. The number 360 always factors to 2³ × 3² × 5, no matter which path you take.

This uniqueness makes prime factorization indispensable. When simplifying √84, you write √(2² × 3 × 7) = 2√21. When finding GCF(84, 126), you compare 84 = 2² × 3 × 7 with 126 = 2 × 3² × 7 and multiply common primes: 2 × 3 × 7 = 42. Cryptography systems like RSA depend on the fact that factoring large numbers remains computationally difficult.

How It Works: Prime Factorization Methods Explained

Two main approaches dominate prime factorization: the factor tree method and successive prime division. Both produce identical results but appeal to different thinking styles.

Successive Prime Division: This systematic method tests primes in ascending order. To factor 504:

  • Divide by 2: 504 ÷ 2 = 252, then 252 ÷ 2 = 126, then 126 ÷ 2 = 63 (three 2's so far)
  • 63 won't divide by 2, try 3: 63 ÷ 3 = 21, then 21 ÷ 3 = 7 (two 3's)
  • 7 is prime, so divide by 7: 7 ÷ 7 = 1 (one 7)
  • Result: 504 = 2³ × 3² × 7

Factor Tree Method: Start with any factor pair and branch out. For 504, you might choose 504 = 24 × 21. Then factor 24 = 6 × 4 = (2 × 3) × (2 × 2) and 21 = 3 × 7. Collecting all the primes at the "leaves": 2, 2, 2, 3, 3, 7. Written compactly: 504 = 2³ × 3² × 7.

When to Stop Testing Primes: You only need to test primes up to the square root of your number. If you're factoring 97, calculate √97 ≈ 9.85. Test primes 2, 3, 5, 7. None divide evenly, so 97 is prime. This works because if 97 had a factor larger than √97, it would also need a factor smaller than √97 to multiply to 97.

Exponential Notation: Writing 2 × 2 × 2 × 3 × 3 × 7 becomes tedious. Exponent notation compresses this to 2³ × 3² × 7. This format makes comparing factorizations easier. To find GCF(504, 720), compare 504 = 2³ × 3² × 7 with 720 = 2⁴ × 3² × 5. Take the smallest exponent for each common prime: 2³ × 3² = 8 × 9 = 72.

Step-by-Step Guide: Factorizing Any Number

Step 1: Check for Small Prime Factors First
Start with 2—if the number is even, divide repeatedly until you get an odd quotient. For 468: 468 ÷ 2 = 234, then 234 ÷ 2 = 117. You've extracted 2². Now work with 117, which is odd.

Step 2: Test Divisibility by 3
Add the digits: if the sum is divisible by 3, so is the number. For 117: 1 + 1 + 7 = 9, which divides by 3. So 117 ÷ 3 = 39. Check 39: 3 + 9 = 12, still divisible by 3. So 39 ÷ 3 = 13. You've extracted 3². Now work with 13.

Step 3: Continue with Larger Primes
Test 5 (does it end in 0 or 5?), then 7, 11, 13, and so on. Our remaining number is 13, which is itself prime. Divide: 13 ÷ 13 = 1. You've completed the factorization.

Step 4: Write the Complete Factorization
Collect all the primes you extracted: 468 = 2² × 3² × 13. Verify by multiplying: 4 × 9 × 13 = 36 × 13 = 468. This quick check catches arithmetic errors before you proceed.

Step 5: Use Exponential Form
Group repeated primes and write exponents. Instead of 2 × 2 × 3 × 3 × 13, write 2² × 3² × 13. This compact form is standard in mathematics and makes comparisons straightforward.

Step 6: Apply to GCF and LCM Problems
Now use your factorization. To find GCF(468, 630), first factor 630 = 2 × 3² × 5 × 7. Compare with 468 = 2² × 3² × 13. Common primes are 2 and 3². GCF = 2 × 9 = 18. For LCM, take the highest power of each prime: 2² × 3² × 5 × 7 × 13 = 4 × 9 × 5 × 7 × 13 = 16,380.

Real-World Examples with Complete Calculations

Example 1: Simplifying a Square Root
Simplify √1,764. First factor 1,764: it's even, so 1,764 ÷ 2 = 882, then 882 ÷ 2 = 441. The sum 4 + 4 + 1 = 9, so 441 ÷ 3 = 147, then 147 ÷ 3 = 49. And 49 = 7². So 1,764 = 2² × 3² × 7². Now √1,764 = √(2² × 3² × 7²) = 2 × 3 × 7 = 42. Perfect squares have all even exponents in their prime factorization.

Example 2: Finding All Factors of a Number
A teacher has 72 students and wants to know all possible equal group sizes. Factor 72 = 2³ × 3². The total number of factors equals (3+1) × (2+1) = 4 × 3 = 12 factors. To list them, combine primes systematically: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 8, 9, 12, 18, 24, 36, 72. The teacher can create groups of any of these 12 sizes.

Example 3: Reducing a Fraction to Lowest Terms
Reduce 252/396. Factor both: 252 = 2² × 3² × 7 and 396 = 2² × 3² × 11. The GCF is 2² × 3² = 4 × 9 = 36. Divide: 252 ÷ 36 = 7 and 396 ÷ 36 = 11. The reduced fraction is 7/11. Prime factorization reveals the common factors instantly without trial and error.

Example 4: Determining Perfect Cube Status
Is 2,744 a perfect cube? Factor it: 2,744 ÷ 2 = 1,372, ÷ 2 = 686, ÷ 2 = 343. Now 343 = 7³ (since 7 × 7 × 7 = 343). So 2,744 = 2³ × 7³. All exponents are divisible by 3, confirming it's a perfect cube. The cube root is 2 × 7 = 14. Check: 14³ = 2,744. Perfect cubes have all exponents divisible by 3.

Example 5: Gear Ratio Optimization
Two gears have 168 and 252 teeth. To find how many rotations before they realign, factor both: 168 = 2³ × 3 × 7 and 252 = 2² × 3² × 7. LCM = 2³ × 3² × 7 = 8 × 9 × 7 = 504. The first gear rotates 504 ÷ 168 = 3 times, and the second rotates 504 ÷ 252 = 2 times before their teeth realign. This calculation ensures smooth mechanical operation.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mistake 1: Including Composite Numbers in the Factorization
A student factors 60 as 4 × 3 × 5 and stops there. But 4 isn't prime—it's 2². The correct factorization is 2² × 3 × 5. Always verify that every factor is prime. If you can divide it further (other than by 1 and itself), keep going. A quick check: none of your factors should appear in the multiplication table except in the 1s row or their own row.

Mistake 2: Stopping Before Reaching 1
When factoring 105, a student divides by 3 to get 35, then by 5 to get 7, and stops because 7 "looks prime." But you must divide by 7 to reach 1. The complete factorization is 3 × 5 × 7, not 3 × 5 × 7 with 7 left over. The process ends only when your quotient equals 1.

Mistake 3: Skipping Primes in Order
Testing primes out of order causes missed factors. If you test 5 before finishing with 3, you might miss factors. Always proceed systematically: 2, 3, 5, 7, 11, 13, 17, 19, 23... When factoring 221, testing 2, 3, 5, 7, 11, 13 reveals 221 = 13 × 17. Randomly jumping to larger primes wastes time and risks errors.

Mistake 4: Miscounting Exponents
Writing 72 = 2 × 3² instead of 2³ × 3² is a common slip. Count carefully: 72 ÷ 2 = 36, ÷ 2 = 18, ÷ 2 = 9 (that's three 2's), then 9 ÷ 3 = 3, ÷ 3 = 1 (that's two 3's). Use tally marks if needed: 72 → ||| for 2's, || for 3's → 2³ × 3². Double-check by expanding: 8 × 9 = 72. Correct.

Pro Tips for Efficient Factorization

Tip 1: Master Divisibility Rules for Speed
Memorize these shortcuts: a number is divisible by 2 if it ends in 0, 2, 4, 6, or 8; by 3 if its digit sum divides by 3; by 4 if its last two digits form a number divisible by 4; by 5 if it ends in 0 or 5; by 6 if divisible by both 2 and 3; by 9 if its digit sum divides by 9; by 10 if it ends in 0. For 1,386: it's even (÷2 = 693), digit sum 6+9+3 = 18 (÷3 = 231), digit sum 2+3+1 = 6 (÷3 = 77), and 77 = 7 × 11. Result: 2 × 3² × 7 × 11.

Tip 2: Use the Square Root Cutoff
When testing whether a number is prime or finding its largest prime factor, stop at √n. For 149, calculate √149 ≈ 12.2. Test only primes 2, 3, 5, 7, 11. None divide evenly, so 149 is prime. For 143, test the same primes. 143 ÷ 11 = 13, so 143 = 11 × 13. This cutoff dramatically reduces work for large numbers.

Tip 3: Recognize Common Factorizations
Certain numbers appear repeatedly. Memorize these: 12 = 2² × 3, 24 = 2³ × 3, 36 = 2² × 3², 48 = 2⁴ × 3, 60 = 2² × 3 × 5, 72 = 2³ × 3², 84 = 2² × 3 × 7, 96 = 2⁵ × 3, 100 = 2² × 5². When you encounter 840, recognize it as 84 × 10 = (2² × 3 × 7) × (2 × 5) = 2³ × 3 × 5 × 7. Pattern recognition accelerates your work.

Tip 4: Factor Out Powers of 10 First
Numbers ending in zeros factor quickly. For 4,800, write it as 48 × 100 = 48 × 10² = 48 × (2 × 5)² = 48 × 2² × 5². Then factor 48 = 2⁴ × 3. Combine: 2⁴ × 3 × 2² × 5² = 2⁶ × 3 × 5². This approach handles trailing zeros efficiently without repeated division.

Tip 5: Verify with Digital Roots
The digital root (repeated digit sum) provides a quick check. For 2,520 = 2³ × 3² × 5 × 7, the digital root of 2,520 is 2+5+2+0 = 9. The product of digital roots of factors should match: 8 (from 2³) × 9 (from 3²) × 5 × 7 = 8 × 9 × 35 = 2,520, digital root 9. While not foolproof, mismatches indicate errors.

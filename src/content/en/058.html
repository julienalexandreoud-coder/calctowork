What Is an HVAC Duct Sizing Calculator?

An HVAC duct sizing calculator determines optimal duct dimensions for heating, ventilation, and air conditioning systems by analyzing airflow requirements, acceptable pressure drop, and available installation space. Proper duct sizing ensures balanced air distribution, minimizes energy consumption, reduces noise, and maintains equipment efficiency. The calculator applies equal friction or static regain methods to size each duct section from the air handler to every diffuser.

Ductwork represents 20-30% of total HVAC system pressure drop. Undersized ducts force fans to work harder, increasing electricity use 15-40% and creating noise complaints. Oversized ducts waste material costs and ceiling space without providing proportional benefits. The calculator finds the economic optimum—ducts large enough for efficient operation but small enough to fit within budget and spatial constraints.

Modern duct design software uses iterative calculations to balance pressure drops across all branches. This calculator provides the same functionality in an accessible format, showing both circular and rectangular duct options, pressure drop per meter, air velocity, and equivalent lengths for fittings. Whether designing a residential system or commercial VAV network, proper duct sizing is foundational to HVAC performance.

Formulas Explained: Duct Design Equations

The Colebrook-White equation relates pressure drop to airflow and duct characteristics:

ΔP/L = (f × ρ × v²) / (2 × D)

Where f is friction factor, ρ is air density (1.2 kg/m³), v is velocity (m/s), D is hydraulic diameter (m). For HVAC applications, this simplifies to:

ΔP (Pa/m) = 0.0226 × Q^1.85 / D^4.97

Where Q is airflow in m³/h and D is diameter in meters. For 500 m³/h in 250mm duct: ΔP = 0.0226 × 500^1.85 / 0.25^4.97 = 0.65 Pa/m.

Velocity calculation from airflow and area:

v (m/s) = Q (m³/h) / (A (m²) × 3,600)

For 800 m³/h in 0.05 m² duct: v = 800 / (0.05 × 3,600) = 4.44 m/s.

Rectangular duct equivalent diameter (Huebscher formula):

D_eq = 1.30 × (a × b)^0.625 / (a + b)^0.25

A 400mm × 250mm rectangular duct: D_eq = 1.30 × (100,000)^0.625 / (650)^0.25 = 341 mm equivalent round.

Fitting equivalent length calculation:

L_eq = K × D / f

Where K is loss coefficient, D is diameter, f is friction factor. A 90° elbow (K = 0.22) in 300mm duct: L_eq = 0.22 × 0.3 / 0.02 = 3.3 m equivalent straight duct.

Total system pressure drop:

ΔP_total = Σ(ΔP_duct × L) + Σ(ΔP_fittings) + ΔP_equipment

Sum pressure drops through the critical path (longest run or highest resistance branch).

Step-by-Step Guide to Size HVAC Ducts

Step 1: Create Duct Layout Diagram
Sketch the complete duct system showing air handler, main trunks, branches, and diffusers. Label each section with required airflow. Start from the farthest diffuser and work backward to the air handler, accumulating airflow as branches merge. A typical residential system might have 8-12 branch ducts feeding from 2-3 main trunks.

Step 2: Determine Airflow per Section
Calculate CFM or m³/h for each room based on heating/cooling load. Sum airflows for each duct section. Main trunk near air handler carries total system airflow. Branch ducts carry airflow for served rooms. Final run to each diffuser carries only that diffuser's airflow. Document airflow for every section on your layout diagram.

Step 3: Select Design Pressure Drop
Choose target friction rate: 0.8-1.2 Pa/m for residential low-pressure systems, 1.2-2.0 Pa/m for commercial systems, 2.0-4.0 Pa/m for high-velocity industrial. Lower pressure drop means larger ducts but quieter operation and lower fan energy. Residential systems typically target 1.0 Pa/m for main trunks, 0.8 Pa/m for branches.

Step 4: Calculate Duct Sizes
Enter airflow and target pressure drop into the calculator for each section. The calculator returns required diameter, air velocity, and equivalent rectangular sizes. For 400 m³/h at 1.0 Pa/m: diameter 225mm, velocity 3.5 m/s. Record sizes on your layout. Check that velocities stay within acceptable ranges (main 4-6 m/s, branches 3-4 m/s, diffuser connections 2-3 m/s).

Step 5: Account for Fittings
Add equivalent length for each fitting in the system. 90° elbow = 10-15 diameters, 45° elbow = 5-8 diameters, tee = 15-20 diameters, damper = 5-10 diameters. A 20m duct run with 4 elbows and 2 tees might have 45m equivalent length. The calculator shows pressure drop including fittings—use this for fan selection.

Step 6: Verify System Balance
Check that pressure drop is similar for all branch paths. If one branch has much lower resistance, air will short-circuit to that branch, starving others. Add balancing dampers to higher-flow branches. Calculate pressure drop for each path from air handler to diffuser. Paths should be within 10-15% of each other for natural balance.

6 Real-World Examples with Specific Calculations

Example 1: Residential Main Trunk
Single-family home: 200 m², 3-ton AC (600 m³/h total). Main trunk carries full 600 m³/h at 1.0 Pa/m design. Calculator: diameter 280mm, velocity 3.4 m/s, pressure drop 0.95 Pa/m. Rectangular alternative: 400mm × 200mm (fits in joist space). Trunk length 12m with 3 takeoffs: equivalent length 12 + (3 × 5) = 27m. Pressure drop: 27 × 0.95 = 25.7 Pa.

Example 2: Bedroom Branch Duct
Master bedroom requires 85 m³/h (14% of total 600 m³/h). Branch duct at 0.8 Pa/m: diameter 125mm, velocity 3.0 m/s. Flexible duct 8m run with 2 elbows: equivalent length 8 + (2 × 10 × 0.125) = 10.5m. Pressure drop: 10.5 × 0.8 = 8.4 Pa. Register box adds 5 Pa. Total branch pressure: 13.4 Pa from trunk to room.

Example 3: Commercial Office VAV Branch
VAV box serves 150 m² office zone, max airflow 720 m³/h. Supply duct from AHU: 30m run, 6 elbows, 2 dampers. At 1.5 Pa/m: diameter 315mm, velocity 4.1 m/s. Equivalent length: 30 + (6 × 4) + (2 × 6) = 66m. Duct pressure: 66 × 1.5 = 99 Pa. VAV box pressure drop: 50 Pa at design flow. Diffuser: 8 Pa. Total: 157 Pa—within fan capability.

Example 4: Restaurant Kitchen Exhaust
Kitchen hood 4m × 1.5m, capture velocity 0.5 m/s. Airflow: 4 × 1.5 × 0.5 × 3,600 = 10,800 m³/h. Exhaust duct: galvanized steel, minimum 8 m/s velocity for grease transport. At 10 m/s: area = 10,800 / 36,000 = 0.3 m². Diameter: 620mm. Use 650mm standard size. Velocity: 9.0 m/s. Pressure drop: 3.2 Pa/m. Vertical run 8m with cap and 2 elbows: equivalent 8 + 15 + (2 × 12) = 47m. Total: 47 × 3.2 = 150 Pa. Fan selection: 11,000 m³/h at 200 Pa (includes hood filter loss).

Example 5: Data Center Raised Floor Supply
Data center: 400 m², 800 kW IT load, 100% outdoor air economizer. Underfloor plenum 600mm deep supplies 40,000 m³/h to perforated tiles. Plenum velocity target: 5 m/s max to prevent tile blowout. Required plenum area: 40,000 / (5 × 3,600) = 2.22 m². With 20m width: plenum depth needed 2.22 / 20 = 0.11m = 110mm. Actual 600mm depth provides ample capacity. Pressure drop through perforated tiles: 15-25 Pa depending on open area.

Example 6: Hospital Operating Room
OR requires 20 air changes/hour, 100% outdoor air. Room 7m × 6m × 3m = 126 m³. Airflow: 126 × 20 = 2,520 m³/h. Laminar flow ceiling diffuser 3m × 3m. Face velocity: 2,520 / (9 × 3,600) = 0.078 m/s (within 0.05-0.1 m/s laminar range). Supply duct: 2,520 m³/h at 1.2 Pa/m = 450mm diameter. HEPA filter box adds 150 Pa initial pressure drop. Total system: 250 Pa. Redundant AHUs with automatic switchover.

4 Common Duct Sizing Mistakes

Mistake 1: Using Same Size for All Branches
Some installers use 150mm ducts for every branch regardless of airflow. A 30 m³/h bedroom and 120 m³/h living room both get 150mm ducts. The bedroom receives excessive airflow (noisy, uncomfortable) while the living room is starved. Size each branch for its specific airflow. A 30 m³/h branch needs 80mm duct; 120 m³/h needs 150mm. Proper sizing ensures balanced comfort.

Mistake 2: Ignoring Duct Leakage
Unsealed ductwork leaks 15-25% of conditioned air. A system designed for 500 m³/h delivery might only deliver 400 m³/h to rooms. Size ducts for design airflow, then seal all joints with mastic (not duct tape). Test with duct blaster—target less than 5% leakage at 25 Pa. Leaky ducts increase energy bills 20-30% and can depressurize homes, causing backdrafting of combustion appliances.

Mistake 3: Sharp Turns and Poor Fittings
Using 90° square elbows instead of radius elbows or 45° pairs doubles fitting pressure drop. A duct run with 6 square elbows might have 40 Pa fitting loss vs 20 Pa with proper fittings. One contractor saved a noisy system by replacing 8 square elbows with radius elbows—noise dropped significantly, airflow increased 15%. Use proper fittings; the extra cost pays back in performance.

Mistake 4: Not Providing Access for Balancing
Installing ducts without access to dampers makes balancing impossible. One 200-room hotel had dampers buried above ceilings—no way to adjust airflow without destructive access. Install balancing dampers at every branch takeoff with accessible handles. Provide access panels above all dampers and VAV boxes. Document damper positions after balancing for future reference.

5 Pro Tips for Ductwork Design

Tip 1: Use Spiral Duct for Mains
Spiral-wound round duct has smoother interior than rectangular, reducing friction 10-15%. It's also self-sealing (fewer joints) and structurally stronger. For main trunks, spiral duct often costs less than rectangular when installation labor is included. Use rectangular only where space constraints require flat profiles. A commercial building saved €8,000 by switching main trunks from rectangular to spiral.

Tip 2: Design for Future Expansion
If building may expand, oversize main trunks to accommodate future branches. A 20% larger main trunk costs marginally more initially but enables future expansion without replacing the entire system. One warehouse installed 500mm main instead of 400mm—when they added 500 m², the existing main handled increased airflow with only new branches needed.

Tip 3: Minimize Flexible Duct Usage
Flexible duct has 20-30% higher friction than rigid and sags over time. Limit flex to final 1-2m connections at diffusers. Use rigid duct for all runs over 2m. One builder replaced 15m of sagging flexible duct with rigid—airflow increased 25%, noise decreased noticeably. Flexible duct is convenient but should be used sparingly.

Tip 4: Install Dampers Before Drywall
Balancing dampers must be accessible. Install them at branch takeoffs before closing walls. Mark damper locations on as-built drawings. Use volume dampers with locking mechanisms so settings don't drift. One technician spent 8 hours locating and accessing dampers in a building—proper initial installation would have reduced balancing time to 2 hours.

Tip 5: Coordinate with Other Trades
Ductwork competes for ceiling space with plumbing, electrical, fire suppression, and structure. Use BIM coordination or detailed ceiling plans to avoid conflicts. Route largest ducts first (they're least flexible). Maintain 50mm clearance from hot pipes and electrical. One project had to relocate 40m of installed duct when sprinkler mains were routed through the same space—coordination meeting would have prevented this.

4 FAQs About HVAC Duct Sizing

Use the equivalent diameter formula or calculator. A 250mm round duct has 0.049 m² area. Rectangular equivalents: 400mm × 150mm (0.06 m², slightly larger), 350mm × 175mm (0.061 m²). The calculator provides multiple rectangular options. Always verify pressure drop for rectangular ducts—they typically have 10-15% higher friction than equivalent round.

Residential: mains 4-6 m/s, branches 3-4 m/s, final runs 2-3 m/s. Commercial: mains 6-8 m/s, branches 4-5 m/s. Industrial: up to 12 m/s for exhaust. Higher velocities increase noise (each doubling adds 15-18 dB) and pressure drop (quadratically). For quiet residential systems, keep velocities below 5 m/s in occupied spaces.

Always insulate ducts in unconditioned spaces (attics, crawlspaces, garages). Use R-4 (25mm) minimum, R-6 (50mm) in extreme climates. Insulation prevents heat loss/gain and condensation. Ducts inside conditioned space don't need insulation for thermal reasons but may benefit from acoustic insulation to reduce noise transmission.

Signs of proper sizing: all rooms reach setpoint, no excessive noise at registers, balanced airflow (similar temperature from all diffusers), system static pressure within equipment ratings (0.3-0.5" WC residential). Measure airflow with anemometer—compare to design values. If airflow is within 10% of design at each diffuser, ducts are properly sized and balanced.

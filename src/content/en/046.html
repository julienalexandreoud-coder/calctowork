What Is a Light Points per Room Calculator?

A light points per room calculator estimates the number of lighting outlets, power sockets, and switch locations required for residential and commercial spaces based on room type and area. For a 15 m² bedroom, the calculator specifies 2-3 light points (ceiling fixture plus bedside lamps), 6-8 power outlets, and 2-3 switch locations per Spanish REBT ITC-BT 25 regulations.

You may also find the LED Lighting Lumen Calculator, Electrical Cable Cross-Section Calculator, and Electrical Panel Calculator useful.

Correct outlet planning prevents dangerous extension cord use, overloaded circuits, and costly retrofitting at €180-€350 per added outlet after walls are finished. Underspecified electrical installations reduce property value and fail home inspection in 40-50% of cases.

The calculation follows European standard EN 12464-1 for lighting levels and national electrical codes (REBT in Spain, NFC 15-100 in France, NEN 1010 in Netherlands) for minimum outlet counts by room type.

Light Points Formulas With Calculations

Light Points = Room Area ÷ Coverage per Fixture

Example: 18 m² bedroom ÷ 10 m² per fixture = 1.8 → 2 light points minimum

Power Outlets by Room Type (REBT ITC-BT 25):

Bedroom under 12 m²: 3 outlets minimum

Bedroom over 12 m²: 4 outlets minimum (1 per 4 m² beyond 12 m²)

Living room: 1 outlet per 6 m², minimum 6 outlets total

Kitchen: 6 outlets minimum plus 1 per appliance

Switch Locations = Number of Entry Points + 1

Single door room: 1 switch (inside, door handle side)

Two door room: 2 three-way switches (one at each door)

Three+ door room: intermediate switching circuit required

Circuit Allocation per ITC-BT 25:

Circuit 1 (C1): Lighting — maximum 16 points per circuit

Circuit 2 (C2): General outlets — maximum 20 outlets per circuit

Circuit 3 (C3): Kitchen and oven — dedicated 25 A circuit

Circuit 4 (C4): Washing machine, dishwasher, water heater — 20 A

6 Steps To Plan Light Points and Outlets

  1. Identify room type and measure area: Classify as bedroom, living room, kitchen, bathroom, hallway, or utility. Measure length × width. A master bedroom 4.5 m × 4.0 m = 18 m². Note room function: bedroom with workspace needs more outlets than sleeping-only room. High-ceiling rooms (3.5 m+) may need wall-washer fixtures in addition to ceiling lights.
  2. Calculate minimum light points per room function: Bedrooms: 1 central ceiling point plus 2 switched bedside points. Living rooms: 1 central point plus 4-6 perimeter points for lamps. Kitchens: 1 central point plus under-cabinet task lighting (4-8 points). Bathrooms: 1 ceiling point plus mirror lighting (2 points). Hallways: 1 point per 4 m length.
  3. Determine power outlet count and locations: Bedrooms: 1 outlet per wall, minimum 3 total, located 40 cm above floor. Living room: outlets every 2 m along walls at 40 cm height. Kitchen: 8-12 outlets at 120 cm above countertop for appliances. Bathrooms: 1 shaver outlet near mirror (IP44 rated), 1 outlet for washing machine if located in bathroom.

  4. Plan switch locations for accessibility: All switches at 110-120 cm height, 15-20 cm from door frame on latch side (not hinge side). Three-way switching for rooms with 2 exits (bedroom with ensuite). Intermediate switching for 3+ exits. Dimmer switches for living rooms and bedrooms (€25-€45 per dimmer versus €8-€12 for standard switch).
  5. Allocate circuits in distribution board: Lighting circuits use 10 A MCB with 1.5 mm² cable. Power circuits use 16 A MCB with 2.5 mm² cable. Kitchen appliances require dedicated 25 A circuit with 4 mm² cable. Air conditioning requires dedicated circuit sized to unit capacity (typically 20-32 A). Label all circuits clearly in panel directory.
  6. Verify compliance with local electrical code: Spain REBT ITC-BT 25: minimum 92 outlets for 100 m² dwelling. France NFC 15-100: minimum 5 outlets per living room under 20 m². Germany DIN 18015: 1 outlet per 4 m² wall length. UK BS 7671: no minimum counts but requires socket placement to avoid flex hazards. Always exceed minimums by 20-30% for future flexibility.

4 Costly Mistakes To Avoid

  • Placing switches behind open doors: Door swings 90°, blocking switch mounted on wrong side. Correction requires relocating switch box, patching drywall, repainting: €180-€350 per switch. Always mark door swing direction on floor plans before electrical rough-in. Test switch locations by swinging door physically during construction walkthrough. Prevention costs nothing; correction costs 15-20 minutes per switch during rough-in versus 2-3 hours after drywall.
  • Installing outlets too close to corners: Outlets within 30 cm of corner become inaccessible when furniture is placed. Baseboard heaters, built-in cabinets, and wardrobes block outlets. Plan outlet locations after furniture layout is finalized. Relocating outlet 60 cm requires fishing new cable through finished walls: €120-€220 per outlet. Proper planning during design phase prevents 80-90% of outlet accessibility complaints.
  • Omitting outdoor outlets for seasonal lighting: Homeowners install Christmas lights using extension cords through windows (fire hazard, energy loss). Outdoor GFCI outlets (€45-€75 each) at eaves corners enable safe seasonal lighting. Two outlets front/back cost €180-€280 installed. Extension cord fires cause 1,200+ home fires annually in EU. Outdoor outlets also enable landscape lighting, power tools, and EV charging access.
  • Using single-gang boxes where double-gang needed: Single-gang box (€2-€4) holds one switch/outlet. Double-gang (€4-€8) holds two. Installing single-gang beside door leaves no room for future smart home controls, dimmers, or fan switches. Upgrading to double-gang during rough-in costs €2-€4 extra. Retrofitting after drywall: €85-€140 per box (cutting, patching, painting). Always use double-gang at entry points and master bedroom doors.

5 Professional Tips

  • Install conduit sleeves for future cable pulls: Empty 25 mm conduit (€8-€12/m) from distribution board to attic, from TV outlet to network cabinet, from garage to garden. Future cables (fiber optic, Cat7, security system) pull through conduit in 10-15 minutes versus €400-€800 wall chasing. Cost for 20 m conduit during construction: €180-€280. Saves €2,000-€4,000 in future renovation labour.
  • Mark all outlet heights on wall before installation: Use laser level to draw horizontal line at 40 cm (outlets) and 115 cm (switches) around entire room. Ensures consistent heights across all rooms. Variations >5 mm are visually obvious after painting. Laser level rental: €25-€40/day. Professional installers own rotating laser (€180-€350). Consistent heights signal quality workmanship to home inspectors and buyers.
  • Add dedicated circuit for home office equipment: Computers, monitors, printers, and networking gear draw 8-12 A continuously. Shared circuits with kitchen or laundry cause nuisance tripping. Dedicated 16 A circuit with 8-10 outlets in office area prevents disruptions. Surge protection at panel (€85-€140) protects €5,000-€15,000 in electronics. Home office circuit: €280-€420 installed, prevents 100% of equipment damage from shared-circuit overloads.
  • Pre-wire for ceiling fans and motorized curtains: Ceiling fan boxes (€18-€28) support 25-35 kg versus 5-8 kg for light fixtures. Install during construction even if fan not planned. Motorized curtain tracks require 230 V at window header. Pre-wire costs €45-€85 per location during construction. Retrofit: €220-€380 per location (fishing cable, patching, painting). 70% of homeowners add ceiling fans or motorized curtains within 5 years of purchase.
  • Document outlet and switch locations with photos: Before drywall installation, photograph all walls showing stud locations, cable routes, and box positions. Store photos with property documents. Future owners hang pictures, shelves, and mirrors without hitting cables. Photo documentation takes 20-30 minutes, prevents 50-60 electrical strikes during renovations. Professional handover package includes circuit directory, test certificates, and photo archive.

4 FAQs

Minimum 8 circuits per REBT ITC-BT 25: C1 lighting (10 A), C2 general outlets (16 A), C3 kitchen/oven (25 A), C4 laundry/water heater (20 A), C5 bathroom outlets (16 A GFCI), C6 air conditioning (25 A), C7 heating (25 A if electric), C8 backup (16 A). Larger homes (150+ m²) benefit from 12-16 circuits with separate circuits for each bedroom and zone. Additional circuits cost €120-€180 each during construction, provide flexibility for future loads.

Standard outlet height: 40 cm from finished floor to bottom of box. Kitchen countertop outlets: 120 cm above floor (15-20 cm above counter). Bathroom mirror outlets: 110-120 cm. Outdoor outlets: 50-60 cm (above snow line in cold climates). TV outlet: 120-140 cm (behind TV, concealed). Desk outlet: 15-20 cm above desk surface (85-90 cm from floor). Consistency matters more than exact height—maintain same height throughout each room type.

GFCI (Ground Fault Circuit Interrupter) required for: bathrooms, kitchens (within 1.8 m of sink), outdoors, garages, basements, laundry areas, and within 1.8 m of water sources. GFCI outlets cost €25-€40 versus €3-€6 for standard. AFCI (Arc Fault Circuit Interrupter) required for bedrooms and living areas per 2023+ codes. AFCI breakers cost €45-€65 versus €12-€18 for standard MCB. Combined AFCI/GFCI protection prevents 85-90% of electrical fires and shocks.

Most smart switches require neutral wire at switch box. Pre-2010 installations often switch only the live wire, omitting neutral. Check switch box: 2 wires = live only (no neutral), 3+ wires = likely has neutral. Neutral-lacking installations require smart switches with bypass capacitor (€35-€55) or rewiring neutral (€180-€350 per switch). New construction: always run neutral to all switch boxes, adding €2-€4 per box for future smart home compatibility.

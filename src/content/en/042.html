What Is a Trap and Drain Calculator?

A trap and drain calculator sizes sanitary traps, waste stacks, and collector pipes for bathroom, kitchen, and laundry drainage systems. For a standard bathroom with 5 fixtures (WC, washbasin, shower, bidet, floor drain), the calculator determines stack diameter (typically 110 mm), collector gradient (2-3%), and individual trap depths (50-75 mm water seal).

Correctly sized drainage prevents sewer gas entry, eliminates slow drainage, and avoids costly rework costing €1,200-€2,500 per bathroom. Undersized stacks cause gurgling, trap siphoning, and violate EN 12056-2 European drainage standards.

The calculation uses fixture unit values, stack length, and pipe gradient to determine diameters from 40 mm (washbasin) to 110 mm (soil stack) with proper ventilation requirements.

Trap and Drain Formulas With Calculations

Total Fixture Units (FU) = Σ Individual Fixture Units

Example: WC (6 FU) + Shower (2 FU) + Washbasin (1 FU) + Bidet (2 FU) + Floor Drain (1 FU) = 12 FU

Stack Diameter Based on FU Load:

75 mm stack: maximum 8 FU (single bathroom)

110 mm stack: maximum 24 FU (2-3 bathrooms)

160 mm stack: maximum 120 FU (apartment building riser)

Collector Pipe Gradient (%) = Drop (cm) ÷ Length (m) × 100

Minimum 2% gradient: 2 cm drop per 1 m length

For 6 m collector run: 6 m × 0.02 = 12 cm total drop required

Trap Seal Depth Requirements:

Standard fixtures: 50 mm minimum water seal

Floor drains: 75 mm minimum (evaporation compensation)

Grease traps: 100 mm minimum seal depth

6 Steps To Size Trap and Drain Systems

  1. List all fixtures with FU values per EN 12056-2: WC flush tank (6 FU), shower (2 FU), washbasin (1 FU), bidet (2 FU), bathtub (3 FU), kitchen sink (2 FU), washing machine (2 FU), floor drain (1 FU). Sum total FU for each branch and main stack.
  2. Determine stack diameter from FU table: Up to 8 FU uses 75 mm diameter (€12-€18/m). 9-24 FU requires 110 mm (€18-€28/m). 25-120 FU needs 160 mm (€35-€50/m). For 5-fixture bathroom totaling 12 FU, select 110 mm soil stack.
  3. Calculate collector pipe slopes: 40 mm pipes need 3% gradient (3 cm/m). 50 mm pipes need 2.5% gradient. 110 mm pipes need minimum 2% gradient. For 8 m horizontal run at 2%: 8 × 0.02 = 16 cm elevation drop from fixture to main.
  4. Size individual trap arms: Washbasin 32-40 mm diameter, maximum 1.5 m length before vent. Shower 50 mm diameter, maximum 2.0 m length. WC 110 mm integral trap in fixture. Each trap arm requires vent within specified distance to prevent siphoning.
  5. Specify trap types and seal depths: Bottle traps P-traps for washbasins (50 mm seal, €15-€35). Shower gully traps (75 mm seal, €25-€55). WC S-traps built-in. Floor drain deep-seal traps (75 mm, €30-€60). All traps accessible for cleaning.
  6. Design vent stack configuration: Main vent stack extends 50 mm diameter through roof, terminating 600 mm above roofline. Individual fixture vents connect within 1.5 m of trap. Wet venting allowed for bathroom groups within 3 m of soil stack per EN 12056-2.

4 Costly Mistakes To Avoid

  • Installing horizontal offsets in soil stacks without cleanouts: EN 12056-2 requires cleanouts at every 90° change of direction and every 15 m on straight runs. Omitting cleanouts makes blockage clearance impossible without cutting walls. Each missing cleanout adds €180-€350 emergency repair cost per occurrence.
  • Using 90° elbows on horizontal drain lines: Sharp 90° elbows create turbulence and trap debris. Use two 45° elbows or long-radius 90° fittings instead. Standard 90° elbows increase blockage frequency by 400-500% in kitchen drains. Long-radius fittings cost €3-€5 more but prevent €400-€800 drain cleaning calls.
  • Connecting WC discharge downstream of shower traps: WC flush creates pressure wave that siphons shower trap seals if connected incorrectly. WC must connect directly to stack or upstream of all other fixtures. Incorrect sequencing causes sewer gas entry in 60-70% of installations within 6 months.
  • Undersizing vent stacks below 50 mm diameter: Some contractors use 32 mm or 40 mm vents to save €80-€120. Undersized vents cannot equalize pressure during fixture discharge, causing trap siphoning. Minimum 50 mm vent required for stacks serving 3+ bathrooms. Replacement cost after wall closure: €600-€1,200 per bathroom.

5 Professional Tips

  • Install test tees at stack base before backfilling: Test tee with removable cap (€25-€40) allows pressure testing and future camera inspection. Insert drain camera through test tee to locate blockages without excavation. Saves €300-€600 per diagnostic call versus digging trial holes.
  • Use air admittance valves for island sinks: Kitchen island sinks cannot connect to conventional vent stacks. AAA valves (€35-€65) open under negative pressure, close under positive pressure. Install 150 mm above flood rim, accessible for replacement every 8-10 years.
  • Slope collector pipes at 3% instead of minimum 2%: Extra 1% gradient increases self-cleansing velocity from 0.6 m/s to 0.9 m/s. Prevents solids deposition in long runs. For 10 m collector, additional 10 cm drop required but eliminates 80-90% of blockage complaints.
  • Wrap underground pipes with geotextile before backfill: Geotextile wrap (€2-€4/m) prevents soil infiltration at joints during settlement. Root intrusion occurs at 70% of unwrapped joints within 5 years. Wrap costs €40-€80 for typical residential run but prevents €1,500-€3,000 root removal or replacement.
  • Pressure test at 1.5× operating head before concealment: Fill system to 3 m head (0.3 bar) and hold 15 minutes. Acceptable drop: less than 0.05 bar. Testing identifies leaking joints before tiles and walls conceal defects. Repair cost during construction: €50-€150. After completion: €800-€2,500.

4 FAQs

EN 12056-2 specifies 50 mm minimum water seal for standard fixtures (washbasins, showers, sinks). Floor drains require 75 mm seal to compensate for evaporation during dry periods. Seals below 25 mm lose effectiveness within 2-3 weeks. Deep-seal traps cost €8-€15 more but prevent sewer gas complaints.

Maximum trap-to-vent distance depends on pipe diameter: 32 mm pipe allows 1.0 m, 40 mm allows 1.5 m, 50 mm allows 2.0 m, 110 mm allows 4.0 m. Exceeding distances causes trap siphoning during discharge. If distance unavoidable, install individual AA valve within specified range.

Never reduce diameter in horizontal drainage flow direction. Increasing diameter is acceptable (e.g., 75 mm to 110 mm) using eccentric reducers to maintain pipe crown. Reducing diameter creates turbulence and traps solids, guaranteeing blockages within 3-6 months of occupancy.

Residential kitchens under 50 L/day grease discharge do not require grease traps per EU regulations. Commercial kitchens, restaurants, and canteens must install grease interceptors sized for flow rate. Typical 100 L/min grease trap costs €850-€1,400 including installation and maintenance access.

What is Potential Energy?

Gravitational potential energy is the energy stored in an object due to its position in a gravitational field. When you lift something against gravity, you do work on it, and that work becomes stored energy waiting to be released. The formula PE = mgh quantifies this relationship, where mass, gravitational acceleration, and height determine the stored energy.

Consider a 2,000 kg elevator cab raised 15 meters above the ground floor. Its potential energy is PE = 2,000 kg × 9.81 m/s² × 15 m = 294,300 joules or 294.3 kJ. If the cable snapped (a scenario prevented by multiple safety systems), this energy would convert to kinetic energy during the fall. The elevator would hit the ground at about 17 m/s (61 km/h) — a devastating impact that safety brakes must prevent. This same principle powers hydroelectric dams, where elevated water's potential energy becomes electricity.

How it Works: Formulas Explained

The potential energy formula PE = mgh breaks down into three components. Mass (m) in kilograms represents how much matter is being lifted. Gravitational acceleration (g) equals 9.81 m/s² on Earth's surface — this varies slightly by location but 9.81 is the standard value. Height (h) in meters measures the vertical distance above a reference point, typically ground level.

Let's work through a complete example. A construction crane lifts a 3,500 kg steel beam to the 20th floor, 65 meters above ground. PE = 3,500 kg × 9.81 m/s² × 65 m = 2,232,225 J or 2.23 MJ. The crane's engine must supply at least this much energy, plus additional energy to overcome friction and inefficiencies. If the crane's motor is 85% efficient, it actually consumes 2.23 MJ ÷ 0.85 = 2.62 MJ of electrical or chemical energy to complete the lift.

The calculator displays results in both joules and kilojoules for convenience. A 70 kg hiker climbing 500 meters of elevation gains PE = 70 × 9.81 × 500 = 343,350 J or 343.4 kJ. This represents the minimum energy the hiker's body must expend — actual caloric burn is 3-4 times higher due to muscle inefficiency. Food energy (calories) converts at 1 Cal = 4,184 J, so this climb burns roughly 82 food Calories just for the elevation gain.

Step-by-Step Guide

  1. Identify the object's mass — Measure mass in kilograms. A standard dumbbell might be 15 kg. For objects weighed in pounds, divide by 2.205. A 50 lb weight equals 22.7 kg.
  2. Use standard gravity — Enter 9.81 m/s² for Earth's surface gravity. On the Moon, use 1.62 m/s². On Mars, use 3.71 m/s². The calculator defaults to Earth's gravity.
  3. Measure vertical height — Determine the height difference in meters. A two-story house is about 6 meters tall. For feet, multiply by 0.305. Twenty feet equals 6.1 meters.
  4. Choose a reference point — Potential energy is relative. Ground level is common, but you might use floor level, sea level, or the lowest point in your system. Consistency matters more than the specific reference.
  5. Multiply all three values — Calculate PE = m × g × h. For a 25 kg object at 12 meters: PE = 25 × 9.81 × 12 = 2,943 J. The calculator performs this automatically.
  6. Consider energy conversion — If the object falls, potential energy becomes kinetic energy. A 25 kg object dropped from 12 meters hits the ground at v = √(2gh) = √(235.4) = 15.3 m/s or 55 km/h.

Real-World Examples

Example 1: Hydroelectric Dam Power Generation
The Hoover Dam holds back Lake Mead, with water 180 meters above the turbines. One cubic meter of water has mass 1,000 kg. PE = 1,000 × 9.81 × 180 = 1,765,800 J per cubic meter. The dam processes about 1,200 cubic meters per second through its turbines, giving 2.12 GJ of energy every second — that's 2.12 gigawatts of power potential. Actual output is about 2 GW after efficiency losses, powering millions of homes.

Example 2: Roller Coaster Design
A roller coaster train with mass 4,500 kg climbs a 75-meter lift hill. PE at the top = 4,500 × 9.81 × 75 = 3,311,625 J or 3.31 MJ. At the bottom of the first drop, nearly all this energy becomes kinetic energy, giving velocity v = √(2 × 9.81 × 75) = 38.4 m/s or 138 km/h (86 mph). Designers use this calculation to ensure the train has enough energy to complete the entire course.

Example 3: Pile Driver Foundation Work
A pile driver uses a 2,000 kg hammer dropped from 8 meters to drive steel beams into the ground. PE = 2,000 × 9.81 × 8 = 156,960 J per drop. This energy transfers to the pile on impact, forcing it deeper. Repeated drops (often 50-100 per pile) drive the beam to the required depth. The loud bang you hear is energy converting to sound and heat.

Example 4: Clock Weight Mechanism
A grandfather clock uses a 5 kg weight that descends 1.2 meters over 7 days. PE = 5 × 9.81 × 1.2 = 58.86 J stored in the wound position. This tiny amount of energy, released slowly through gears, powers the clock for a week. The energy rate is 58.86 J ÷ 604,800 seconds = 0.000097 watts — remarkably efficient mechanical engineering.

Example 5: Rock Climbing Fall Energy
A 75 kg climber falls 4 meters before the rope catches them. PE lost = 75 × 9.81 × 4 = 2,943 J. The climbing rope stretches to absorb this energy gradually, limiting the force on the climber's body. A dynamic rope might stretch 30%, extending the stopping distance to 1.2 meters and reducing peak force to about 2,500 N — survivable with a proper harness.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Using horizontal distance instead of vertical height: Potential energy depends only on vertical displacement. Pushing a 100 kg cart 50 meters horizontally adds no potential energy. Lifting it 2 meters vertically adds 1,962 J. When calculating PE for a ramp or incline, use the vertical rise, not the ramp length. A 10-meter ramp at 15° has vertical height of only 2.59 meters.

Forgetting to convert units: Entering height in centimeters or feet without converting produces wrong answers. A 500 cm height should be entered as 5 meters. Similarly, mass in grams must be divided by 1,000. A 500 g ball at 2 meters has PE = 0.5 × 9.81 × 2 = 9.81 J, not 9,810 J.

Confusing the reference point: Potential energy is always relative to a chosen zero point. A book on a table has PE relative to the floor, but zero PE relative to the table. When solving problems, clearly define your reference level and stick with it throughout. Energy differences matter, not absolute values.

Neglecting that g varies slightly: For most calculations, 9.81 m/s² is fine. But precise work requires local gravity values. At sea level at the equator, g = 9.78 m/s². At the poles, g = 9.83 m/s². At 3,000 meters altitude, g drops to about 9.77 m/s². These variations matter for calibration work and precision measurements.

Pro Tips

Use energy conservation for motion problems: When an object falls without air resistance, PE converts entirely to KE. Set mgh = ½mv² and solve for velocity: v = √(2gh). A rock dropped from 100 meters hits at v = √(2 × 9.81 × 100) = 44.3 m/s or 159 km/h. This approach avoids calculating time and acceleration separately.

Calculate pumped storage efficiency: Hydroelectric plants pump water uphill at night using cheap electricity, then generate power during peak hours. If pumping 1,000,000 kg up 300 meters requires PE = 2.94 GJ, and the plant recovers 2.35 GJ during generation, efficiency is 2.35/2.94 = 80%. This is typical for pumped storage facilities.

Estimate human work capacity: A fit person can sustain about 100 watts of mechanical power. Lifting 20 kg up 3 meters requires PE = 20 × 9.81 × 3 = 588.6 J. At 100 W, this takes 5.9 seconds. Repeating this 100 times (lifting 2 tonnes total height 300 m) takes about 10 minutes of continuous work — a useful benchmark for manual labor planning.

Understand elastic vs. gravitational potential energy: Springs store elastic PE = ½kx², while elevated objects store gravitational PE = mgh. Both can convert to kinetic energy. A drawn bow (elastic) and a raised hammer (gravitational) both store energy for later release. The formulas differ but the concept of stored energy is identical.

Apply to orbital mechanics: For satellites, use PE = -GMm/r where G is the gravitational constant, M is Earth's mass, and r is distance from Earth's center. The negative sign indicates bound orbits. A 1,000 kg satellite at 400 km altitude has PE ≈ -59 GJ relative to infinity. This advanced formula reduces to mgh near Earth's surface.

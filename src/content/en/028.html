What is the Small Drainage Chamber (Arqueta) Calculator?

The drainage chamber calculator determines the material volumes required to construct a small rectangular drainage chamber (arqueta) used at junction points in surface water and foul sewer drainage systems. These chambers collect and redirect flow from multiple inlet pipes to a single outlet, providing access for inspection and maintenance. The calculator accounts for wall volumes, base slab, and helps estimate brick, block, or concrete quantities.

You may also find the Wall Tile Calculator, Ceramic Floor Tile Calculator, and Tile Adhesive Calculator useful.

In Spanish drainage systems, small chambers (arquetas de saneamiento, arquetas de pluviales) follow CTE DB-HS 5 (Evacuación de aguas residuales) and UNE-EN 752 for drainage systems. The calculator helps contractors working on residential drainage (saneamiento residencial), urban surface water systems (redes de pluviales), and private wastewater installations determine brick, concrete block, or precast quantities for chambers typically 400-900 mm internal dimensions.

Formula Explained

V_paredes = 2 × (L + A) × P × e   |   V_solera = L_ext × A_ext × e_s

Where L and A are the internal length and width of the chamber in metres (m), P is the depth in metres (m), e is the wall thickness in metres (m), e_s is the base slab thickness in metres (m), and L_ext = L + 2e, A_ext = A + 2e are the external dimensions.

The wall volume calculation sums all four walls. For brick chambers, divide the wall volume by the volume of a single brick including mortar joints to estimate brick count. Standard Spanish ladrillo macizo (240×115×52 mm) with 10 mm joints yields approximately 60 bricks per m² of single-skin wall.

Step-by-Step Calculation Process

  1. Determine internal chamber dimensions: Based on pipe diameters and access requirements. For pipes ≤150 mm, use 450×450 mm minimum. For pipes 150-250 mm, use 600×600 mm. For pipes 250-400 mm, use 800×600 mm or 900×600 mm.
  2. Specify wall thickness: Brick walls: 225 mm (one brick length) for depths ≤1.50 m, 340 mm (brick and a half) for deeper chambers. Concrete block walls: 150 mm for ≤1.50 m, 200 mm for deeper. In-situ concrete: 150 mm typical.
  3. Calculate external dimensions: Add twice wall thickness to internal length and width. For 600×600 mm internal with 225 mm walls: L_ext = 0.60 + 2×0.225 = 1.05 m, A_ext = 1.05 m.
  4. Calculate wall volume: Multiply 2 × (internal length + internal width) × depth × wall thickness. This calculates total volume of four walls.
  5. Calculate base slab volume: Multiply external length × external width × base thickness. Base slabs typically 100-150 mm thick depending on chamber depth and soil conditions.
  6. Convert to material quantities: For brickwork, divide wall volume by brick volume (including mortar) or multiply wall area by bricks per m². For blocks, calculate blocks per course and number of courses.

Example 1: Standard brick drainage chamber 600 × 600 mm

A small drainage chamber has internal dimensions of 600 mm × 600 mm, depth of 1.00 m, brick walls 225 mm thick (one brick length), and a 100 mm thick concrete base slab.

Calculation: L_ext = 0.60 + 2 × 0.225 = 1.05 m. A_ext = 0.60 + 2 × 0.225 = 1.05 m. Wall volume = 2 × (0.60 + 0.60) × 1.00 × 0.225 = 2 × 1.20 × 1.00 × 0.225 = 0.54 m³. Base slab = 1.05 × 1.05 × 0.10 = 0.11 m³. Total = 0.54 + 0.11 = 0.65 m³.

The chamber requires 0.54 m³ of brickwork and 0.11 m³ of concrete for the base. Wall area = 2 × (0.60 + 0.60) × 1.00 = 2.40 m². At 60 bricks/m² (ladrillo macizo 240×115×52 mm with 10 mm joints), bricks needed = 2.40 × 60 = 144 bricks. At €0.45/brick, brick cost is €64.80. Mortar (1:4 cement:sand) for brickwork: 0.08 m³ at €85/m³ (€6.80). Base concrete: €10.50. Total materials: €82.10.

Common Mistakes

  • Not deducting pipe openings from brick count: Pipe penetrations (typically 3-4 per chamber) displace 5-10 bricks each. For a chamber with four DN150 pipes, this saves 20-30 bricks (€9-13.50). While not critical, accurate estimates improve bid competitiveness.
  • Using internal dimensions for base slab: Base slabs extend to external wall faces. Using 600×600 mm instead of 1050×1050 mm for a chamber with 225 mm walls underestimates base volume by 75% (0.036 m³ vs. 0.11 m³). Always use external dimensions for base calculations.
  • Forgetting internal render for waterproofing: Foul sewer chambers require internal cement render (12-15 mm, two coats) to prevent infiltration/exfiltration. For a 1 m² chamber 1 m deep, this is 4 m² of render (two faces) at €10-15/m² (€40-60). Omitting this violates CTE DB-HS requirements.
  • Ignoring chamber base depth below pipe invert: Chamber floor must extend 150-200 mm below pipe invert for benching formation. A chamber specified as 1.00 m deep from ground to pipe invert actually requires 1.15-1.20 m excavation and wall height. This adds 15-20% to material quantities.

Pro Tips from Specialists

  1. Specify waterproof render for foul sewers: Internal render (revoco impermeable) should be cement:sand 1:3 with integral waterproofer, applied in two coats: 10-12 mm scratch coat keyed into brickwork, then 5-8 mm finishing coat. Total thickness 15-20 mm. This prevents groundwater infiltration (which overloads treatment plants) and sewage exfiltration (which contaminates groundwater).
  2. Form proper benching for self-cleansing: Benching (solera de apoyo) should slope minimum 1:20 (5%) from pipe invert to chamber walls. Shape half-round channel matching pipe diameter to maintain flow velocity. Smooth trowelled finish reduces friction and prevents fatberg formation in foul sewers.
  3. Use precast covers for standard chambers: Precast concrete cover slabs (losas prefabricadas) with 600×600 mm openings cost €60-100 and save 2-3 hours on-site forming time. Specify D400 load class for trafficable areas, B125 for pedestrian zones. Include cast iron manhole cover and frame (tapa y bastidor) at €80-150.
  4. Install step irons during construction: For chambers deeper than 1.00 m, embed galvanised step irons (escalones) during bricklaying. Space at 300 mm vertical intervals, staggered 150 mm horizontally. Retrofitting requires drilling and epoxy anchoring at €15-20 per step vs. €5-8 during construction.
  5. Provide ventilation for foul chambers: Foul sewer chambers require ventilation to prevent hydrogen sulphide and methane accumulation. Use vented manhole covers or install 100 mm PVC vent pipe extending 300 mm above ground. Surface water chambers generally don't require ventilation unless connected to combined sewers.

Arqueta refers to small shallow chambers (typically <1.50 m deep, <900 mm dimensions) used for pipe junctions and access in drainage systems. Pozo de visita (manhole) refers to larger deeper chambers (1.50-5.00 m deep, ≥1000 mm diameter) designed for manned entry and major pipe junctions. Both serve similar functions but differ in size, construction method, and access provisions.

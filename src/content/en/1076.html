Capacitor Energy Calculator: Find Stored Energy in Any Capacitor

The Capacitor Energy calculator computes the electrical energy stored in a capacitor based on its capacitance and the voltage applied across its terminals. This tool is essential for electronics engineers designing power supplies, technicians repairing flash circuits, students learning about energy storage, and anyone working with capacitive energy systems.

Capacitor Energy Formula

E = ½ × C × V²

Where E is the energy stored in joules (J), C is the capacitance in farads (F), and V is the voltage in volts (V). The formula shows that energy scales linearly with capacitance but quadratically with voltage, meaning doubling the voltage quadruples the stored energy. This relationship explains why high-voltage capacitors can be extremely dangerous even with modest capacitance values.

The charge stored on a capacitor is Q = C × V, measured in coulombs. The energy can also be expressed as E = ½ QV = ½ Q²/C, which is useful when you know the charge but not the voltage. Capacitors are used for energy storage in applications ranging from power supply filtering to pulsed laser systems.

Worked Examples

Example 1: Power Supply Filter Capacitor

A power supply uses a 2200 µF electrolytic capacitor rated at 25 V. What energy does it store at full voltage?

Calculation: C = 2200 µF = 2200 × 10⁻⁶ = 0.0022 F. E = ½ × 0.0022 × 25² = 0.5 × 0.0022 × 625 = 0.6875 J

The capacitor stores about 0.69 J of energy. While this may not sound like much, if the capacitor discharges suddenly through a short circuit, it can produce a loud pop and visible spark. This is why power supply capacitors have bleed resistors to safely discharge them when the device is turned off.

Example 2: Camera Flash Capacitor

A camera flash unit uses a capacitor of 330 µF charged to 300 V. When triggered, the energy discharges through the xenon flash tube almost instantaneously.

Calculation: C = 330 × 10⁻⁶ = 3.3 × 10⁻⁴ F. E = ½ × 3.3 × 10⁻⁴ × 300² = 0.5 × 0.00033 × 90,000 = 14.85 J

This 14.85 J of energy released in approximately 1 millisecond creates a peak power of 14.85 kW, which is why camera flashes can illuminate an entire room. This same principle is used in defibrillators, where 200 to 360 J is delivered in a few milliseconds to restart a patient's heart.

Common Uses

  • Sizing filter capacitors in power supply designs to ensure adequate energy storage for ripple reduction
  • Calculating the energy available in flash and strobe circuits for photography and warning beacons
  • Determining the safe discharge time and required bleed resistor values for high-voltage capacitor banks
  • Evaluating capacitor energy in pulse power applications such as defibrillators, railguns, and lasers
  • Selecting capacitors for energy harvesting systems that store small amounts of energy from solar or vibration sources
  • Analyzing the energy efficiency of switched-mode power supplies and DC-DC converter circuits

Common Mistakes

  • Forgetting to convert microfarads (µF) to farads (F) — 1 µF = 10⁻⁶ F; using µF directly in the formula gives an answer that is off by a factor of one million
  • Forgetting the ½ factor in the energy formula — using E = CV² instead of E = ½ CV² would double the calculated energy, potentially leading to unsafe design decisions
  • Assuming the capacitor is fully discharged when power is off — capacitors can hold a dangerous charge for hours or days if no bleed resistor is present
  • Confusing rated voltage with operating voltage — a capacitor rated at 50 V will store less energy at 30 V (E ∝ V²) than at its maximum rating

Pro Tip

When designing circuits with large capacitors, always include a bleed resistor in parallel with the capacitor to ensure safe discharge within a reasonable time. A good rule of thumb is to choose a bleed resistor that discharges the capacitor to below 50 V within 30 seconds. Use the formula t = 5RC to calculate the time to reach 99% discharge. For a 100 µF capacitor charged to 400 V, a 100 kΩ bleed resistor gives a 50-second discharge time constant (5 × 100,000 × 0.0001 = 50 s). The power dissipated in the bleed resistor is V²/R = 160,000 / 100,000 = 1.6 W, so use a 2 W rated resistor.

What Is a Travel and Subsistence Calculator?

A travel and subsistence calculator is an essential financial tool for construction professionals, contractors, and self-employed tradespeople that determines allowable expense claims for work-related travel, accommodation, and meal costs. This calculator ensures compliance with tax authority regulations while maximizing legitimate deductions that reduce taxable income.

Construction work often requires travel beyond normal workplace — remote sites, client locations, temporary assignments away from home base. These expenses are legitimate business costs but subject to specific rules governing deductibility. Understanding and correctly calculating allowable travel and subsistence expenses can reduce tax liability by €1,000-5,000+ annually for typical contractors.

This calculator processes variables including days working away from home, subsistence type (full day rate, partial day, overnight), daily kilometers traveled, and applicable tax authority rates to deliver total allowable deductions. Whether claiming for daily site travel, weekly away assignments, or extended project work, accurate expense calculation is fundamental to construction business tax optimization.

Formulas Explained: Travel and Subsistence Calculations

Travel and subsistence calculations follow tax authority guidelines that vary by jurisdiction. The calculator uses European-standard rates with jurisdiction-specific adjustments.

For Daily Travel (Home to Temporary Site):

Travel Allowance = Business Kilometers × Approved Rate (€/km)

Approved Mileage Rates (typical EU rates):

  • First 10,000-15,000 km/year: €0.45-0.55/km — covers fuel, wear, depreciation, insurance
  • Above threshold km/year: €0.25-0.35/km — reduced rate as fixed costs already covered
  • Motorcycle: €0.35-0.45/km — lower rates than cars/vans
  • Bicycle: €0.20-0.30/km — where applicable for urban work

For Subsistence (Meals and Incidentals):

Daily Subsistence Allowance = Days Away × Daily Rate

Subsistence Rate Categories:

  • Full day rate (10+ hours away from home): €25-35/day — breakfast, lunch, dinner, incidentals
  • Partial day rate (5-10 hours away): €15-22/day — lunch and dinner or extended day
  • Short day rate (under 5 hours away): €0-8/day — typically no allowance unless exceptional
  • Overnight accommodation: Actual cost + €10-15 incidental rate — hotel/B&B receipt required

For Combined Travel and Subsistence:

Total Allowable = (Kilometers × Rate) + (Days × Subsistence Rate) + Accommodation

Key Conditions for Allowability:

  • Temporary workplace: Site attended for limited duration (typically under 24 months)
  • Not regular commute: Travel from home to permanent workplace not allowable
  • Wholly and exclusively: Travel must be for business purposes, not personal
  • Record keeping: Contemporaneous mileage logs, receipts for accommodation
  • Reasonable costs: Expenses must be reasonable for the circumstances

Example Calculation: Contractor working on temporary site 45 km from home, 4 days/week for 8 weeks, full day subsistence:

Weekly kilometers: 45 km × 2 (return) × 4 days = 360 km/week

Weekly travel allowance: 360 km × €0.48/km = €172.80

Weekly subsistence: 4 days × €28/day = €112.00

Weekly total: €284.80

Project total (8 weeks): €284.80 × 8 = €2,278.40 allowable deduction

Tax saving (30% rate): €2,278.40 × 30% = €683.52 reduced tax liability

Step-by-Step Guide

  1. Determine if Workplace Qualifies as Temporary: Tax authorities distinguish between permanent workplace (regular place of work, travel not deductible) and temporary workplace (limited duration, travel deductible). Tests: 1) Duration test — expected to attend less than 24 months (EU standard). 2) Regularity test — not attending regularly over extended period. 3) Purpose test — attending for specific task/project, not ongoing operations. Examples qualifying as temporary: 6-month renovation project, 3-week emergency repairs, rotating site visits (different locations each week), client site for specific installation. Examples NOT qualifying: your regular office/workshop, site where you work indefinitely, multiple sites but same client long-term. If workplace is temporary, travel from home to site is deductible. If permanent, only travel between work locations (site to supplier, site to site) is deductible. Document: project start/end dates, client contract showing temporary nature, variation if extended beyond original period.
  2. Track Kilometers with Contemporaneous Mileage Logs: Tax authorities require contemporaneous (at the time) records, not reconstructed at year-end. Log must include: date, journey start (from), journey end (to), purpose (client name/job reference), odometer start, odometer end, kilometers. Format: paper logbook in vehicle, mobile app (MileIQ, Drivewise, HMRC-approved apps), spreadsheet updated daily. Categorize journeys: business travel (deductible), commuting to permanent workplace (not deductible), personal (not deductible). Example week: Mon 52 km (Home to Site A), Tue 48 km (Home to Site A), Wed 65 km (Home to Site B), Thu 48 km (Home to Site A), Fri 35 km (Home to workshop for materials). Total business: 248 km. Weekly log signed/dated. Monthly summary: total km, business %, calculation. Retain logs 5-7 years (tax audit period). Apps automate tracking (GPS), categorize journeys, generate reports — worth subscription fee for compliance and time savings.
  3. Calculate Subsistence Based on Time Away from Home: Subsistence (meals, incidentals) allowable when working away from normal workplace for extended day or overnight. Time thresholds (typical): Full day rate — depart home before 6am, return after 8pm, away 10+ hours. Partial day rate — away 5-10 hours, or depart before 9am return after 6pm. Overnight — away from home overnight, actual accommodation cost + incidental rate. Calculate daily: record departure time from home, arrival time at site, departure from site, arrival home. Hours away = arrival at site to departure from site (not travel time). Example: Home 6:30am, Site 7:15am, Leave site 5:45pm, Home 6:30pm. Hours on site: 10.5 hours. Qualifies for full day rate €28. Keep simple log: date, site, departure/arrival times, rate claimed. For overnight: keep hotel/B&B receipts, claim actual cost (reasonable standard — not luxury) + incidental rate €10-15/day. Self-catering (holiday let with kitchen): may only claim incidental rate, not full subsistence — rules vary by jurisdiction.
  4. Apply Correct Mileage Rates for Vehicle Type: Use tax authority approved rates — these are "safe harbor" rates automatically accepted without receipt requirements. Current EU typical rates: Cars/vans first 10,000-15,000 km/year: €0.48/km (varies €0.45-0.55 by country). Cars/vans above threshold: €0.30/km (varies €0.25-0.35). Motorcycles: €0.40/km. Bicycles: €0.25/km. Calculate annual kilometers across all business travel. Apply higher rate to first threshold km, lower rate to excess. Example: 18,000 business km/year, threshold 15,000 km. First 15,000 × €0.48 = €7,200. Remaining 3,000 × €0.30 = €900. Total: €8,100 allowable. Alternative: actual costs method — track all vehicle expenses (fuel, maintenance, insurance, depreciation), apply business use percentage. Choose higher of mileage rate or actual costs. Mileage rate simpler, usually higher for efficient vehicles. Actual costs better for: high fuel consumption vehicles, expensive maintenance years, new vehicles (high depreciation). Once method chosen for vehicle, generally must continue — consult accountant before switching.
  5. Document and Retain Supporting Evidence: Audit-proof your claims with proper documentation. Required records: Mileage logs (contemporaneous, as above). Subsistence log (date, location, times, rate claimed). Accommodation receipts (original or digital, showing date, amount, establishment). Project documentation (contract showing temporary nature, start/end dates). Expense summary spreadsheet (monthly totals, categories, calculations). Retention period: 5-7 years from end of tax year (varies by jurisdiction). Digital records: photos of receipts, cloud storage, apps with audit trails. Best practice: weekly review — ensure all journeys logged, receipts captured, calculations accurate. Monthly: reconcile to bank statements (fuel purchases, accommodation payments). Quarterly: review totals, identify anomalies. Annual: prepare summary for tax return, cross-check to logs. Red flags for audit: round numbers (suggests estimation not actual), inconsistent patterns (same km every day suggests not actual), high claims relative to income (subsistence exceeding 40-50% of income triggers review). Keep records organized, retrievable within 48 hours if requested.
  6. Include Allowable Expenses in Tax Return: Self-employed (sole trader): report expenses on annual tax return (Schedule C equivalent in EU jurisdictions). Categories: travel (mileage or actual vehicle costs), subsistence (meals when away), accommodation (overnight stays). Total expenses reduce taxable profit. Example: Income €65,000, Expenses €42,000 (including €8,000 travel/subsistence), Taxable profit €23,000. Tax saving on travel/subsistence: €8,000 × 30% (marginal rate) = €2,400. Limited company: expenses paid by company reduce corporation tax. Reimburse employee (including director) for business expenses — not taxable benefit if wholly business. Pay through payroll: some jurisdictions allow mileage allowance payments (MAPs) up to approved rates tax-free. Excess over approved rates taxable. Deadlines: self-employed tax return typically due 10 months after tax year end (e.g., 31 January following tax year ending 31 December). Quarterly estimates may be required. Penalties for late filing: 5-10% of tax due plus interest. Set calendar reminders, engage accountant if complex.

Real-World Examples

Example 1: Electrician Weekly Site Work — Self-employed electrician, 12-week contract at temporary site 38 km from home. Vehicle: medium van. Pattern: 5 days/week, full days (7am-5pm on site). Mileage: 38 km × 2 × 5 = 380 km/week. Subsistence: 10 hours/day on site, qualifies for full day rate. Rates: €0.48/km mileage, €28/day subsistence. Weekly: Travel 380 × €0.48 = €182.40. Subsistence 5 × €28 = €140.00. Total: €322.40/week. Project (12 weeks): €3,868.80 allowable. Tax saving (28% marginal rate): €1,083.26. Annual business km: 380 × 12 = 4,560 km (plus other work 6,000 km = 10,560 km total). All within first threshold, €0.48/km applies. Documentation: daily mileage log, weekly subsistence summary, contract showing 12-week duration. After 12 weeks, site extended 6 months — now exceeds 24-month test, becomes permanent workplace. Travel from home no longer deductible. Only travel between sites, to suppliers deductible. Must update records accordingly.

Example 2: Plumber Overnight Assignments — Plumber covering regional emergency callouts, occasionally requires overnight stays. Pattern: 2-3 nights/month away, hotels. Annual: 32 nights away, 45 days full-day subsistence (no overnight), 8,500 km business travel. Calculation: Mileage 8,500 × €0.48 = €4,080. Day subsistence 45 × €28 = €1,260. Overnight: 32 nights hotel €85 average = €2,720 actual. Overnight incidental 32 × €12 = €384. Total allowable: €8,444. Tax saving (32% rate): €2,702. Hotel receipts retained (digital photos). Mileage log maintained via app. Subsistence log: date, location, times, rate. Note: hotel costs must be reasonable — €85/night acceptable, €250/night luxury may be challenged. Incidentals cover: laundry, phone calls, tips, small expenses. Keep incidental log if questioned.

Example 3: Construction Consultant Multi-Site Week — Independent construction consultant visiting multiple client sites weekly. Week pattern: Mon Home to Client A (52 km), overnight near Client A. Tue Client A to Client B (78 km), overnight near Client B. Wed Client B to Client C (45 km), home same day (68 km). Thu Home to Client D (95 km), overnight. Fri Client D to home (95 km). Total km: 52+78+45+68+95+95 = 433 km. Overnight: 3 nights × €95 hotel = €285 + 3 × €12 incidental = €36. Subsistence: Mon (10 hours) €28, Tue (11 hours) €28, Wed (8 hours, partial) €18, Thu (9 hours) €22, Fri (7 hours) €18. Total subsistence: €114. Mileage: 433 × €0.48 = €207.84. Weekly total: €207.84 + €114 + €285 + €36 = €642.84. Annual (45 weeks): €28,928 allowable. Consultant income €95,000. Taxable reduced to €66,072. Tax saving (40% marginal + 4% USC): €12,743. High travel role — expenses significant but legitimate. Documentation critical: client visit logs, hotel receipts, mileage app records.

Example 4: Painter Working on New Build Development — Painter contracted to paint 20 houses on new development, 8-month project. Site is 22 km from home workshop. Daily: home to site (22 km), site to workshop for materials (15 km), workshop to home (18 km). Question: which journeys deductible? Analysis: Development is temporary workplace (8 months, under 24 months). Home to site: deductible (temporary workplace). Site to workshop: deductible (business to business). Workshop to home: deductible if workshop is business premises (yes, registered business address). Total daily: 55 km × €0.48 = €26.40/day. 5 days/week × 35 weeks = 175 days. Total: €4,620 allowable. Alternative interpretation: if workshop is permanent workplace, then home-site-home is deductible, but site-workshop-home may be viewed as two commutes. Conservative approach: claim home-site-home (44 km/day), treat workshop trips as within work day. 44 × €0.48 × 175 = €3,696. Lower but audit-safe. Consult accountant for jurisdiction-specific interpretation.

Example 5: Scaffolding Team Regional Work — 3-person scaffolding team, work within 80 km radius from depot. Crew travels together in company van from depot to sites. Individual homes to depot (commute, not deductible). Depot to site (business travel, but company vehicle). Subsistence: when work finishes late or site remote, crew meals provided. Company policy: full day subsistence €25/person when 10+ hours, partial €15 when 6-10 hours. Annual per employee: 180 full days × €25 = €4,500, 50 partial days × €15 = €750. Total subsistence per employee: €5,250. Company expense, deductible for corporation tax. Not taxable on employees (within approved rates). Mileage: employees using personal vehicles for occasional site visits (not regular) — 850 km/year × €0.48 = €408 reimbursed tax-free. Company van fuel: all business, company deducts actual fuel costs. Total team (3 employees): subsistence €15,750, mileage reimbursements €1,224. Company tax saving (25% corp tax): €4,244. Employees receive tax-free reimbursements.

Example 6: Self-Employed vs. Limited Company Comparison — Same contractor, choice of structure. Scenario: €85,000 income, €12,000 travel/subsistence expenses. Self-employed: Income €85,000 - Expenses €58,000 (including €12,000 travel) = Taxable €27,000. Income tax (progressive): €5,400. Self-employment tax (15.3%): €4,131. Total tax: €9,531. After-tax: €17,469. Limited company: Company income €85,000 - Expenses €58,000 = Profit €27,000. Corporation tax (25%): €6,750. Post-tax profit: €20,250. Director salary €35,000 (personal allowance utilized), dividend €20,250. Personal tax on salary: €3,200. Dividend tax (8.75%): €1,772. Total tax: €6,750 + €3,200 + €1,772 = €11,722. After-tax: €18,278. Difference: limited company €809 better annually. Travel/subsistence treatment similar both structures. Decision factors beyond tax: liability protection, IR35/off-payroll rules, admin burden, client preferences. Consult accountant for personal situation.

Common Mistakes

Mistake 1: Claiming Regular Commute as Business Travel — Travel from home to permanent workplace is personal commuting, not deductible. Common errors: claiming home to regular office/workshop, claiming home to site where working indefinitely, claiming home to same client location long-term. Example: contractor works at same client site 3 days/week for 18 months. Claims 45 km × 3 × 48 weeks × €0.48 = €3,110. Audit: site is permanent workplace (exceeds 24-month test, regular pattern). Disallowed: full €3,110. Tax + penalty (50%): €1,555 + €1,555 = €3,110 total cost. Correct: only travel between different work locations deductible. If site is temporary (under 24 months, specific project), home to site is deductible. Document project end date. If extended beyond 24 months, stop claiming from month 25 onwards.

Mistake 2: Reconstructing Mileage Logs at Year-End — Tax authorities require contemporaneous records. Reconstructed logs (created months later from memory) are non-compliant and may be fully disallowed. Example: contractor claims €6,500 mileage. Audit: requests logs. Contractor creates spreadsheet from calendar, estimates distances. Auditor notes: round numbers (50 km every day), no variation, inconsistent with known site locations. Disallowed: 60% of claim (€3,900). Tax + penalty: €1,950 + €1,950 = €3,900. Prevention: use mileage tracking app (GPS automatic), keep paper log in vehicle, update daily. 5 minutes/day prevents €3,900 loss. Digital apps: timestamped, GPS-verified, audit-proof. Worth €10-15/month subscription.

Mistake 3: Claiming Subsistence Without Meeting Time Thresholds — Subsistence rates have minimum time requirements. Claiming full day rate for 6-hour day is non-compliant. Example: contractor claims 200 days × €28 = €5,600 full day rate. Audit: timesheets show average day 7 hours (9am-4pm). Threshold for full rate is 10 hours. Recalculated: 200 days × €18 (partial rate) = €3,600. Over-claimed: €2,000. Tax + penalty: €800 + €800 = €1,600. Correct: track actual times. Depart 6am, return 7pm = 11 hours on site = full rate. Depart 9am, return 5pm = 7 hours = partial rate or no claim depending on jurisdiction. Keep simple time log: date, site, departure home, arrival home, hours away, rate claimed.

Mistake 4: Mixing Personal and Business Travel in Claims — Claiming 100% vehicle expenses when vehicle used personally. Example: van used 70% business, 30% personal (family trips, shopping, holidays). Contractor claims 100% of €5,200 fuel = €5,200 deduction. Allowable: 70% = €3,640. Over-claimed: €1,560. Tax + penalty: €624 + €624 = €1,248. Correct: maintain mileage log categorizing each journey. Annual: business km ÷ total km = business %. Apply to all vehicle expenses (fuel, maintenance, insurance, depreciation). Or use approved mileage rate for business km only (simpler, no personal allocation needed). Self-employed: honest apportionment. Limited company: fuel benefit charges apply for personal use — director pays tax on benefit, company pays NIC.

Pro Tips

Tip 1: Use Approved Mileage Rates Rather Than Actual Costs — Approved mileage rates (AMR) are tax authority "safe harbor" rates — automatically accepted without receipt requirements, simpler administration, often higher than actual costs. Comparison: Actual costs method — fuel €3,200 + maintenance €1,400 + insurance €650 + tax €180 + depreciation €4,500 = €9,930. Business use 75% = €7,448. Requires: all receipts, depreciation calculation, business/personal split. AMR method — 15,000 km × €0.48 = €7,200. Requires: mileage log only. Difference: €248 less, but saves 4-6 hours/year admin, no receipt storage, audit-proof. Recommendation: use AMR unless actual costs significantly higher (20%+). AMR particularly advantageous for: fuel-efficient vehicles (actual fuel low), older vehicles (low depreciation), low maintenance years. Actual costs better for: new vehicles (high depreciation), high fuel consumption, expensive maintenance years. Calculate both year 1, choose higher, continue same method.

Tip 2: Maximize Subsistence Through Proper Time Recording — Small time adjustments can qualify for higher subsistence rates. Thresholds typically: 5 hours for any subsistence, 10 hours for full day rate. Example: contractor regularly on site 8:30am-4:30pm (8 hours, no travel time). Subsistence: none (under 5 hours on site) or partial if jurisdiction allows. Adjust: depart home 6:30am, arrive site 7:15am, leave site 5:45pm, arrive home 6:30pm. On-site hours: 7:15am-5:45pm = 10.5 hours. Qualifies for full day rate €28 vs. €0. Difference: €28/day × 200 days = €5,600 additional deduction. Tax saving: €1,680. Legitimate adjustment — work day genuinely starts when you depart home for site, ends when you leave site. Ensure times are accurate (don't falsify), but record actual full day including travel. Keep time log: date, depart home, arrive site, leave site, arrive home, hours away, rate. Consistency important — pattern of exactly 10 hours every day looks suspicious. Record actual times.

Tip 3: Plan Overnight Work to Maximize Allowable Costs — Overnight assignments generate higher allowable expenses (hotel + subsistence vs. subsistence only). Evaluate whether overnight is commercially sensible. Example: job 180 km from home (3.5 hours drive). Option 1: daily travel — 360 km × €0.48 = €172.80/day travel allowance, 14 hours away (subsistence €28), 7 hours driving (unpaid), arrive home late. Option 2: overnight — 180 km × €0.48 = €86.40 travel, hotel €85, subsistence day 1 €28, subsistence day 2 €28, incidental €12. Total: €239.40 vs. €200.80 daily. Difference: €38.60 more for overnight, but: 7 hours driving saved (billable work or rest), safer (no tired driving), more productive on site. Commercial decision: overnight costs more but enables productive work, reduces fatigue. For multi-day trips, overnight clearly better. Document business reason for overnight (distance, early start required, late finish, client requirement).

Tip 4: Keep Digital Records for Easy Audit Retrieval — Digital record-keeping simplifies compliance and audit response. System: 1) Mileage app (MileIQ, Drivewise, HMRC-approved) — GPS tracking, automatic categorization, cloud storage. 2) Receipt scanning app (Expensify, Receipt Bank, Dext) — photo receipts, OCR extraction, categorization, export to accounting. 3) Cloud storage (Google Drive, Dropbox, OneDrive) — organized folders (Year/Month/Category), accessible anywhere, backed up. 4) Spreadsheet summary — monthly totals, annual summary, cross-check to bank statements. Setup: 30 minutes initial, 5 minutes/week maintenance. Audit scenario: tax authority requests records. Digital: download reports, email within hours. Paper: box of receipts, logbooks, days/weeks to organize. Digital also enables: automatic backups (no fire/flood loss), search (find specific receipt), sharing (accountant access), integration (accounting software). Cost: €15-30/month apps. Benefit: compliance, time savings, audit confidence.

Tip 5: Review and Optimize Quarterly — Quarterly review catches errors early, identifies optimization opportunities. Review checklist: 1) Mileage log complete? Any missing days/weeks? 2) Subsistence log accurate? Times recorded, rates correct? 3) Receipts captured? All accommodation, any large expenses? 4) Calculations correct? Re-check sample months. 5) Trends — km/month consistent? Spikes explained? 6) Rate updates — tax authority rate changes (usually April)? 7) Project status — any temporary sites becoming permanent? 8) Total vs. income — expenses reasonable percentage (travel/subsistence 10-25% typical for mobile trades)? Example quarterly review: Q1 travel €2,850, subsistence €1,420. Q2 travel €3,100, subsistence €1,580. Increase Q2: new site farther from home (legitimate). Annual projection: €17,800 travel/subsistence on €72,000 income = 24.7%. Within normal range. If 40%+, may trigger audit — review if all claims legitimate. Quarterly: 30 minutes, prevents year-end surprises, spreads admin burden.

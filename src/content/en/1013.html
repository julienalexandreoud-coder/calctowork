What Is a Half-Life Calculator?

A half-life calculator determines the time required for a quantity to decrease to half its initial value during exponential decay processes. Carbon-14 has a half-life of exactly 5,730 years — meaning a 1.0 g sample of pure ¹⁴C decays to 0.5 g after 5,730 years, 0.25 g after 11,460 years, and 0.125 g after 17,190 years. This predictable decay pattern enables archaeologists to date organic artifacts up to 50,000 years old by measuring remaining ¹⁴C content.

You may also find the Voltage Divider Calculator and Inductor Energy Calculator useful.

Half-life calculations extend far beyond radioactive decay. Pharmacologists use half-life to determine drug dosing schedules — ibuprofen has a 2-hour half-life in humans, requiring doses every 4-6 hours to maintain therapeutic levels. Environmental scientists track pollutant degradation — DDT persists in soil with a 15-year half-life, meaning fields treated in 1970 still contain 25% of original contamination today. Understanding half-life enables engineers to design nuclear waste storage facilities (plutonium-239: 24,100-year half-life requires geological-scale containment) and medical physicists to calculate radiation therapy doses (iodine-131: 8-day half-life delivers therapeutic radiation to thyroid tumors while minimizing long-term exposure).

Half-Life Formula With Calculations

For first-order decay processes, the relationship between half-life (t₁/₂), decay constant (λ), and remaining quantity is:

N(t) = N₀ × (1/2)^(t/t₁/₂) = N₀ × e^(-λt)

Where N(t) is quantity at time t, N₀ is initial quantity, and λ = ln(2)/t₁/₂ = 0.693/t₁/₂. Solving for half-life:

t₁/₂ = ln(2) / λ = 0.693 / λ

Time to reach any fraction of initial quantity:

t = t₁/₂ × log₂(N₀/N) = t₁/₂ × ln(N₀/N) / ln(2)

Practical example: A patient receives 100 MBq of technetium-99m (⁹⁹ᵐTc) for a bone scan. Half-life: 6.01 hours. After 24 hours: number of half-lives = 24 / 6.01 = 4.0. Remaining activity = 100 × (1/2)^4 = 100 × 0.0625 = 6.25 MBq. Using exponential form: λ = 0.693 / 6.01 = 0.1153 hour⁻¹. N(24) = 100 × e^(-0.1153 × 24) = 100 × e^(-2.767) = 100 × 0.0629 = 6.29 MBq (slight difference from rounding). After 5 half-lives (30 hours), only 3.125 MBq remains — below diagnostic threshold, which is why ⁹⁹ᵐTc imaging must occur within 6-12 hours of administration.

For radioactive decay, activity (decays per second) follows the same exponential law: A(t) = A₀ × e^(-λt). Activity units: 1 Becquerel (Bq) = 1 decay/second; 1 Curie (Ci) = 3.7 × 10¹⁰ Bq (activity of 1 g radium-226). A 1 Ci source of cobalt-60 (t₁/₂ = 5.27 years) decays to 0.5 Ci after 5.27 years, 0.25 Ci after 10.54 years. After 50 years: (1/2)^(50/5.27) = (1/2)^9.49 = 0.0014, so 1.4 mCi remains — still hazardous, requiring secure storage for centuries.

6 Steps To Calculate Half-Life Problems

  1. Identify initial and final quantities: Determine N₀ (starting amount) and N (remaining amount or target amount). For radiocarbon dating: a bone sample contains 3.2 × 10⁹ atoms of ¹⁴C per gram of carbon. Living organisms have 1.3 × 10¹⁰ atoms ¹⁴C/g C (modern standard). N₀ = 1.3 × 10¹⁰, N = 3.2 × 10⁹. For drug dosing: patient needs minimum 50 mg/L therapeutic level, initial dose produces 400 mg/L. N₀ = 400 mg/L, N = 50 mg/L (minimum effective concentration). Clearly define what "quantity" means — mass, moles, activity, concentration, or number of atoms.
  2. Look up or determine the half-life: Consult reference tables for known substances. Common half-lives: ¹⁴C = 5,730 years, ²³⁸U = 4.47 billion years, ¹³¹I = 8.02 days, ⁹⁹ᵐTc = 6.01 hours, polonium-214 = 164 microseconds. For pharmaceuticals: ibuprofen = 2 hours, diazepam = 48 hours, caffeine = 5 hours. If half-life is unknown but decay constant λ is given: t₁/₂ = 0.693/λ. If experimental data is available (N₀, N, and t), calculate t₁/₂ = t × ln(2) / ln(N₀/N).
  3. Calculate number of half-lives elapsed: n = t / t₁/₂. For a 100 g sample of cesium-137 (t₁/₂ = 30.1 years) after 90 years: n = 90 / 30.1 = 2.99 half-lives. For carbon-14 dating with 30% of original ¹⁴C remaining: N/N₀ = 0.30, so (1/2)^n = 0.30, n = log₂(1/0.30) = log₂(3.33) = ln(3.33)/ln(2) = 1.20/0.693 = 1.73 half-lives. Age = 1.73 × 5,730 years = 9,930 years. Keep extra significant figures during intermediate calculations to avoid rounding errors.
  4. Apply the decay equation: N = N₀ × (1/2)^n or N = N₀ × e^(-λt). For the cesium-137 example: N = 100 × (1/2)^2.99 = 100 × 0.126 = 12.6 g remaining. Mass decayed = 100 - 12.6 = 87.4 g. For the carbon-14 example: N/N₀ = (1/2)^1.73 = 0.30 (confirms 30% remaining). Alternative calculation: λ = 0.693 / 5730 = 1.21 × 10⁻⁴ year⁻¹. N/N₀ = e^(-1.21×10⁻⁴ × 9930) = e^(-1.20) = 0.301 ✓. Both methods give identical results — use whichever is more convenient for your calculator.
  5. Solve for unknown time: When asked "how long until X remains?", rearrange: t = t₁/₂ × ln(N₀/N) / ln(2). For drug elimination: N₀ = 400 mg/L, N = 50 mg/L, t₁/₂ = 2 hours. t = 2 × ln(400/50) / 0.693 = 2 × ln(8) / 0.693 = 2 × 2.079 / 0.693 = 6.0 hours. After 6 hours (3 half-lives), concentration drops to 400 × (1/2)³ = 400 × 0.125 = 50 mg/L — the patient needs another dose. For radioactive waste: how long until 1 kg of plutonium-239 (t₁/₂ = 24,100 years) decays to 1 g (0.1% remaining)? t = 24,100 × ln(1000) / 0.693 = 24,100 × 6.91 / 0.693 = 240,000 years. This illustrates why nuclear waste requires long-term geological storage.
  6. Account for biological elimination (for pharmaceuticals): Drugs have both physical half-life (radioactive decay, if applicable) and biological half-life (metabolic elimination). Effective half-life combines both: 1/t_eff = 1/t_physical + 1/t_biological. For iodine-131 therapy: t_physical = 8.02 days, t_biological (thyroid) = 180 days. 1/t_eff = 1/8.02 + 1/180 = 0.1247 + 0.00556 = 0.1303. t_eff = 7.67 days. The effective half-life is slightly shorter than physical half-life because the body eliminates iodine through urine and feces. For most drugs (non-radioactive), only biological half-life matters. For diagnostic radiopharmaceuticals, effective half-life determines patient radiation dose and imaging window.

4 Practical Half-Life Examples

Example 1 — Radiocarbon Dating an Archaeological Artifact: A wooden artifact from an ancient tomb contains 6.8 × 10⁸ atoms of ¹⁴C per gram of carbon. Modern carbon (pre-1950, before atmospheric nuclear testing) contains 1.3 × 10¹⁰ atoms ¹⁴C/g. Half-life of ¹⁴C: 5,730 years. Ratio N/N₀ = 6.8×10⁸ / 1.3×10¹⁰ = 0.0523 = 5.23%. Number of half-lives: n = log₂(1/0.0523) = ln(19.1) / ln(2) = 2.95 / 0.693 = 4.26 half-lives. Age = 4.26 × 5,730 = 24,400 years. Uncertainty analysis: counting statistics give ±2% error in N/N₀ ratio, translating to ±400 years uncertainty. Calibration: atmospheric ¹⁴C concentration varied over time due to solar activity and geomagnetic field changes. Tree-ring calibration curves adjust raw radiocarbon ages — 24,400 radiocarbon years corresponds to approximately 28,500 calendar years. The artifact dates to the Last Glacial Maximum, when humans created cave paintings in Lascaux, France.

Example 2 — Nuclear Medicine Dosimetry: A thyroid cancer patient receives 5.55 GBq (150 mCi) of iodine-131. Physical half-life: 8.02 days. Biological half-life in thyroid: 80 days (cancerous tissue retains iodine longer than healthy thyroid). Effective half-life: 1/t_eff = 1/8.02 + 1/80 = 0.1247 + 0.0125 = 0.1372, t_eff = 7.29 days. Activity after 7 days: A = 5.55 × e^(-0.693 × 7 / 7.29) = 5.55 × e^(-0.666) = 5.55 × 0.514 = 2.85 GBq. Total radiation dose delivered (integrated activity): ∫A·dt from 0 to ∞ = A₀ × t_eff / ln(2) = 5.55 × 7.29 / 0.693 = 58.4 GBq·days. Converting to absorbed dose: thyroid mass = 20 g, energy per decay = 192 keV (beta) + 364 keV (gamma, 81% abundance). Absorbed dose ≈ 300 Gy to thyroid tumor — lethal to cancer cells while sparing surrounding tissue (typical therapeutic range: 100-300 Gy). Patient must isolate for 3-5 days until activity drops below regulatory release limits (typically <1.2 GBq remaining).

Example 3 — Caffeine Metabolism and Sleep: A person drinks a large coffee (300 mg caffeine) at 2:00 PM. Caffeine half-life in adults: 5 hours (varies 3-7 hours based on genetics, liver function, pregnancy, and medication use). At 10:00 PM (8 hours later): n = 8/5 = 1.6 half-lives. Remaining caffeine = 300 × (1/2)^1.6 = 300 × 0.330 = 99 mg. This equals the caffeine in one espresso shot — enough to delay sleep onset by 30-60 minutes in sensitive individuals. At 2:00 AM (12 hours later): 300 × (1/2)^2.4 = 300 × 0.189 = 57 mg remains. For a person with slow caffeine metabolism (t₁/₂ = 8 hours due to CYP1A2 gene variant): at 10:00 PM, 300 × (1/2)^(8/8) = 150 mg remains — equivalent to a full cup of coffee. This explains why some people can drink espresso after dinner and sleep fine, while others experience insomnia from morning coffee.

Example 4 — Environmental Persistence of PCBs: Polychlorinated biphenyls (PCBs) contaminated soil at an industrial site in 1975 with initial concentration 500 mg/kg. PCBs degrade via microbial action with half-life of 12 years in temperate soil. In 2025 (50 years later): n = 50/12 = 4.17 half-lives. Remaining concentration = 500 × (1/2)^4.17 = 500 × 0.055 = 27.5 mg/kg. Regulatory cleanup threshold for residential soil: 10 mg/kg. Time to reach threshold: t = 12 × ln(500/10) / 0.693 = 12 × ln(50) / 0.693 = 12 × 3.91 / 0.693 = 67.7 years from 1975 = year 2043. Bioremediation can accelerate degradation — adding specialized bacteria and nutrients reduces half-life to 3 years. With bioremediation starting in 2025: t = 3 × ln(27.5/10) / 0.693 = 3 × 1.01 / 0.693 = 4.4 years. Site reaches cleanup standard by 2029 instead of 2043, enabling earlier redevelopment. Bioremediation cost: €250,000; avoided monitoring and liability costs: €1.2 million over 14 years.

4 Common Half-Life Mistakes

Mistake 1 — Assuming Linear Decay Instead of Exponential: A common error: "If half-life is 10 years, then after 20 years all is gone" or "After 5 years (half of half-life), 75% remains." Both are wrong. Decay is exponential, not linear. Correct: after 5 years (0.5 half-lives), (1/2)^0.5 = 0.707 = 70.7% remains (not 75%). After 20 years (2 half-lives), (1/2)² = 0.25 = 25% remains (not 0%). After 100 years (10 half-lives), (1/2)^10 = 0.00098 = 0.098% remains — never truly zero. This misconception causes problems in nuclear waste planning and drug dosing. A medication with 6-hour half-life doesn't "wear off" after 12 hours; 25% remains in the body, potentially causing cumulative effects with repeated dosing.

Mistake 2 — Confusing Half-Life with Mean Lifetime: Half-life (t₁/₂) and mean lifetime (τ, tau) are related but different. Mean lifetime is the average time an individual atom survives before decaying: τ = 1/λ = t₁/₂ / ln(2) = t₁/₂ / 0.693 = 1.44 × t₁/₂. For carbon-14: t₁/₂ = 5,730 years, but τ = 8,270 years. After one mean lifetime (8,270 years), fraction remaining is e⁻¹ = 0.368 = 36.8%, not 50%. After one half-life (5,730 years), 50% remains. Physics texts often use τ; medical and chemistry texts use t₁/₂. Confusing them introduces 44% error. Formula check: if equation uses e^(-t/τ), use mean lifetime; if equation uses (1/2)^(t/t₁/₂), use half-life. Both describe the same decay, just different mathematical forms.

Mistake 3 — Ignoring Daughter Product Decay: When a radioactive parent decays to a radioactive daughter, both contribute to total activity. Radon-222 (t₁/₂ = 3.82 days) decays to polonium-218 (t₁/₂ = 3.10 minutes), which decays through several short-lived daughters to stable lead-210. A 1 kBq radon source initially has 1 kBq total activity. After 1 hour, radon activity is essentially unchanged (1 hour ≪ 3.82 days), but daughters have grown in. At secular equilibrium (after ~5 daughter half-lives = 15 minutes), each daughter has activity equal to parent. Total activity: 1 kBq (Rn-222) + 1 kBq (Po-218) + 1 kBq (Pb-214) + 1 kBq (Bi-214) + 1 kBq (Po-214) = 5 kBq. Radiation dose is 5× higher than parent alone! Radon exposure risk comes primarily from inhaling daughters (solid particles that lodge in lungs), not radon gas itself. Calculations ignoring daughters underestimate hazard by factor of 5-10.

Mistake 4 — Using Wrong Half-Life for the Chemical Form: Half-life depends on the isotope, not the chemical compound, BUT biological half-life depends strongly on chemical form. Tritium (³H) has physical half-life 12.3 years regardless of form. However: tritiated water (HTO) has biological half-life 10 days (rapidly excreted in urine); organically bound tritium (OBT, incorporated into DNA/proteins) has biological half-life 40 days (turnover of biomolecules). Effective half-life: for HTO, 1/t_eff = 1/12.3 years + 1/10 days ≈ 1/10 days (physical decay negligible); for OBT, 1/t_eff ≈ 1/40 days. A worker inhaling 1 MBq of HTO clears it in ~50 days; the same amount as OBT takes ~200 days. Chemical form also affects which organs receive dose — HTO distributes uniformly; OBT concentrates in rapidly dividing tissues. Always specify both isotope and chemical compound when calculating biological or effective half-life.

4 Professional Tips for Half-Life Calculations

Tip 1 — Use Logarithmic Plotting for Data Analysis: When analyzing experimental decay data, plot ln(N) vs. t on semi-log graph paper or using software. For first-order decay, this produces a straight line with slope = -λ. From slope, calculate t₁/₂ = 0.693/λ. Example: measure activity of unknown isotope at t = 0, 1, 2, 4, 8, 16 hours. Activities: 1000, 800, 640, 410, 170, 28 Bq. Plot ln(activity) vs. time: points fall on line with slope -0.223 hour⁻¹. λ = 0.223 hour⁻¹, t₁/₂ = 0.693/0.223 = 3.11 hours. Compare to known isotopes — likely copper-64 (t₁/₂ = 12.7 hours) or fluorine-18 (t₁/₂ = 110 minutes = 1.83 hours). The 3.11-hour half-life suggests gallium-68 (t₁/₂ = 68 minutes) is too short; check tables for 3-hour isotopes (phosphorus-32: 14.3 days, sulfur-35: 87 days — neither matches). The unknown may be a mixture or an isotope with different half-life. Linear regression on ln(N) vs. t gives best-fit λ with confidence intervals, enabling identification within experimental uncertainty.

Tip 2 — Apply Bateman Equations for Decay Chains: For sequential decay A → B → C → ... → stable, Bateman equations give quantity of each nuclide at time t. For simple chain A → B → C (C stable): N_A(t) = N_A0 × e^(-λA·t). N_B(t) = N_A0 × λA/(λB-λA) × (e^(-λA·t) - e^(-λB·t)) + N_B0 × e^(-λB·t). Example: molybdenum-99 (t₁/₂ = 66 hours) decays to technetium-99m (t₁/₂ = 6 hours), used in medical generators. Start with 100 GBq Mo-99, no Tc-99m initially. After 24 hours: N_Mo = 100 × e^(-0.693×24/66) = 100 × 0.776 = 77.6 GBq. N_Tc = 100 × (0.693/6)/(0.693/66 - 0.693/6) × (e^(-0.693×24/66) - e^(-0.693×24/6)) = 100 × 0.1155/(-0.1045) × (0.776 - 0.0625) = -110.5 × 0.7135 = 57.3 GBq. The generator is "milked" to extract Tc-99m for medical imaging. Maximum Tc-99m occurs at t = ln(λB/λA)/(λB - λA) = 23 hours — hospitals elute generators daily at optimal time.

Tip 3 — Consider Secular and Transient Equilibrium: When parent half-life greatly exceeds daughter half-life (t₁/₂,parent >> t₁/₂,daughter), secular equilibrium develops: daughter activity equals parent activity after ~5 daughter half-lives. Uranium-238 (t₁/₂ = 4.47 billion years) decays through 14 daughters to stable lead-206. In old uranium ore, all 14 daughters have activity equal to U-238 — a 1 g uranium sample (12.4 kBq) actually emits radiation from 15 isotopes at 12.4 kBq each, total 186 kBq. For transient equilibrium (parent half-life moderately longer than daughter, e.g., 10:1 ratio), daughter activity exceeds parent at equilibrium by factor t₁/₂,parent/(t₁/₂,parent - t₁/₂,daughter). Mo-99/Tc-99m generator: ratio = 66/6 = 11:1. At equilibrium, Tc-99m activity = 10/11 × Mo-99 activity = 91% of parent. Understanding equilibria is crucial for radiation safety (daughters may emit more hazardous radiation than parent) and medical isotope production (optimize elution timing).

Tip 4 — Account for Continuous Production: When a radioactive isotope is continuously produced (nuclear reactor, cosmic rays, medical infusion), steady-state activity is reached when production rate equals decay rate. For carbon-14: cosmic rays produce 7.5 kg of ¹⁴C globally per year. At equilibrium, global inventory = production rate × mean lifetime = 7.5 kg/year × 8,270 years = 62,000 kg. This matches measured atmospheric ¹⁴C inventory (~60 tonnes). For medical infusion: patient receives ¹³¹I at 10 MBq/hour for 6 hours. During infusion: dA/dt = production - decay = 10 - λA. At end of 6-hour infusion: A = (10/λ) × (1 - e^(-λ×6)) = (10 × 8.02/0.693) × (1 - e^(-0.693×6/8.02)) = 115.7 × (1 - 0.595) = 46.9 MBq. Without decay, would be 60 MBq; decay during infusion reduces accumulated activity by 22%. After infusion stops, activity decays normally: A(t) = 46.9 × e^(-0.693×t/8.02). This model applies to any continuous production scenario: reactor isotope production, radon accumulation in basements, drug IV infusions.

4 Half-Life FAQs

Radioactive decay is a quantum mechanical process — each atom has a fixed probability of decaying per unit time, independent of external conditions (temperature, pressure, chemical form) and independent of other atoms. A single ¹⁴C atom has 50% chance of decaying within 5,730 years. Whether that atom is alone or part of a 1 kg sample doesn't matter. With 10²³ atoms, statistical averaging makes the 50% probability manifest as exactly half decaying in one half-life. This differs from chemical reactions where concentration affects rate. You cannot speed up radioactive decay by heating, pressurizing, or catalyzing — only nuclear reactions (neutron bombardment, particle accelerators) can alter decay rates, and these require extreme conditions not found in normal environments.

Stable isotopes have infinite half-life — they do not decay. Examples: ¹²C, ¹⁶O, ⁵⁶Fe. Some isotopes are "observationally stable" — predicted to decay but with half-lives exceeding the age of the universe. Tellurium-128 has t₁/₂ = 2.2 × 10²⁴ years (2 septillion years), measured by detecting rare double-beta decay events. Bismuth-209 was considered stable until 2003, when alpha decay was observed with t₁/₂ = 1.9 × 10¹⁹ years — 1.4 billion times the age of the universe. For practical purposes, these are stable: a 1 kg sample of ²⁰⁹Bi undergoes only 1 decay per 5 minutes, undetectable without ultra-sensitive equipment. The distinction between "stable" and "extremely long half-life" depends on measurement capability and timescale of interest.

Under ideal conditions, radiocarbon dating achieves ±30-50 years precision for samples up to 10,000 years old. Accuracy depends on: (1) Sample quality — well-preserved bone collagen or charcoal gives best results; contaminated samples (root penetration, conservation treatments) produce erroneous dates. (2) Measurement method — accelerator mass spectrometry (AMS) counts individual ¹⁴C atoms, achieving ±30 years on 1 mg samples; decay counting requires 1-10 g samples and gives ±60-100 years. (3) Calibration — atmospheric ¹⁴C varied over time; tree-ring calibration (dendrochronology) corrects raw radiocarbon ages to calendar years. Uncalibrated dates have systematic errors up to 800 years. (4) Reservoir effects — marine organisms appear 400 years older due to ocean carbon mixing; freshwater fish can appear 500-1000 years older from limestone dissolution. With proper sample selection, AMS measurement, and calibration, radiocarbon dating is accurate to ±1-2% of the age.

After 10 half-lives, (1/2)^10 = 1/1024 = 0.00098 = 0.098% of original quantity remains — approximately 1 part in 1000. For radiation safety, the "10 half-life rule" states that radioactive waste can be disposed as non-radioactive after 10 half-lives of decay storage. A 100 MBq source becomes 0.1 MBq (100 kBq) — often below regulatory concern. After 20 half-lives: (1/2)^20 = 1/1,048,576 = 0.000095% remains (1 ppm). After 50 half-lives: (1/2)^50 = 8.9 × 10⁻¹⁶ — less than one atom remains from Avogadro's number (6 × 10²³ atoms) of starting material. Mathematically, exponential decay never reaches zero; practically, after 50-100 half-lives, the last atom decays and the sample is truly gone. For plutonium-239 (24,100-year half-life), 10 half-lives = 241,000 years — explaining why nuclear waste requires geological timescale containment.

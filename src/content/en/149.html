What is the Ideal Gas Law?

The ideal gas law relates pressure, volume, temperature, and amount of gas in a single elegant equation: PV = nRT. This fundamental relationship describes how gases behave under different conditions — from inflating a tire to predicting weather patterns to calculating the volume of hydrogen needed to fill a balloon. While no real gas is perfectly "ideal," most gases at ordinary temperatures and pressures follow this law closely enough for accurate engineering calculations.

Imagine a scuba tank with internal volume 12 liters, filled with air at 200 bar pressure and 20°C temperature. Using the ideal gas law, we can calculate how much air is stored. First convert to SI: V = 0.012 m³, P = 200×10⁵ Pa = 2×10⁷ Pa, T = 293 K. Solving for moles: n = PV/(RT) = (2×10⁷ × 0.012)/(8.314 × 293) = 240,000/2436 = 98.5 moles. Air's molar mass is 29 g/mol, so mass = 98.5 × 0.029 = 2.86 kg of air compressed into the tank. At surface pressure (1 bar), this same air would occupy V = nRT/P = (98.5 × 8.314 × 293)/10⁵ = 2.4 m³ — enough for about 40-60 breaths.

The ideal gas law combines three historical discoveries: Boyle's law (P ∝ 1/V at constant T), Charles's law (V ∝ T at constant P), and Avogadro's law (V ∝ n at constant P and T). Benoît Paul Émile Clapeyron unified these in 1834. The constant R = 8.314 J/(mol·K) appears throughout thermodynamics, connecting macroscopic measurements to molecular behavior. This law underpins HVAC design, internal combustion engines, chemical reactors, and atmospheric science.

How it Works: Formulas Explained

The ideal gas equation PV = nRT has five variables: pressure P (pascals), volume V (cubic meters), amount n (moles), temperature T (kelvin), and the universal gas constant R = 8.314462618 J/(mol·K). Given any four, you can solve for the fifth. For processes where the amount of gas is fixed, the combined gas law P₁V₁/T₁ = P₂V₂/T₂ relates initial and final states without needing n or R.

Pressure must be absolute pressure (not gauge pressure). Absolute pressure = gauge pressure + atmospheric pressure. At sea level, atmospheric pressure is 101,325 Pa = 1.01325 bar = 14.7 psi = 1 atm. A tire gauge reading 35 psi means absolute pressure is 35 + 14.7 = 49.7 psi = 343 kPa. Temperature must be absolute (Kelvin): T(K) = T(°C) + 273.15. Never use Celsius or Fahrenheit directly in gas law calculations.

For mass-based calculations, use PV = mR_specificT, where m is mass in kg and R_specific = R/M is the specific gas constant. For air (M = 28.97 g/mol): R_specific = 8.314/0.02897 = 287 J/(kg·K). For helium (M = 4 g/mol): R_specific = 8.314/0.004 = 2079 J/(kg·K) — helium's low molecular weight explains why it leaks faster and provides more lift than air.

Working through a complete example: A weather balloon contains 5 moles of helium at ground level (P = 101,325 Pa, T = 20°C = 293 K). Initial volume: V = nRT/P = (5 × 8.314 × 293)/101,325 = 12,180/101,325 = 0.120 m³ = 120 liters. At 30 km altitude, pressure drops to 1,200 Pa and temperature is -45°C = 228 K. New volume: V = (5 × 8.314 × 228)/1,200 = 9,478/1,200 = 7.90 m³ — 66 times larger! The balloon expands dramatically as it rises, which is why weather balloons are only partially inflated at launch.

Step-by-Step Guide

  1. Identify known variables and what you need to find. List P, V, n (or m), and T. Mark the unknown. Determine if the amount of gas changes (open system) or stays constant (closed system). For closed systems with fixed n, use P₁V₁/T₁ = P₂V₂/T₂ to relate two states without calculating n explicitly.
  2. Convert all values to consistent SI units. Pressure to pascals (1 bar = 10⁵ Pa, 1 atm = 101,325 Pa, 1 psi = 6,895 Pa). Volume to cubic meters (1 L = 0.001 m³, 1 cm³ = 10⁻⁶ m³). Temperature to kelvin (K = °C + 273.15). Amount to moles (n = mass/molar mass). Example: 2.5 L at 2.5 bar and 25°C becomes V = 0.0025 m³, P = 2.5×10⁵ Pa, T = 298 K.
  3. Choose the appropriate gas constant. For moles: R = 8.314 J/(mol·K). For mass: use R_specific = R/M. Common values: air = 287 J/(kg·K), helium = 2079 J/(kg·K), nitrogen = 297 J/(kg·K), oxygen = 260 J/(kg·K), CO₂ = 189 J/(kg·K). Alternatively, calculate n from mass first, then use universal R.
  4. Rearrange the equation to solve for the unknown. For pressure: P = nRT/V. For volume: V = nRT/P. For temperature: T = PV/(nR). For moles: n = PV/(RT). For mass: m = PVM/(RT). Write the rearranged equation before plugging in numbers to avoid algebra errors.
  5. Substitute values and calculate. Example: Find pressure of 0.5 moles of gas in a 2 L container at 300 K. P = nRT/V = (0.5 × 8.314 × 300)/0.002 = 1,247/0.002 = 623,500 Pa = 6.24 bar = 6.15 atm. Check units: (mol × J/(mol·K) × K) / m³ = J/m³ = N·m/m³ = N/m² = Pa. ✓
  6. Verify your answer is physically reasonable. Does the pressure seem realistic? (Atmospheric is ~1 bar, car tires ~2-3 bar gauge, scuba tanks ~200 bar.) Is the volume plausible? (1 mole of ideal gas at STP occupies 22.4 L.) Is the temperature in a reasonable range? Negative Kelvin is impossible. If your answer seems off by orders of magnitude, check unit conversions — especially pressure (gauge vs. absolute) and temperature (Celsius vs. Kelvin).

Real-World Examples

Example 1: Car tire pressure change with temperature. Tires are inflated to 35 psi gauge (49.7 psi absolute = 343 kPa) at 20°C (293 K). After highway driving, tire temperature reaches 60°C (333 K). Volume is essentially constant. Using P₁/T₁ = P₂/T₂: P₂ = P₁ × T₂/T₁ = 343 × 333/293 = 390 kPa absolute = 289 kPa gauge = 42 psi gauge. The pressure increased 7 psi just from heating! This is why tire manufacturers specify "cold" pressure — measure before driving, not after.

Example 2: Hot air balloon lift calculation. A balloon envelope holds V = 2,800 m³ of air. Outside air is at 15°C (288 K) and 101,325 Pa. Density of outside air: ρ = P/(R_specificT) = 101,325/(287 × 288) = 1.225 kg/m³. Total mass of displaced air: 2,800 × 1.225 = 3,430 kg — this is the maximum lift. Heating the air inside to 100°C (373 K): ρ_inside = 101,325/(287 × 373) = 0.947 kg/m³. Mass of hot air inside: 2,800 × 0.947 = 2,652 kg. Net lift: 3,430 - 2,652 = 778 kg — enough for basket, burner, fuel, and 3-4 passengers.

Example 3: Scuba tank duration. A diver's tank contains 12 L at 200 bar. At 10 m depth, ambient pressure is 2 bar (1 bar atmospheric + 1 bar from water). The regulator delivers air at ambient pressure. Available air at surface pressure: V_surface = 12 L × 200 = 2,400 L. At 10 m depth (2 bar), this becomes 1,200 L available. A resting diver consumes ~15 L/min at surface; at 10 m, consumption doubles to ~30 L/min (same lung volume, but denser air). Dive time: 1,200/30 = 40 minutes. At 30 m depth (4 bar), consumption is 60 L/min and available air is 2,400/4 = 600 L, giving only 10 minutes.

Example 4: Engine cylinder compression. A motorcycle engine has displacement 600 cm³ (0.6 L) per cylinder. At bottom dead center, the cylinder contains air at 1 atm and 300 K. Compression ratio is 12:1, so volume at top dead center is 0.6/12 = 0.05 L = 50 cm³. Assuming adiabatic compression (no heat loss), PV^γ = constant, where γ = 1.4 for air. P₂ = P₁ × (V₁/V₂)^γ = 1 × (12)^1.4 = 32.4 atm. Temperature: T₂ = T₁ × (V₁/V₂)^(γ-1) = 300 × (12)^0.4 = 300 × 2.7 = 810 K = 537°C. This high temperature ignites the fuel-air mixture in diesel engines.

Example 5: Chemical reactor gas feed. A reactor requires 50 kg/h of nitrogen (N₂, M = 28 g/mol) at 5 bar and 150°C. Molar flow rate: 50/0.028 = 1,786 mol/h. Volumetric flow rate: V = nRT/P = (1,786 × 8.314 × 423)/(5×10⁵) = 6,276,000/500,000 = 12.55 m³/h. The mass flow controller must be set for this volumetric flow at operating conditions. At standard conditions (0°C, 1 bar), the same mass would occupy V = (1,786 × 8.314 × 273)/10⁵ = 40.5 m³/h — important for flow meter calibration.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Using gauge pressure instead of absolute pressure. Gas laws require absolute pressure. A tire at "35 psi" (gauge) is actually at 35 + 14.7 = 49.7 psi absolute. Using 35 psi directly underestimates the amount of gas by about 30%. In SI, gauge pressure in bar must have 1.013 bar added. Vacuum pressures (below atmospheric) are often given as negative gauge pressure — convert to absolute by adding atmospheric pressure.

Using Celsius or Fahrenheit instead of Kelvin. The ideal gas law requires absolute temperature. Using 25°C directly gives nonsense: PV = nR(25) instead of nR(298) — an error of nearly 12×. Worse, negative Celsius temperatures would give negative volumes or pressures, which are physically impossible. Always convert: K = °C + 273.15. For Fahrenheit: first convert to Celsius, then to Kelvin.

Assuming ideal behavior at extreme conditions. The ideal gas law works well at moderate temperatures and pressures (near ambient). At high pressure (> 10 bar) or low temperature (near boiling point), real gases deviate from ideal behavior. Intermolecular forces and molecular volume matter. Use the van der Waals equation or compressibility factor Z: PV = ZnRT. For nitrogen at 100 bar and 0°C, Z ≈ 0.9 — ideal gas law overestimates volume by 10%. For steam near saturation, deviations are much larger.

Confusing molar mass with molecular weight. Molar mass M has units (g/mol or kg/mol); molecular weight is dimensionless (ratio to ¹²C). Numerically they're the same, but you must use M in kg/mol when using SI units with R = 8.314 J/(mol·K). Air's molecular weight is 28.97; molar mass is 0.02897 kg/mol. Using 28.97 directly in m = PVM/(RT) gives answers 1000× too large.

Pro Tips

Memorize STP and SATP molar volumes. At STP (0°C, 1 atm), 1 mole of ideal gas occupies 22.414 L. At SATP (25°C, 1 bar), 1 mole occupies 24.79 L. These benchmarks enable quick estimates: 5 moles at STP ≈ 112 L. If your calculation gives 1.12 L or 1120 L, you've misplaced a decimal. At room temperature and pressure, ~25 L/mol is a useful mental shortcut.

Use the combined gas law for state changes.** When n is constant, P₁V₁/T₁ = P₂V₂/T₂ eliminates R and n. This is perfect for problems like "a gas at 2 bar, 3 L, 300 K changes to 4 bar and 400 K — what's the new volume?" V₂ = V₁ × (P₁/P₂) × (T₂/T₁) = 3 × (2/4) × (400/300) = 3 × 0.5 × 1.33 = 2 L. No need to calculate moles or use R.

Apply Dalton's law for gas mixtures. For a mixture of ideal gases, total pressure equals the sum of partial pressures: P_total = P₁ + P₂ + P₃ + ... Each gas behaves as if it alone occupied the volume. Partial pressure P_i = n_iRT/V = x_i × P_total, where x_i is the mole fraction. Air is ~78% N₂, ~21% O₂, ~1% Ar. At 1 atm, P_N₂ = 0.78 atm, P_O₂ = 0.21 atm. This is essential for gas-phase reactions and respiratory physiology.

Recognize when to use density form. For buoyancy, aerodynamics, and flow calculations, the density form is often more useful: ρ = PM/(RT). Density is proportional to pressure and inversely proportional to temperature. Hot air is less dense (rises). High-pressure air is denser (more mass per volume). At altitude, lower pressure means lower density (aircraft need longer runways). This form avoids calculating moles explicitly.

FAQs

The ideal gas law assumes molecules have zero volume and no intermolecular forces — good approximations when molecules are far apart (low pressure, high temperature). It fails near the condensation point (gas turning to liquid), at very high pressures (> 100 bar), or at very low temperatures. Near these conditions, use the van der Waals equation: (P + an²/V²)(V - nb) = nRT, where a and b are substance-specific constants accounting for intermolecular attraction and molecular volume.

Avogadro's hypothesis: equal volumes of gases at the same temperature and pressure contain equal numbers of molecules, regardless of the gas type. At STP, 1 mole (6.022×10²³ molecules) of any ideal gas occupies 22.4 L. A mole of helium (4 g) and a mole of xenon (131 g) occupy the same volume — the xenon is just 33× denser. This remarkable fact emerges because gas pressure depends on molecular collisions (number and speed), not molecular mass.

The gas constant appears in many unit systems: R = 8.314 J/(mol·K) = 0.08206 L·atm/(mol·K) = 8.314 m³·Pa/(mol·K) = 62.36 L·torr/(mol·K) = 1.987 cal/(mol·K). Choose R to match your units. Using liters and atmospheres? Use R = 0.08206. Using SI (m³ and Pa)? Use R = 8.314. Don't mix systems — if you use R = 0.08206, pressure must be in atm and volume in liters.

Ideal gases follow PV = nRT exactly. Real gases deviate due to finite molecular size (reduces available volume) and intermolecular forces (attractive forces reduce pressure, repulsive forces increase it). The compressibility factor Z = PV/(nRT) quantifies deviation: Z = 1 for ideal, Z < 1 when attraction dominates (moderate pressure), Z > 1 when repulsion dominates (high pressure). For engineering accuracy, use tables or equations of state (van der Waals, Redlich-Kwong, Peng-Robinson) for real gases.

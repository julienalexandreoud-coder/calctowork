What Is a RAID Capacity Calculator?

A RAID capacity calculator determines usable storage space and redundancy overhead for RAID arrays based on number of drives, drive capacity, and RAID level. Four 4 TB drives in RAID 5 provide 12 TB usable space with one-drive fault tolerance. The same drives in RAID 10 provide only 8 TB usable but survive two drive failures. Understanding these tradeoffs informs storage architecture decisions for servers, NAS devices, and workstations.

For 4 drives at 2000 GB each in RAID 5, the calculator determines 6000 GB usable capacity with 2000 GB parity overhead (25% loss). RAID 10 with the same drives yields 4000 GB usable (50% loss) but better performance and redundancy. RAID 0 provides all 8000 GB but zero redundancy — any single drive failure destroys all data. The calculator quantifies these capacity vs. protection tradeoffs.

IT administrators size storage arrays for servers. Home lab builders plan NAS configurations. Video editors calculate shared storage capacity. Database architects design redundant storage. Backup administrators determine archive capacity. The calculator translates drive specifications into actionable capacity and redundancy metrics for infrastructure planning.

The Formula Behind RAID Capacity Calculations

Capacity formulas vary by RAID level:

RAID 0 (Striping): Usable = N × Drive Size
No redundancy. All capacity usable. Minimum 2 drives.

RAID 1 (Mirroring): Usable = Drive Size
Full redundancy. 50% capacity loss (2 drives). Minimum 2 drives.

RAID 5 (Striping + Distributed Parity): Usable = (N - 1) × Drive Size
Single-drive fault tolerance. Capacity loss = 1 drive equivalent. Minimum 3 drives.

RAID 6 (Striping + Dual Parity): Usable = (N - 2) × Drive Size
Dual-drive fault tolerance. Capacity loss = 2 drives equivalent. Minimum 4 drives.

RAID 10 (Mirror + Stripe): Usable = (N / 2) × Drive Size
Single-drive fault tolerance per mirror. 50% capacity loss. Minimum 4 drives (even number).

RAID 50 (RAID 5 + Stripe): Usable = (N - S) × Drive Size
Where S = number of RAID 5 sets. Combines RAID 5 capacity efficiency with RAID 0 performance. Minimum 6 drives.

RAID 60 (RAID 6 + Stripe): Usable = (N - 2S) × Drive Size
Where S = number of RAID 6 sets. Dual parity with striping performance. Minimum 8 drives.

Redundancy characteristics:

  • RAID 0: No redundancy — any drive failure = total data loss
  • RAID 1: Survives 1 drive failure (of 2)
  • RAID 5: Survives 1 drive failure (of 3+)
  • RAID 6: Survives 2 simultaneous drive failures (of 4+)
  • RAID 10: Survives 1 drive per mirror (up to N/2 drives if different mirrors)
  • RAID 50: Survives 1 drive per RAID 5 set
  • RAID 60: Survives 2 drives per RAID 6 set

Drive size note: Use identical drive capacities for optimal RAID. Mixed sizes waste capacity — array operates at smallest drive capacity.

6 Steps to Calculate RAID Capacity Accurately

Step 1: Count Total Number of Drives
Determine how many physical drives will populate the array. Common configurations: 2 drives (RAID 0/1), 3-4 drives (RAID 5), 4-8 drives (RAID 6/10), 6-16+ drives (RAID 50/60). Some RAID levels require specific counts: RAID 10 needs even numbers, RAID 5 minimum 3, RAID 6 minimum 4. Plan for expansion slots if future growth is anticipated.

Step 2: Identify Individual Drive Capacity
Find drive capacity in GB or TB. Common sizes: 1 TB, 2 TB, 4 TB, 8 TB, 10 TB, 12 TB, 14 TB, 16 TB, 18 TB, 20 TB. Use identical capacities for all drives in the array. If mixing sizes (not recommended), the array uses the smallest drive's capacity for all drives — a 4 TB + 4 TB + 8 TB RAID 5 array uses only 4 TB per drive, wasting 4 TB on the larger drive.

Step 3: Select RAID Level Based on Requirements
Choose RAID level balancing capacity, performance, and redundancy needs:
- Maximum capacity, no redundancy needed: RAID 0
- Simple redundancy, 2 drives: RAID 1
- Good capacity efficiency, single-drive protection: RAID 5
- Maximum redundancy, large arrays: RAID 6
- Best performance + redundancy: RAID 10
- Large arrays needing performance: RAID 50/60
Consider rebuild times: RAID 5/6 rebuilds take hours for multi-TB drives, increasing failure risk during rebuild.

Step 4: Apply the Capacity Formula
Calculate usable capacity based on RAID level. For 6 × 4 TB drives:
- RAID 0: 6 × 4 = 24 TB usable
- RAID 1: 4 TB usable (only 2 drives used)
- RAID 5: (6 - 1) × 4 = 20 TB usable
- RAID 6: (6 - 2) × 4 = 16 TB usable
- RAID 10: (6 / 2) × 4 = 12 TB usable
RAID 5 offers best capacity efficiency (83%), RAID 10 worst (50%) but best performance.

Step 5: Calculate Redundancy Overhead Percentage
Overhead = (Total Raw - Usable) / Total Raw × 100%. For 6 × 4 TB (24 TB raw):
- RAID 0: (24 - 24) / 24 = 0% overhead
- RAID 5: (24 - 20) / 24 = 16.7% overhead
- RAID 6: (24 - 16) / 24 = 33.3% overhead
- RAID 10: (24 - 12) / 24 = 50% overhead
Higher overhead buys better redundancy. RAID 6's 33% overhead protects against 2 failures; RAID 10's 50% protects against 1 per mirror.

Step 6: Account for Real-World Capacity Loss
Formatted capacity is 7-10% less than advertised due to: (1) Binary vs. decimal — manufacturers use 1 TB = 1,000,000,000,000 bytes, OS uses 1 TiB = 1,099,511,627,776 bytes. A "4 TB" drive shows 3.64 TiB. (2) File system overhead — NTFS, ZFS, ext4 consume 1-3% for metadata. (3) RAID controller overhead — some controllers reserve space. Plan for 90-93% of calculated usable capacity as actual available space.

5 Worked Examples With Complete Calculations

Example 1: Home NAS Build
Drives: 4 × 8 TB. RAID level: RAID 5 (balance of capacity and redundancy).
Raw capacity: 4 × 8 = 32 TB
Usable (RAID 5): (4 - 1) × 8 = 24 TB
Redundancy: Survives 1 drive failure
Overhead: (32 - 24) / 32 = 25%
Real-world available: 24 × 0.93 = 22.32 TB (after formatting)
Alternative RAID 10: (4 / 2) × 8 = 16 TB usable
RAID 6: (4 - 2) × 8 = 16 TB usable, survives 2 failures
Verdict: RAID 5 maximizes capacity for home media storage. RAID 6 preferred for critical data.

Example 2: Enterprise Database Server
Drives: 8 × 2 TB SSD. RAID level: RAID 10 (performance critical).
Raw capacity: 8 × 2 = 16 TB
Usable (RAID 10): (8 / 2) × 2 = 8 TB
Redundancy: Survives 1 drive per mirror (up to 4 drives if different mirrors)
Overhead: (16 - 8) / 16 = 50%
Real-world available: 8 × 0.95 = 7.6 TB (SSDs have less overhead)
Alternative RAID 5: (8 - 1) × 2 = 14 TB, but slower writes
Alternative RAID 6: (8 - 2) × 2 = 12 TB, better redundancy, slower than RAID 10
Verdict: RAID 10 chosen for database IOPS, not capacity. 8 TB sufficient for active dataset.

Example 3: Video Editing Shared Storage
Drives: 12 × 16 TB. RAID level: RAID 60 (large capacity + performance + redundancy).
Configuration: 2 RAID 6 sets of 6 drives each, striped.
Raw capacity: 12 × 16 = 192 TB
Usable (RAID 60): (12 - 4) × 16 = 128 TB (2 drives parity per 6-drive set)
Redundancy: Survives 2 drives per RAID 6 set (up to 4 total)
Overhead: (192 - 128) / 192 = 33.3%
Real-world available: 128 × 0.93 = 119 TB
Alternative RAID 50: (12 - 2) × 16 = 160 TB, less redundancy
Alternative RAID 6: (12 - 2) × 16 = 160 TB, single set, slower rebuild
Verdict: RAID 60 balances capacity, performance for multiple editors, and redundancy.

Example 4: Budget Home Server
Drives: 3 × 4 TB. RAID level: RAID 5 (minimum drives for RAID 5).
Raw capacity: 3 × 4 = 12 TB
Usable (RAID 5): (3 - 1) × 4 = 8 TB
Redundancy: Survives 1 drive failure
Overhead: (12 - 8) / 12 = 33.3%
Real-world available: 8 × 0.93 = 7.44 TB
Alternative RAID 1: Only 2 drives used = 4 TB usable, wastes 1 drive
Alternative RAID 0: 12 TB usable, zero redundancy (not recommended)
Verdict: RAID 5 is only sensible choice for 3-drive array with redundancy. Add 4th drive later for more capacity.

Example 5: Cloud Backup Archive
Drives: 6 × 18 TB. RAID level: RAID 6 (maximum redundancy for archival).
Raw capacity: 6 × 18 = 108 TB
Usable (RAID 6): (6 - 2) × 18 = 72 TB
Redundancy: Survives 2 simultaneous drive failures
Overhead: (108 - 72) / 108 = 33.3%
Real-world available: 72 × 0.93 = 66.96 TB
Alternative RAID 5: (6 - 1) × 18 = 90 TB, but risky during rebuild
RAID 6 rebuild time for 18 TB drive: 8-12 hours — high risk of second failure
Verdict: RAID 6 essential for large drives. 90 TB RAID 5 not worth 25% extra capacity risk.

4 Critical Mistakes That Skew RAID Capacity Planning

Mistake 1: Mixing Drive Capacities Without Understanding Waste
RAID arrays operate at the capacity of the smallest drive. A RAID 5 with 4 TB + 4 TB + 8 TB drives uses only 4 TB per drive — the 8 TB drive wastes 4 TB. Total usable: (3 - 1) × 4 = 8 TB, not (4 + 4 + 8 - 4) = 12 TB. Some modern RAID controllers (ZFS, Unraid, Synology SHR) handle mixed sizes more efficiently, but traditional hardware RAID wastes the excess. Always use identical drive capacities for predictable results.

Mistake 2: Not Accounting for Binary vs. Decimal Capacity
Drive manufacturers use decimal TB (1 TB = 10¹² bytes). Operating systems use binary TiB (1 TiB = 2⁴⁰ bytes). A "4 TB" drive shows as 3.64 TiB in Windows — a 9% difference. Planning for 24 TB raw RAID 5 (20 TB usable) but expecting 20 TiB available results in 18.2 TiB actual — 10% shortfall. Always calculate using binary: 4 TB drive = 3.64 TiB, then apply RAID formula. Or plan for 90-93% of calculated capacity.

Mistake 3: Choosing RAID 5 for Large Drives Without Considering Rebuild Risk
RAID 5 survives one drive failure, but rebuild times for multi-TB drives are 4-12 hours. During rebuild, remaining drives operate under stress — a second failure destroys the array. For drives >4 TB, RAID 6 or RAID 10 is safer despite capacity cost. A 12 TB RAID 5 rebuild at 150 MB/s takes 22+ hours. RAID 6's extra parity drive prevents data loss from second failures during this vulnerable window.

Mistake 4: Assuming All Drives Are Available for Data
Some RAID configurations reserve drives for specific purposes. RAID 1 uses 2 drives for 1 drive's capacity. RAID 10 uses half the drives for mirrors. Hot spare drives sit idle until failures occur — a 4-drive RAID 5 + 1 hot spare array has only 3 drives active, not 4. Plan for actual usable capacity, not raw drive count × size. Document hot spare allocations separately from active array capacity.

4 Professional Tips for RAID Planning

Tip 1: Use RAID 6 or RAID 10 for Drives Larger Than 4 TB
Large drives take longer to rebuild, increasing failure probability during rebuild. RAID 5 was acceptable for 500 GB drives (2-3 hour rebuild) but risky for 12 TB drives (10+ hour rebuild). RAID 6 adds one parity drive (33% overhead vs. 25% for RAID 5) but survives 2 failures. RAID 10 offers faster rebuilds (copy mirror, not parity recalculation) at 50% overhead. For critical data with large drives, capacity efficiency isn't worth the risk.

Tip 2: Include Hot Spares for Large Arrays
Hot spare drives sit idle in the array, automatically replacing failed drives and triggering immediate rebuild. A 6-drive RAID 6 + 1 hot spare array has 5 active drives (64 TB usable for 16 TB drives) but rebuilds automatically on failure. Without hot spare, failed drives sit unreplaced for days/weeks until manual intervention — during which a second failure causes data loss. Hot spares cost 16-20% of array capacity but dramatically reduce risk.

Tip 3: Plan for Expansion from the Start
RAID expansion options vary by level. RAID 5/6 can often add drives online (controller-dependent). RAID 10 typically requires rebuilding the entire array to add drives. Plan chassis with extra bays and power supply headroom. A 4-bay NAS filled completely has no expansion path — you must replace all drives with larger ones to grow. Leave 1-2 bays empty or use RAID 5/6 for future expansion flexibility.

Tip 4: Monitor Drive Health and Replace Proactively
SMART data predicts drive failures weeks in advance. Monitor attributes like Reallocated Sector Count, Current Pending Sector, and Uncorrectable Error Count. Replace drives showing warning signs before failure. For arrays with 20+ drives, expect 1-2 failures per year statistically. Keep compatible spare drives on hand. RAID protects against failures but isn't a backup — maintain offsite copies of critical data regardless of RAID level.

4 FAQs About RAID Capacity

Depends on RAID level and controller. RAID 5/6: Many hardware controllers support online capacity expansion (OCE) — add drives and expand without data loss. Software RAID (mdadm, ZFS) handles expansion well. RAID 10: Usually requires backing up, rebuilding array with more drives, restoring data — destructive operation. RAID 0: Cannot expand — must backup, recreate, restore. Plan initial capacity for 3-5 year needs, or choose RAID 5/6 for expansion flexibility.

Modern RAID controllers and software RAID resume rebuilds after power restoration without data loss. The rebuild progress is tracked on disk. However, power loss during rebuild extends the vulnerable window — drives remain stressed longer. Use a UPS (uninterruptible power supply) for RAID arrays, especially during rebuilds. A $100-200 UPS protecting a $2000+ storage array is cheap insurance against rebuild corruption from power events.

Hardware RAID: Dedicated controller with cache, battery backup, offloads CPU. Better for write-heavy workloads (databases). Vendor lock-in — migrating to different controller can be difficult. Software RAID (mdadm, ZFS, Windows Storage Spaces): Free, flexible, no vendor lock-in. Uses CPU but modern CPUs handle RAID easily. ZFS offers superior data integrity (checksums, self-healing). For home/SMB: software RAID. For enterprise databases: hardware RAID with BBU.

Test quarterly by pulling one drive (for RAID 5/6/10) and verifying array rebuilds successfully. This validates backups work, drives are functional, and you understand the rebuild process before a real emergency. For RAID 6, test twice yearly pulling one drive at a time. Also run regular scrub/patrol read operations (weekly for ZFS, monthly for traditional RAID) to detect and correct silent data corruption before it becomes unrecoverable.

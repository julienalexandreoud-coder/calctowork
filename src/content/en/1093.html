Beam Deflection Calculator: Analyze Structural Loads and Bending

The Beam Deflection Calculator computes the bending displacement, slope angle, and bending stress in beams subjected to point loads and distributed loads. Civil engineers, structural designers, mechanical engineers, and architects use this tool to verify that beam designs meet safety and serviceability requirements defined in building codes. Accurate deflection analysis prevents structural failures, ensures occupant comfort, and extends the lifespan of building components.

Cantilever Beam Deflection Formula (Point Load at Free End)

δ = (P × L³) / (3 × E × I)

Where δ is the maximum deflection at the free end in meters, P is the point load in newtons, L is the beam length in meters, E is the elastic modulus (Young's modulus) in pascals, and I is the area moment of inertia in meters⁴. For simply supported beams with a midspan point load, the formula is δ = (P × L³) / (48 × E × I), and for a uniformly distributed load, δ = (5 × w × L⁴) / (384 × E × I) where w is the load per unit length.

These formulas derive from the Euler-Bernoulli beam theory, which assumes that plane cross-sections remain plane during bending. The theory is valid for beams where the length-to-depth ratio is greater than 10. For shorter, deeper beams, shear deformation becomes significant and Timoshenko beam theory may provide more accurate results.

Worked Examples

Example 1: Steel Cantilever Beam

A 2-meter steel cantilever beam (E = 200 GPa = 2 × 10¹¹ Pa) with a rectangular cross-section 0.05 m wide and 0.10 m deep is subjected to a 500 N point load at its free end.

Calculation: I = b × h³ / 12 = 0.05 × (0.10)³ / 12 = 4.167 × 10⁶⁺ m⁴. δ = (500 × 2³) / (3 × 2 × 10¹¹ × 4.167 × 10⁶⁺) = 4000 / (2.5 × 10⁶⁺) = 0.0032 m = 3.2 mm. The maximum bending stress at the fixed end is σ = (P × L × h/2) / I = (500 × 2 × 0.05) / (4.167 × 10⁶⁺) = 12 MPa, well below steel's yield strength of approximately 250 MPa.

Example 2: Simply Supported Wood Beam

A 4-meter simply supported wood beam (E = 10 GPa) with a 0.10 m by 0.20 m cross-section carries a uniformly distributed load of 2000 N/m (including self-weight).

Calculation: I = 0.10 × (0.20)³ / 12 = 6.667 × 10⁶⁬ m⁴. δ = (5 × 2000 × 4⁴) / (384 × 10 × 10⁵ × 6.667 × 10⁶⁬) = (5 × 2000 × 256) / (384 × 6.667 × 10⁸⁸) = 2560000 / 2.56 × 10⁷⁸ = 0.010 m = 10.0 mm. The allowable deflection per typical building codes is span/360 = 4000/360 = 11.1 mm, so this design meets the serviceability requirement.

Common Uses

  • Verifying structural beam designs in buildings, bridges, and industrial frameworks against building code deflection limits
  • Designing cantilever elements such as balconies, awnings, canopy structures, and cantilevered walkways
  • Sizing steel and aluminum beams for mezzanine floors, conveyor supports, and material handling systems
  • Analyzing shaft deflection in mechanical systems such as rotating equipment supports and transmission shafts
  • Evaluating existing structures for increased load capacity when repurposing buildings or adding equipment
  • Selecting appropriate beam cross-sections and materials to minimize deflection while optimizing material cost

Common Mistakes

  • Using inconsistent units — mixing millimeters for length with pascals for modulus leads to errors by factors of 10⁶; always work in consistent SI units (meters, newtons, pascals)
  • Applying the wrong formula for the support condition — the cantilever formula gives much larger deflections than the simply supported formula for the same load and dimensions
  • Ignoring the beam's self-weight in long-span designs — self-weight can contribute significantly to total deflection, especially in longer spans exceeding 6 meters
  • Neglecting shear deformation in short, deep beams — the Euler-Bernoulli theory underestimates deflection when the span-to-depth ratio is below 10
  • Forgetting that deflection limits are for serviceability, not just strength — a beam may be strong enough but still deflect excessively, causing cracked ceilings or jammed doors

Pro Tip

When designing beams for deflection control, increasing the beam depth is far more effective than increasing its width. Since the moment of inertia (I) is proportional to the cube of the depth (h³), doubling the beam depth increases stiffness by a factor of 8. Doubling the width only doubles the stiffness. This is why structural engineers use deep, narrow I-beams rather than square sections — they maximize stiffness per unit of material. For a given cross-sectional area, a rectangular beam with a 2:1 depth-to-width ratio is four times stiffer than a square beam of the same area.

Frequently Asked Questions

Beam deflection is the degree to which a beam bends under load. It is critical because excessive deflection can cause cracking, misalignment, and structural failure. Building codes specify maximum deflections, typically span/360 for live loads and span/240 for total loads.

Steel: 200 GPa. Aluminum: 69 GPa. Concrete: 14 to 40 GPa depending on mix. Wood: 5 to 15 GPa by species. Glulam: 10 to 13 GPa. Always verify the specific grade and manufacturer data for your material.

A cantilever is fixed at one end and free at the other (like a diving board). A simply supported beam rests on supports at both ends (like a bridge girder). Cantilevers have maximum deflection at the free end; simply supported beams have maximum deflection at midspan.

For a rectangle: I = bh^3/12. For a circle: I = pi*r^4/4. For standard I-beams, values are in manufacturer tables. The moment of inertia depends only on the cross-sectional shape, not the material.

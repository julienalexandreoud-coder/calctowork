What is Break-Even Calculator?

A break-even calculator determines the exact number of units you need to sell — or revenue you need to generate — to cover all your costs and start making profit. This is the fundamental question every business owner faces: how much do I need to sell before I stop losing money? For a small bakery with €5,000 monthly fixed costs (rent, utilities, salaries), selling pastries at €5 each with €2 variable cost per unit (ingredients, packaging): break-even = €5,000 ÷ (€5 - €2) = €5,000 ÷ €3 = 1,667 pastries per month. Sell 1,666? You lose money. Sell 1,667? You break even. Sell 1,668? You profit €3. Entrepreneurs, startup founders, product managers, and small business owners use break-even analysis before launching products, setting prices, or making investment decisions — knowing your break-even point tells you whether a business idea is viable or a fast track to bankruptcy.

How Break-Even Calculator Works: The Formulas Explained

Break-Even Units Formula: Break-Even Units = Fixed Costs ÷ (Selling Price - Variable Cost per Unit). The denominator (Selling Price - Variable Cost) is called "contribution margin" — how much each unit contributes to covering fixed costs. Example: Fixed costs €10,000/month, selling price €50, variable cost €30. Contribution margin = €50 - €30 = €20 per unit. Break-even = €10,000 ÷ €20 = 500 units. Sell 500 units, you cover all costs. Sell 501, you profit €20. Break-Even Revenue Formula: Break-Even Revenue = Fixed Costs ÷ Contribution Margin Ratio, where Contribution Margin Ratio = (Selling Price - Variable Cost) ÷ Selling Price. Same example: Margin ratio = €20 ÷ €50 = 0.40 (40%). Break-even revenue = €10,000 ÷ 0.40 = €25,000. You need €25,000 monthly revenue to break even. Profit Target Formula: Want €5,000 monthly profit? Required Units = (Fixed Costs + Target Profit) ÷ Contribution Margin. Example: (€10,000 + €5,000) ÷ €20 = 750 units. Margin of Safety: How much buffer do you have? Margin of Safety = (Actual Sales - Break-Even Sales) ÷ Actual Sales. If you sell 800 units vs. 500 break-even: (800 - 500) ÷ 800 = 37.5% safety margin.

Step-by-Step Guide to Using This Calculator

  1. Calculate total fixed costs: Add all costs that don't change with sales volume: rent, insurance, salaries, software subscriptions, loan payments, utilities (base charge). For a coffee shop: €3,000 rent + €2,000 salaries + €500 insurance + €300 software + €200 utilities = €6,000/month fixed. Don't include ingredients or hourly labor — those are variable.
  2. Determine selling price per unit: What do you charge customers? Use your actual average selling price. If you sell multiple products at different prices, use a weighted average or calculate break-even separately for each product line. A bookstore selling books at €15 average: enter 15.
  3. Calculate variable cost per unit: What does it cost to produce/deliver ONE unit? Include materials, packaging, shipping, payment processing fees (2-3% of sale), commissions. For a t-shirt business: €5 blank shirt + €3 printing + €1 packaging + €0.50 payment fee = €9.50 variable cost per shirt.
  4. Enter your values and click Calculate: The calculator shows: (1) Break-even units (how many to sell), (2) Break-even revenue (€ amount), (3) Contribution margin per unit, (4) Contribution margin ratio (%). Review each metric — they tell different parts of your financial story.
  5. Test different scenarios: What if you raise price to €55? Break-even drops to €10,000 ÷ (€55 - €30) = 400 units (100 fewer). What if you negotiate variable cost down to €25? Break-even = €10,000 ÷ (€50 - €25) = 400 units. Small changes dramatically impact viability.
  6. Compare to realistic sales capacity: Can you actually sell 500 units/month? That's 500 ÷ 30 = 16.7 units/day. If your shop gets 50 customers/day and 20% buy, that's 10 sales/day — you'd operate at 60% of break-even and lose €4,000/month. Be honest about realistic demand.

Real-World Examples

Example 1 — Food Truck Business: Maria wants to launch a taco truck. Fixed costs: €2,500 truck payment + €800 insurance + €400 permits + €300 propane = €4,000/month. She sells tacos at €4 each. Variable cost: €1.20 tortilla + €0.80 meat + €0.30 toppings + €0.20 wrapper = €2.50 per taco. Contribution margin: €4 - €2.50 = €1.50 per taco. Break-even: €4,000 ÷ €1.50 = 2,667 tacos/month = 89 tacos/day (30 days). At lunch rush, she sells 120 tacos/day — 31% above break-even. Monthly profit: (120 - 89) × 30 × €1.50 = €1,395. The business works.

Example 2 — SaaS Startup: TechCo built project management software. Fixed costs: €15,000/month (€8,000 developer salaries, €3,000 servers, €2,000 marketing, €2,000 office). They charge €29/month per user. Variable cost: €2/user (hosting, support). Contribution margin: €29 - €2 = €27. Break-even: €15,000 ÷ €27 = 556 paying users. They currently have 340 users = €9,180 revenue - €15,680 costs = €6,500 monthly loss. They need 216 more customers OR must raise price to €39 (break-even drops to 417 users) OR cut costs to €10,000 (break-even drops to 370 users).

Example 3 — E-commerce Product Launch: You're selling wireless earbuds on Amazon. Fixed costs: €5,000 inventory purchase + €1,000 product photography + €500 Amazon setup = €6,500 one-time (not monthly). Selling price: €79. Variable cost: €25 manufacturing + €8 shipping + €12 Amazon fees (15%) = €45 per unit. Contribution margin: €79 - €45 = €34. Break-even: €6,500 ÷ €34 = 191 units. You must sell 191 earbuds to recover your upfront investment. Unit 192+ is pure profit. If Amazon ads cost €15/customer acquisition, add that to variable cost: €45 + €15 = €60, margin drops to €19, break-even jumps to 342 units.

Example 4 — Pricing Decision Analysis: Your handmade jewelry sells 200 necklaces/month at €80 each. Variable cost: €35. Fixed costs: €3,000. Current break-even: €3,000 ÷ (€80 - €35) = €3,000 ÷ €45 = 67 necklaces. You're selling 200, so profit = (200 - 67) × €45 = €5,985/month. You consider raising price to €95. Market research suggests you'll sell 15% fewer units (170 necklaces). New scenario: margin = €95 - €35 = €60. Profit = (170 - 50) × €60 = €7,200/month. Higher price, fewer sales, MORE profit: €1,215 additional monthly profit. The price increase is worth it.

Example 5 — Restaurant Menu Analysis: A restaurant has €25,000 monthly fixed costs. They analyze two dishes: Pasta (€18 price, €5 food cost, €3 labor = €8 variable, €10 margin) vs. Steak (€35 price, €14 food cost, €4 labor = €18 variable, €17 margin). Pasta contribution margin ratio: €10 ÷ €18 = 55.6%. Steak: €17 ÷ €35 = 48.6%. Pasta has higher margin percentage, but steak has higher absolute margin per dish. If kitchen capacity is 500 dishes/week, maximizing profit means focusing on steak: 500 × €17 = €8,500/week vs. pasta: 500 × €10 = €5,000/week. Break-even analysis guides menu strategy.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Classifying costs incorrectly: The most common break-even error. Fixed costs don't change with volume; variable costs do. Rent is fixed. Ingredients are variable. But what about electricity? Base charge is fixed, usage is variable. What about labor? Salaried employees are fixed; hourly workers are variable. Misclassification skews your break-even point. A bakery counting the baker's €3,000 salary as variable (when it's actually fixed) would overestimate break-even units and make wrong pricing decisions.
  • Ignoring semi-variable costs: Many costs are hybrid. Phone bill: €30 base + €0.10/minute. Shipping: €5 base + €2/kg. These need to be split. If you ship 1,000 packages averaging 2kg: fixed portion = €5 × 1,000 = €5,000 (actually variable with number of shipments!), variable portion = €2 × 2kg × 1,000 = €4,000. For break-even, treat the base as fixed if you pay it regardless of sales, the per-unit as variable.
  • Using break-even for multi-product businesses without weighting: A store selling laptops (€500 margin) and mouse pads (€5 margin) can't just average prices. If 90% of sales are mouse pads and 10% laptops, your weighted average margin = (0.90 × €5) + (0.10 × €500) = €4.50 + €50 = €54.50. Use actual sales mix to calculate weighted contribution margin, or calculate break-even separately for each product category.
  • Not updating break-even when costs change: Your break-even point isn't static. Rent increases 10%? Break-even rises. Supplier raises material costs 15%? Break-even rises. You negotiate better payment processing rates? Break-even drops. Recalculate quarterly or whenever significant cost changes occur. A business that calculated break-even at startup but never updated it through 3 rent increases and a 20% material cost spike may be losing money without realizing it.

Pro Tips for Better Break-Even Analysis

  • Calculate break-even for each major product separately: Don't just analyze overall business break-even. A company selling 5 products might be overall profitable while 2 products lose money on every sale (subsidized by the other 3). Calculate contribution margin per product: Product A (€50 margin, 100 units/month = €5,000), Product B (€-5 margin, 50 units/month = €-250 loss). Discontinue Product B or raise price — you're paying customers to buy it. This analysis reveals which products drive profit and which drain it.
  • Use break-even to evaluate fixed cost investments: Considering hiring a €4,000/month salesperson? Calculate how many additional units they need to sell to justify their cost. With €40 contribution margin: €4,000 ÷ €40 = 100 additional units/month. Can they realistically generate 100 extra sales? If current close rate is 20% and they make 200 calls/month (50 qualified leads), that's 10 sales — not enough. The hire doesn't break even. This prevents expensive hiring mistakes.
  • Build in a safety margin: Breaking even at 500 units/month when you typically sell 520 units is risky — one bad month and you lose money. Aim for break-even at 60-70% of realistic sales capacity. If you can sell 1,000 units max, target break-even of 600-700 units. This gives you a 30-40% margin of safety for slow seasons, economic downturns, or unexpected competition. Conservative break-even targets prevent cash flow crises.
  • Model best/worst/likely case scenarios: Don't calculate one break-even point — calculate three. Best case: optimistic sales, low costs (€4,000 fixed, €25 variable, 600 units break-even). Worst case: pessimistic sales, high costs (€6,000 fixed, €35 variable, 1,200 units break-even). Likely case: realistic middle ground (€5,000 fixed, €30 variable, 833 units break-even). If the business isn't viable in the worst case, don't launch — or secure enough cash reserves to survive 6-12 months of worst-case performance.

Frequently Asked Questions

What's the difference between break-even and profitability?

Break-even is the point where revenue equals total costs — you make €0 profit and €0 loss. Profitability is everything ABOVE break-even. If break-even is 500 units and you sell 700 units, you're profitable on 200 units. Example: Fixed costs €10,000, margin €20/unit. At 500 units: €10,000 revenue - €10,000 costs = €0 (break-even). At 700 units: €14,000 revenue - €10,000 fixed - €4,000 variable = €0... wait, that's still break-even. Let me recalculate: 700 × €20 margin = €14,000 contribution - €10,000 fixed = €4,000 profit. The 200 units above break-even generate €4,000 profit. Break-even tells you the threshold; everything beyond it is profit.

How do I calculate break-even for a service business?

Same formula, different units. Instead of "units sold," use "billable hours" or "clients served." Freelance consultant: Fixed costs €3,000/month (software, insurance, home office). Rate: €100/hour. Variable cost: €10/hour (taxes, materials). Margin: €90/hour. Break-even: €3,000 ÷ €90 = 33.3 billable hours/month. That's 8.3 hours/week. If you can realistically bill 25 hours/week, you profit on 16.7 hours/week × €90 = €1,503/month profit. Service businesses often have lower variable costs than product businesses, resulting in lower break-even points.

Can break-even analysis help me set prices?

Yes — work the formula backward. Know your fixed costs (€10,000), variable cost (€30), and realistic sales volume (400 units)? Calculate minimum price: Rearrange formula: Selling Price = (Fixed Costs ÷ Units) + Variable Cost. Minimum price = (€10,000 ÷ 400) + €30 = €25 + €30 = €55. At €55, you break even. To profit €5,000: Price = (€10,000 + €5,000) ÷ 400 + €30 = €37.50 + €30 = €67.50. This tells you the absolute minimum price to survive and the price needed to hit profit targets. Then check if the market will pay it.

What if my break-even point seems impossible to reach?

You have four levers to pull: (1) Raise price — increases margin per unit. €50 price → €60 price with €30 variable cost: margin goes from €20 to €30, break-even drops 33%. (2) Reduce variable costs — negotiate with suppliers, find cheaper materials, improve efficiency. €30 variable → €25 variable: margin €20 → €25, break-even drops 20%. (3) Cut fixed costs — move to cheaper location, eliminate unused subscriptions, outsource instead of hire. €10,000 fixed → €8,000 fixed: break-even drops 20%. (4) Increase volume — more marketing, new sales channels, expand product line. Often you'll combine all four: small improvements across all levers make an impossible break-even achievable.

What is Mortgage Calculator?

This mortgage calculator provides a comprehensive breakdown of your home loan payments, including monthly principal and interest, total interest paid over the life of the loan, and a full amortization schedule showing how your payment split changes over time. Whether you are purchasing your first home, considering refinancing, comparing loan options, or modeling extra payment strategies, this tool gives you the financial clarity to make confident decisions. The single most important insight a mortgage calculator provides is the total cost of borrowing: on a $400,000 loan at 7% over 30 years, you pay $558,741 in interest — more than the original loan amount. Understanding this motivates borrowers to explore shorter terms, extra payments, and rate shopping, all of which can save tens to hundreds of thousands of dollars. The calculator also models private mortgage insurance (PMI), property taxes, homeowners insurance, and HOA fees to give you the complete monthly housing payment, not just principal and interest.

How Mortgage Calculation Works: The Formula Explained

The monthly mortgage payment (principal + interest) is calculated using the standard amortization formula: M = P × [r(1+r)^n] / [(1+r)^n - 1], where P is the loan principal, r is the monthly interest rate (annual rate ÷ 12), and n is the total number of payments (years × 12). This formula produces a level payment where the interest component decreases and the principal component increases over the life of the loan. In month 1 of a $400,000 loan at 7% for 30 years, the payment is $2,661, of which $2,333 is interest and only $328 is principal. By month 360, the split reverses — $15 in interest and $2,646 in principal. This front-loaded interest structure means that extra payments early in the loan have a disproportionate impact: paying an additional $100/month from month 1 on the same loan saves $86,000 in interest and pays off the mortgage 5 years early. The amortization schedule also reveals the "crossover point" — the month when more of your payment goes to principal than interest. On a 30-year loan at 7%, this occurs around month 17 (year 14), which underscores why term length and interest rate are the two most powerful levers in mortgage planning.

Step-by-Step Guide to Using This Calculator

  1. Enter the home price: This is the total purchase price, not the loan amount. The calculator subtracts your down payment to determine the loan principal.
  2. Set the down payment: Enter as a percentage or dollar amount. A 20% down payment ($80,000 on a $400,000 home) eliminates the need for PMI. Anything below 20% triggers PMI of 0.5–1.5% of the loan amount per year.
  3. Enter the interest rate: Use the APR quoted by your lender, which includes most fees. Current rates change weekly — check multiple lenders. Even a 0.25% rate difference saves thousands over 30 years.
  4. Choose the loan term: 30 years is the most common (lower payment, higher total interest). 15 years has a higher payment but saves dramatically on interest. Compare both to find your optimal balance.
  5. Add property tax and insurance: Property tax runs 0.5–2.5% of home value annually depending on location. Homeowners insurance averages $1,200–$3,000/year. PMI applies if down payment is below 20%.
  6. Model extra payments (optional): Enter additional monthly or one-time payments to see their impact on total interest and loan term. Even $50–$100/month extra saves tens of thousands in interest.

Real-World Examples

Example 1 — First-Time Homebuyer: Home price: $380,000. Down payment: 10% ($38,000). Loan: $342,000 at 6.75% for 30 years. Monthly P&I: $2,218. Property tax at 1.2%: $380/month. Homeowners insurance: $140/month. PMI at 0.8%: $228/month. Total monthly payment: $2,966. Total interest over 30 years: $456,465. Total cost: $798,465 (more than double the home price). Adding $200/month extra payment from month 1: pays off in 24 years 3 months, saves $146,000 in interest. PMI drops off at ~78% LTV (approximately year 9), reducing the payment by $228/month.

Example 2 — 15 vs. 30 Year Comparison: Loan amount: $350,000. 30-year at 6.5%: Monthly P&I $2,212, total interest $446,233. 15-year at 5.9% (rates are typically 0.5% lower for 15-year): Monthly P&I $2,939, total interest $179,073. The 15-year loan saves $267,160 in interest but costs $727 more per month. Invested at 7% average return, that $727/month grows to $479,000 over 30 years — more than the $267,000 interest savings. The 30-year loan with disciplined investing wins for many borrowers, but only if they actually invest the difference.

Example 3 — Refinance Analysis: Current loan: $320,000 balance at 7.25%, 26 years remaining. Monthly P&I: $2,189. Refinancing to 6.0% for a new 30-year term: Monthly P&I: $1,919 (saving $270/month). Refinancing to 6.0% for a new 26-year term (matching remaining term): Monthly P&I: $1,976 (saving $213/month). The lower payment of the 30-year refinance extends the loan by 4 years and costs $68,000 more in total interest despite the lower rate. The 26-year refinance maintains the same payoff date with true savings. Closing costs of $4,500 are recouped in 21 months ($213 savings × 21 months = $4,473).

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Forgeting PITI: Principal and Interest is only part of your housing payment. Property tax, insurance, and PMI can add $300–$800/month. A $2,200 P&I payment becomes $2,800+ with full PITI — a difference that can make or break your budget.
  • Assuming you will always have PMI: PMI is not permanent. By law, it automatically terminates when your loan balance reaches 78% of the original home value based on the amortization schedule. You can request cancellation at 80% LTV. Aggressive extra payments can eliminate PMI in 3–5 years instead of 9+ years.
  • Ignoring closing costs: Refinancing to save $200/month sounds great until you factor in $4,000–$8,000 in closing costs. Calculate the break-even point: closing costs ÷ monthly savings = months to break even. If you plan to sell or refinance again before the break-even, it is not worth it.
  • Choosing a 30-year loan by default: The 30-year mortgage is the most popular because of the lower monthly payment, but it costs 2–3 times the original loan amount in interest. If you can afford the 15-year payment (which is typically 40–50% higher), you save hundreds of thousands in interest and own your home outright in half the time.

Pro Tips for Better Results

  • Shop at least 4 lenders: Rate differences of 0.25–0.5% between lenders on the same day are common. On a $400,000 loan, 0.5% saves $133/month and $48,000 over 30 years. Get quotes from banks, credit unions, online lenders, and mortgage brokers.
  • Make one extra payment per year: Adding 1/12th of your monthly payment to each payment ($222 extra on a $2,661 payment) results in one extra payment per year. On a $400,000 loan at 7%, this single strategy saves $86,000 in interest and pays off 5 years early.
  • Recast your mortgage after lump-sum payments: If you receive a bonus, inheritance, or other windfall, apply it as a principal payment and ask your lender for a recast. This re-amortizes the remaining balance over the original term, lowering your monthly payment without the cost of refinancing. Most lenders charge $150–$500 for a recast.
  • Consider the break-even on buying down your rate: One discount point costs 1% of the loan amount and typically reduces your rate by 0.25%. On a $400,000 loan, one point costs $4,000 and reduces the monthly payment by $67. Break-even: 4,000 ÷ 67 = 60 months (5 years). If you plan to stay in the home for more than 5 years, buy the point. If not, take the higher rate.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much house can I afford?

The 28/36 rule is the standard guideline: your total housing payment (PITI) should not exceed 28% of gross monthly income, and your total debt payments (housing + car + student loans + credit cards) should not exceed 36%. On a $100,000 salary ($8,333/month gross), your maximum PITI is $2,333. With $500/month in taxes and insurance, that supports roughly a $300,000–$350,000 loan at current rates. However, many financial advisors recommend the 25% take-home rule: housing costs should not exceed 25% of your net (after-tax) income for a more sustainable budget.

When should I refinance?

The traditional rule of thumb is to refinance when you can reduce your rate by at least 0.75–1%. However, the real question is break-even time: closing costs ÷ monthly savings = months to break even. If you can save $200/month on a $5,000 refinance, you break even in 25 months. If you plan to stay in the home longer than that, it is worth refinancing. Also consider refinancing from a 30-year to a 15-year term — even if the rate improvement is small, the term reduction can save $200,000+ in interest.

Should I pay off my mortgage early or invest the extra money?

Mathematically, investing extra money at a 7%+ average return beats paying off a 6–7% mortgage early. However, paying off the mortgage provides a guaranteed "return" equal to your interest rate (after tax deduction), eliminates the largest monthly expense, and provides psychological security. A hybrid approach: make one extra payment per year (splits the difference) and invest any additional savings. You get the interest savings of early payoff plus the compounding returns of investment.

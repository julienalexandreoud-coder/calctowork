What Is an Evapotranspiration Calculator?

An evapotranspiration calculator estimates the combined water loss from soil evaporation and plant transpiration over a specific time period. This measurement, expressed in millimeters per day (mm/day), tells farmers, landscapers, and irrigation managers exactly how much water their crops or plants have lost to the atmosphere — information critical for scheduling irrigation without wasting water or stressing plants.

You may also find the Dew Point Calculator, Rainfall Volume Calculator, and Heat Index Calculator useful.

Evapotranspiration (ET) represents one of the largest water outflows in the agricultural water balance. In arid regions like California's Central Valley, daily ET rates can exceed 8 mm during peak summer, meaning a hectare of crops loses over 80,000 liters of water per day. Without accurate ET calculations, farmers either underwater (reducing yields) or overwater (wasting resources and leaching nutrients).

The calculator uses meteorological data — air temperature, relative humidity, wind speed, and solar radiation — to compute reference evapotranspiration (ET₀) using the FAO-56 Penman-Monteith equation, the international standard adopted by the United Nations Food and Agriculture Organization. This method accounts for energy availability, aerodynamic resistance, and surface characteristics to produce reliable estimates across diverse climates from humid Florida to arid Arizona.

The Evapotranspiration Formula Explained with Examples

The FAO-56 Penman-Monteith equation calculates reference evapotranspiration (ET₀) using this formula:

ET₀ = [0.408 × Δ × (Rn - G) + γ × (900 / (T + 273)) × u₂ × (es - ea)] / [Δ + γ × (1 + 0.34 × u₂)]

Where:

  • ET₀ = Reference evapotranspiration (mm/day)
  • Rn = Net radiation at the crop surface (MJ/m²/day)
  • G = Soil heat flux density (MJ/m²/day), typically negligible for daily calculations
  • T = Mean daily air temperature at 2 meters height (°C)
  • u₂ = Wind speed at 2 meters height (m/s)
  • es = Saturation vapor pressure (kPa)
  • ea = Actual vapor pressure (kPa)
  • es - ea = Saturation vapor pressure deficit (kPa)
  • Δ = Slope of the vapor pressure curve (kPa/°C)
  • γ = Psychrometric constant (kPa/°C)

Worked Example: A farm in Fresno, California records the following on July 15:

  • Maximum temperature: 36°C
  • Minimum temperature: 22°C
  • Mean temperature: 29°C
  • Relative humidity: 35%
  • Wind speed: 3.2 m/s
  • Solar radiation: 28 MJ/m²/day

Step 1: Calculate saturation vapor pressure (es) = 0.6108 × exp((17.27 × 29) / (29 + 273.3)) = 4.01 kPa

Step 2: Calculate actual vapor pressure (ea) = es × (RH/100) = 4.01 × 0.35 = 1.40 kPa

Step 3: Calculate vapor pressure deficit = 4.01 - 1.40 = 2.61 kPa

Step 4: Calculate slope (Δ) = (4098 × 4.01) / (29 + 273.3)² = 0.178 kPa/°C

Step 5: Calculate psychrometric constant (γ) = 0.000665 × atmospheric pressure ≈ 0.067 kPa/°C

Step 6: Plug into the equation:

ET₀ = [0.408 × 0.178 × (28 - 0) + 0.067 × (900/302) × 3.2 × 2.61] / [0.178 + 0.067 × (1 + 0.34 × 3.2)]

ET₀ = [2.03 + 1.68] / [0.178 + 0.213] = 3.71 / 0.391 = 9.5 mm/day

This means the crop loses 95,000 liters of water per hectare that day — irrigation must replace this amount to maintain optimal growth.

6 Steps to Calculate Evapotranspiration

  1. Gather meteorological data: Collect daily maximum and minimum temperature (°C), relative humidity (%), wind speed at 2 meters (m/s), and solar radiation (MJ/m²/day) from an on-site weather station or nearby meteorological service. Data quality directly affects ET accuracy — avoid using regional averages when field-specific measurements are available.
  2. Calculate mean temperature: Add the maximum and minimum temperatures, then divide by 2. For the Fresno example: (36 + 22) / 2 = 29°C. Some advanced methods use hourly data to compute a more precise mean, but the max-min average works for most agricultural applications.
  3. Compute vapor pressure parameters: Calculate saturation vapor pressure (es) using the mean temperature, then derive actual vapor pressure (ea) by multiplying es by relative humidity (as a decimal). The difference (es - ea) represents the atmosphere's drying power — higher deficits indicate greater evaporative demand.
  4. Determine psychrometric constants: Calculate the slope of the vapor pressure curve (Δ) and the psychrometric constant (γ). These values adjust the formula for local atmospheric conditions and temperature sensitivity. At sea level, γ ≈ 0.067 kPa/°C; at 1,500 meters elevation, it drops to approximately 0.056 kPa/°C.
  5. Apply the Penman-Monteith equation: Insert all calculated values into the FAO-56 formula. The numerator combines radiation-driven evaporation (first term) and aerodynamic-driven transpiration (second term). The denominator normalizes the result based on energy partitioning between sensible and latent heat.
  6. Adjust for crop-specific needs: Multiply ET₀ by the crop coefficient (Kc) to get actual crop evapotranspiration (ETc). For example, corn at mid-season has Kc = 1.20, so ETc = 9.5 × 1.20 = 11.4 mm/day. Kc values vary by crop type and growth stage — seedlings have lower coefficients than mature plants.

4 Common Evapotranspiration Calculation Mistakes

  • Using air temperature alone to estimate ET: Some simplified methods rely only on temperature (like Hargreaves equation), but these can deviate by 30-50% from Penman-Monteith results in humid or windy conditions. Temperature-based formulas ignore humidity's critical role — 35°C at 20% humidity produces twice the evaporative demand of 35°C at 80% humidity.
  • Ignoring wind speed measurement height: Wind speed must be measured or adjusted to 2 meters above the crop canopy. Anemometers mounted at 10 meters (standard weather station height) record speeds 40-60% higher than at crop level. Use the logarithmic wind profile equation to adjust: u₂ = u₁₀ × (2/10)^0.14.
  • Applying wrong crop coefficients: Kc values change throughout the growing season. Using mid-season Kc (1.20 for corn) during the initial growth stage (Kc = 0.30) causes 300% overwatering. FAO-56 provides Kc curves for 80+ crops across four growth stages: initial, development, mid-season, and late season.
  • Neglecting effective rainfall: Irrigation scheduling should subtract effective rainfall from ETc. A 15 mm rain event doesn't mean 15 mm less irrigation — some water runs off or percolates below root zones. Effective rainfall typically equals 70-80% of total rainfall for light soils, 85-90% for clay soils.

5 Pro Tips for Accurate Evapotranspiration Calculations

  • Install an on-site weather station: Even a basic $500 station measuring temperature, humidity, and rainfall improves ET accuracy by 25-40% compared to regional airport data. Place sensors 2 meters above the crop canopy, away from buildings and trees that create microclimates. Solar radiation sensors cost extra but reduce ET uncertainty from ±20% to ±10%.
  • Use cumulative ET for irrigation scheduling: Track weekly ET totals rather than daily values. Irrigation systems have minimum application rates — applying 3 mm daily wastes energy compared to 18 mm once weekly. Most crops tolerate mild water stress between irrigations if soil moisture stays above 50% available water capacity.
  • Adjust for irrigation system efficiency: Drip systems operate at 90-95% efficiency; sprinklers at 75-85%; flood irrigation at 60-70%. Divide ETc by efficiency to determine actual water needed. If ETc = 10 mm and sprinkler efficiency = 80%, apply 10 / 0.80 = 12.5 mm to compensate for evaporation losses and uneven distribution.
  • Monitor soil moisture to validate ET estimates: Install tensiometers or capacitance probes at 30 cm and 60 cm depths. When soil moisture readings diverge from ET-based predictions by more than 15%, recalibrate your calculations. Discrepancies often reveal incorrect Kc values, clogged emitters, or undetected leaks.
  • Account for mulch and ground cover: Plastic mulch reduces soil evaporation by 40-60%, lowering effective ET by 15-25%. Living mulches (cover crops between rows) increase ET by 10-20% but improve soil health. Adjust Kc downward for plastic mulch (multiply by 0.80) and upward for cover crops (multiply by 1.15).

4 Evapotranspiration FAQs

ET₀ (reference evapotranspiration) represents water loss from a standardized grass surface under specific conditions — it's a climate index independent of crop type. ETc (crop evapotranspiration) = ET₀ × Kc, adjusting for specific crop characteristics. ETa (actual evapotranspiration) is what actually occurs when soil water is limited — ETa equals ETc when irrigation is adequate but drops below ETc during drought stress.

Yes, use the FAO-56 radiation estimation method based on sunshine hours or temperature range. For clear-sky conditions, Rn ≈ 0.75 × (extraterrestrial radiation). Alternatively, the Hargreaves-Samani equation uses only temperature: ET₀ = 0.0023 × Ra × (Tmean + 17.8) × (Tmax - Tmin)^0.5, where Ra is extraterrestrial radiation. Accuracy drops to ±20% versus ±10% for full Penman-Monteith.

Elevation reduces atmospheric pressure, lowering the psychrometric constant (γ) and increasing ET₀ by 5-15% per 1,000 meters. At 2,000 meters (Denver, Colorado), γ = 0.055 kPa/°C versus 0.067 kPa/°C at sea level. The calculator should adjust γ using: γ = 0.000665 × P, where P = 101.3 × ((293 - 0.0065 × z) / 293)^5.26 and z = elevation in meters.

ET varies significantly across short distances due to microclimates. A weather station on a south-facing slope records 15-25% higher solar radiation than one in a valley 500 meters away. Wind exposure, proximity to water bodies, and urban heat islands also create differences. Use your own measurements when possible, or apply elevation and aspect corrections to regional data.

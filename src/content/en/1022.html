What Is Angular Momentum?

Angular momentum measures the quantity of rotational motion an object possesses, combining moment of inertia and angular velocity into a single conserved quantity. A figure skater spinning at 3 rad/s with arms extended (I = 4.0 kg·m²) has angular momentum L = Iω = 12 kg·m²/s. Pulling arms in reduces moment of inertia to 2.0 kg·m², but angular momentum stays constant at 12 kg·m²/s — so angular velocity doubles to 6 rad/s. This conservation law, as fundamental as energy or linear momentum conservation, governs everything from subatomic particle spin to galaxy rotation curves.

Engineers apply angular momentum principles to gyroscope stabilization, satellite attitude control, and vehicle dynamics. A communications satellite (I = 500 kg·m²) spinning at 0.1 rad/s for stability has L = 50 kg·m²/s. To reorient the satellite, reaction wheels spin up internally — conservation of angular momentum means the satellite body rotates opposite to the wheel direction. No external torque is needed; the satellite can point its antennas precisely using only internal angular momentum exchange. Quantum mechanics extends this concept: electrons have intrinsic spin angular momentum of ℏ/2 (where ℏ = 1.055×10⁻³⁴ J·s), determining atomic structure and magnetic properties.

Angular Momentum Formulas With Worked Calculations

For rigid body rotation about a fixed axis:

L = Iω

Where L is angular momentum in kg·m²/s, I is moment of inertia in kg·m², and ω is angular velocity in rad/s.

For a point mass moving in a circle:

L = mvr

Where m is mass in kg, v is tangential velocity in m/s, and r is radius in meters.

More generally, using the vector cross product:

L⃗ = r⃗ × p⃗ = r⃗ × (mv⃗)

Magnitude: L = mvr sin(θ), where θ is the angle between position and velocity vectors.

Torque relates to angular momentum change (rotational analog of F = dp/dt):

τ = dL/dt

For constant torque: ΔL = τΔt (angular impulse)

Practical example: A 0.15 kg baseball thrown at 40 m/s with 0.02 m offset from the bat's rotation axis. Angular momentum about the axis: L = mvr = 0.15 × 40 × 0.02 = 0.12 kg·m²/s. If the bat (I = 0.5 kg·m²) is initially stationary, after impact (assuming elastic collision and ball rebounds at -30 m/s): Lball,final = 0.15 × (-30) × 0.02 = -0.09 kg·m²/s. Change in ball's L: ΔL = -0.09 - 0.12 = -0.21 kg·m²/s. Bat gains +0.21 kg·m²/s: ωbat = L/I = 0.21 / 0.5 = 0.42 rad/s.

For Earth's rotation: I ≈ 8×10³⁷ kg·m², ω = 2π/(24×3600) = 7.27×10⁻⁵ rad/s. L = 8×10³⁷ × 7.27×10⁻⁵ = 5.8×10³³ kg·m²/s. This enormous angular momentum keeps Earth's rotation axis stable — the 23.5° tilt changes only 0.013° per year due to gravitational torques from the Moon and Sun (precession).

6 Steps To Calculate Angular Momentum

  1. Define the reference point or axis: Angular momentum depends on where you measure from — specify the origin or rotation axis clearly. A planet orbiting the Sun has different L measured from the Sun vs. from Earth. For rigid body rotation, use the rotation axis. For orbital motion, use the central body's center. For collisions, use the impact point or center of mass. Draw a diagram showing the axis, position vectors, and velocity directions. In 3D problems, define a coordinate system (x, y, z axes) for vector calculations.
  2. Determine moment of inertia: For rigid body rotation, calculate or look up I for the object's shape and axis. Use standard formulas: solid cylinder I = ½mr², sphere I = (2/5)mr², etc. For composite objects, sum component moments. For point masses, I = mr² where r is perpendicular distance to axis. Example: A dumbbell with two 3 kg masses at ends of a 1 m massless rod, rotating about center: I = 3 × 0.5² + 3 × 0.5² = 1.5 kg·m². About one end: I = 3 × 0² + 3 × 1² = 3 kg·m².
  3. Measure angular or linear velocity: For rotation, measure ω in rad/s (convert from RPM: ω = RPM × 2π/60). For orbital or linear motion, measure v in m/s and note the direction relative to the position vector. A satellite at 7,000 km altitude (r = 13,371 km from Earth center) traveling at 7,500 m/s perpendicular to radius: use L = mvr directly. A car wheel at 600 RPM: ω = 600 × 0.1047 = 62.8 rad/s. Use tachometers, encoders, or video analysis for measurements.
  4. Calculate angular momentum magnitude: Apply L = Iω for rigid rotation or L = mvr sin(θ) for point masses. For a solid disc (m = 5 kg, r = 0.2 m) at 300 RPM: I = ½ × 5 × 0.2² = 0.1 kg·m², ω = 300 × 0.1047 = 31.4 rad/s, L = 0.1 × 31.4 = 3.14 kg·m²/s. For a 2 kg object moving at 10 m/s in a 3 m radius circle: L = 2 × 10 × 3 = 60 kg·m²/s (θ = 90°, sin 90° = 1). If velocity is at 30° to the tangent: L = 2 × 10 × 3 × sin(30°) = 30 kg·m²/s.
  5. Determine angular momentum direction: Use the right-hand rule: curl fingers in the rotation direction, thumb points along the angular momentum vector. For 2D problems, positive L means counterclockwise rotation (vector out of page); negative means clockwise (into page). In 3D, express as a vector: L⃗ = Lxî + Lyĵ + Lzk̂. For a wheel rotating in the xy-plane about the z-axis at +10 rad/s with I = 2 kg·m²: L⃗ = 20k̂ kg·m²/s. Direction matters for vector addition — two counter-rotating flywheels with equal L have total L = 0.
  6. Apply conservation laws: In isolated systems (no external torque), total angular momentum is conserved: Linitial = Lfinal. Use this to solve collision and interaction problems. A stationary turntable (I = 2 kg·m²) catches a 0.5 kg·m²/s spinning disc: final combined I = 2.5 kg·m², L = 0.5 kg·m²/s, so ωfinal = 0.5/2.5 = 0.2 rad/s. For variable-I systems (skater pulling arms in): I₁ω₁ = I₂ω₂. Initial ω₁ = 2 rad/s, I₁ = 4 kg·m², I₂ = 2 kg·m²: ω₂ = 4×2/2 = 4 rad/s.

4 Practical Angular Momentum Examples

Example 1 — Gyroscope Stabilization: A ship stabilizer gyroscope (solid cylinder, m = 50,000 kg, r = 2 m) spins at 1,000 RPM. I = ½ × 50,000 × 2² = 100,000 kg·m². ω = 1,000 × 0.1047 = 104.7 rad/s. L = 100,000 × 104.7 = 1.047×10⁷ kg·m²/s. When the ship rolls (rotates about longitudinal axis), the gyroscope resists due to conservation of angular momentum. To change the gyroscope's axis by 1° requires torque τ = dL/dt. For a 1° (0.0175 rad) precession in 10 seconds: τ = L × dθ/dt = 1.047×10⁷ × 0.00175 = 18,300 N·m. This enormous resisting torque stabilizes the ship — passengers barely feel waves that would otherwise cause 10-15° rolling.

Example 2 — Ice Skater Spin: A skater (mass 55 kg) begins a spin with arms and one leg extended (I₁ = 5.2 kg·m²) at 1.8 rad/s. Pulling into a tight tuck reduces moment of inertia to I₂ = 1.8 kg·m². Conservation: I₁ω₁ = I₂ω₂. ω₂ = 5.2 × 1.8 / 1.8 = 5.2 rad/s — nearly triples rotation speed. Initial rotational KE: KE₁ = ½ × 5.2 × 1.8² = 8.42 J. Final KE: KE₂ = ½ × 1.8 × 5.2² = 24.34 J. The 15.92 J increase comes from work done by the skater's muscles pulling limbs inward against centrifugal effects. Elite skaters achieve 6-7 revolutions in 2 seconds (18-22 rad/s) starting from ~2 rad/s entry speed — an I ratio of 9:1 to 11:1.

Example 3 — Satellite Attitude Control: A 1,200 kg satellite (I = 800 kg·m² about yaw axis) uses reaction wheels for pointing. Each wheel (Iw = 0.5 kg·m²) can spin at ±6,000 RPM (±628 rad/s). Maximum wheel angular momentum: Lw,max = 0.5 × 628 = 314 kg·m²/s. To rotate the satellite by 90° (π/2 rad), the wheel accelerates from 0 to some speed. Conservation: Isatωsat + Iwωw = 0. For a 0.01 rad/s satellite rotation: ωw = -Isatωsat/Iw = -800 × 0.01 / 0.5 = -16 rad/s. The wheel spins at -16 rad/s while the satellite rotates at +0.01 rad/s. To stop satellite rotation, decelerate the wheel back to zero. Three orthogonal wheels control pitch, yaw, and roll independently.

Example 4 — Neutron Star Spin-Up: A massive star's core (initial radius 100,000 km, rotation period 100 days) collapses to a neutron star (radius 10 km). Initial I₁ ∝ r₁² = (10⁸ m)² = 10¹⁶ m². Final I₂ ∝ r₂² = (10⁴ m)² = 10⁸ m². Ratio I₁/I₂ = 10⁸. Conservation: I₁ω₁ = I₂ω₂, so ω₂ = ω₁ × 10⁸. Initial ω₁ = 2π/(100 × 24 × 3600) = 7.27×10⁻⁷ rad/s. Final ω₂ = 7.27×10⁻⁷ × 10⁸ = 72.7 rad/s — period = 2π/72.7 = 0.086 seconds (86 ms). This matches observed millisecond pulsars. The fastest known pulsar (PSR J1748-2446ad) spins at 716 Hz (4,500 rad/s) — matter at its equator moves at 0.24c, nearing the breakup limit where centrifugal force exceeds gravity.

4 Common Angular Momentum Mistakes

Mistake 1 — Confusing Angular and Linear Momentum: Angular momentum (L = Iω, units kg·m²/s) and linear momentum (p = mv, units kg·m/s) are distinct conserved quantities. A car moving at 30 m/s has p = 1,500 × 30 = 45,000 kg·m/s linear momentum. About a point 10 m from its path, it has L = mvr = 1,500 × 30 × 10 = 450,000 kg·m²/s angular momentum. They can't be added or compared directly — different units, different conservation conditions. Linear momentum is conserved when net external force is zero; angular momentum when net external torque is zero. A force through the axis changes p but not L; a tangential force changes both.

Mistake 2 — Forgetting That L Depends on Reference Point: Angular momentum is not absolute — it depends on where you place the origin. A particle moving in a straight line has L = 0 about any point on that line (r and v are parallel, cross product is zero). About a point 5 m from the line, the same particle has L = mvr = 2 × 10 × 5 = 100 kg·m²/s. Both are correct; they describe rotation about different points. In collisions, choose the impact point or system center of mass as the reference — external forces at that point produce zero torque, so L is conserved. Using a different origin introduces external torque terms that complicate the analysis.

Mistake 3 — Ignoring Vector Direction: Angular momentum is a vector quantity — direction matters for addition. Two identical flywheels (L = 100 kg·m²/s each) spinning in opposite directions have total L = 100 - 100 = 0, not 200. A common error is adding magnitudes without considering direction. In 3D, use vector components: L⃗₁ = (50, 0, 0), L⃗₂ = (0, 50, 0), total L⃗ = (50, 50, 0), magnitude = √(50² + 50²) = 70.7 kg·m²/s — not 100. Gyroscopic precession direction follows from τ⃗ = dL⃗/dt — torque perpendicular to L causes L to rotate toward the torque direction, not align with it.

Mistake 4 — Assuming Conservation When External Torques Exist: Angular momentum is conserved only when net external torque is zero. A spinning top slows down because gravity and the normal force create a torque (weight acts at center of mass, normal force at the contact point — not collinear, so there's a torque). Earth's rotation slows (day lengthens by 1.7 ms per century) because tidal friction from the Moon exerts an external torque. In a car crash, angular momentum about the impact point is conserved during the collision (forces at that point produce zero torque), but friction with the road afterward provides external torque that changes L. Always identify all external torques before applying conservation.

4 Professional Tips for Angular Momentum Applications

Tip 1 — Use Angular Momentum for Collision Analysis: In impacts involving rotation, angular momentum conservation often simplifies problems that would be complex with forces. A bullet (m = 0.01 kg, v = 500 m/s) embeds in a door (I = 10 kg·m², width 1 m) at the edge. About the hinge: Linitial = mvr = 0.01 × 500 × 1 = 5 kg·m²/s. After impact, bullet + door rotate together: Itotal = 10 + 0.01 × 1² = 10.01 kg·m². ω = L/I = 5/10.01 = 0.499 rad/s. Door edge speed: v = ωr = 0.499 × 1 = 0.499 m/s. This approach avoids calculating impact forces (which vary unpredictably during the millisecond collision).

Tip 2 — Design Reaction Wheel Systems for Satellites: Size reaction wheels based on required satellite slew rate and disturbance torques. For a 500 kg satellite (I = 400 kg·m²) needing 0.1 rad/s slew rate: required wheel L = Isatωsat = 400 × 0.1 = 40 kg·m²/s. A wheel with Iw = 0.3 kg·m² must reach ωw = 40/0.3 = 133 rad/s (1,270 RPM). Add margin for disturbance torques (gravity gradient, solar pressure, magnetic): typically 2-3×. Three wheels (one per axis) provide full 3-axis control. A fourth wheel in a skewed orientation provides redundancy — any three can control all axes if one fails. Momentum dumping (using magnetic torquers or thrusters) prevents wheel saturation.

Tip 3 — Analyze Orbital Mechanics with Angular Momentum: For satellites and planets, specific angular momentum h = r × v (per unit mass) is conserved in Keplerian orbits. At periapsis (closest approach) and apoapsis (farthest), velocity is perpendicular to radius, so h = rpvp = rava. For Earth orbit: rp = 6,771 km (400 km altitude), ra = 42,164 km (geostationary). Velocity ratio: vp/va = ra/rp = 6.23. If vp = 10 km/s, then va = 1.6 km/s. This relationship determines orbital transfer requirements — the Hohmann transfer from LEO to GEO requires Δv = 3.9 km/s at periapsis plus 1.5 km/s at apoapsis.

Tip 4 — Implement Control Moment Gyroscopes: For large spacecraft (ISS, space telescopes), control moment gyroscopes (CMGs) provide higher torque than reaction wheels. A CMG has a constant-speed rotor (Lrotor fixed) mounted on a gimbal. Rotating the gimbal at rate ωg produces output torque τ = Lrotor × ωg. The ISS uses four CMGs, each with Lrotor = 4,500 kg·m²/s. Gimballing at 0.1 rad/s produces τ = 450 N·m per CMG — enough to rotate the 420,000 kg station. CMGs can saturate (gimbals reach limits), requiring momentum dumping via thrusters. The James Webb Space Telescope uses reaction wheels (smaller, no saturation from internal momentum exchange) for ultra-stable pointing.

4 Angular Momentum FAQs

Gravity tries to rotate the top downward, creating a torque perpendicular to the spin axis. Instead of falling, the top precesses — its axis rotates horizontally. The torque τ = r × mg changes angular momentum: dL⃗/dt = τ⃗. Since τ is horizontal (perpendicular to L which points along the spin axis), L rotates horizontally rather than tilting down. Precession rate: Ω = τ/L = mgr/(Iω). A fast-spinning top (large L) precesses slowly; as friction slows ω, L decreases and precession speeds up until the top becomes unstable and falls.

Yes — angular momentum is conserved in all collisions (elastic or inelastic) as long as net external torque is zero. Kinetic energy is not conserved in inelastic collisions (some converts to heat, sound, deformation), but angular momentum always is. Example: A stationary disc (I = 2 kg·m²) is dropped onto a spinning disc (I = 2 kg·m², ω = 10 rad/s). After friction locks them together: Itotal = 4 kg·m². Conservation: 2 × 10 = 4 × ωfinal, so ωfinal = 5 rad/s. Initial KE = 100 J, final KE = 50 J — half lost to heat. But Linitial = Lfinal = 20 kg·m²/s exactly.

Cats use angular momentum conservation and body flexibility. Dropped upside-down with zero initial L, the cat can't create net rotation — but it can redistribute L internally. The cat bends at the waist, creating two body segments. It rotates the front half one way while rotating the rear half the opposite way (keeping total L = 0). By extending/retracting legs, the cat changes each segment's moment of inertia, controlling rotation rates (I₁ω₁ = I₂ω₂). The sequence: (1) tuck front legs, extend rear legs, rotate front body toward ground; (2) extend front legs, tuck rear legs, rotate rear body. Net result: 180° reorientation with zero net angular momentum. Takes ~0.3 seconds — minimum fall height ~50 cm.

Electrons have two types of angular momentum: orbital (from motion around nucleus) and spin (intrinsic quantum property). Orbital angular momentum is quantized: L = √[ℓ(ℓ+1)]ℏ, where ℓ = 0, 1, 2,... (s, p, d orbitals) and ℏ = 1.055×10⁻³⁴ J·s. For ℓ = 1 (p orbital): L = √2 ℏ = 1.49×10⁻³⁴ J·s. Spin angular momentum: S = √[s(s+1)]ℏ where s = 1/2 for electrons. S = √(3/4)ℏ = 0.91×10⁻³⁴ J·s. Spin projection along any axis is ±ℏ/2 = ±5.27×10⁻³⁵ J·s ("spin up" or "spin down"). This intrinsic spin gives electrons magnetic moments, enabling MRI, hard drive storage, and quantum computing.

What is Net Salary Calculator?

The Net Salary Calculator transforms a gross salary into the actual take-home pay you receive after all mandatory deductions — federal and state income tax, Social Security and Medicare (FICA), retirement contributions, health insurance premiums, and other withholdings. Most job offers quote gross annual salary, but your actual spending power is determined by your net pay, which can be significantly lower. A $100,000 gross salary in California yields approximately $65,000–$70,000 net, while the same salary in Texas (no state income tax) yields roughly $72,000–$76,000. Understanding your net pay is essential for budgeting, negotiating salaries, comparing job offers across states or countries, and making informed career decisions. This calculator handles federal and state tax brackets, standard and itemized deductions, pre-tax contributions (401k, HSA, FSA), and post-tax deductions, giving you a complete picture of where every dollar of your paycheck goes.

How Net Salary Calculation Works: The Formula Explained

Net salary = Gross pay - Federal income tax - State income tax - FICA (Social Security + Medicare) - Pre-tax deductions - Post-tax deductions. Federal income tax uses progressive brackets (2024): 10% on income up to $11,600 (single), 12% up to $47,150, 22% up to $100,525, 24% up to $191,950, 32% up to $243,725, 35% up to $609,350, and 37% above that. Each bracket only applies to income within that range — your effective rate is always lower than your top bracket rate. A single filer earning $100,000 pays approximately $14,768 in federal income tax (effective rate 14.8%, not 22%). FICA consists of Social Security (6.2% on wages up to $168,600 in 2024) and Medicare (1.45% on all wages, plus 0.9% Additional Medicare Tax on wages over $200,000). State income tax varies widely: 0% in Texas, Florida, Nevada, and 7 other states; up to 13.3% in California (the highest). Pre-tax deductions (401k, HSA, health insurance premiums) reduce taxable income before tax calculations. Post-tax deductions (Roth 401k, garnishments, union dues) are taken after taxes.

Step-by-Step Guide to Using This Calculator

  1. Enter your gross salary: Input your annual gross pay as stated in your offer letter or employment contract. The calculator converts this to biweekly, semimonthly, and monthly equivalents automatically.
  2. Select your filing status: Single, Married Filing Jointly, Married Filing Separately, or Head of Household. This determines your tax brackets and standard deduction amount. Head of Household provides the most favorable brackets for single parents.
  3. Choose your state: Select your work state for state income tax calculations. If you work remotely in a different state than your employer, you may owe taxes in both — consult a tax professional for multi-state situations.
  4. Add pre-tax deductions: Enter your 401(k) contribution percentage (up to $23,000 for 2024, $30,500 if age 50+), HSA contributions ($4,150 individual / $8,300 family), and health insurance premiums (typically $1,500–$8,000/year for employee share).
  5. Add post-tax deductions: Enter any Roth 401(k) contributions, charity donations through payroll, union dues, or wage garnishments.
  6. Review the results: The calculator shows gross pay, each deduction category with amounts, and net pay. It also displays your effective tax rate and marginal tax rate, plus a pie chart of where your money goes.

FICA: Social Security $7,429.20 (6.2% up to cap) + Medicare $1,740 (1.45%) = $9,169.20. 401(k) contribution at 10%: $12,000 (reduces taxable income). Health insurance premium: $3,600/year. Net annual pay: approximately $76,410. Effective federal tax rate: 15.7%. Monthly take-home: $6,368.

Example 2 — Married Marketing Manager in California: Gross salary: $95,000 (household income $170,000, filing jointly). Federal tax on $170,000 (standard deduction $29,200): approximately $22,521. California state tax (on $95,000): approximately $5,800. FICA: $5,890 + $1,377.50 = $7,267.50. 401(k) at 6%: $5,700. Health insurance: $4,800. Net annual pay on the $95,000 salary: approximately $63,633. Effective combined tax rate: ~28%. Monthly take-home: $5,303.

Example 3 — Comparing Job Offers Across States: A $110,000 offer in New York vs. $100,000 in Florida. NY: Federal tax ~$15,500, NY state tax ~$5,300, NYC tax ~$2,800, FICA ~$7,430. Net: ~$78,970. FL: Federal tax ~$14,000, state tax $0, FICA ~$7,430. Net: ~$78,570. The $10,000 higher gross in NY yields only $400 more net — virtually identical take-home pay. When considering cost of living, the Florida offer is significantly better since NYC housing costs 80%+ more than Florida averages.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Confusing marginal and effective tax rates: Your marginal rate is the rate on your last dollar earned (e.g., 22%). Your effective rate is the average rate on all your income (often 12–16%). A raise from $95,000 to $100,000 does not push all your income into the 24% bracket — only the dollars between $100,525 and your actual income are taxed at 24%.
  • Forgetting FICA: Social Security (6.2%) and Medicare (1.45%) add 7.65% to your tax burden. On a $100,000 salary, that is $7,650 that many people forget to account for when estimating take-home pay.
  • Not maximizing pre-tax deductions: Every dollar you contribute to a 401(k) or HSA reduces your taxable income by a dollar but only reduces your take-home pay by $0.78 (in the 22% bracket). A $23,000 401(k) contribution might only reduce your net pay by $17,940 — you save $5,060 in taxes while still investing $23,000.
  • Ignoring local taxes: Some cities impose additional income taxes: New York City (3.07%), Philadelphia (3.75%), Columbus (2.5%). These can significantly reduce take-home pay.
  • Budgeting based on gross salary: A $75,000 salary sounds comfortable until you see $5,000/month take-home after all deductions. Always budget based on net pay.

Pro Tips for Better Results

  • Max your 401(k) match first: If your employer matches 50% up to 6%, contributing 6% gives you a 3% free contribution. That is an immediate 50% return on your contribution — far better than any investment.
  • Use an HSA as a triple tax advantage: Contributions are tax-deductible, growth is tax-free, and withdrawals for medical expenses are tax-free. After age 65, it functions like a traditional IRA for non-medical withdrawals. The 2024 limit is $4,150 (individual) or $8,300 (family).
  • Consider Roth vs. Traditional 401(k): If you are early in your career and in a low bracket (12–22%), Roth 401(k) contributions are often better — you pay tax now at a low rate and all growth is tax-free. If you are peak earning years (32%+ bracket), traditional 401(k) deductions save more now.
  • Check your withholding: Use the IRS Tax Withholding Estimator annually. Under-withholding results in a surprise tax bill and potential penalties. Over-withholding means giving the government an interest-free loan. Aim for a small refund ($0–$500).

Frequently Asked Questions

Why is my paycheck less than the calculator shows?

The calculator estimates standard deductions and typical scenarios. Your actual paycheck may be lower due to: employer-specific benefits (life insurance, disability insurance, commuter benefits), local taxes not modeled, garnishments, or different pay period calculations. Some employers pay biweekly (26 pay periods) while others pay semimonthly (24 pay periods), which changes the per-check amount even though annual totals are identical. Compare your first pay stub to the calculator and adjust inputs to match.

How does the Additional Medicare Tax work?

For single filers earning over $200,000, an additional 0.9% Medicare tax applies to wages above that threshold. For married filing jointly, the threshold is $250,000. This is in addition to the standard 1.45% Medicare tax, bringing the total Medicare rate to 2.35% on wages above the threshold. Your employer withholds this automatically once your YTD wages exceed $200,000, but if you have multiple jobs, you may need to request additional withholding on Form W-4.

Does this calculator handle self-employment tax?

The current version calculates W-2 employee withholdings. Self-employed individuals pay both the employee and employer portions of FICA (15.3% total on net earnings up to the Social Security cap), plus income tax on net profit. If you are self-employed, use the calculator as a starting point but add 7.65% to your effective tax rate to account for the employer FICA portion that W-2 employees do not pay directly.

How do state taxes affect my take-home when working remotely?

Remote work creates complex tax situations. Generally, you owe income tax in the state where you physically perform the work. If you live in one state and your employer is in another, you may owe taxes to both states. Most states have reciprocity agreements or credits for taxes paid to other states, preventing double taxation. However, "convenience of employer" rules in states like New York and Connecticut can tax remote workers based on the employer's location, even if you never set foot in that state. Always consult a tax professional for multi-state remote work situations.

What Is Pressure Altitude?

Pressure altitude is the height above the standard datum plane — a theoretical level where atmospheric pressure equals 29.92 inches of mercury (1013.25 hPa). An aircraft flying at what the altimeter indicates as 10,000 feet with the altimeter set to 29.92" Hg is at 10,000 feet pressure altitude, regardless of actual sea-level pressure. This standardization allows all aircraft to use the same vertical separation (1,000-foot intervals above 18,000 feet in the US), preventing mid-air collisions. At Denver International Airport (field elevation 5,431 feet), a typical high-pressure day (30.25" Hg) produces pressure altitude of 5,132 feet — 299 feet lower than field elevation. On a low-pressure storm day (29.50" Hg), pressure altitude becomes 5,731 feet — 300 feet higher.

Pilots use pressure altitude to calculate aircraft performance, determine true altitude, and set flight levels. High pressure altitude (from low atmospheric pressure or high temperature) reduces engine power, propeller efficiency, and wing lift — a fully loaded Cessna 172 that climbs at 700 feet per minute at sea level may climb at only 200 feet per minute at 8,000 feet pressure altitude. "Density altitude" combines pressure altitude with temperature to predict actual aircraft performance. On a hot day (35°C/95°F) at Denver, density altitude can exceed 9,000 feet — the aircraft performs as if it were at 9,000 feet elevation, not 5,431 feet. Understanding pressure altitude is critical for safe takeoff, climb, and landing calculations.

Pressure Altitude Formula With Worked Calculations

Basic pressure altitude from altimeter setting:

PA = Elevation + (29.92 - Altimeter_Setting) × 1,000 (feet, inches Hg)

Or in metric units:

PA = Elevation + (1013.25 - Altimeter_Setting) × 30 (feet, hPa)

For precise calculations using the barometric formula:

PA = 145,442 × [1 - (P / 1013.25)0.1903] (feet)

Where P is station pressure in hPa.

Converting pressure altitude to true altitude (approximate):

True_Altitude ≈ PA + (ISA_Deviation × 4 × PA / 1,000)

Where ISA_Deviation is temperature difference from standard atmosphere in °C.

Practical example: An airport at 2,500 feet elevation reports altimeter setting 29.62" Hg. Pressure altitude: PA = 2,500 + (29.92 - 29.62) × 1,000 = 2,500 + 0.30 × 1,000 = 2,500 + 300 = 2,800 feet. The aircraft performs as if it were at 2,800 feet, not 2,500 feet. If temperature is 30°C (ISA standard at 2,500 feet is 10°C, so ISA deviation = +20°C): density altitude ≈ 2,800 + (20 × 4 × 2.8) = 2,800 + 224 = 3,024 feet. The aircraft performs as if at 3,024 feet — noticeably degraded from the 2,500 feet field elevation.

For a station reporting pressure 950 hPa: PA = 145,442 × [1 - (950 / 1013.25)0.1903] = 145,442 × [1 - 0.93760.1903] = 145,442 × [1 - 0.9876] = 145,442 × 0.0124 = 1,803 feet. This station's pressure altitude is 1,803 feet. If the station's actual elevation is 1,650 feet, the altimeter setting would be approximately 29.92 + (1,803 - 1,650)/1,000 = 29.92 + 0.15 = 30.07" Hg.

6 Steps To Calculate Pressure Altitude

  1. Obtain the current altimeter setting: Get the altimeter setting (also called QNH or barometric pressure corrected to sea level) from ATIS, AWOS, ASOS, or flight service. Format is inches of mercury (US) or hectopascals/millibars (most other countries). Example: "Altimeter two niner niner two" = 29.92" Hg. "QNH one zero one three" = 1013 hPa. Verify the reading is current — pressure changes 1-3 hPa per hour with weather systems. A pressure change of 0.10" Hg (3.4 hPa) changes pressure altitude by 100 feet — significant for terrain clearance.
  2. Determine field elevation: Find the airport or location elevation above mean sea level (MSL). Check sectional charts, airport/facility directory, or GPS. Example: KDEN (Denver International) = 5,431 feet MSL. KLAS (Las Vegas) = 2,181 feet MSL. KSEA (Seattle-Tacoma) = 433 feet MSL. Elevation is fixed — it doesn't change with weather. Pressure altitude varies around this fixed elevation as atmospheric pressure changes. High pressure systems lower pressure altitude; low pressure systems raise it.
  3. Apply the pressure altitude formula: PA = Elevation + (29.92 - Altimeter) × 1,000. For KLAS with altimeter 30.15" Hg: PA = 2,181 + (29.92 - 30.15) × 1,000 = 2,181 + (-0.23) × 1,000 = 2,181 - 230 = 1,951 feet. The pressure altitude is 230 feet below field elevation due to high pressure. For KDEN with altimeter 29.45" Hg: PA = 5,431 + (29.92 - 29.45) × 1,000 = 5,431 + 470 = 5,901 feet — 470 feet above field elevation due to low pressure. In metric: PA = Elevation + (1013.25 - Altimeter_hPa) × 30.
  4. Calculate density altitude for performance: Density altitude = PA + [120 × (OAT - ISA_Temperature)]. OAT is outside air temperature in °C. ISA temperature at sea level = 15°C, decreasing 2°C per 1,000 feet. At PA = 5,000 feet: ISA_temp = 15 - (2 × 5) = 5°C. If OAT = 25°C: DA = 5,000 + [120 × (25 - 5)] = 5,000 + 2,400 = 7,400 feet. The aircraft performs as if at 7,400 feet — 2,400 feet higher than pressure altitude due to warm air. Cold day (OAT = -10°C): DA = 5,000 + [120 × (-10 - 5)] = 5,000 - 1,800 = 3,200 feet — better performance than pressure altitude suggests.
  5. Set the altimeter correctly: Below 18,000 feet MSL (in the US), set altimeter to local QNH — altimeter displays true altitude MSL. At or above 18,000 feet (Class A airspace), set altimeter to 29.92" Hg — altimeter displays pressure altitude (called "flight level"). FL350 = 35,000 feet pressure altitude. Transition altitude varies by country: US = 18,000 feet, Europe = typically 3,000-6,000 feet, Japan = 14,000 feet. When climbing through transition altitude, set 29.92. When descending through transition level, set local QNH. Incorrect altimeter settings cause altitude errors — 0.10" Hg error = 100 feet altitude error.
  6. Verify terrain and obstacle clearance: True altitude can differ from indicated altitude due to non-standard temperature. In cold air, true altitude is lower than indicated — "From high to low, look out below." At -20°C OAT and indicated 10,000 feet, true altitude may be 9,400 feet — 600 feet lower. This is critical for mountain flying and instrument approaches. Apply temperature correction: True_Alt = Indicated_Alt + (Indicated_Alt / 1,000) × (OAT - ISA) × 4 / 10. For IFR approaches in cold temperatures, many countries mandate altitude corrections below certain temperatures (e.g., -10°C or colder).

4 Practical Pressure Altitude Examples

Example 1 — Pre-Flight Performance Calculation: A pilot plans departure from KPHX (Phoenix Sky Harbor, elevation 1,135 feet) on a July afternoon. AWOS reports altimeter 29.85" Hg, temperature 43°C (110°F). Pressure altitude: PA = 1,135 + (29.92 - 29.85) × 1,000 = 1,135 + 70 = 1,205 feet. ISA temperature at 1,135 feet: 15 - (2 × 1.135) = 12.7°C. ISA deviation: 43 - 12.7 = +30.3°C. Density altitude: DA = 1,205 + (120 × 30.3) = 1,205 + 3,636 = 4,841 feet. The Cessna 172S POH shows: at 5,000 feet DA, max gross weight takeoff requires 1,035 feet ground roll, climb rate 470 fpm. At sea level: 805 feet ground roll, 735 fpm climb. Hot day performance is 30% worse. With four passengers and full fuel (2,400 lbs, 100 lbs under max), the pilot calculates climb gradient over nearby terrain — may need to reduce fuel or delay departure until cooler morning hours.

Example 2 — High Altitude Airport Operations: KASE (Aspen-Pitkin County, elevation 7,820 feet) in winter. Altimeter 30.45" Hg (strong high pressure), temperature -5°C. Pressure altitude: PA = 7,820 + (29.92 - 30.45) × 1,000 = 7,820 - 530 = 7,290 feet. ISA temperature at 7,820 feet: 15 - (2 × 7.82) = -0.6°C. ISA deviation: -5 - (-0.6) = -4.4°C (colder than standard). Density altitude: DA = 7,290 + (120 × -4.4) = 7,290 - 528 = 6,762 feet. Cold, high-pressure day gives 1,058 feet better performance than field elevation suggests. A King Air 350 that struggles on summer days (DA > 10,000 feet) performs well in winter. However, cold temperature correction for approach is required: at -5°C and 8,000 feet indicated, true altitude is ~300 feet lower — critical for terrain clearance in mountainous terrain.

Example 3 — Flight Level Assignment: A jet climbs from KDFW (Dallas, elevation 607 feet) to cruise at FL380 (38,000 feet pressure altitude). En route, ATC assigns FL380 — pilot sets altimeter to 29.92" Hg at 18,000 feet during climb. Actual sea-level pressure along route varies: 29.75" Hg in Texas, 30.10" Hg in Colorado. At FL380 with 29.92" set, the altimeter reads 38,000 feet regardless. True altitude varies: over Texas (low pressure), true altitude ≈ 37,830 feet (170 feet lower). Over Colorado (high pressure), true altitude ≈ 38,180 feet (180 feet higher). All aircraft at FL380 have 29.92" set, so they maintain 1,000-foot vertical separation regardless of true altitude variations. This standardization prevents collisions — everyone's altimeter has the same error.

Example 4 — Mountain Flying Safety: A pilot flies through the Rocky Mountains at indicated 11,500 feet MSL (MEA for the airway). Outside temperature is -15°C. ISA temperature at 11,500 feet: 15 - (2 × 11.5) = -8°C. ISA deviation: -15 - (-8) = -7°C (colder than standard). True altitude correction: -7 × 4 × 11.5 = -322 feet. True altitude ≈ 11,500 - 322 = 11,178 feet. Terrain along the route peaks at 11,200 feet — the aircraft is actually 22 feet below the highest terrain! This is why cold temperature corrections are critical in mountains. Solution: add 400-500 feet to indicated altitude when temperature is -15°C. Fly at indicated 12,000 feet to ensure 500+ feet terrain clearance. Many mountain airports have mandatory cold temperature correction tables for instrument approaches.

4 Common Pressure Altitude Mistakes

Mistake 1 — Confusing Pressure Altitude and True Altitude: Pressure altitude is height above the standard datum plane (29.92" Hg); true altitude is height above mean sea level. They're equal only when sea-level pressure is exactly 29.92" Hg. With altimeter 30.25" Hg at sea level, pressure altitude = 0 + (29.92 - 30.25) × 1,000 = -330 feet — negative pressure altitude! The aircraft is "below" the standard datum plane. With altimeter 29.50" Hg: PA = +420 feet. Pilots below 18,000 feet use true altitude (set local QNH); at/above 18,000 feet, everyone uses pressure altitude (set 29.92). Confusing them causes altitude errors — setting 29.92 when you should use local QNH makes your altimeter read pressure altitude, not true altitude, potentially putting you hundreds of feet off your assigned altitude.

Mistake 2 — Ignoring Temperature Effects on Altitude: Altimeters measure pressure, not altitude. Cold air is denser — pressure decreases more rapidly with height in cold air than warm air. At -30°C and indicated 10,000 feet, true altitude is ~1,000 feet lower. The saying "From high to low, look out below; from hot to cold, don't be bold" warns about this. Flying from high pressure (30.20" Hg) to low pressure (29.60" Hg) without updating altimeter: altimeter still reads 10,000 feet, but true altitude dropped 600 feet. Flying from warm air (25°C) to cold air (-10°C) at constant indicated altitude: true altitude drops ~500 feet at 10,000 feet indicated. Always update altimeter when receiving new settings; apply cold temperature corrections in mountainous terrain.

Mistake 3 — Using Wrong Formula for Density Altitude: Density altitude requires both pressure altitude and temperature. Some pilots use elevation instead of pressure altitude, introducing errors. At KDEN (5,431 feet) with altimeter 29.50" Hg: PA = 5,851 feet — 420 feet higher than elevation. Using elevation in DA calculation underestimates density altitude by 420 feet. On a hot day, this could mean the difference between clearing obstacles or not. Correct: calculate PA first, then apply temperature correction. Also use the correct temperature — OAT (outside air temperature), not TAT (total air temperature, which includes ram rise from airspeed). At 200 knots, ram rise is ~3-4°C — significant for DA calculations.

Mistake 4 — Not Setting Altimeter During Cross-Country Flight: Atmospheric pressure changes with weather systems — typically 1-3 hPa (0.03-0.09" Hg) per hour. A 4-hour flight can encounter 0.20-0.30" Hg pressure change (200-300 feet altitude error). ATC provides updated altimeter settings every 15-30 minutes en route. Failure to update: flying into lower pressure without resetting altimeter means you're lower than indicated. The 1974 TWA Flight 514 crash resulted from altitude confusion — crew descended to 1,800 feet indicated, but low pressure meant true altitude was lower, striking terrain at 1,670 feet. Always set the current altimeter when given by ATC; monitor AWOS/ASOS at nearby airports for updates when not in contact with ATC.

4 Professional Tips for Pressure Altitude Applications

Tip 1 — Pre-Calculate Performance Before Flight: Use E6B flight computer or smartphone app (ForeFlight, Garmin Pilot) to calculate pressure altitude and density altitude before departure. Input forecast altimeter setting and temperature for your departure time. If density altitude exceeds your aircraft's certified maximum (often 10,000-15,000 feet), plan alternatives: reduce weight (less fuel, fewer passengers), depart in cooler morning hours, or choose a different route with lower terrain. For mountain airports, calculate climb gradient at expected DA — can you clear surrounding terrain at 500 fpm climb? POH performance charts are based on test conditions — add safety margins (20-30% longer takeoff distance, 20% lower climb rate) for real-world conditions.

Tip 2 — Use Electronic Flight Bags for Real-Time DA: Modern EFBs (ForeFlight, Garmin Pilot, Stratus) display density altitude in real-time using GPS altitude, weather data, and temperature probes. Stratus ADS-B receivers include temperature sensors for accurate DA calculation. Set up alerts: "Notify me when DA > 8,000 feet" for high-altitude operations. Some systems integrate with aircraft sensors (G1000, G3000) to display DA directly on the PFD. This is invaluable for mountain flying — DA can change 500-1,000 feet as you fly from sunlit valleys to shaded canyons with different temperatures. Real-time DA helps decide whether to continue climbing or turn back.

Tip 3 — Understand Altimeter Setting Sources: Different sources provide altimeter settings with varying accuracy. ATIS/AWOS/ASOS at the departure airport is most accurate for takeoff. En route, ATC provides settings from nearby airports — verify the source airport's elevation and distance. A setting from an airport 100 miles away may differ by 0.05-0.10" Hg. Remote AWOS (in mountains) may be outdated — check time of last update. GPS altitude (MSL) can verify altimeter accuracy: if GPS shows 5,500 feet and altimeter shows 5,200 feet with local setting, there's a 300-foot discrepancy — possibly outdated altimeter setting or instrument error. Use GPS as cross-check, not primary altitude source (GPS has vertical error of 100-200 feet).

Tip 4 — Apply Cold Temperature Corrections Systematically: Many countries require cold temperature corrections for instrument approaches when temperature is below published minimums (often -10°C or colder). Canada mandates corrections below -10°C; US recommends corrections below -20°C. Correction tables are in approach chart supplements or calculated as: Correction = (ISA_Deviation × 4 × Altitude_AGL) / 1,000. For an approach with 2,000 feet AGL minimums at -25°C (ISA deviation ≈ -30°C): correction = 30 × 4 × 2 = 240 feet. Add 240 feet to all minimum altitudes — DA becomes 540 feet instead of 300 feet. Some aircraft (Bombardier, Embraer) have automatic cold temperature compensation in their FMS — verify it's activated.

4 Pressure Altitude FAQs

Standardizing on 29.92" Hg (1013.25 hPa) above the transition altitude (18,000 feet in the US) ensures all aircraft have the same altimeter reference, maintaining vertical separation regardless of actual sea-level pressure variations. If each aircraft used local QNH at cruise, two aircraft at "35,000 feet" could have significantly different true altitudes — one in high pressure, one in low pressure — risking collision. With everyone on 29.92", an aircraft at FL350 and another at FL360 are always 1,000 feet apart vertically, even if their true altitudes differ by hundreds of feet. Below 18,000 feet, terrain clearance is priority, so aircraft use local QNH for true altitude. Above 18,000 feet, terrain is not a concern (minimum is 18,000 feet, highest terrain is 20,320 feet in Denali), so separation takes priority.

The simple formula PA = Elevation + (29.92 - Altimeter) × 1,000 is accurate to ±50 feet for typical aviation use (altimeter settings 28.50-31.00" Hg, elevations below 15,000 feet). The barometric formula PA = 145,442 × [1 - (P/1013.25)^0.1903] is more accurate, especially at extreme pressures and altitudes, but requires station pressure (not sea-level corrected pressure). Altimeter setting accuracy is the limiting factor — AWOS/ASOS sensors are accurate to ±0.01" Hg (±10 feet). Human error in reading/copying altimeter settings causes most mistakes. Always verify: "Altimeter three zero zero five" = 30.05, not 30.50. A misplaced decimal creates 450-foot altitude error.

These are ICAO altimeter setting codes. QNH: altimeter setting adjusted to sea level — altimeter reads elevation above MSL when on the ground. Used below transition altitude worldwide. QFE: altimeter setting adjusted to field elevation — altimeter reads zero when on the runway. Used in some countries (Russia, Eastern Europe) for circuit patterns and landings. QNE: standard 29.92" Hg (1013.25 hPa) — altimeter reads pressure altitude. Used above transition altitude globally. In the US: below 18,000 feet use QNH (local altimeter), at/above 18,000 feet use QNE (29.92). QFE is rarely used in the US. Converting: QFE ≈ QNH - (Elevation / 1,000) × 0.03. For 5,000 feet airport: QFE ≈ QNH - 0.15" Hg.

What Is a Specific Energy Calculator?

A specific energy calculator determines the energy content per unit mass of fuels, batteries, food, and other energy storage systems, expressed in joules per kilogram (J/kg) or watt-hours per kilogram (Wh/kg). Gasoline contains exactly 46.4 MJ/kg of specific energy — burning 1 kilogram releases enough heat to lift a 1,000 kg car 4,730 meters vertically (assuming 100% efficiency). This fundamental property explains why liquid hydrocarbon fuels dominate transportation: no battery technology comes close to gasoline's energy density.

You may also find the Voltage Divider Calculator and Electrical Power Calculator useful.

Electric vehicle engineers use specific energy calculations to determine driving range. A Tesla Model 3 Long Range carries a 75 kWh lithium-ion battery pack weighing 480 kg, achieving specific energy of 156 Wh/kg (562 kJ/kg). Compare this to gasoline at 12,900 Wh/kg — batteries store 83 times less energy per kilogram. However, electric motors achieve 90% efficiency versus 25% for internal combustion engines, narrowing the practical gap to 23:1. The 75 kWh battery provides 280 miles of range; an equivalent gasoline car would need only 3.3 kg (4.6 liters) of fuel for the same distance, but the engine, transmission, and exhaust system add weight, partially offsetting the fuel advantage. Understanding specific energy enables realistic comparisons between competing energy storage technologies.

Specific Energy Formula With Calculations

Specific energy is defined as energy per unit mass:

e = E / m

Where e is specific energy (J/kg or Wh/kg), E is total energy (J or Wh), and m is mass (kg). For fuels undergoing combustion:

e = ΔH_combustion / M

Where ΔH_combustion is molar enthalpy of combustion (J/mol) and M is molar mass (kg/mol). For batteries:

e = (V × Q) / m = (V × I × t) / m

Where V is voltage (V), Q is charge capacity (Ah), I is current (A), and t is discharge time (h). For gravitational potential energy:

e = g × h

Where g = 9.81 m/s² and h is height (m). For kinetic energy:

e = ½ × v²

Where v is velocity (m/s). Unit conversions: 1 Wh/kg = 3,600 J/kg = 3.6 kJ/kg; 1 MJ/kg = 277.8 Wh/kg; 1 kcal/g = 4.184 MJ/kg = 1,162 Wh/kg.

Practical example: Calculate specific energy of hydrogen fuel. Combustion reaction: 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O, ΔH = -285.8 kJ/mol (for liquid water product). Molar mass of H₂ = 2.016 g/mol = 0.002016 kg/mol. Specific energy = 285,800 J/mol / 0.002016 kg/mol = 141,800,000 J/kg = 141.8 MJ/kg = 39,400 Wh/kg. Hydrogen has 3× higher specific energy than gasoline (46.4 MJ/kg). However, hydrogen storage is challenging: compressed H₂ at 700 bar has density 42 kg/m³, so volumetric energy density is only 5.96 MJ/L versus 34.2 MJ/L for gasoline. Liquid H₂ (at -253°C) achieves 8.5 MJ/L but requires cryogenic tanks with boil-off losses. The high specific energy of hydrogen is offset by low volumetric density and storage complexity.

For a lithium-ion battery cell: nominal voltage 3.7 V, capacity 3.5 Ah, mass 48 g. Energy = 3.7 V × 3.5 Ah = 12.95 Wh. Specific energy = 12.95 Wh / 0.048 kg = 270 Wh/kg = 972 kJ/kg. This represents the cell level; complete battery packs include cooling systems, structural supports, and battery management electronics, reducing pack-level specific energy to 150-200 Wh/kg (540-720 kJ/kg).

6 Steps To Calculate Specific Energy

  1. Identify the energy storage mechanism: Determine whether energy is stored chemically (fuels, food, batteries), mechanically (flywheels, springs, compressed air), gravitationally (pumped hydro, raised weights), electrically (capacitors, supercapacitors), or nuclear (fission, fusion). Each mechanism has characteristic specific energy ranges: chemical fuels 10-50 MJ/kg, batteries 0.1-1 MJ/kg, capacitors 0.001-0.01 MJ/kg, nuclear fuels 80,000,000 MJ/kg (uranium-235 fission). The mechanism determines which formula applies and what efficiency factors to consider.
  2. Measure or obtain total energy content: For fuels: use bomb calorimetry to measure heat of combustion, or look up standard values (gasoline 46.4 MJ/kg, diesel 45.6 MJ/kg, natural gas 55.5 MJ/kg, coal 24-35 MJ/kg depending on rank). For batteries: discharge at constant current while measuring voltage over time; energy = ∫V(t) × I dt. A 12 V, 50 Ah lead-acid battery discharged at 5 A from 12.6 V to 10.5 V over 10 hours: average voltage ≈ 11.8 V, energy = 11.8 V × 5 A × 10 h = 590 Wh. For food: use Atwater factors (4 kcal/g protein, 4 kcal/g carbohydrate, 9 kcal/g fat, 7 kcal/g alcohol). A 100 g chocolate bar with 50 g carbs, 10 g protein, 35 g fat: energy = 50×4 + 10×4 + 35×9 = 200 + 40 + 315 = 555 kcal = 2,323 kJ.
  3. Measure total mass of energy storage system: Include all components required for energy storage and delivery. For fuels: mass of fuel only (not the tank). For batteries: cell-level (active materials only) or pack-level (including casing, cooling, BMS). For flywheels: rotor mass plus containment vessel. For pumped hydro: only the water mass that moves (not the reservoir structure). Example: hydrogen fuel cell vehicle — fuel mass is H₂ only (not the 700 bar tank weighing 87 kg for 5 kg H₂ capacity). System-level specific energy = 5 kg × 141.8 MJ/kg / (5 kg + 87 kg) = 709 MJ / 92 kg = 7.7 MJ/kg — much lower than fuel alone due to heavy tank. Always specify whether reporting material-level, cell-level, or system-level specific energy.
  4. Apply the specific energy formula: e = E / m. For 1 liter of gasoline (density 0.75 kg/L, energy 34.2 MJ): mass = 0.75 kg, specific energy = 34.2 MJ / 0.75 kg = 45.6 MJ/kg = 12,700 Wh/kg. For comparison, 1 kg of lithium metal (theoretical battery anode) stores 11,600 Wh/kg — similar to gasoline but requires oxygen from air (lithium-air battery). Practical Li-air batteries achieve 500-700 Wh/kg due to inactive components. For a 70 kg human consuming 2,500 kcal/day food: specific energy intake = 2,500 kcal / 70 kg = 35.7 kcal/kg/day = 149 kJ/kg/day. Basal metabolic rate: 70 kcal/hour / 70 kg = 1 kcal/kg/hour = 4.184 kJ/kg/hour — the human body consumes energy at 1.16 W/kg resting, up to 15-20 W/kg during intense exercise.
  5. Account for usable energy and efficiency: Not all stored energy is extractable. Lead-acid batteries: only 50% depth of discharge (DoD) recommended for long life — usable specific energy = 50% × 40 Wh/kg = 20 Wh/kg. Lithium-ion: 80-90% DoD usable — 180 Wh/kg × 0.85 = 153 Wh/kg usable. Gasoline engines: 25% thermal efficiency — 12,700 Wh/kg × 0.25 = 3,175 Wh/kg at wheels. Electric motors: 90% efficiency — 180 Wh/kg × 0.90 = 162 Wh/kg at wheels. After efficiency correction, gasoline still has 20× advantage, but EVs compensate with regenerative braking (recovers 15-20% of kinetic energy) and ability to use lighter chassis (no heavy engine block, transmission, exhaust). For food: human metabolism extracts ~90% of Atwater energy; fiber and resistant starch pass undigested, reducing effective specific energy of high-fiber foods by 10-20%.
  6. Compare to application requirements: Determine if specific energy meets the application's needs. Electric aircraft: target 500 Wh/kg battery for 500 km range (current Li-ion: 250 Wh/kg — insufficient). Electric trucks: 200 Wh/kg adequate for 500 km range with 4-5 tonne battery pack. Smartphones: 250-300 Wh/kg enables all-day use; 500 Wh/kg would allow multi-day battery life. Grid storage: specific energy less critical than cost — lead-acid (40 Wh/kg, €100/kWh) competes with Li-ion (200 Wh/kg, €150/kWh) because weight doesn't matter for stationary applications. Rockets: specific impulse (I_sp) relates to specific energy — liquid hydrogen/oxygen achieves I_sp = 450 s (exhaust velocity 4,410 m/s), requiring fuel specific energy >10 MJ/kg. Match specific energy to application: high specific energy for weight-critical applications (aviation, space, portable electronics); lower specific energy acceptable for weight-insensitive applications (grid storage, ground vehicles with frequent charging).

4 Practical Specific Energy Examples

Example 1 — Electric vs. Gasoline Vehicle Range Analysis: Compare a Tesla Model 3 (75 kWh battery, 480 kg battery mass, 1,847 kg total mass) to a BMW 330i (59 L fuel tank, 44 kg gasoline, 1,570 kg total mass). Tesla: battery specific energy = 75,000 Wh / 480 kg = 156 Wh/kg. Energy consumption: 185 Wh/km (EPA). Range = 75,000 Wh / 185 Wh/km = 405 km. BMW: gasoline specific energy = 46.4 MJ/kg = 12,900 Wh/kg. Fuel energy = 44 kg × 12,900 Wh/kg = 567,600 Wh. Engine efficiency 25%, drivetrain 90%: usable energy = 567,600 × 0.25 × 0.90 = 127,710 Wh. Energy consumption: 8.2 L/100 km = 0.082 L/km × 0.75 kg/L × 46.4 MJ/kg / 0.25 = 11.4 MJ/km = 3,167 Wh/km at wheels... wait, recalculate: 8.2 L/100 km = 0.082 L/km, energy per km = 0.082 × 0.75 × 46.4 / 0.25 = 11.4 MJ/km... this is wrong. Correct: 8.2 L/100 km = 0.082 L/km, mass per km = 0.082 × 0.75 = 0.0615 kg/km, energy per km = 0.0615 × 46.4 = 2.85 MJ/km = 792 Wh/km at wheels (after 25% efficiency). Range = 127,710 Wh / 792 Wh/km = 161 km... this is also wrong. Let me recalculate properly: BMW 330i fuel economy: 7.5 L/100 km combined. Tank: 59 L. Range = 59 / 0.075 = 787 km. Energy analysis: 7.5 L/100 km = 0.075 L/km × 0.75 kg/L = 0.056 kg/km × 46.4 MJ/kg = 2.60 MJ/km fuel energy. At 25% efficiency: 0.65 MJ/km = 180 Wh/km at wheels — similar to Tesla! The BMW achieves 787 km range because it carries only 44 kg of fuel versus Tesla's 480 kg battery. Specific energy advantage (12,900 vs. 156 Wh/kg) enables 11× more energy storage (568 vs. 75 kWh) despite similar power delivery to wheels.

Example 2 — Food Energy for Endurance Athletics: A marathon runner (70 kg) completes 42.2 km in 3:30:00 (210 minutes). Energy expenditure: ~1 kcal/kg/km for running. Total energy = 70 kg × 42.2 km × 1 kcal/kg/km = 2,954 kcal = 12,364 kJ. Average power = 12,364 kJ / (210 × 60 s) = 12,364 / 12,600 = 0.98 kW = 980 W. The runner carries two energy gels (25 g each, 100 kcal/gel = 200 kcal total) and a 500 mL sports drink (140 kcal). Total carried: 340 kcal = 1,423 kJ. Specific energy of gels: 100 kcal / 25 g = 4 kcal/g = 16.7 kJ/g = 16.7 MJ/kg — similar to pure carbohydrate (17 kJ/g). The runner's body stores ~2,000 kcal as glycogen (muscles + liver) and ~100,000 kcal as body fat (15% body fat = 10.5 kg fat × 9 kcal/g = 94,500 kcal). During the marathon: 50% energy from glycogen, 50% from fat oxidation. Glycogen depletion ("hitting the wall") occurs around 30-35 km if pace exceeds glycogen availability. The 340 kcal of carried fuel provides ~10% of total energy need — enough to top up glycogen and prevent depletion. Ultramarathon racers (100 km+) must carry 300-500 kcal/hour to match expenditure, requiring specific energy maximization —坚果 (nuts, 6 kcal/g) and chocolate (5.5 kcal/g) are preferred over gels (4 kcal/g) for longer events.

Example 3 — Grid-Scale Energy Storage Comparison: A utility needs 100 MWh of energy storage for load leveling. Compare technologies by specific energy and cost: (1) Lithium-ion: 200 Wh/kg, €150/kWh. Mass = 100,000 kWh / 0.200 kWh/kg = 500,000 kg = 500 tonnes. Cost = €15 million. (2) Flow battery (vanadium redox): 25 Wh/kg, €200/kWh. Mass = 100,000 / 0.025 = 4,000,000 kg = 4,000 tonnes. Cost = €20 million. (3) Pumped hydro: 0.5 Wh/kg (water lifted 180 m), €5/kWh for civil works. Mass of water = 100,000 / 0.0005 = 200,000,000 kg = 200 million kg (200,000 m³). Cost = €500,000 (excavation and turbines). (4) Compressed air (CAES): 50 Wh/kg (at 70 bar), €80/kWh. Mass = 100,000 / 0.050 = 2,000,000 kg = 2,000 tonnes. Cost = €8 million. Pumped hydro has lowest specific energy but also lowest cost — acceptable for stationary applications where weight doesn't matter. Li-ion dominates portable applications (high specific energy justifies cost). Flow batteries suit long-duration storage (8-12 hours) where cycle life (20,000 cycles) offsets lower specific energy. Specific energy requirements: portable electronics >200 Wh/kg, EVs >150 Wh/kg, grid storage >20 Wh/kg acceptable.

Example 4 — Rocket Propellant Specific Energy: SpaceX Falcon 9 first stage uses RP-1 (refined kerosene) and liquid oxygen (LOX). RP-1 specific energy: 43 MJ/kg. LOX is oxidizer, not fuel — energy comes from chemical reaction. RP-1/LOX combination: specific impulse I_sp = 282 s (sea level), exhaust velocity v_e = I_sp × g = 282 × 9.81 = 2,766 m/s. Kinetic energy of exhaust: ½ × v_e² = ½ × 2,766² = 3,826,000 J/kg = 3.83 MJ/kg of propellant. Chemical energy released: RP-1 + 2.56 O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O, heat of combustion ≈ 13 MJ/kg of propellant mixture (fuel + oxidizer). Efficiency: 3.83 / 13 = 29% converted to kinetic energy; remainder lost as heat in combustion chamber and nozzle. Compare to hydrogen/LOX: I_sp = 366 s (sea level), v_e = 3,590 m/s, KE = 6.44 MJ/kg. Higher specific energy conversion explains why LH₂/LOX is used for upper stages (efficiency critical in vacuum) despite lower density requiring larger tanks. Nuclear thermal rockets (project NERVA): I_sp = 850 s, v_e = 8,340 m/s, KE = 34.8 MJ/kg — no chemical reaction, just heating hydrogen to 2,500 K in nuclear reactor. Specific energy limited by reactor temperature, not chemical bonds. Theoretical fusion rockets: I_sp = 10,000 s, v_e = 98,000 m/s, KE = 4,800 MJ/kg — enabling interplanetary travel in weeks instead of months.

4 Common Specific Energy Mistakes

Mistake 1 — Confusing Specific Energy with Energy Density: Specific energy is energy per unit mass (Wh/kg); energy density is energy per unit volume (Wh/L). They are related by density: energy density = specific energy × density. Gasoline: specific energy 12,900 Wh/kg, density 0.75 kg/L, energy density = 12,900 × 0.75 = 9,675 Wh/L = 34.8 MJ/L. Lithium-ion: specific energy 250 Wh/kg, density 2.5 kg/L, energy density = 250 × 2.5 = 625 Wh/L = 2.25 MJ/L. Gasoline has 52× higher specific energy AND 15× higher energy density. Hydrogen: specific energy 39,400 Wh/kg (highest of any fuel), but density at 700 bar = 0.042 kg/L, energy density = 39,400 × 0.042 = 1,655 Wh/L = 5.96 MJ/L — only 17% of gasoline's volumetric density. This explains why hydrogen vehicles have large fuel tanks despite hydrogen's high specific energy. Confusing these terms leads to incorrect conclusions — "hydrogen has 3× the energy of gasoline" is true by mass but false by volume. Always specify whether comparing mass-based (specific energy) or volume-based (energy density) metrics.

Mistake 2 — Ignoring Inactive Mass in Battery Packs: Cell-level specific energy (active materials only) differs dramatically from pack-level (complete system). A lithium-ion cell: 270 Wh/kg. Battery pack for EV: includes cell casings (5% of mass), cooling system (10%), structural frame (15%), battery management electronics (3%), wiring and connectors (2%). Active cells are only 65% of pack mass. Pack-level specific energy = 270 × 0.65 = 175 Wh/kg. Further reduction for thermal management in extreme climates (heating/cooling energy not available for propulsion). Tesla Model 3: cell-level ~260 Wh/kg (2170 cells), pack-level 156 Wh/kg — 40% reduction. Solid-state batteries promise 500 Wh/kg at cell level, but pack-level may be 300-350 Wh/kg after accounting for inactive components. When comparing battery technologies, ensure consistent basis: cell-level for materials research, pack-level for vehicle design, system-level (including chassis integration) for final product comparison. Marketing claims often cite cell-level numbers; real-world performance depends on pack-level.

Mistake 3 — Not Accounting for Depth of Discharge: Rated battery capacity assumes 100% discharge, but practical operation limits depth of discharge (DoD) to extend cycle life. Lead-acid: 50% DoD recommended — a 10 kWh rated battery provides only 5 kWh usable energy. Effective specific energy = 50% × rated specific energy. Lithium-ion (LFP chemistry): 90% DoD acceptable — 10 kWh rated provides 9 kWh usable. NMC chemistry: 80% DoD typical. Nickel-cadmium: 100% DoD acceptable (no memory effect with modern cells). Example: off-grid solar system requires 20 kWh usable energy. Lead-acid option: 40 kWh rated (€4,000), mass 1,200 kg, specific energy 33 Wh/kg. Li-ion option: 22 kWh rated (€3,300), mass 120 kg, specific energy 183 Wh/kg. After DoD adjustment, Li-ion is cheaper AND lighter despite higher upfront cost per kWh rated. Total cost of ownership: lead-acid lasts 500 cycles (3-5 years), Li-ion lasts 5,000 cycles (15-20 years). Always calculate usable specific energy = rated specific energy × DoD × efficiency when comparing storage technologies.

Mistake 4 — Using Theoretical Instead of Practical Values: Theoretical specific energy assumes ideal chemical reactions with 100% utilization of active materials. Lithium-air battery: theoretical 11,600 Wh/kg (lithium + oxygen from air). Practical demonstrations: 500-700 Wh/kg at cell level — only 4-6% of theoretical. Reasons: incomplete lithium utilization, parasitic reactions, inactive components (current collectors, separator, electrolyte), limited oxygen diffusion. Lithium-sulfur: theoretical 2,600 Wh/kg, practical 400-500 Wh/kg (15-20% of theoretical). Silicon anode: theoretical 4,200 mAh/g vs. graphite 372 mAh/g (11× higher), but silicon expands 300% during charging, causing pulverization. Practical silicon composites: 1,500-2,000 mAh/g (4-5× graphite). Theoretical values are useful for identifying upper bounds and research directions, but practical specific energy determines commercial viability. When evaluating new battery claims, distinguish between: theoretical (materials only, 100% utilization), demonstrated (lab prototype, small scale), and commercial (mass production, quality control). Gap between theoretical and practical typically 5-20× for emerging technologies.

4 Professional Tips for Specific Energy Calculations

Tip 1 — Use Ragone Plots for Technology Comparison: Ragone plots display specific energy (x-axis) versus specific power (y-axis) on logarithmic scales, revealing trade-offs. High specific energy technologies (fuels, batteries) typically have low specific power; high specific power technologies (capacitors, flywheels) have low specific energy. Example data points: gasoline (12,900 Wh/kg, 500 W/kg), Li-ion (200 Wh/kg, 1,000 W/kg), supercapacitor (5 Wh/kg, 10,000 W/kg), flywheel (50 Wh/kg, 5,000 W/kg). Applications map to regions: EVs need 150-250 Wh/kg and 500-1,000 W/kg (Li-ion ideal); power tools need 100 Wh/kg and 3,000 W/kg (high-power Li-ion or NiCd); grid frequency regulation need 10 Wh/kg and 5,000 W/kg (supercapacitors or flywheels). Ragone plots reveal hybrid solutions: combine Li-ion (energy) with supercapacitor (power) for regenerative braking capture (high power needed) and cruising (high energy needed). Bus systems use this hybrid approach, reducing battery size by 30% while improving capture efficiency. Always consider both specific energy AND specific power — the application's power requirement may dictate technology choice even if energy density is adequate.

Tip 2 — Apply Peukert's Law for Battery Discharge: Battery capacity depends on discharge rate — higher currents reduce usable capacity due to internal resistance and diffusion limitations. Peukert's equation: C_p = I^k × t, where C_p is Peukert capacity (constant), I is current, t is time, k is Peukert exponent (1.0-1.4 for lead-acid, 1.05-1.15 for Li-ion). For lead-acid (k = 1.2): rated 100 Ah at 20-hour rate (5 A discharge). At 50 A discharge: t = C_p / I^k = (5^1.2 × 20) / 50^1.2 = (6.90 × 20) / 111.8 = 138 / 111.8 = 1.23 hours. Usable capacity = 50 A × 1.23 h = 61.5 Ah — only 61.5% of rated capacity! Effective specific energy drops proportionally. For Li-ion (k = 1.08): same 100 Ah at 20-hour rate. At 50 A: t = (5^1.08 × 20) / 50^1.08 = (5.87 × 20) / 72.4 = 117.4 / 72.4 = 1.62 hours. Capacity = 50 × 1.62 = 81 Ah — 81% of rated. Peukert effect explains why EV range decreases at high speed (higher power draw) and why starter batteries (high current, short duration) use lead-acid despite poor specific energy — the high surface area of lead plates minimizes Peukert losses at 200-400 A cranking currents.

Tip 3 — Consider Round-Trip Efficiency for Storage: Energy storage systems lose energy during charge/discharge cycles. Round-trip efficiency (RTE) = energy out / energy in. Pumped hydro: 70-85% RTE — pump 100 kWh uphill, recover 70-85 kWh through turbine. Li-ion: 90-95% RTE. Lead-acid: 70-80% RTE. Compressed air: 40-60% RTE (heat of compression lost). Flywheel: 85-90% RTE (bearing friction losses). Hydrogen (electrolysis + fuel cell): 30-40% RTE (electrolysis 70%, compression 10%, fuel cell 50%). For daily cycling, RTE dramatically affects economics. Grid storage cycling daily: 100 kWh input, Li-ion returns 92 kWh, pumped hydro returns 78 kWh, hydrogen returns 35 kWh. Over 10 years (3,650 cycles): Li-ion delivers 335,800 kWh, pumped hydro 284,700 kWh, hydrogen 127,750 kWh. At €0.10/kWh value: Li-ion €33,580 revenue, pumped hydro €28,470, hydrogen €12,775. Hydrogen's low RTE makes it unsuitable for daily cycling despite low storage cost — better for seasonal storage (summer solar to winter heating) where RTE matters less than storage duration capability.

Tip 4 — Factor in Self-Discharge for Long-Term Storage: All energy storage systems lose charge over time through self-discharge. Rates: Li-ion 2-5%/month, NiMH 20-30%/month, lead-acid 5-15%/month, supercapacitor 20-50%/day, flywheel 5-20%/hour (bearing friction), pumped hydro negligible (evaporation only). For emergency backup (infrequent use), self-discharge determines maintenance requirements. UPS system with lead-acid batteries: 10% monthly self-discharge means batteries drop to 50% charge in 7 months without maintenance charging. Manufacturer recommends monthly equalization charge. Li-ion UPS: 3%/month, reaches 50% in 23 months — annual maintenance sufficient. For seasonal applications (snowblower, boat motor), fuel stability matters: gasoline degrades in 3-6 months (oxidation, water absorption), diesel 6-12 months, propane/LPG indefinite (sealed container), batteries require trickle charging. Lithium primary (non-rechargeable) batteries have lowest self-discharge: 0.5-1%/year — suitable for 10-20 year applications (smoke detectors, emergency beacons). Match self-discharge characteristics to use case: daily cycling (self-discharge irrelevant), weekly use (NiMH acceptable), monthly use (Li-ion required), annual use (primary lithium or fuel).

4 Specific Energy FAQs

Antimatter has the highest theoretical specific energy: matter-antimatter annihilation converts 100% of mass to energy via E=mc². Specific energy = c² = (3×10⁸ m/s)² = 9×10¹⁶ J/kg = 25,000,000,000,000 Wh/kg = 25 petawatt-hours per kilogram. One gram of antimatter + one gram matter = 1.8×10¹⁴ J = 50 million kWh — enough to power 5,000 homes for a year. However, antimatter production is impractical: CERN produces ~10¹⁵ antiprotons per year (1.7 nanograms), costing ~€100 billion per gram. Nuclear fusion (deuterium-tritium) achieves 340,000,000 MJ/kg — 8,000× higher than chemical fuels but only 0.4% of antimatter. Fission (uranium-235): 80,000,000 MJ/kg. Chemical fuels max out at ~50 MJ/kg (hydrogen). The gap between chemical and nuclear is 10⁶; between nuclear and antimatter is 10³. Practical energy storage for foreseeable future: chemical (fuels, batteries) for portable applications, nuclear for base-load power, antimatter remains science fiction.

Hydrogen has excellent specific energy (141.8 MJ/kg) but poor volumetric density and high infrastructure costs. Challenges: (1) Storage — 700 bar tanks weigh 87 kg for 5 kg H₂ (6% system efficiency by mass), occupy 150 L volume. Liquid H₂ requires -253°C cryogenics with 1-3%/day boil-off. (2) Production — 95% of H₂ from natural gas reforming (not carbon-neutral); electrolysis from renewable electricity is 3× more expensive than direct battery charging. (3) Distribution — H₂ embrittles pipelines, requires compression to 200-700 bar, energy cost = 10-15% of H₂ energy content. (4) Fuel cells cost €200-500/kW vs. €50/kW for batteries. (5) Round-trip efficiency: electricity → H₂ (electrolysis 70%) → compression (90%) → fuel cell (50%) = 32% overall vs. 90% for batteries. Hydrogen excels for: long-haul trucks (fast refueling, weight critical), shipping (no batteries for transoceanic), seasonal storage (summer solar to winter heat). Batteries excel for: cars (daily charging OK), phones, laptops, grid storage (daily cycling). Each technology fits specific niches based on specific energy, power, cost, and infrastructure requirements.

Food specific energy (caloric density) determines how many calories you consume per gram of food. High specific energy foods: oils (9 kcal/g = 37.7 kJ/g), nuts (6-7 kcal/g), chocolate (5.5 kcal/g), cheese (4 kcal/g). Low specific energy foods: vegetables (0.2-0.5 kcal/g), fruits (0.5-0.8 kcal/g), cooked grains (1-1.5 kcal/g), lean proteins (1-1.5 kcal/g). To lose weight, create caloric deficit while maintaining satiety. Strategy: replace high specific energy foods with low specific energy alternatives. Example: 50 g potato chips (540 kcal/g × 50 g = 270 kcal) vs. 300 g boiled potatoes (0.8 kcal/g × 300 g = 240 kcal) — same calories, 6× more food volume, higher satiety. Volume matters: stomach stretch receptors signal fullness. High-fiber foods (2-4 kcal/g after accounting for indigestible fiber) provide bulk with fewer absorbable calories. Water content also reduces effective specific energy: grapes (0.7 kcal/g, 80% water) vs. raisins (3 kcal/g, 15% water) — same fruit, 4× different specific energy due to dehydration. For weight loss: target average meal specific energy <1.5 kcal/g (6.3 kJ/g); most people naturally reduce intake to 1,500-1,800 kcal/day at this density without hunger.

Solid-state batteries (SSB) replace liquid electrolyte with solid ceramic or polymer electrolyte, enabling lithium metal anodes (theoretical 3,860 mAh/g vs. graphite 372 mAh/g). Advantages: (1) Higher specific energy — 400-500 Wh/kg cell-level (vs. 270 Wh/kg for Li-ion), 250-300 Wh/kg pack-level (vs. 160 Wh/kg). (2) Safety — no flammable liquid electrolyte, no thermal runaway. (3) Faster charging — solid electrolytes tolerate higher current density. Challenges: (1) Manufacturing — solid-solid interfaces have high resistance, requiring high pressure or thin films. (2) Cost — ceramic electrolytes (LLZO, LGPS) require high-temperature sintering; current cost €500-1,000/kWh vs. €100-150/kWh for Li-ion. (3) Cycle life — dendrite formation still occurs, causing short circuits after 500-1,000 cycles (vs. 2,000-5,000 for Li-ion). Timeline: Toyota plans SSB vehicles by 2027-2028 (initially premium models); QuantumScape targeting 2025-2026 for EV applications. SSB will likely complement, not replace, Li-ion: SSB for premium EVs (range >800 km), aviation (weight critical); Li-ion for cost-sensitive applications (grid storage, budget EVs). Both technologies will coexist as specific energy requirements and cost targets diverge across applications.

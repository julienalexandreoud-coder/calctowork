What Is a Torque Converter Calculator?

A torque converter calculator determines the torque multiplication, slip ratio, and efficiency of fluid coupling systems used in automatic transmissions. A typical automotive torque converter with 2.5:1 stall ratio multiplies engine torque from 300 N·m at idle to 750 N·m at stall — enabling a 2,000 kg SUV to accelerate from rest without a clutch pedal. This hydraulic torque multiplication is the key difference between automatic and manual transmissions, allowing smooth launches and automatic gear changes.

You may also find the Parallel Resistance Calculator and Capacitor Energy Calculator useful.

Heavy equipment engineers rely on torque converter calculations for machinery design. A Caterpillar D9 bulldozer (52 tonnes) uses a torque converter with 3.5:1 multiplication ratio — the twin-turbine converter multiplies the diesel engine's 2,050 N·m to 7,175 N·m at the transmission input, providing enough torque to push 15 tonnes of soil per pass. Without torque multiplication, the engine would stall under load. Marine transmissions use torque converters with 4:1 to 6:1 ratios — a tugboat with 8,000 N·m engine torque achieves 32,000-48,000 N·m at the propeller shaft, enabling precise low-speed maneuvering while docking massive vessels. Understanding torque converter behavior enables proper matching of engines to loads, optimization of fuel economy, and prediction of acceleration performance.

Torque Converter Formula With Calculations

The torque ratio (K factor) relates output torque to input torque:

K = T_out / T_in

Where K is torque ratio (typically 1.0-3.5), T_out is turbine (output) torque, and T_in is impeller (input) torque. The speed ratio relates output speed to input speed:

S = N_turbine / N_impeller = N_out / N_in

Efficiency is the product of torque ratio and speed ratio:

η = K × S = (T_out / T_in) × (N_out / N_in) = P_out / P_in

The capacity factor (C factor) relates input torque to input speed:

C = N_in / √T_in or T_in = (N_in / C)²

Where C is typically 50-150 rpm/√(N·m) for automotive converters. Stall speed occurs when output is stationary (S = 0):

T_stall = (N_stall / C)²

Practical example: A torque converter has stall speed 2,200 rpm, stall torque ratio 2.5:1, capacity factor C = 75 rpm/√(N·m). At stall (output stationary, input at 2,200 rpm): input torque T_in = (2,200 / 75)² = (29.33)² = 860 N·m. Output torque T_out = 2.5 × 860 = 2,150 N·m. Efficiency η = K × S = 2.5 × 0 = 0% — all input power converts to heat in the fluid. At coupling point (S = 0.9, K = 1.05): input speed 3,000 rpm, output speed 2,700 rpm. Input torque from engine curve: 650 N·m. Output torque: 1.05 × 650 = 682.5 N·m. Efficiency: η = 1.05 × 0.9 = 0.945 = 94.5% — nearly direct drive. The converter automatically transitions from high-torque multiplication at low speed to high-efficiency coupling at cruising speed.

Power loss (converted to heat) equals: P_loss = P_in - P_out = T_in × ω_in - T_out × ω_out. At stall with 860 N·m at 2,200 rpm: P_in = 860 × 2,200 × 2π/60 = 198,000 W = 198 kW. P_out = 0 (stationary). All 198 kW becomes heat — enough to boil 3 liters of transmission fluid per minute! This is why torque converters require substantial cooling — a heavy-duty transmission cooler dissipates 50-150 kW during hard acceleration.

6 Steps To Calculate Torque Converter Performance

  1. Identify converter specifications: Obtain stall speed, stall torque ratio, and capacity factor from manufacturer data. Automotive torque converters: stall speed 1,800-3,500 rpm (higher for performance applications), stall ratio 1.8:1 to 2.8:1, C factor 60-100 rpm/√(N·m). Heavy-duty converters: stall speed 1,400-2,000 rpm (lower for diesel torque), stall ratio 2.5:1 to 4.0:1, C factor 40-70 rpm/√(N·m). Performance converters: stall speed 3,500-5,500 rpm (match engine power band), stall ratio 2.0:1 to 2.5:1. Without specifications, estimate C factor from similar applications: passenger car 75-90, light truck 60-75, heavy truck 45-60, industrial 35-50.
  2. Determine engine torque curve: Torque converter performance depends on engine torque at each RPM. A V8 engine might produce: 1,500 rpm: 450 N·m, 2,000 rpm: 520 N·m, 2,500 rpm: 580 N·m, 3,000 rpm: 600 N·m, 3,500 rpm: 590 N·m, 4,000 rpm: 560 N·m. Peak torque 600 N·m at 3,000 rpm. The converter's stall speed should match engine peak torque RPM for maximum acceleration — a 3,000 rpm stall converter allows the engine to reach peak torque before the vehicle starts moving. Mismatched stall speed (e.g., 2,000 rpm stall with 3,000 rpm peak torque) causes sluggish launches — engine cannot reach power band before converter couples.
  3. Calculate torque at operating points: For a given input speed, calculate input torque from capacity factor: T_in = (N_in / C)². With C = 75 rpm/√(N·m): at 1,500 rpm: T_in = (1,500/75)² = 400 N·m; at 2,200 rpm: T_in = (2,200/75)² = 860 N·m; at 3,000 rpm: T_in = (3,000/75)² = 1,600 N·m. However, engine torque limits actual input torque — if engine produces only 600 N·m at 3,000 rpm, the converter cannot absorb 1,600 N·m. Actual operating point is where converter capacity equals engine torque. Solve: (N / 75)² = T_engine(N). For T_engine = 600 N·m: N = 75 × √600 = 75 × 24.5 = 1,837 rpm — the engine cannot exceed 1,837 rpm at wide-open throttle with this converter! This indicates C factor is too low (converter too "tight"); increase C to 100 for better match: N = 100 × √600 = 2,450 rpm — engine can now reach 2,450 rpm, closer to peak torque RPM.
  4. Determine torque ratio from speed ratio: Torque ratio K varies with speed ratio S according to converter characteristics. Typical curve: S = 0 (stall): K = 2.5; S = 0.2: K = 2.2; S = 0.4: K = 1.8; S = 0.6: K = 1.4; S = 0.8: K = 1.1; S = 0.9: K = 1.05; S = 1.0 (coupling): K = 1.0. Interpolate for intermediate values. At S = 0.5: K ≈ 1.6 (linear interpolation between S=0.4, K=1.8 and S=0.6, K=1.4). Output torque: T_out = K × T_in. At stall: T_out = 2.5 × T_in. At coupling: T_out = 1.0 × T_in (no multiplication). The torque ratio automatically adjusts based on load — high load (low S) gives high multiplication; low load (high S) gives high efficiency.
  5. Calculate efficiency and power loss: Efficiency η = K × S. At stall (S=0, K=2.5): η = 0%. At S=0.5, K=1.6: η = 0.8 = 80%. At S=0.8, K=1.1: η = 0.88 = 88%. At S=0.9, K=1.05: η = 0.945 = 94.5%. Maximum efficiency occurs near coupling point (S = 0.85-0.95), typically 92-96% for modern converters. Power loss: P_loss = P_in × (1 - η). At 100 kW input, 90% efficiency: P_loss = 100 × 0.10 = 10 kW heat. This heat must be dissipated by transmission cooler — a typical automotive cooler handles 10-30 kW; heavy-duty coolers handle 50-100 kW. Excessive heat (>120°C fluid temperature) degrades transmission fluid, causing slippage and premature wear. Monitor transmission temperature during towing or mountain driving.
  6. Match converter to application: Select stall speed and capacity factor based on use case. Daily driver: stall speed 1,800-2,200 rpm (matches engine torque peak 2,000-2,500 rpm), prioritize fuel economy and smooth shifts. Towing: stall speed 2,000-2,400 rpm (diesel torque peak 1,600-2,000 rpm), higher C factor for better low-end multiplication. Performance: stall speed 3,000-4,000 rpm (match peak power 5,500-6,500 rpm), lower C factor for tighter coupling. Heavy equipment: stall speed 1,400-1,800 rpm (diesel lug RPM), stall ratio 3.0:1 to 4.0:1 for maximum torque multiplication. Marine: stall speed 2,000-2,600 rpm, stall ratio 4:1 to 6:1 for propeller load matching. Incorrect matching causes: stall too low (sluggish acceleration, engine bogs), stall too high (excessive slip, poor fuel economy, transmission overheating), C factor too low (engine cannot reach power band), C factor too high (converter slips excessively, poor highway efficiency).

4 Practical Torque Converter Examples

Example 1 — Automatic Transmission Vehicle Acceleration: A pickup truck (2,500 kg) has a 5.3L V8 engine producing 520 N·m at 2,500 rpm, 355 kW at 5,600 rpm. Torque converter: stall speed 2,400 rpm, stall ratio 2.3:1, C factor = 80 rpm/√(N·m). First gear ratio: 4.0:1, final drive: 3.42:1, tire radius: 0.4 m. At launch (wide-open throttle): engine accelerates to stall speed 2,400 rpm. Input torque: T_in = (2,400 / 80)² = 900 N·m — but engine only produces 480 N·m at 2,400 rpm (from torque curve). Actual T_in = 480 N·m (engine-limited). Turbine torque: T_out = 2.3 × 480 = 1,104 N·m. Transmission output: 1,104 × 4.0 = 4,416 N·m. Wheel torque: 4,416 × 3.42 = 15,103 N·m. Tractive force: 15,103 / 0.4 = 37,758 N. Acceleration: F = m × a, a = 37,758 / 2,500 = 15.1 m/s² = 1.54g — exceeds tire traction! Actual acceleration limited to ~0.6-0.7g (tire slip). As vehicle speeds up, speed ratio increases: at 30 km/h (wheel RPM = 30,000 / (2π × 0.4 × 60) = 199 rpm), transmission output RPM = 199 × 3.42 = 680 rpm, turbine RPM = 680 / 4.0 = 170 rpm, impeller RPM = 2,400 rpm (engine still at stall), S = 170/2,400 = 0.07. Torque ratio still near stall (K ≈ 2.3). At 60 km/h: turbine RPM ≈ 340 rpm, S = 340/2,400 = 0.14, K ≈ 2.1. Converter remains in multiplication range until ~80 km/h, then transitions to coupling. 0-100 km/h time: approximately 6.5 seconds with this setup.

Example 2 — Bulldozer Pushing Force: A Caterpillar D9 dozer has a C18 diesel engine: 522 kW at 1,800 rpm, 2,850 N·m at 1,200-1,600 rpm. Torque converter: twin-turbine design, stall speed 1,650 rpm, stall ratio 3.5:1, C factor = 55 rpm/√(N·m). First gear (forward 1): 5.0:1, final drive: 2.4:1, track radius: 0.5 m. At maximum push (blade fully loaded, vehicle nearly stationary): engine at 1,650 rpm produces 2,850 N·m. Converter input torque: T_in = (1,650 / 55)² = 900 N·m — but engine produces 2,850 N·m, so T_in = 2,850 N·m (engine-limited). Turbine torque: T_out = 3.5 × 2,850 = 9,975 N·m. Transmission output: 9,975 × 5.0 = 49,875 N·m. Final drive output: 49,875 × 2.4 = 119,700 N·m per track. Two tracks: 239,400 N·m total. Tractive force: 239,400 / 0.5 = 478,800 N = 48.8 tonnes of push force! This enables the D9 to push 15-20 tonnes of soil per pass. Without torque multiplication (direct drive, K=1.0): tractive force = 478,800 / 3.5 = 136,800 N = 13.9 tonnes — insufficient for heavy dozing. The torque converter's 3.5:1 multiplication is essential for heavy equipment productivity. Fuel consumption during heavy dozing: 80-120 L/hour, with 40-60% of engine power converted to heat in the converter (requiring massive oil coolers).

Example 3 — Marine Tugboat Bollard Pull: A harbor tug has a 12-cylinder diesel engine: 2,400 kW at 1,800 rpm, 14,500 N·m at 1,400 rpm. Marine torque converter: stall speed 1,500 rpm, stall ratio 5.0:1, C factor = 40 rpm/√(N·m). Reduction gear: 4.5:1, propeller diameter: 2.8 m, pitch: 2.2 m. At bollard pull (tug stationary, pulling maximum load): engine at 1,500 rpm produces 14,200 N·m. Converter input torque: T_in = (1,500 / 40)² = 1,406 N·m — engine-limited to 14,200 N·m. Turbine torque: T_out = 5.0 × 14,200 = 71,000 N·m. Propeller shaft torque: 71,000 × 4.5 = 319,500 N·m. Propeller thrust (simplified): T = (torque × 2π × efficiency) / pitch = (319,500 × 6.28 × 0.65) / 2.2 = 1,309,000 / 2.2 = 595,000 N = 60.7 tonnes of bollard pull. This tug can maneuver 100,000 DWT cargo ships in harbor. Without torque converter (direct drive): bollard pull = 60.7 / 5.0 = 12.1 tonnes — inadequate for large vessel assistance. The 5:1 multiplication ratio is critical for low-speed, high-thrust marine operations. Transmission efficiency at bollard pull: 0% (all power to heat); at cruising (15 knots, S = 0.92, K = 1.03): efficiency = 94%, enabling 25-knot maximum speed with minimal losses.

Example 4 — Drag Racing Torque Converter Selection: A drag racing car (1,200 kg) has a 540 cubic inch V8: 1,100 hp (820 kW) at 7,000 rpm, 1,050 N·m at 5,500 rpm. Converter selection: stall speed must match engine power band for optimal 60-foot time. Option A: 3,500 rpm stall, C = 90. At 3,500 rpm, engine produces ~850 N·m. T_in = (3,500/90)² = 1,512 N·m — engine-limited to 850 N·m. T_out = 2.0 × 850 = 1,700 N·m. Option B: 4,500 rpm stall, C = 85. At 4,500 rpm, engine produces ~950 N·m. T_in = (4,500/85)² = 2,806 N·m — engine-limited to 950 N·m. T_out = 2.1 × 950 = 1,995 N·m. Option C: 5,500 rpm stall, C = 80. At 5,500 rpm, engine produces 1,050 N·m (peak torque). T_in = (5,500/80)² = 4,727 N·m — engine-limited to 1,050 N·m. T_out = 2.2 × 1,050 = 2,310 N·m. Option C provides highest launch torque but requires high RPM before vehicle moves — may cause excessive wheel spin. Option B balances torque and traction. Option A provides softer launch, better for street driving. Racing choice: Option B (4,500 rpm stall) with trans-brake (holds vehicle stationary while engine revs to stall speed). At launch, trans-brake releases, engine already at 4,500 rpm in power band, converter multiplies torque 2.1:1. 60-foot time: 1.1-1.2 seconds with Option B; 1.3-1.4 seconds with Option A. Quarter-mile: 9.5 seconds @ 240 km/h with Option B; 9.8 seconds @ 235 km/h with Option A. Proper converter selection improves ET by 0.3-0.5 seconds — the difference between winning and losing.

4 Common Torque Converter Mistakes

Mistake 1 — Confusing Stall Speed with Stall Torque Ratio: Stall speed (RPM) and stall torque ratio (multiplication factor) are independent specifications. A common error: assuming a 3,500 rpm stall converter has higher torque multiplication than a 2,000 rpm stall converter. In reality: stall ratio depends on blade angles and stator design, not stall speed. A heavy-duty converter (2,000 rpm stall) may have 3.5:1 ratio; a performance converter (4,500 rpm stall) may have 2.0:1 ratio. Stall speed determines at what RPM the engine reaches maximum torque at launch; stall ratio determines how much that torque is multiplied. For towing: low stall speed (1,800-2,200 rpm, matches diesel torque peak) with high ratio (3.0:1 to 4.0:1). For racing: high stall speed (4,000-5,500 rpm, matches gasoline power band) with moderate ratio (1.8:1 to 2.5:1). Selecting based on stall speed alone ignores torque multiplication — critical for heavy loads.

Mistake 2 — Ignoring Capacity Factor Matching: The C factor (capacity factor) determines how much torque the converter absorbs at a given RPM. A "loose" converter (high C factor, e.g., 100 rpm/√(N·m)) absorbs less torque, allowing engine to rev quickly but may slip excessively at cruise. A "tight" converter (low C factor, e.g., 50 rpm/√(N·m)) absorbs more torque, providing better coupling but may prevent engine from reaching power band. Mismatch example: 500 hp engine (650 N·m peak) with C = 50 converter. At 3,000 rpm: T_capacity = (3,000/50)² = 3,600 N·m — far exceeds engine torque. Engine cannot load the converter, bogs down at 2,000 rpm, never reaches power band. Solution: increase C to 85-95. Conversely: 200 hp engine (280 N·m) with C = 100 converter. At 3,000 rpm: T_capacity = (3,000/100)² = 900 N·m — engine can easily spin converter, excessive slip, poor efficiency, overheating. Solution: decrease C to 60-70. Always match C factor to engine torque: C ≈ N_peak_torque / √T_peak. For 3,000 rpm, 600 N·m engine: C = 3,000 / √600 = 3,000 / 24.5 = 122 — too high for most applications; use 80-100 for balance.

Mistake 3 — Overlooking Transmission Cooling Requirements: Torque converters generate substantial heat during slip. Power loss = P_in × (1 - η). At stall with 200 kW engine: P_loss = 200 kW (100% loss). During hard acceleration (average 50% efficiency): P_loss = 100 kW. A typical automotive transmission cooler handles 10-20 kW — insufficient for sustained high-load operation. Consequences: fluid temperature exceeds 120°C, fluid oxidizes and forms varnish, clutch packs glaze, seals harden, transmission fails prematurely. Towing a 3,500 kg trailer up a 6% grade for 10 km: converter generates 50-80 kW heat continuously. Factory cooler (15 kW capacity) cannot keep up — fluid reaches 140°C+, causing damage. Solution: add auxiliary cooler (30-50 kW capacity), monitor transmission temperature gauge, downshift to reduce converter slip. Heavy equipment: coolers sized for 50-100% of engine power (dissipating 200-500 kW). Marine transmissions: seawater-cooled heat exchangers handle 100-300 kW. Always size cooler for worst-case continuous load, not average driving.

Mistake 4 — Assuming 100% Efficiency at Highway Speed: Even at coupling point (S ≈ 0.95-0.98), torque converters have 2-5% slip, resulting in 92-96% efficiency — not 100%. A vehicle cruising at 100 km/h with 50 kW wheel power: converter input power = 50 / 0.94 = 53.2 kW, loss = 3.2 kW heat. Over 100,000 km of highway driving (1,000 hours), energy lost = 3.2 kW × 1,000 h = 3,200 kWh = 320 liters of gasoline (at 10 kWh/L). Lock-up torque converters eliminate this loss by mechanically coupling impeller and turbine above 50 km/h — efficiency jumps to 99%+, improving fuel economy by 3-5%. Older vehicles (pre-1990) without lock-up converters waste 5-8% more fuel than equivalent manual transmissions. Modern 8-10 speed automatics have lock-up in all gears above 2nd, achieving manual-transmission fuel economy. If fuel economy is critical, verify lock-up converter operation — a malfunctioning lock-up clutch (never engages) causes 5-10% fuel penalty and transmission overheating at highway speeds.

4 Professional Tips for Torque Converter Applications

Tip 1 — Use Lock-Up Converters for Fuel Economy: Lock-up torque converters include a friction clutch that engages at cruising speed, eliminating converter slip. Engagement typically occurs above 50-70 km/h in top gear. Fuel economy improvement: 3-5% highway, 1-2% combined. Diagnosis: at steady 80 km/h in top gear, engine RPM should match transmission output RPM × gear ratio × final drive. If RPM is 100-200 higher than calculated, lock-up is not engaging. Common causes: faulty lock-up solenoid, worn clutch friction material, low transmission fluid level, TCM (transmission control module) fault codes. Aftermarket lock-up kits available for older transmissions — retrofit cost €500-1,000, payback period 2-3 years through fuel savings (€200-400/year for 30,000 km/year driving). Heavy-duty towing: lock-up prevents converter overheating on long grades — manually force lock-up in 3rd or 4th gear when climbing sustained grades.

Tip 2 — Match Stall Speed to Engine Power Band: Optimal stall speed equals engine RPM at peak torque for maximum acceleration, or peak power for racing. Street engine (peak torque 2,500 rpm): 2,200-2,500 rpm stall. Racing engine (peak power 6,500 rpm): 5,500-6,500 rpm stall. Diesel (peak torque 1,600 rpm): 1,500-1,800 rpm stall. Too-low stall: engine bogs during launch, cannot reach power band, sluggish 0-60 mph. Too-high stall: excessive slip, poor fuel economy, transmission overheating, vehicle creeps at idle. Test stall speed: hold brake firmly, apply wide-open throttle for 3-5 seconds (do not exceed 5 seconds to prevent overheating), note maximum RPM achieved. If stall speed is 500+ RPM below specification: converter is "tight" (wrong C factor, or engine lacks torque). If stall speed is 500+ RPM above specification: converter is "loose" (wrong C factor, or clutches slipping). Correct stall speed ensures engine operates in optimal RPM range during acceleration.

Tip 3 — Monitor Transmission Fluid Temperature: Install a transmission temperature gauge — fluid temperature is the #1 predictor of transmission life. Normal operating range: 70-100°C. Elevated: 100-120°C (reduce load, add cooling). Dangerous: 120-140°C (immediate action required — downshift, reduce throttle, pull over if persistent). Failure imminent: >140°C (fluid breaks down, seals fail, metal warps). Rule of thumb: every 10°C above 100°C halves transmission fluid life. At 120°C: fluid lasts 25% of normal. At 140°C: fluid lasts 6% of normal. Heavy towing in mountains: fluid can reach 130-150°C without adequate cooling — causing failure in 10-20 km. Solutions: (1) Add auxiliary cooler (doubles cooling capacity). (2) Use synthetic transmission fluid (withstands 20°C higher temperature). (3) Downshift manually (reduces converter slip). (4) Make multiple trips (allow cooling between runs). Temperature monitoring prevents €3,000-6,000 transmission replacements.

Tip 4 — Consider Stator Design for Application: The stator (stationary blade assembly between impeller and turbine) determines torque multiplication characteristics. Three stator types: (1) Standard stator: moderate multiplication (2.0:1 to 2.5:1), good efficiency, suitable for daily driving. (2) High-stall stator: aggressive blade angles, higher multiplication (2.5:1 to 3.5:1), lower efficiency, for towing and heavy equipment. (3) One-way clutch stator: freewheels above coupling speed, reducing drag and improving highway efficiency — used in most modern automotive converters. Performance applications: select stator based on power band. Low-RPM torque engines (big-block V8, diesel): standard stator works well. High-RPM power engines (small-block turbo, race engines): high-stall stator allows engine to reach power band before converter couples. Marine applications: variable-pitch stators adjust blade angle based on load, providing 4:1 multiplication at bollard pull and 95% efficiency at cruising speed — essential for vessels requiring both high thrust and fuel-efficient transit.

4 Torque Converter FAQs

Torque converters typically last 200,000-300,000 km in passenger vehicles with normal driving and proper maintenance (fluid changes every 60,000-100,000 km). Failure modes: (1) Clutch material wear — lock-up clutch friction material wears, causing shudder during lock-up engagement, slippage at highway speed. Repair: replace clutch friction lining or entire converter (€400-800). (2) Stator one-way clutch failure — stator freewheels in both directions, eliminating torque multiplication. Symptoms: poor acceleration from stop, normal cruising. Repair: converter replacement (stator not serviceable separately). (3) Seal failure — internal leaks reduce torque capacity, cause slipping. Symptoms: delayed engagement, high stall speed. Repair: rebuild or replace. (4) Overheating damage — sustained operation above 140°C warps impeller/turbine, degrades fluid, damages seals. Prevention: adequate cooling, temperature monitoring. Heavy-duty use (towing, commercial vehicles): expect 100,000-150,000 km lifespan; budget for replacement as maintenance item. Racing applications: converters rebuilt every 50-100 passes due to extreme heat cycles.

(4) Difficulty parking — high idle creep requires constant brake application. Benefits: only realized if engine is modified to match. A modified 500 hp engine (peak power 6,000 rpm) with 3,500 stall: 0-60 mph improves 0.5-0.8 seconds, quarter-mile improves 0.3-0.5 seconds — worthwhile for racing. Converter shudder during acceleration (typically 50-80 km/h under light throttle) indicates: (1) Lock-up clutch engagement issue — clutch friction material is glazed or contaminated, causing grab-release cycle. Solution: transmission fluid and filter change with friction modifier additive (€150). Severe cases: converter replacement (€600-900). (2) Worn engine/transmission mounts — allow drivetrain movement, felt as shudder. Solution: replace mounts (€300-500). (3) Torque converter imbalance — damaged impeller/turbine blades from debris. Solution: converter replacement. (4) Low transmission fluid — causes air entrainment, spongy engagement. Check fluid level with engine running, transmission warm, in park. Add fluid if low (use correct type — Dexron, Mercon, ATF+4, etc.). Diagnosis: shudder only during lock-up (specific speed range, disappears when throttle changes) = lock-up clutch issue. Shudder at all speeds = mounts or imbalance. Shudder when cold only = fluid viscosity issue (wrong fluid type or low level).

Stall speed test procedure: (1) Warm transmission to normal operating temperature (70-90°C). (2) Park on level surface, apply parking brake firmly. (3) Block front and rear wheels (use wooden blocks or wheel chocks). (4) Start engine, allow idle for 30 seconds. (5) Firmly press brake pedal with left foot. (6) Shift to Drive (D). (7) Quickly press accelerator to wide-open throttle with right foot. (8) Note maximum RPM achieved within 3-5 seconds (DO NOT exceed 5 seconds — risk of overheating and damage). (9) Release throttle immediately, shift to Park, allow idle for 2 minutes to cool. Compare to specification: typical 1,800-2,500 rpm for stock street vehicles, 3,000-4,500 rpm for performance, 1,400-1,800 rpm for diesel/towing. Results: within ±150 rpm of spec = normal. 500+ rpm below spec = engine lacks torque or converter is too tight. 500+ rpm above spec = converter slipping (worn clutches) or too loose (wrong C factor). Repeat in Reverse (R) — stall speed should be similar. Significantly different R vs. D indicates transmission clutch/band issues, not converter problems.

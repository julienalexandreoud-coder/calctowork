What Is Fence Post & Panel Calculator?

Fence Post & Panel Calculator determines the exact number of posts, panels, and concrete bags needed for a complete fence installation. Accurate calculations prevent costly mid-project supply runs and eliminate wasted materials. Whether building a privacy fence, garden boundary, or livestock enclosure, knowing precise material quantities enables accurate budgeting and efficient project planning.

Consider a 20-meter straight fence line with posts spaced every 2 meters. Number of posts equals (Length / Spacing) + 1: (20 / 2) + 1 = 11 posts. The +1 accounts for the starting post. Number of panels equals Length / Spacing: 20 / 2 = 10 panels. Each post requires a hole approximately 30cm diameter × 60cm deep, needing 0.042 m³ of concrete. Total concrete: 11 × 0.042 = 0.46 m³, or about 18 bags of 25kg concrete mix.

Fence calculations become complex with gates, corners, and sloped ground. Gates require double posts (hinge post and latch post) set closer together. Corners need reinforced posts handling tension from two directions. Sloped ground requires stepped or racked panels, affecting post height calculations. This calculator handles these variables, providing complete material lists for straightforward and complex fence layouts.

How Fence Post & Panel Calculator Works: Formulas Explained

Number of posts formula: Posts = (Total Length / Post Spacing) + 1. The +1 includes the starting post. A 30-meter fence with 2.5m spacing: (30 / 2.5) + 1 = 12 + 1 = 13 posts. For fences with gates, add 2 extra posts per gate (one hinge post, one latch post). A 30m fence with one gate needs 13 + 2 = 15 posts total.

Number of panels formula: Panels = Total Length / Post Spacing. Using the same 30m fence with 2.5m spacing: 30 / 2.5 = 12 panels. Panels span between posts, so there's always one fewer panel than posts in a straight run. For fences with gates, subtract gate width from total length first. A 30m fence with 1m gate and 2.5m panel spacing: (30 - 1) / 2.5 = 11.6 → round up to 12 panels.

Post hole concrete volume: Concrete per post = π × (Hole Radius)² × Hole Depth - Post Volume. Typical hole: 30cm diameter (0.15m radius), 60cm deep. Hole volume: 3.14159 × 0.15² × 0.6 = 0.042 m³. Post volume (10cm × 10cm × 0.6m buried): 0.006 m³. Concrete needed: 0.042 - 0.006 = 0.036 m³ per post. In 25kg bags (yield ~0.012 m³ each): 0.036 / 0.012 = 3 bags per post.

Post height calculation: Total Post Length = Above-Ground Height + Buried Depth. Standard practice: bury 1/3 of total post length. For a 1.8m high fence, posts need 1.8m above ground + 0.9m buried = 2.7m total length. Use 3m posts and trim if needed. Corner and gate posts should be 30cm longer for additional stability.

Working through a complete example: A homeowner wants to fence a rectangular garden 25m × 15m with a 1m gate. Perimeter: (25 + 15) × 2 = 80m. Subtract gate: 80 - 1 = 79m of panels. Post spacing: 2m. Number of posts: (80 / 2) + 1 = 41 posts, plus 2 extra for gate = 43 posts. Number of panels: 79 / 2 = 39.5 → 40 panels (some may need trimming). Concrete: 43 posts × 3 bags = 129 bags of 25kg concrete. Post height for 1.8m fence: 2.7m posts (43 pieces). Total materials: 43 posts, 40 panels, 129 concrete bags, plus hardware (screws, brackets, post caps).

Step-by-Step Guide to Fence Material Calculation

  1. Measure the total fence line length. Walk the fence line with a 30m tape measure or measuring wheel. For rectangular areas, measure each side and sum. Example: rectangular yard 18m × 12m has perimeter (18 + 12) × 2 = 60m. For irregular boundaries, break into straight segments and measure each. Record total length in meters.
  2. Determine post spacing based on panel width. Standard fence panels are 1.8m, 2.0m, or 2.4m wide. Posts are spaced to match panel width (center-to-center). Using 2.0m panels means 2.0m post spacing. Closer spacing (1.5m) increases strength for windy areas or heavy panels. Wider spacing (2.5m+) risks sagging. Match spacing to your panel choice.
  3. Calculate number of posts. Formula: (Total Length / Spacing) + 1. For 60m perimeter with 2m spacing: (60 / 2) + 1 = 31 posts. Add 2 posts per gate. One gate: 31 + 2 = 33 posts. For corners, the corner post serves both sides — don't double-count. If your fence has multiple straight runs, calculate each run separately then sum.
  4. Calculate number of panels. Formula: Total Length / Spacing. For 60m with 2m spacing: 60 / 2 = 30 panels. Subtract gate width first if applicable. A 60m fence with 1m gate: (60 - 1) / 2 = 29.5 → 30 panels (one panel cut to fit). Always round up — partial panels require full panel purchases.
  5. Calculate concrete requirements. Each post needs approximately 3 bags of 25kg concrete mix (for 30cm diameter × 60cm deep holes). For 33 posts: 33 × 3 = 99 bags. Add 10% extra for uneven holes and spillage: 99 × 1.10 = 109 bags. For bulk concrete, calculate volume: 33 posts × 0.036 m³ = 1.19 m³ — order 1.5 m³ ready-mix.
  6. Determine post lengths and hardware. Post length = Fence height + 1/3 for burial. For 1.8m fence: 1.8 + 0.9 = 2.7m — use 3m posts. Hardware includes: post caps (33 pieces), panel screws or nails (40 panels × 8 screws = 320 screws), gate hinges and latch (1 set), concrete reinforcing bars (optional, 1 per post for soft ground).

Real-World Fence Calculation Examples

Example 1: Small Garden Privacy Fence. Straight run of 15m, 1.8m high, with one 1m gate. Panel width: 1.8m. Posts: (15 / 1.8) + 1 = 9.3 → 10 posts, plus 2 for gate = 12 posts. Panels: (15 - 1) / 1.8 = 7.8 → 8 panels (last panel cut to 1.4m). Concrete: 12 × 3 = 36 bags. Posts: 12 × 3m length. Hardware: 12 post caps, 64 panel screws, 1 gate hinge set. Estimated cost: €800-1,200 depending on materials (wood vs. composite).

Example 2: Large Rural Property Boundary. Three sides of rectangular property: 50m + 80m + 50m = 180m total. No gates planned initially. Post spacing: 2.5m (agricultural standard). Posts: (180 / 2.5) + 1 = 73 posts. Panels: 180 / 2.5 = 72 panels. Concrete: 73 × 3 = 219 bags — consider bulk concrete delivery (73 × 0.036 = 2.6 m³). Posts: 73 × 3m treated pine. This is a major project requiring 2-3 weekends and €3,000-5,000 budget.

Example 3: Paddock Fence with Corner Reinforcement. Square paddock 30m × 30m, perimeter 120m. Four corners require reinforced corner posts (double posts or braced). Post spacing: 3m for wire fencing. Posts: (120 / 3) + 1 = 41 posts, plus 4 corner reinforcements = 45 post positions. For wire fencing, concrete isn't always used — driven steel posts work in firm ground. If concreting: 45 × 3 = 135 bags. Wire: 120m × 5 strands = 600m of high-tensile wire plus 600 staples.

Example 4: Sloped Backyard Step-Down Fence. Fence runs 25m along a slope dropping 2m over the length. Using stepped panels (each panel level, posts cut at different heights). Post spacing: 2m. Posts: (25 / 2) + 1 = 13.5 → 14 posts. Panels: 25 / 2 = 12.5 → 13 panels. First post height: 2.4m (1.8m above + 0.6m buried). Last post height: 2.4m + 2m slope = 4.4m — requires special ordering or splicing. Stepped fencing looks cleaner than racked (sloped) panels but needs varying post lengths.

Example 5: Deck Railing (Balustrade) Calculation. Deck perimeter 12m with railing posts every 1.2m (building code maximum). Posts: (12 / 1.2) + 1 = 11 posts. Railing panels or glass infill: 12 / 1.2 = 10 sections. Post mounting: surface-mounted base plates (not buried), requiring M12 anchor bolts (4 per post = 44 bolts). Post height: 1.1m above deck (code minimum). Total post length: 1.1m (no burial). This is a structural application — use engineered posts and verify local building codes.

Common Mistakes in Fence Calculations

Forgetting the +1 for starting post. A 20m fence with 2m spacing needs (20/2)+1 = 11 posts, not 10 posts. Missing the starting post leaves you one post short — a costly mid-project delay. The formula works because posts exist at both ends: position 0m, 2m, 4m... 20m = 11 positions. Always add 1 to the division result.

Not accounting for gate posts properly. Gates require two posts set closer than standard spacing. A 1m gate needs posts spaced approximately 1.05m apart (allowing for hinges and latch clearance). These are additional posts beyond the regular spacing count. A 30m fence with one gate needs regular posts plus 2 gate posts, not replacement posts. Gate posts should also be set in more concrete for strength.

Ignoring corner post requirements. Corner posts experience tension from two fence directions and need reinforcement. Options include: double posts (two posts bolted together), braced posts (diagonal support), or larger-diameter posts. Standard corner treatment adds 1 extra post per corner. A rectangular fence with 4 corners needs 4 additional posts for proper corner reinforcement.

Underestimating concrete quantities. Calculating exact hole volume often produces optimistic estimates. Real-world factors increase concrete needs: irregular hole shapes, soil collapse, spillage, and mixing losses. Add 10-15% buffer to calculated concrete. For 30 posts needing 90 bags, buy 100-105 bags. Running short of concrete mid-project forces a second trip and delays curing.

Pro Tips for Fence Installation

Set corner and gate posts first. Establish the fence line by setting corner posts and gate posts before intermediate posts. Run a string line between corners to align intermediate posts. This ensures a straight fence line. Corner posts should be perfectly plumb (vertical) in both directions — they anchor the entire fence. Use a post level (attaches to post with two bubble levels) for accuracy.

Dig post holes below frost line in cold climates. In regions with ground freezing, dig post holes below the frost depth (typically 60-120cm depending on location). Posts set above frost line heave upward during freeze-thaw cycles, misaligning the fence. Check local building codes for frost depth requirements. In warm climates, 45-60cm burial is sufficient for 1.8m fences.

Use gravel for drainage at post base. Before setting posts in concrete, add 10cm of gravel to the hole bottom. This provides drainage and prevents post-end rot from standing water. Place the post on the gravel bed, then pour concrete around it. The post end sits in gravel, not submerged in concrete. This simple step extends wood post life by 5-10 years.

Allow concrete to cure before attaching panels. Fresh concrete reaches only 50% strength after 24 hours and full strength after 28 days. Wait minimum 48 hours before attaching panels to allow concrete to set sufficiently. Attaching panels too early can shift posts before concrete hardens, creating a wavy fence. In cold weather (<10°C), extend curing time to 72+ hours.

Frequently Asked Questions

Bury 1/3 of the total post length for standard fences. A 2.4m post should have 0.8m buried, leaving 1.6m above ground. For taller fences (2m+ above ground), bury 40% of length. In sandy or loose soil, increase burial depth or use larger-diameter posts. In clay soil, standard 1/3 depth suffices. Always dig below frost line in cold climates regardless of post height.

Concrete provides maximum stability for permanent fences, heavy panels, and windy locations. Gravel (crushed stone) allows drainage and is easier to remove later — suitable for temporary fences or dry climates. Combination approach: 10cm gravel base for drainage, then concrete around the post. This prevents water pooling at the post base while providing concrete stability.

Match post spacing to your panel width for pre-made panels (typically 1.8m, 2.0m, or 2.4m). For custom-built fences, 2-2.5m spacing is standard. Closer spacing (1.5m) increases strength for tall fences, heavy panels, or high-wind areas. Wider spacing (3m+) risks panel sagging and requires heavier posts. Consider soil type — soft soil needs closer spacing than firm clay.

Most jurisdictions require permits for fences over 1.5-2m height, front yard fences, or fences near property lines. Check local building codes before starting. Typical requirements: maximum 2m height for rear yards, 1.2m for front yards, setback from property lines (5-30cm). Homeowner associations may have additional restrictions on materials and appearance. Always verify before purchasing materials.

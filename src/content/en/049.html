What Is a Battery Storage Calculator?

A battery storage calculator sizes the battery bank capacity (kWh) required for solar energy storage or backup power based on daily consumption, desired days of autonomy, battery depth of discharge, and system voltage. For a household consuming 10 kWh daily with 1 day autonomy using 5 kWh usable capacity batteries, the calculator specifies 2 batteries (10 kWh usable, 11.8 kWh installed considering 85% depth of discharge).

Undersized batteries fail to provide meaningful backup during outages (running empty after 4-6 hours), while oversized batteries waste €3,000-€8,000 on unused capacity that degrades over time. A properly sized 10-15 kWh battery system covers 60-80% of nighttime loads for typical European households, achieving 70-85% energy self-sufficiency with solar panels.

The calculation uses daily energy consumption, autonomy days (1-7 days), battery chemistry (lithium 85-95% DoD, lead-acid 50% DoD), and system efficiency losses to determine total storage capacity required.

Battery Storage Formulas With Calculations

Daily Energy Required (kWh) = Consumption × Autonomy Days

Example: 10 kWh/day × 1 day = 10 kWh (grid-tied with backup)

10 kWh/day × 3 days = 30 kWh (off-grid reliability)

Usable Battery Capacity = Installed Capacity × Depth of Discharge

Lithium (LiFePO4): 90-95% DoD. Lead-acid (AGM/GEL): 50% DoD

10 kWh lithium × 0.90 = 9 kWh usable

10 kWh lead-acid × 0.50 = 5 kWh usable

Required Installed Capacity = Daily Energy ÷ DoD

10 kWh ÷ 0.90 (lithium) = 11.1 kWh installed

10 kWh ÷ 0.50 (lead-acid) = 20 kWh installed

Number of Batteries = Required Capacity ÷ Battery Unit Capacity

11.1 kWh ÷ 5 kWh per battery = 2.22 → 3 batteries

System Voltage Configuration:

48 V system: 15 kWh ÷ 48 V = 312.5 Ah battery bank

24 V system: 15 kWh ÷ 24 V = 625 Ah (higher current, larger cables)

Backup Runtime (hours) = Usable Capacity ÷ Hourly Load

10 kWh usable ÷ 0.8 kW average load = 12.5 hours backup

6 Steps To Size Battery Storage Systems

  1. Analyze daily consumption and load profile: Review electricity bills for kWh/day average. Identify critical loads requiring backup: refrigerator (1-2 kWh/day), lighting (0.5-1 kWh), internet/TV (0.5 kWh), well pump (0.5-1 kWh). Total critical loads: 3-5 kWh/day. Non-essential loads (washing machine, dishwasher, EV charging) can be deferred during outages. Size battery for critical loads minimum, total consumption optimal.
  2. Determine autonomy days based on application: Grid-tied with occasional outages: 0.5-1 day autonomy (€3,000-€6,000). Grid-tied with frequent outages: 1-2 days (€6,000-€12,000). Off-grid weekend cabin: 2-3 days (€8,000-€15,000). Full off-grid residence: 3-7 days (€15,000-€40,000). More autonomy exponentially increases cost—evaluate generator backup (€2,500-€5,000) for extended outages versus massive battery banks.
  3. Select battery chemistry based on budget and usage: Lithium LiFePO4: €450-€650/kWh, 6,000-10,000 cycles, 10-15 year life, 90-95% DoD. Lead-acid AGM: €180-€280/kWh, 500-800 cycles, 3-5 year life, 50% DoD. Saltwater batteries: €500-€700/kWh, 10,000+ cycles, 15+ year life, 80% DoD. Lithium costs 2-3× more upfront but 40-50% less per cycle over lifetime. Choose lithium for daily cycling, lead-acid for emergency backup only.
  4. Calculate required capacity with efficiency losses: Battery round-trip efficiency: lithium 95%, lead-acid 80-85%. Inverter efficiency: 92-96%. Combined system efficiency: 87-91% lithium, 74-81% lead-acid. Required capacity = (Daily kWh × Autonomy) ÷ (DoD × System Efficiency). For 10 kWh/day, 1 day, lithium: 10 ÷ (0.90 × 0.90) = 12.3 kWh installed. For lead-acid: 10 ÷ (0.50 × 0.80) = 25 kWh installed.
  5. Configure battery bank voltage and series/parallel arrangement: Small systems (under 5 kWh): 24 V nominal. Medium systems (5-20 kWh): 48 V nominal. Large systems (20+ kWh): 48 V or high-voltage (200-400 V) for hybrid inverters. Higher voltage = lower current = smaller cables. 48 V system at 5 kW = 104 A. 24 V system at 5 kW = 208 A (requires 2× cable size). Match battery voltage to inverter input—mismatched systems require DC-DC converters (€400-€800, 5-8% efficiency loss).

  6. Size battery management system (BMS) and safety components: BMS (included with quality lithium batteries) monitors cell voltage, temperature, current. Specifications: continuous current rating must exceed maximum load. 5 kW inverter at 48 V = 104 A. Use 150 A BMS minimum (50% headroom). Fuse between battery and inverter: 125-150 A ANL fuse (€25-€45). Battery disconnect switch: 200 A DC-rated (€45-€85). Enclosure: IP65 rated for outdoor installation (€120-€200). Temperature sensors required for cold weather charging protection.

4 Costly Mistakes To Avoid

  • Mixing old and new batteries in same bank: Older batteries have higher internal resistance (5-10 mΩ versus 2-3 mΩ new). During discharge, old batteries drop voltage faster, triggering BMS cutoff while new batteries still have 40-60% capacity remaining. During charging, old batteries reach voltage limits first, preventing full charge of new batteries. Result: 50-70% capacity loss for entire bank. Always replace all batteries simultaneously. Cost of premature replacement: €2,000-€6,000 versus €400-€800 for single battery "test".
  • Installing lithium batteries in unheated spaces without low-temp protection: Lithium batteries cannot accept charge below 0°C without permanent damage (lithium plating reduces capacity 20-40% per incident). BMS with temperature sensors (€35-€65) disconnects charging below 5°C. Heated enclosures (€180-€350) maintain 10-20°C in winter. Outdoor installations in Scandinavia/Alps require insulated cabinets with thermostat-controlled heaters (€400-€700 total). Lead-acid batteries tolerate -20°C but lose 40-50% capacity temporarily.
  • Undersizing cables between battery and inverter: High current demands undersized cables overheat, wasting 5-15% energy as heat and creating fire hazard. 5 kW inverter at 48 V = 104 A continuous, 200+ A surge. Minimum cable: 50 mm² copper for 1 m run, 70 mm² for 2-3 m, 95 mm² for 4-6 m. Voltage drop should be under 0.5 V (1% at 48 V). Undersized 25 mm² cables drop 2-3 V at 100 A, reducing inverter efficiency 4-6% and triggering low-voltage shutdowns prematurely. Proper cables: €180-€350. Undersized cables cost: €80-€140 plus €600-€1,500 annual energy waste.
  • Omitting proper fusing and disconnect switches: Battery banks can deliver 1,000-5,000 A short-circuit current—enough to vaporize tools and cause arc flash burns. DC arcs do not self-extinguish like AC. Required: Class T fuse or DC circuit breaker within 45 cm of battery terminal (€45-€120). Manual disconnect switch within sight of battery (€65-€120). Emergency shutdown button at building exit (€35-€55). Non-compliant installations void insurance for electrical fires. Proper safety equipment: €200-€400. Fire damage from battery short: €50,000-€500,000+.

5 Professional Tips

  • Implement load shedding for extended autonomy: Smart energy management systems (€450-€800) automatically disconnect non-critical loads when battery drops below 30% SOC. Priorities: Level 1 (always on): refrigerator, lighting, internet. Level 2 (medium priority): TV, computers, well pump. Level 3 (shed first): washing machine, dishwasher, water heater, EV charging. Load shedding extends backup runtime 40-70% without adding battery capacity. Systems with predictive AI learn usage patterns, optimizing discharge for 15-20% effective capacity gain.
  • Install battery monitoring with remote alerts: Battery monitors (€180-€350) track state of charge (SOC), state of health (SOH), voltage, current, and temperature. WiFi connectivity sends alerts for: low SOC (<20%), high temperature (>45°C), cell imbalance (>50 mV difference), unexpected discharge. Early warning prevents 80-90% of battery failures. Popular models: Victron SmartShunt, DaisyChain BMS monitoring. Commercial systems: €600-€1,200 for three-phase monitoring with cloud dashboard.
  • Configure time-of-use arbitrage for maximum savings: Smart inverters (€3,200-€5,000) charge batteries during off-peak electricity (€0.12-€0.18/kWh, 22:00-06:00) and discharge during peak (€0.35-€0.45/kWh, 08:00-22:00). Daily arbitrage: 10 kWh × (€0.40 - €0.15) = €2.50/day savings. Annual: €900-€950. Requires 365 cycles/year—lithium batteries only (lead-acid would fail in 18-24 months). Payback on smart inverter premium: 2-3 years. Combine with solar for 120-150% ROI versus grid-only operation.
  • Design for modular expansion: Install battery racks with space for 2× initial capacity. Run conduit for future cable expansions. Choose inverters with parallel capability (2-10 units) for capacity growth. Start with 10 kWh, expand to 20-30 kWh as budget allows. Modular systems cost 10-15% more initially but enable phased investment. Contract electrician to document all cable routes and termination points. Future expansion cost: €400-€600/kWh versus €600-€900/kWh for ground-up expansion.
  • Perform quarterly capacity tests: Discharge battery at known load (2-3 kW resistive heater) from 100% to 20% SOC, measuring time. Actual capacity = Load (kW) × Time (hours) ÷ 0.80. Compare to rated capacity. Acceptable degradation: <5% year 1, <10% year 3, <20% year 10. Rapid degradation indicates: cell imbalance, temperature damage, or overcharging. Early detection enables warranty claims (most lithium batteries have 10-year/6,000-cycle warranties with <30% degradation guarantee).

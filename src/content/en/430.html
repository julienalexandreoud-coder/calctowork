The target weight calculator helps you determine how long it will take to reach your desired weight based on a specific daily calorie deficit or surplus. By understanding the mathematical relationship between energy balance and body weight change, you can set realistic timelines and adjust your approach to achieve sustainable results.

You may also find the BMI Calculator (Body Mass Index), BMI Prime Calculator, and Lean Body Mass Calculator (Boer) useful.

Target Weight Time Formula

Days = (Current Weight − Target Weight) × 7,700 / Daily Calorie Deficit

The formula is based on the approximation that 1 kg of body fat contains approximately 7,700 kcal of stored energy. To lose weight, you create a daily calorie deficit (eating less than your TDEE). The total calorie deficit needed equals the weight to lose multiplied by 7,700. Dividing by your daily deficit gives the number of days required. For example, to lose 10 kg with a daily deficit of 500 kcal: Days = (10 × 7,700) / 500 = 77,000 / 500 = 154 days (approximately 22 weeks or 5 months).

Worked Examples

Example 1: Moderate Weight Loss

A person weighing 90 kg wants to reach 80 kg with a daily calorie deficit of 500 kcal.

Weight to lose: 90 − 80 = 10 kg
Total deficit needed: 10 × 7,700 = 77,000 kcal
Days = 77,000 / 500 = 154 days

At a 500 kcal daily deficit, this person can expect to lose approximately 0.5 kg per week (500 × 7 = 3,500 kcal/week ÷ 7,700 = 0.45 kg/week). The 154-day timeline assumes consistent adherence and no metabolic adaptation. In practice, the actual timeline may be 10–20% longer due to the body’s metabolic slowdown during weight loss.

Example 2: Aggressive Weight Loss

A person weighing 100 kg wants to reach 85 kg with a daily calorie deficit of 1,000 kcal.

Weight to lose: 100 − 85 = 15 kg
Total deficit needed: 15 × 7,700 = 115,500 kcal
Days = 115,500 / 1,000 = 115.5 days

While the math suggests 116 days, a 1,000 kcal daily deficit is aggressive and may not be sustainable or healthy for everyone. A deficit this large can trigger significant metabolic adaptation, muscle loss, and nutrient deficiencies. A more sustainable approach would be a 500–750 kcal deficit, extending the timeline to 154–231 days but preserving muscle mass and metabolic health.

Common Uses

  • Setting realistic weight loss timelines for personal goal-setting and motivation
  • Planning the duration of cutting phases in bodybuilding and athletic preparation cycles
  • Comparing different calorie deficit strategies to find the optimal balance between speed and sustainability
  • Calculating the calorie surplus needed and timeline for muscle-building (bulking) phases
  • Healthcare providers establishing expected weight loss trajectories for patients in weight management programs
  • Evaluating whether a desired weight loss goal is achievable within a specific deadline (e.g., before an event)

Common Mistakes

  • Assuming linear weight loss when the body’s metabolic rate decreases as weight drops, making each subsequent kilogram take longer to lose than the previous one
  • Creating deficits larger than 1,000 kcal/day, which leads to muscle loss, nutrient deficiencies, and metabolic adaptation that ultimately slows progress
  • Not accounting for the fact that initial weight loss includes significant water weight (2–4 kg in the first week), making early progress appear faster than the long-term rate
  • Ignoring that the 7,700 kcal/kg rule is an approximation that becomes less accurate at lower body fat percentages, where a greater proportion of weight loss comes from lean tissue

Pro Tip

Use a phased approach to weight loss: start with a moderate deficit of 500–750 kcal/day for the first 8–12 weeks, then implement a 1–2 week “diet break” at maintenance calories to restore leptin levels and metabolic rate. After the break, resume the deficit. This approach, supported by the MATADOR study, results in greater fat loss and better metabolic health compared to continuous dieting. Additionally, recalculate your TDEE and adjust your calorie target every 5 kg of weight loss, as your energy needs decrease with your body size.

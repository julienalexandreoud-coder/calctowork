When planning a renovation or new construction, accurately estimating the materials for your windows is crucial for budgeting and ordering. The Window Calculator is a free online tool that simplifies this process, allowing you to quickly compute the total glass area, frame proportions, and material needs for aluminium or PVC windows. Whether you are a builder, an architect, or a homeowner tackling a DIY project, this metric-based calculator provides instant results using a straightforward formula.

What the Window Calculator Does and When to Use It

The Window Calculator is designed to compute key measurements for multiple windows of identical size. You enter the number of windows and their width and height in meters, and the tool returns four critical values: the net hole (glass) area per window, the total glass area for all windows, the total perimeter length of the frames, and the estimated frame material area. It automatically subtracts a 2-centimeter gap from the width and height (as is standard in many installations), ensuring the results reflect realistic glazing sizes.

This calculator is most useful during the early planning stages of a project. Use it when you need to compare material costs for different window dimensions, estimate the amount of glass to order, or calculate the approximate volume of aluminium or PVC profiles required for the frames. It is especially helpful in renovation projects where you are replacing multiple windows in a facade or when designing a new building with repetitive window modules. Because it uses metric units (meters), it is ideal for projects in Europe, Asia, Australia, and other regions using the metric system, though the principles apply universally.

The Formula Explained Variable by Variable

The calculator uses a simple geometric approach. Below is a breakdown of every variable and how it contributes to the final calculations.

Variable Description How It Is Calculated
num_ventanas (n) Number of identical windows User input – the total count of windows being measured.
ancho_m (W) Total width of one window in meters User input – measured from outer frame edge to outer frame edge.
alto_m (H) Total height of one window in meters User input – measured from outer frame edge to outer frame edge.
Wh and Hh Adjusted width and height for the glass hole Wh = W - 0.02 and Hh = H - 0.02 (subtracting 1 cm from each side for the frame overlap).
hueco Glass area per window (in m²) Wh × Hh. This is the net area of the glass pane before any frame subtraction.
total Total glass area for all windows (in m²) hueco × n. The sum of glass areas across all windows.
perfc Total perimeter of all window frames (in meters) 2 × (W + H) × n. This is the combined length of the outer edges of all frames.
ancho_marco Assumed frame profile width (fixed at 0.06 m) A standard value (6 cm) representing the thickness of a typical aluminium or PVC frame profile.
marco_m2 Total frame material area (in m²) [2 × (W + H) × ancho_marco × n] + [4 × ancho_marco² × n]. The second term accounts for the four corner joints of each window.
vidrio Net visible glass area (in m²) If total - marco_m2 is positive, that is the result; otherwise, zero. This represents the glass area after subtracting the part covered by the frame profile.

The formula ensures that all material estimates are conservative, meaning they slightly overestimate frame material and slightly underestimate glass – a common safety margin in construction planning.

2 Worked Examples with Concrete Numbers

Example 1: Small Renovation Project

You are replacing four identical windows in a bedroom. Each window measures 0.80 meters wide by 1.20 meters high. Enter the following into the calculator:

  • num_ventanas = 4
  • ancho_m = 0.80
  • alto_m = 1.20

Step-by-step calculation:

  1. Wh = 0.80 - 0.02 = 0.78 m
  2. Hh = 1.20 - 0.02 = 1.18 m
  3. hueco (glass per window) = 0.78 × 1.18 = 0.9204 m², rounded to 0.92 m²
  4. total glass for four windows = 0.92 × 4 = 3.68 m²
  5. perfc = 2 × (0.80 + 1.20) × 4 = 2 × 2.00 × 4 = 16.0 m
  6. marco_m2 = [2 × (0.80 + 1.20) × 0.06 × 4] + [4 × 0.06² × 4] = [2 × 2.00 × 0.06 × 4] + [4 × 0.0036 × 4] = [0.96] + [0.0576] = 1.02 m²
  7. vidrio = 3.68 - 1.02 = 2.66 m²

This means you need roughly 2.66 square meters of glass and about 1.02 square meters of frame profile material (which can be converted to linear meters by dividing by 0.06).

Example 2: Larger Commercial Installation

For a shop front, you are installing ten windows, each 1.50 meters wide and 2.10 meters high. Input:

  • num_ventanas = 10
  • ancho_m = 1.50
  • alto_m = 2.10

Step-by-step:

  1. Wh = 1.50 - 0.02 = 1.48 m
  2. Hh = 2.10 - 0.02 = 2.08 m
  3. hueco = 1.48 × 2.08 = 3.0784 m², rounded to 3.08 m²
  4. total = 3.08 × 10 = 30.80 m²
  5. perfc = 2 × (1.50 + 2.10) × 10 = 2 × 3.60 × 10 = 72.0 m
  6. marco_m2 = [2 × (1.50 + 2.10) × 0.06 × 10] + [4 × 0.06² × 10] = [2 × 3.60 × 0.06 × 10] + [4 × 0.0036 × 10] = [4.32] + [0.144] = 4.46 m²
  7. vidrio = 30.80 - 4.46 = 26.34 m²

This larger project requires just over 26 square meters of glass and nearly 4.5 square meters of frame material. Note that the perfc value of 72 meters is the total linear length of the frame edges, which is useful for estimating sealing tape or gaskets.

Common Mistakes When Using This Calculator

Users often misunderstand a few key points, leading to inaccurate material orders.

1. Confusing window dimensions with glass dimensions. The inputs ancho_m and alto_m refer to the overall window frame dimensions, not the glass pane size. The calculator automatically subtracts 2 cm from each to estimate the hole size. If you accidentally input the glass size instead, the results for frame material will be too small, and the glass area will be underestimated.

2. Mixing measurement units. While the calculator uses meters, many people measure windows in centimeters or millimeters. For example, if your window is 90 cm wide, you must input 0.90, not 90. A common error is entering 90 for width, which the calculator interprets as 90 meters, producing absurdly large values. Always convert to meters before entering data.

3. Ignoring the fixed frame width assumption. The calculator assumes a frame profile width of 6 cm (0.06 m). If your aluminium or PVC frames are significantly thicker (e.g., 8 cm or 10 cm), the marco_m2 result will be too low. For non-standard profiles, you may need to manually adjust the formula or use this result as a rough estimate only.

4. Forgetting about waste and cut-offs. The calculator gives theoretical net values. In practice, glass cutters and frame fabricators require 5-10% extra material for breakage, cutting errors, and test pieces. Always add a waste factor when ordering.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I use this calculator for imperial measurements (feet and inches)?

Yes, but you must first convert all dimensions to meters. One foot equals 0.3048 meters, and one inch equals 0.0254 meters. For example, a window that is 3 feet wide becomes 3 × 0.3048 = 0.9144 meters. The calculator itself only accepts metric inputs, so the conversion must happen before you enter the numbers.

Why does the calculator subtract 2 cm from the width and height?

This 2 cm deduction (1 cm from each side) accounts for the overlap between the glass pane and the frame rebate. In standard aluminium and PVC window installations, the glass does not extend to the very edge of the frame; it sits inside a groove. The 1 cm margin per side is a common industry standard, but if your specific window system uses a different rebate depth, you should adjust the input dimensions manually. For instance, if the rebate is 1.5 cm per side, subtract 0.03 m from both width and height before entry.

How do I interpret the perfc (total perimeter) value?

The perfc value is the combined perimeter length of all window frames. It is useful for ordering gaskets, weatherstripping, or sealing tapes. For example, if your perfc is 72 meters (as in Example 2), you would need approximately 72 meters of rubber gasket to seal the edges of all ten windows. Always add extra length for corners and overlaps when purchasing such materials.

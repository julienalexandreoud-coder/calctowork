The BMR Harris-Benedict Calculator is one of the most well-known tools for estimating your basal metabolic rate, or the number of calories your body burns while at complete rest. First developed in 1919 by James Arthur Harris and Francis G. Benedict, this classic formula uses your weight, height, age, and sex to give you a reliable baseline for understanding your daily energy needs. Whether you are planning a weight-loss diet, designing a fitness programme, or simply curious about how your metabolism works, this calculator offers a simple starting point that has stood the test of time.

What the Calculator Does and When to Use It

This calculator applies the original Harris-Benedict equations to estimate your BMR in calories per day. Your basal metabolic rate represents the minimum amount of energy your body requires to perform essential functions like breathing, circulating blood, and maintaining body temperature. It does not account for physical activity, digestion, or exercise, so it is best thought of as a foundation upon which you build your total daily energy expenditure (TDEE).

Use this calculator when you want a quick, evidence-based estimate of your resting calorie needs. It is particularly helpful for:

  • Setting a calorie target for weight loss or muscle gain, by multiplying your BMR by an activity factor (e.g., sedentary, light, moderate, heavy, athlete).
  • Comparing your metabolic rate across different stages of life or after significant changes in body composition.
  • Understanding the concept of metabolic rate in nutrition or fitness education.
  • Providing a starting point for clinical nutrition plans under professional guidance.

Keep in mind that this formula was developed using data from a relatively small sample of healthy individuals in the early 20th century, so it may not be perfectly accurate for everyone. However, it remains a popular and useful tool for initial calculations.

The Formula Explained Variable by Variable

The original Harris-Benedict equations are different for men and women, and they require three key inputs: weight, height, and age. Here are the formulas, with each variable clearly defined.

Variable Unit How to Measure
Weight (W) kilograms (kg) Weigh yourself in the morning after using the toilet, on a digital scale. If you use pounds, divide by 2.205.
Height (H) centimetres (cm) Stand against a wall without shoes; measure from the floor to the top of your head. For feet and inches, multiply feet by 30.48 and add inches multiplied by 2.54.
Age years (complete years) Use your current age in whole years as of your last birthday.

For men: BMR = 88.36 + (13.4 × W) + (4.8 × H) – (5.7 × age)

For women: BMR = 447.6 + (9.2 × W) + (3.1 × H) – (4.3 × age)

In these equations, the constant numbers (88.36 and 447.6) are derived from the original research data and represent baseline metabolic values. The coefficients in front of each variable reflect how strongly each factor influences the BMR. For example, the coefficient for weight in men (13.4) is higher than in women (9.2), meaning that each extra kilogram of body mass typically has a greater impact on a man’s resting energy expenditure. Similarly, age reduces BMR, which aligns with the natural decline in muscle mass and metabolic rate as people get older.

It is crucial to use metric units—kilograms for weight and centimetres for height—when applying these equations directly. If you prefer imperial units, many online calculators perform the conversion automatically, but always double-check that your inputs are in the correct system to avoid errors.

Worked Example 1: A 45-Year-Old Man

Inputs: Weight = 95 kg (approximately 209 lb), Height = 183 cm (about 6 ft 0 in), Age = 45 years.

  1. Write the formula for men: BMR = 88.36 + (13.4 × W) + (4.8 × H) – (5.7 × age)
  2. Plug in the numbers: BMR = 88.36 + (13.4 × 95) + (4.8 × 183) – (5.7 × 45)
  3. Calculate each term:
    • 13.4 × 95 = 1,273
    • 4.8 × 183 = 878.4
    • 5.7 × 45 = 256.5
  4. Add the constants and positive terms: 88.36 + 1,273 + 878.4 = 2,239.76
  5. Subtract the age term: 2,239.76 – 256.5 = 1,983.26

Result: The estimated basal metabolic rate is about 1,983 calories per day. This means that if this man were to rest in bed all day, his body would burn roughly 1,983 kcal just to keep his organs functioning. To estimate his total daily calorie needs for weight maintenance, he would multiply this BMR by an activity factor—for instance, 1.55 for moderate exercise, giving a TDEE of about 3,074 calories.

Worked Example 2: A 22-Year-Old Woman

Inputs: Weight = 63 kg (about 139 lb), Height = 168 cm (approximately 5 ft 6 in), Age = 22 years.

  1. Write the formula for women: BMR = 447.6 + (9.2 × W) + (3.1 × H) – (4.3 × age)
  2. Substitute the values: BMR = 447.6 + (9.2 × 63) + (3.1 × 168) – (4.3 × 22)
  3. Calculate each term:
    • 9.2 × 63 = 579.6
    • 3.1 × 168 = 520.8
    • 4.3 × 22 = 94.6
  4. Add the constant and positive multiplication results: 447.6 + 579.6 + 520.8 = 1,548.0
  5. Subtract the age term: 1,548.0 – 94.6 = 1,453.4

Result: This woman’s estimated BMR is approximately 1,453 calories per day. If she leads a sedentary lifestyle, multiplying by an activity factor of 1.2 gives a TDEE of 1,744 calories. For weight loss, she might reduce this by 300–500 calories, but she should never eat below her BMR for extended periods without medical supervision. The numbers in this example are deliberately different from the source to show how the same formula works across a range of ages, body sizes, and sexes.

Common Mistakes When Using the Harris-Benedict Formula

Even a well-established formula can give misleading results if used incorrectly. Here are the most frequent errors and how to avoid them.

  • Using the wrong units: The formula is designed for kilograms and centimetres. If you enter weight in pounds (e.g., 150 instead of 68 kg) or height in inches (e.g., 66 instead of 168 cm), your BMR can be off by hundreds of calories. Always convert first or use a calculator that does it for you.
  • Selecting the wrong sex: The equations for men and women are very different. Mistakenly using the female formula for a male adds about 360 calories to the constant, skewing the result significantly. Double-check this setting before proceeding.
  • Confusing BMR with total calorie needs: Your BMR is only part of the picture. Many people assume that eating exactly the BMR value will maintain weight, but this ignores the calories burned through walking, fidgeting, exercise, and digesting food. Always use an activity factor to get your TDEE.
  • Rounding too early: Rounding intermediate multiplication results to the nearest whole number can introduce cumulative errors, especially when planning diets over several weeks. Carry two decimal places throughout your calculation, then round only at the final step.
  • Applying the formula to individuals with extreme body composition: The original Harris-Benedict equations are based on average body types from 1919. People with very high muscle mass or very low body fat percentages may get more accurate results from body-composition-based formulas like Katch-McArdle.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is the Harris-Benedict formula the most accurate one available?

No, it is not the most accurate for every individual. While it is one of the oldest and most widely used equations, more recent formulas such as Mifflin-St Jeor (1990) have been shown to be slightly more accurate for the general population, especially in overweight and obese individuals. The Harris-Benedict formula tends to overestimate BMR in some people, so it is best considered an approximation rather than an absolute measure. For personalised accuracy, consider using indirect calorimetry, which measures your oxygen consumption directly.

Can I use this formula if I am very athletic or very overweight?

Yes, but with caution. The original equations were developed from a sample that did not include many athletes or severely overweight individuals. If you have a high proportion of lean muscle mass, your BMR may be higher than the formula predicts, because muscle burns more calories at rest than fat. Conversely, if you have a very high body fat percentage, the formula may overestimate your BMR. In these cases, look into the Katch-McArdle equation, which uses lean body mass instead of total weight, or consult a sports nutritionist.

How do I convert my BMR into a daily calorie target for weight loss?

First, multiply your BMR by an activity factor to get your total daily energy expenditure (TDEE). For weight loss, a safe and sustainable approach is to subtract 300 to 500 calories from your TDEE, aiming for a loss of about 0.5 to 1 kg (1–2 lb) per week. Never drop below your BMR for extended periods, as this can slow your metabolism and lead to muscle loss. For weight gain, add a similar calorie surplus while focusing on strength training to ensure the extra calories build muscle rather than just fat.

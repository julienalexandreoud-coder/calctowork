By entering your lawn dimensions and roll size, this tool tells you exactly how many rolls to buy, accounting for waste around flower beds, trees, and curved edges.

What the Sod & Turf Calculator Does and When to Use It

This calculator is designed for anyone installing rolled sod—also called turf—on a residential lawn, commercial site, or sports field. It works for any grass type, including warm-season varieties like St. Augustine and Zoysia, as well as cool-season grasses such as tall fescue and perennial ryegrass. You can use it for simple rectangular lawns, L-shaped gardens, or large open areas with irregular boundaries.

The calculator accepts any roll dimensions you provide. Common sizes include 0.6 m by 1.5 m (0.9 m² per roll) in the UK and Europe, 0.4 m by 1.2 m (0.48 m² per roll) in the United States, and 0.75 m by 2.0 m (1.5 m² per roll) in Australia. If your supplier sells by the square metre rather than by the roll, simply divide your total area by the area per roll to find the number of rolls needed.

Use this tool before you place your order to avoid two common problems: running short of sod, which leads to colour mismatches between batches, or ordering too many rolls and wasting money. It also helps you schedule delivery so the sod is fresh when you are ready to install.

The Formula Explained Variable by Variable

The core formula behind the Sod & Turf Calculator is straightforward:

Number of rolls = (Length of lawn × Width of lawn) ÷ (Roll width × Roll length) × (1 + Waste percentage)

Here is what each variable means:

  • Length of lawn – The longer side of your lawn area, measured in metres or feet. For irregular shapes, measure the longest straight dimension.
  • Width of lawn – The shorter side perpendicular to the length. For non-rectangular areas, break the lawn into rectangles, calculate each separately, and add them together.
  • Roll width – The actual width of a single sod roll, typically between 0.4 m (16 inches) and 0.75 m (30 inches). Always confirm with your supplier rather than guessing.
  • Roll length – The length of one unrolled sod piece, usually from 1.0 m (40 inches) to 2.0 m (80 inches). Multiply width by length to get the area of one roll.
  • Waste percentage – A buffer for cutting around obstacles. Use 5% for simple rectangles, 10% for lawns with a few tree beds or straight-edged gardens, and 15% for highly irregular boundaries, curved beds, or lots of obstacles.

To apply the formula, first subtract areas that will not be grassed, such as patios, driveways, ponds, or flower beds. Take your total lawn area, subtract the square footage of each non-grass section, and then divide by the area of one sod roll. Multiply by the waste factor to get your final order quantity.

Two Worked Examples with Concrete Numbers

Example 1: Rectangular Front Lawn (12 m by 6 m)

A homeowner is sodding a new front lawn that measures 12 metres long by 6 metres wide. They choose standard UK rolls at 0.6 m by 1.5 m, giving 0.9 m² per roll.

The lawn area is 12 × 6 = 72 m². Dividing by the roll area: 72 ÷ 0.9 = 80 rolls exactly. With a 5% waste factor for the simple rectangle: 80 × 1.05 = 84 rolls. The homeowner orders 84 rolls.

At a price of £4.80 per roll for standard fescue sod, the total cost is 84 × £4.80 = £403.20. If they upgraded to a premium blend at £7.50 per roll, the total would be 84 × £7.50 = £630.00.

Example 2: L-Shaped Back Garden with Two Flower Beds (Metric)

A gardener is renovating an L-shaped back garden. The main section is 14 m by 9 m, and an extension is 6 m by 4 m. Two flower beds need subtracting: one is 4 m by 3 m and the other is 2.5 m by 2.5 m. They use Australian-size rolls at 0.75 m by 2.0 m (1.5 m² per roll).

Main area: 14 × 9 = 126 m². Extension: 6 × 4 = 24 m². Total gross area: 126 + 24 = 150 m². Flower bed 1: 4 × 3 = 12 m². Flower bed 2: 2.5 × 2.5 = 6.25 m². Total deductions: 12 + 6.25 = 18.25 m². Net sod area: 150 − 18.25 = 131.75 m².

Rolls needed before waste: 131.75 ÷ 1.5 = 87.8, which rounds up to 88 rolls. With a 10% waste factor for flower beds and corners: 88 × 1.10 = 96.8, so order 97 rolls. The gardener should plan for delivery one day before installation and keep the pallet in a shaded, damp spot.

Common Mistakes When Ordering and Installing Sod

Mistake 1: Letting sod dry out before installation. Sod is living grass and can die within hours if roots dry out. Never leave rolls stacked on a pallet in direct sunlight. If you cannot install the same day, unroll the sod and water it, or store it in deep shade and mist it regularly.

Mistake 2: Skimping on waste allowance. Even a small flower bed or tree creates cutting waste. Ordering exactly the net area without a waste buffer often leaves you short by 5% to 15%. It is cheaper to buy a few extra rolls than to match colours from a later batch.

Mistake 3: Ignoring soil preparation. Sod will not root in compacted, nutrient-poor soil. Till the ground to at least 15 cm (6 inches) deep, add compost or topsoil, level the surface, and firm it with a roller before laying sod. Test soil pH; most grasses prefer a range of 6.0 to 7.0.

Mistake 4: Leaving gaps between rolls. Even a 1 cm gap dries out and turns brown. Sod shrinks slightly as it dries, so butt pieces tightly together, edge to edge. Do not overlap them, as overlapping creates ridges that die. Fill any small gaps that appear with topsoil mixed with grass seed.

Mistake 5: Walking on new sod too soon. Keep foot traffic off freshly laid sod for at least two weeks. If you must walk on it, lay wooden planks to distribute your weight. After two weeks, light walking is fine; wait four to six weeks before heavy use such as sports or pets running.

Short FAQ: Your Top Questions Answered

How long does it take for sod to root?

Sod typically roots into the soil within 10 to 14 days if watered correctly. To test, gently lift a corner of a piece. If it resists and you see white roots growing into the ground, the sod is established. Avoid mowing or heavy use for the first two to three weeks.

Can I lay sod over existing grass?

No, you must remove old grass first. Sod cannot root through an existing lawn layer, and the old grass dies underneath, creating an uneven surface. Remove old turf with a sod cutter or herbicide, then till and level before laying new sod. This ensures direct soil-to-root contact.

What is the best time of year to install sod?

Spring (March to May) and early autumn (September to October) are ideal in most climates. Avoid summer heat, which dries sod too fast, and winter, when dormant grass does not root. Cool-season grasses such as fescue and bluegrass prefer autumn planting, while warm-season types such as Bermuda and Zoysia do best in late spring.

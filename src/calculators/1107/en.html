The Laminate Flooring Calculator helps you determine exactly how many packs of laminate flooring to buy for your room, saving you from costly over-ordering or frustrating shortages halfway through installation. By accounting for room dimensions, pack coverage, and waste allowance, this tool takes the guesswork out of your flooring project.

What the Calculator Does and When to Use It

This calculator is designed for anyone planning to install laminate flooring in a single room or across multiple connected spaces. It asks for your room’s length and width, the coverage area per pack of laminate (usually stated on the box in square metres or square feet), and an estimated waste percentage. It then calculates the number of full packs required, plus an optional breakdown of how many planks you might need if you know the plank dimensions.

Use this calculator at the planning stage of any laminate flooring project—whether you’re fitting a small bedroom, a long hallway, or an open-plan living area. It is especially valuable when ordering materials online, where you cannot easily ask a store assistant for advice. The tool also helps you compare different laminate products by adjusting the pack coverage to see which option gives you the least leftover material.

Formula Explained Variable by Variable

The core calculation is straightforward: find the total floor area, add waste, and divide by the coverage per pack. The formula is:

Number of packs = (room area × (1 + waste percentage)) ÷ pack coverage

Where:

  • Room area = length × width, measured in square metres (m²) or square feet (ft²). If your room is not a perfect rectangle, break it into smaller rectangles, calculate each area, and add them together.
  • Waste percentage = the extra material you need for cutting planks to fit edges, corners, and doorways. For a simple rectangular room, 5–10% is typical; for rooms with many angles, alcoves, or a diagonal layout, use 15–20%.
  • Pack coverage = the total area one pack of laminate will cover, as stated by the manufacturer. This usually ranges from 1.5 to 2.5 m² (roughly 16 to 27 ft²) per pack.
  • Number of packs = the result, always rounded up to the next whole number because you cannot buy a fraction of a pack.

If you want to estimate the number of individual planks, use this additional step:

Number of planks = (total area needed) ÷ (plank length × plank width)

Where plank dimensions are in the same units as the room. This helps when you plan to order loose planks instead of sealed packs, or when you want to double-check pack counts against plank counts.

Two Worked Examples with Concrete Numbers

Example 1: A Rectangular Living Room

You are fitting laminate in a living room measuring 5.2 metres long by 4.8 metres wide. Each pack of laminate covers 1.98 m². You expect a waste factor of 8% because the room is fairly simple with only one doorway.

Step 1 – Room area: 5.2 × 4.8 = 24.96 m²

Step 2 – Add waste: 24.96 × (1 + 0.08) = 24.96 × 1.08 = 26.9568 m²

Step 3 – Divide by pack coverage: 26.9568 ÷ 1.98 = 13.61 packs

Step 4 – Round up: You need 14 packs of laminate. Always round up; buying 13 packs would leave you short by about 0.6 m².

If each pack contains 8 planks, you will have 14 × 8 = 112 planks total, which is more than enough to cover the 26.96 m² needed.

Example 2: An L-Shaped Room with High Waste

A kitchen-diner is L-shaped. The main section is 6.3 metres by 4.0 metres, and the smaller section is 2.8 metres by 3.2 metres. You choose a laminate with pack coverage of 2.15 m². Because of many cuts around cabinets and a bay window, you estimate 18% waste.

Step 1 – Room areas: Main section = 6.3 × 4.0 = 25.2 m². Small section = 2.8 × 3.2 = 8.96 m². Total area = 25.2 + 8.96 = 34.16 m²

Step 2 – Add waste: 34.16 × 1.18 = 40.3088 m²

Step 3 – Divide by pack coverage: 40.3088 ÷ 2.15 = 18.75 packs

Step 4 – Round up: You need 19 packs of laminate. Even though the calculator says 18.75, you cannot buy 0.75 of a pack, so 19 packs are required. This gives you roughly 19 × 2.15 = 40.85 m² of material, leaving about 0.54 m² of extra laminate you might keep for future repairs.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Forgetting to add waste allowance. Many people measure the room area and divide directly by pack coverage. This leads to shortages because cuts and mistakes always eat up material. Always include at least 5% waste, even for perfect rectangles.
  • Using the wrong units. Laminate pack coverage is sometimes stated in square feet while you measure your room in metres. Convert everything to the same unit before calculating. One square metre equals approximately 10.76 square feet.
  • Rounding down instead of up. A result of 9.1 packs means you need 10 packs, not 9. Half a pack will not cover the remaining area without buying a full pack anyway. Rounding down is the most common reason for running out of flooring mid-project.
  • Ignoring plank direction and pattern. If you plan a herringbone or diagonal installation, waste increases significantly—sometimes to 20–25%. The calculator’s waste slider should be adjusted higher for non-straight layouts.
  • Not accounting for doorways and transitions. When laminate runs continuously from one room to another, you need material for the entire combined area, not just each room separately. Measure the full footprint of the connected space.

Frequently Asked Questions

What waste percentage should I use for a beginner installer?

If you are installing laminate for the first time, use 15–20% waste. Beginners typically make more cutting errors and need extra planks to replace damaged pieces. Experienced DIYers can often manage with 8–10% for simple rooms, while professionals may use as little as 5% on straightforward jobs.

How do I measure an irregularly shaped room?

Break the room into rectangles or squares. For each section, multiply length by width, then add all the results together. For triangular sections, multiply the base by the height and divide by two. Add these areas to your total before applying the waste factor. The calculator works with total area, so the shape does not matter as long as you supply the correct square metres.

Can I mix packs from different batches?

It is not recommended. Laminate colours and patterns can vary slightly between production batches, leading to visible differences on your floor. Always order all packs needed for the entire project at once, and if possible, buy one extra pack to keep for future repairs. Check that all packs have the same batch number printed on the box before you start installing.

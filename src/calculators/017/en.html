The Cement Mortar Calculator is a free online tool that instantly estimates the quantities of cement, sand, and water needed for any mortar mix. Whether you are laying bricks, plastering a wall, or repairing concrete, this calculator eliminates guesswork by converting your project’s area and thickness into precise material requirements.

What the Cement Mortar Calculator Does and When to Use It

This calculator determines the required volume of mortar based on your input area and thickness, then converts that volume into the exact kilograms of cement, the number of 50 kg cement bags, the cubic meters of sand, and the cubic meters of water. It is ideal for:

  • Bricklaying and block work – ensuring you order enough mortar for walls, columns, or partitions.
  • Plastering and rendering – calculating the precise mix for internal or external wall finishes.
  • Floor screeding and tiling – estimating the mortar base needed before laying tiles or leveling surfaces.
  • Small-scale construction – home renovation, garden walls, or DIY projects where manual calculations are error-prone.

The tool uses metric units (meters, centimeters, kilograms) and provides results in both metric and common imperial equivalents, such as converting sand volume to cubic feet or yards if needed. It saves time, reduces material waste, and helps you stick to project budgets.

Formula Explained Variable by Variable

The calculator uses a straightforward three-step mathematical process. Below is the underlying JavaScript formula broken down into its core components:

Variable Meaning Typical Value or Unit
A Area of the surface to cover (in square meters) Example: 10 m²
E Thickness of the mortar layer (in centimeters) Default: 2 cm (common for plaster)
D Cement dosage (kg of cement per cubic meter of mortar) Default: 300 kg/m³ (typical for general bricklaying)
vol Total mortar volume (cubic meters). Calculated as (A × E) / 100 Example: 10 m² × 0.02 m = 0.2 m³
ckg Cement mass in kilograms. Calculated as vol × D 0.2 m³ × 300 kg/m³ = 60 kg
sacos Number of 50 kg cement bags (rounded up) 60 kg / 50 = 1.2 → 2 bags
arena Sand volume in cubic meters. Calculated as vol × 1.3 0.2 m³ × 1.3 = 0.26 m³
agua Water volume in cubic meters. Calculated as vol × 0.25 0.2 m³ × 0.25 = 0.05 m³

Key assumptions: The formula uses a standard 1:4 cement-to-sand ratio by volume (equating to 300 kg cement per m³ of mortar). Sand volume is multiplied by 1.3 to account for bulking and wastage. Water is set at 0.25 m³ per m³ of mortar (about 250 liters per m³). These values are adjustable by changing the dosificacion or thickness input.

Two Worked Examples with Concrete Numbers

Example 1: Plastering a Wall

Situation: You need to plaster a wall that is 5 meters long and 3 meters high (area = 15 m²) with a 1.5 cm thick layer. You plan to use a standard mix of 300 kg cement per m³.

  1. Calculate mortar volume: Area × Thickness in meters = 15 m² × (1.5 / 100) = 15 × 0.015 = 0.225 m³
  2. Cement required: 0.225 m³ × 300 kg/m³ = 67.5 kg
  3. Cement bags: 67.5 ÷ 50 = 1.35, rounded up to 2 bags
  4. Sand required: 0.225 m³ × 1.3 = 0.2925 m³ (approximately 10.3 cubic feet)
  5. Water required: 0.225 m³ × 0.25 = 0.05625 m³ (56.25 liters)

Result: For a 15 m² wall plastered 1.5 cm thick, you need 2 bags of cement (50 kg each), 0.29 m³ of sand, and about 56 liters of water.

Example 2: Laying a Brick Patio

Situation: A rectangular patio area of 24 m² (6 m × 4 m) needs a 3 cm thick mortar bed for brick laying. You prefer a stronger mix with 350 kg cement per m³ (adjusting the dosage input accordingly).

  1. Mortar volume: 24 m² × (3 / 100) = 24 × 0.03 = 0.72 m³
  2. Cement required: 0.72 m³ × 350 kg/m³ = 252 kg
  3. Cement bags: 252 ÷ 50 = 5.04, rounded up to 6 bags
  4. Sand required: 0.72 m³ × 1.3 = 0.936 m³ (about 33.1 cubic feet)
  5. Water required: 0.72 m³ × 0.25 = 0.18 m³ (180 liters)

Result: For a 24 m² brick patio with a 3 cm mortar bed and stronger mix, buy 6 cement bags, 0.94 m³ of sand, and prepare 180 liters of water.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Using incorrect units: The calculator expects area in square meters and thickness in centimeters. Inputting area in square feet or thickness in millimeters will produce wildly inaccurate results. Always convert imperial measurements to metric first (1 meter = 3.28 feet; 1 inch = 2.54 cm).
  • Ignoring wastage and bulking: Sand is measured in a compacted state, but delivered sand contains air pockets. The 1.3 multiplier accounts for this bulking factor. Skipping this adjustment leads to a shortage of sand on site.
  • Rounding cement bags down: The calculator always rounds the number of cement bags up to the nearest whole bag because you cannot buy a fraction of a bag at a store. Rounding down can leave you 10-20 kg short for the final mix.
  • Using the default dosage for all jobs: The standard 300 kg/m³ is for general purpose bricklaying and plastering. For structural work like foundations or heavy-load walls, increase the dosage to 350-400 kg/m³. For thin renders, you may reduce it to 250 kg/m³. Adjust the dosificacion field accordingly.
  • Forgetting water adjustment: The water volume (0.25 m³ per m³ of mortar) is an estimate. Actual water depends on sand moisture, temperature, and workability preference. Add water gradually and check consistency – do not add the full calculated amount all at once.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

Can I use this calculator for concrete instead of mortar?

The formula is optimized for cement mortar (cement + sand + water) used in bricklaying and plastering. Concrete includes coarse aggregates (gravel or crushed stone) and has a different water-cement ratio, typically needing 0.5 to 0.6 water by weight. For concrete estimates, use a dedicated concrete calculator. However, you can approximate thin concrete layers (e.g., <5 cm) by increasing the dosage to 400 kg/m³ and adjusting water content manually.

How do I convert the results to cubic feet or bags of sand?

1 cubic meter equals 35.315 cubic feet. To convert the sand volume from m³ to ft³, multiply by 35.3. For example, 0.3 m³ of sand = 0.3 × 35.3 = 10.6 ft³. Sand is usually sold in 25 kg or 50 lb bags. A 50 lb bag of dry sand occupies about 0.5 ft³, so divide your ft³ result by 0.5 to estimate bag count. For instance, 10.6 ft³ ÷ 0.5 = 21 bags of sand. Always check local bag sizes.

What if my mortar thickness is greater than 5 cm?

For thicknesses above 5 cm, pure mortar becomes prone to shrinkage cracking and may not cure properly. It is more economical and structurally sound to use a layer of concrete (with aggregate) or to apply the mortar in two coats separated by a few days. The calculator will still give a numerical result, but we strongly advise consulting a structural engineer or using a concrete mix for layers exceeding 5 cm.

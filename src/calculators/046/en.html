Planning the lighting layout for a room can be confusing, but the Light Points Calculator simplifies this task by calculating the number of light points and electrical outlets you need based on your room area and the type of use. Whether you are designing a new home, renovating a garage, or setting up a home office, this tool uses recommended lux levels to ensure your space is neither dim nor overlit. Instead of guessing or relying on expensive consultants, you can get a quick, data-driven estimate in seconds.

What the Light Points Calculator Does and When to Use It

The Light Points Calculator is a practical tool for interior design, electrical planning, and construction projects. It takes your room’s area in square metres (or square feet) and the room’s primary use—such as a living room, bedroom, kitchen, bathroom, office, or garage. Based on this, it outputs three critical values:

  • Number of light points (puntos_luz): The recommended minimum number of ceiling or wall-mounted light fixtures.
  • Number of electrical outlets (enchufes): The minimum number of power sockets for general use.
  • Recommended lux level (lux_recomendados): The target illuminance in lux for that type of room.
  • Estimated LED wattage (w_led_estimados): An approximate total wattage needed if using standard 12-watt LED bulbs.

Use this calculator when you are drafting an electrical plan, comparing lighting options, or ensuring your renovation meets basic illumination standards. It is ideal for homeowners, interior designers, electricians, and DIY enthusiasts working in metric regions or those willing to convert imperial measurements.

The Formula Explained Variable by Variable

The calculator uses a simple but effective formula based on standard building practices. Here is how each variable works:

  • area_m2 (A): The floor area of the room in square metres. If you have dimensions in feet, multiply length by width to get square feet, then divide by 10.764 to convert to square metres.
  • uso (lux table): A lookup table assigns a target lux value for each room type: sala (living room) = 150 lux, dormitorio (bedroom) = 100 lux, cocina (kitchen) = 300 lux, bano (bathroom) = 200 lux, oficina (office) = 400 lux, garaje (garage) = 150 lux. These values follow common European lighting recommendations.
  • puntos_luz (light points): Calculated as Math.ceil(A / 10). This means you get one light point for every 10 square metres (roughly 108 square feet), rounded up to at least 1. For example, a 25 m² room needs 3 light points.
  • enchufes (outlets): Calculated as Math.ceil(A / 4), giving one outlet for every 4 square metres (43 square feet), with a minimum of 2 outlets. A 20 m² room therefore needs 5 outlets.
  • w_led_estimados (estimated LED wattage): Multiplies the number of light points by 12 watts, assuming each fixture uses a typical 12-watt LED bulb. This provides a quick energy consumption estimate.

The formula does not account for ceiling height or natural light, but it is a reliable baseline for most standard rooms.

Common Mistakes When Using the Light Points Calculator

Even with a simple tool, users often make errors that affect results. Here are the most frequent ones:

  • Entering area in square feet instead of square metres. The calculator expects metric. A 200 sq ft room entered as 200 m² would return 20 light points and 50 outlets—far too many. Always convert: divide square feet by 10.764.
  • Confusing room types. A "cocina" (kitchen) requires 300 lux, while a "dormitorio" (bedroom) uses only 100 lux. Selecting the wrong type under- or overestimates the lighting need. For example, using "bedroom" for a kitchen could leave you with unsafe dim lighting for cooking.
  • Forgetting the minimum values. The formula enforces at least 1 light point and 2 outlets. Very small rooms like a 2 m² closet still get 1 light point and 2 outlets, which might seem excessive but ensures safety and code compliance in many regions.
  • Assuming the LED wattage is exact. The 12-watt per light point is an average. If you use 9-watt or 15-watt LEDs, adjust accordingly. The wattage estimate is a starting point, not a precise electrical load.
  • Ignoring task lighting. The calculator gives ambient lighting levels. For reading, detailed work, or makeup application, you may need additional desk lamps or under-cabinet lights, especially in kitchens and offices.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I use the Light Points Calculator for rooms in feet and inches?

Yes, but you must convert the area to square metres first. Measure your room in feet, multiply length by width to get square feet, then divide by 10.764. For example, a 12 ft x 14 ft room is 168 sq ft, which equals about 15.6 m². Enter that value into the calculator. If you prefer not to convert manually, many online area converters are available.

Why does the calculator recommend multiple light points for a small room?

The calculation uses one light point per 10 m². In a very small room like a 5 m² bathroom, the formula gives 1 light point (since Math.ceil(0.5) = 1). However, for rooms slightly larger, like 11 m², you get 2 light points. This ensures even distribution of light, avoiding shadows and dark corners. Modern electrical codes in many countries also require a minimum number of switched lighting outlets per room.

What if my room has high ceilings or large windows?

This calculator assumes a standard ceiling height of 2.4 to 3 metres and typical natural light. For high ceilings (over 3.5 metres), you may need more light points or higher wattage to maintain the same lux level at the floor. Large windows can reduce the need for artificial light during the day, but for evening use, the calculated light points still apply. The tool is a baseline; adjust based on your specific conditions, such as adding dimmers or layered lighting.

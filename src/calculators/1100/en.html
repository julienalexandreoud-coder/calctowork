Whether you are planning a new garden deck, repairing an existing one, or estimating materials for a client, the Decking Calculator helps you quickly determine how many boards, screws, and joists you will need for your project. By entering a few basic measurements, you can avoid costly over-ordering or frustrating shortages, and you will get a clear breakdown of the materials required for a professional finish.

What the Decking Calculator Does and When to Use It

The Decking Calculator is designed to take the guesswork out of material estimation. You provide the total area of your deck (in square metres or square feet), the dimensions of the decking boards you plan to use, and the spacing between boards. The calculator then works out the number of boards needed, the linear metres of joist support, and the approximate quantity of fixings such as screws or hidden clips. It is ideal for homeowners tackling a weekend DIY project, builders preparing a quote, or landscape architects specifying materials for a large commercial deck.

You should use this calculator at the planning stage, before you visit the timber yard or place an online order. It also helps you compare different board widths and gap sizes to see how they affect your total cost. For example, a deck measuring 6 m by 4 m (roughly 20 ft by 13 ft) with 140 mm wide boards and a 5 mm gap will require a different number of boards than if you choose 90 mm boards with a 3 mm gap. The calculator handles both metric and imperial inputs, so you can work in whichever system you prefer.

The Formula Explained: Variable by Variable

The core calculation for decking boards is straightforward, but the calculator also accounts for wastage and spacing. Here is how each variable works:

  • Deck Area (A) – The total surface you want to cover, measured in square metres (m²) or square feet (ft²). Multiply length by width to get this value.
  • Board Width (W) – The width of a single decking board, including any tongue or groove if applicable. Common widths are 90 mm, 120 mm, and 140 mm (roughly 3½ in, 4¾ in, and 5½ in).
  • Board Length (L) – The length of each board you intend to buy. Standard lengths are 2.4 m, 3.0 m, 3.6 m, or 4.8 m (8 ft, 10 ft, 12 ft, or 16 ft).
  • Gap (G) – The spacing between adjacent boards, usually 3–6 mm (⅛ in to ¼ in) for drainage and expansion.
  • Number of Boards (N) – Calculated by: (Deck Width in the direction perpendicular to the boards) ÷ (Board Width + Gap). Then round up to the nearest whole number.
  • Wastage Factor (WF) – A percentage added to account for cut-offs, mistakes, and irregular shapes. A typical figure is 10% for rectangular decks and 15% for complex shapes.

For joist calculation, the formula uses the span capacity of the chosen timber. The calculator multiplies the number of joist rows by the deck length to give total linear metres of joist material. Screws or clips are estimated at a rate of around 4–6 fixings per square metre, depending on the fixing system.

Two Worked Examples with Concrete Numbers

Example 1: A Simple Rectangular Deck (Metric)

You are building a deck that measures 5.4 m long by 3.6 m wide. You have chosen 120 mm wide boards that come in 3.0 m lengths, and you want a 4 mm gap between boards.

  • Deck area: 5.4 m × 3.6 m = 19.44 m²
  • Board width plus gap: 120 mm + 4 mm = 124 mm = 0.124 m
  • Number of boards needed across the width: 3.6 m ÷ 0.124 m = 29.03 → round up to 30 boards
  • Total linear metres of board: 30 boards × 5.4 m long = 162 m
  • Number of 3.0 m boards to buy: 162 m ÷ 3.0 m = 54 boards
  • Adding 10% wastage: 54 × 1.1 = 59.4 → round up to 60 boards

So you would order 60 boards of 3.0 m length. For joists spaced 400 mm apart, you would need about 9 rows of joists running the full 5.4 m length, giving 48.6 m of joist timber, plus 10% for trimming.

Example 2: A Deck with an Irregular Shape (Imperial)

Suppose your deck is L-shaped: a main rectangle 12 ft by 10 ft, plus an extension 6 ft by 4 ft. You are using 5½ in wide boards in 12 ft lengths, with a ⅛ in gap. Wastage is 15% because of the shape.

  • Total area: (12 ft × 10 ft) + (6 ft × 4 ft) = 120 ft² + 24 ft² = 144 ft²
  • Board width plus gap: 5.5 in + 0.125 in = 5.625 in = 0.46875 ft
  • Number of boards across the 10 ft width: 10 ft ÷ 0.46875 ft = 21.33 → round up to 22 boards
  • Each board length: you will need some boards at 12 ft and some at 6 ft to cover both sections. The calculator simplifies this by treating the whole area as a single rectangle for boarding and then allowing extra for cuts.
  • Using the full 12 ft length: if you lay boards continuously, the 12 ft length covers both the main area and the extension without a join. Total linear metres: 22 boards × 12 ft = 264 ft.
  • Add 15% wastage: 264 ft × 1.15 = 303.6 ft. At 12 ft per board, you need 303.6 ÷ 12 = 25.3 → round up to 26 boards.

This shows how a more complex shape increases the board count. The calculator would also recommend additional joists around the L‑shaped corner.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Forgetting the gap between boards – Many DIYers calculate as if boards sit flush, but a 3–5 mm gap is essential for drainage and to prevent buckling in wet weather. Omitting the gap leads to ordering too many boards.
  • Not accounting for board direction – If your deck is 4 m wide and you run boards lengthwise, the number of boards is based on the width. If you run them the other way (across the width), the count changes. Always decide the orientation before using the calculator.
  • Using the wrong wastage percentage – A simple rectangle needs only 10% extra, but a deck with many angles, curves, or cut‑outs around trees may need 20% or more. Underestimating wastage can leave you short on material halfway through the job.
  • Mixing metric and imperial without converting – Entering board width in inches and deck area in square metres will give nonsense results. Stick to one unit system for all inputs.
  • Ignoring joist spacing requirements – The calculator assumes a standard spacing (often 400 mm or 16 in), but if you use a softer timber or a heavier decking material, you may need closer spacing. Always check the manufacturer’s recommendations.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can the calculator handle composite decking boards?

Yes. Composite boards are usually wider than timber and have different expansion gaps. Enter the exact board width and the recommended gap (often 5 mm or ¼ in) from the composite manufacturer. The calculator will adjust the board count accordingly. Note that composite decking often requires hidden clips, so the fixing estimate may differ from timber.

How do I measure a non‑rectangular deck for the calculator?

Break the deck into rectangular sections. Measure each rectangle’s length and width, calculate the area separately, then add them together. Enter the total area into the calculator. For the board count, use the widest section’s width to determine how many boards run across. The calculator’s wastage factor will handle the extra cuts needed for the shape.

What if my deck boards are sold in random lengths?

If the timber yard sells boards in random lengths (e.g., 2.1 m to 4.8 m), enter the average length you expect to use. You can also run the calculation twice: once with a shorter length and once with a longer length, to see which option gives less waste. Many calculators allow you to specify a range of lengths to optimise the order.
